Bloodless and Wretched

The Curse Tablet Industry of Greek Sicily, 550--100 BCE

Lead

The ancient Mediterranean had many writing surfaces. Papyrus was common in Egypt, expensive elsewhere. Pottery sherds – ostraka – were used for casual writing across the Greek world. Wax tablets served for temporary notes. Stone was reserved for permanent public inscriptions. Wood was used for documents that needed to last but not forever. But for cursing, from the earliest surviving examples in the late sixth century BCE to the latest in the fifth century CE, the material of choice was almost universally lead.6

The answer lies in the properties of the metal itself. Lead is soft enough to be scratched with a bronze or iron stylus without special tools or training. It can be beaten into thin sheets with a hammer. It does not corrode easily in the acidic soils of Mediterranean cemeteries. It is cheap – a byproduct of silver mining, which was extensive in the Greek world, and available in quantity from the mines of Laurion in Attica, from Spanish and Sardinian deposits, and from local Sicilian sources.7 A scrap of lead large enough for a curse could be had for almost nothing.

But the practical qualities were inseparable from the symbolic ones. Lead is cold in the hand. It is heavy. It does not shine. It looks like something already half-buried. Greek writers linked it to death and downward drag, and the curse-makers seem to have agreed.8 One tablet from Gela says it outright: “As this lead does, so may my spells against those men draw away their honour.”9 The logic is physical. Let the victim become what the metal already is: cold, pinned, inert, pulled toward the dead.10

The Greek name for such a thing meant, literally, a binding. The Latin word pointed to nailing down.1112 Both languages understood the act as physical. Fold the lead. Pierce it. Fix the rival where you want him. At least three of the Kamarina tablets still carry the punctures.13

The ritual was simple. A person wishing to harm, constrain, or disadvantage another would inscribe the target’s name on a thin sheet of lead – sometimes with a formula, sometimes with an invocation to underworld deities, sometimes with nothing more than the name itself. The tablet would then be folded, often rolled into a small cylinder, and deposited in a place where it could reach the powers of the dead: a grave, a well, a chthonic sanctuary, a body of water connected to the underworld.14 Graves were preferred, and the graves of those who had died young or violently were considered most effective – the aoroi (those dead before their time) and the biaiothanatoi (those dead by violence), whose restless spirits were believed to linger near their remains, available for recruitment by the living.15

The depositor may have come alone at night, carrying a lamp, stylus, and folded sheet of lead. Or perhaps a specialist had already prepared the words for a fee. The graves received both possibilities, and the lead does not tell us whose hand scratched the letters.

The Island Where It Started

Sicily was not the only place in the Greek world where curse tablets were produced. They have been found in Athens, in Boeotia, in the Peloponnese, on the islands, in the Black Sea colonies, in Magna Graecia, and eventually throughout the Roman Empire and beyond.16 But Sicily was, as far as we can tell, where the practice began – or at least where the earliest surviving examples were made.

The oldest known curse tablets in the Greek world come from Selinus (modern Selinunte) on the southwestern coast of Sicily, dating to the late sixth century or early fifth century BCE.17 Twenty-two tablets have been recovered from Selinus, most from the early fifth century, many from funerary contexts in the western necropolis and from the sanctuary area associated with Zeus Meilichios – Zeus in his chthonic, underworld aspect, the “gentle” or “appeasing” Zeus to whom the dead were commended.18 Close behind are the tablets from Gela and Kamarina, dating to the first half of the fifth century. A remarkable inscribed lead sheet from Monte Casasia, in the interior of the island, has been dated to the first half of the sixth century BCE and may be the oldest lead inscription in the Sicilian corpus – though whether it is a curse tablet, a religious text, or something else entirely remains debated.19

Why Sicily? The island’s position at the centre of the Mediterranean made it a place where Greek, Phoenician, and indigenous Sikel, Sikan, and Elymian cultures met, collided, and interpenetrated. Greek colonists had been arriving since the eighth century BCE – Syracuse was founded around 733, Gela around 689, Selinus around 628, Kamarina around 598.20 These were not isolated outposts but thriving cities with temples, theatres, agoras, and necropoleis that rivalled those of the Greek mainland. Gela was wealthy enough to attract the tragedian Aeschylus, who spent time there in the 470s and died there around 456 BCE.21 Selinus built some of the largest temples in the Greek world. Kamarina, though smaller and repeatedly destroyed and refounded, maintained a civic culture complete with phratries, magistracies, and – if the curse tablets are any guide – fiercely competitive public life.

Jessica Lamont has argued that the tradition may have spread from west to east, out of Sicily and Italy rather than into them.22 If she is right, these are not provincial imitations. They are early experiments from a colonial world where cities were still making themselves and where rivalry needed every available tool.

The western half of the island was especially suited to this kind of mixing. Greek, Phoenician, and indigenous practices sat close together; sanctuaries and ports linked them; language itself slid between religion and coercion.2324

The Names at Kamarina

Let us look more closely at the Kamarina tablet – corpus ID 8420, designated ISic030032 – because it is one of the most revealing documents in the entire Sicilian curse corpus.

The text opens by enrolling its targets “for failure.”25 The phrasing sounds almost administrative, as if the men named below were being entered into a civic list from which there would be no appeal. Not one lost lawsuit, not one missed chorus prize, but failure in general.

Then come the names. Kerdon is listed first – a name meaning “the profitable one,” from kerdos, “gain” or “profit,” perhaps an artisan or trader whose very livelihood is what the curser envies.26 Puthon son of Diokles follows, the patronymic identifying him precisely within the community. Then Melanthios – “the dark-haired one” or “the one associated with dark things,” a name that appears on the Apellis tablet as well, suggesting it was common in the region, or that certain individuals attracted curses from multiple sources.27 Dion son of Parmenon. Onasimos – “the profitable” or “the useful.” Athanios. Darchon. Tellon. Euthymos – “the good-spirited.” Geloios – a name meaning either “from Gela” or “the laughable one.” Gerys. Hagnos – “the pure.” Chairis. Xenippos.

Then comes the closing blow: may they all be bloodless and wretched.28

To call a person bloodless in Greek was to wish more than injury. The dead were bloodless. Odysseus had to feed the shades with blood before they could speak.29 The curse asks for something close to a living death: not merely pain, but the draining away of force, speech, and agency.

The second word, “wretched,” belongs less to high literature than to the spoken register of ordinary curse and complaint.30 This is not polished poetry. It is direct malice.

The cemetery where these tablets were found – Passo Marinaro – has been the subject of extensive archaeological investigation. Established southeast of the ancient walls of Kamarina, the necropolis was in use from the fifth through the third centuries BCE. Approximately 2,905 burials have been excavated in the roughly 8,000 square metres explored by archaeologists, making it one of the most thoroughly documented colonial Greek cemeteries in Sicily.31

The Italian archaeologist Federica Cordano studied the katadesmoi from Passo Marinaro and identified eleven tablets as curse texts among the much larger assemblage of lead documents recovered from the site.32 Since lead is a soft metal and Kamarina’s soil is acidic, many of the inscribed surfaces are degraded, preventing complete translation. Those for which readings are available present primarily lists of names – sometimes with phratry affiliations, sometimes with patronymics, sometimes bare names alone.

But it was not only curse tablets that were found on lead at Kamarina. The majority of the lead inscriptions from Passo Marinaro are civic documents: registration lists, probably connected to the phratry system that organized Kamarina’s citizen body. These tablets record a man’s name, his patronymic, and his phratry – for example, “Herakleidas son of Archonidas, fourth” (phratry), or “Silanos son of Ithaigeues, fourteenth,” or “Daikrates son of Lykiskos, fifth phratry.”33 Over a hundred such tablets survive, all dating to 480–450 BCE, all from the same cemetery, all on the same material.

This is an extraordinary fact. The same material – lead – served both for mundane civic record-keeping and for magical cursing. The same cemetery received both administrative documents and ritual deposits. The same hands that inscribed phratry lists may have inscribed curse tablets. The technology was identical; only the intent differed.

Why were civic records deposited in a cemetery? Several explanations have been proposed. Perhaps the records were placed in the tombs of deceased phratry members as a form of identification – a permanent register accompanying the citizen to the afterlife.34 Perhaps they were archival copies, stored in the cemetery as a secondary record in case the city’s own archives were destroyed – as happened repeatedly in Kamarina’s turbulent history. Perhaps the boundary between civic and religious life was less distinct than modern categories suggest, and the cemetery was simply a place where important documents were kept, under the protection of the dead and the gods below.

Whatever the explanation, the coexistence matters. Cursing was not an exotic fringe practice set apart from civic life. It used the same metal, the same habits of listing, and often the same burial ground as routine administration. The polis and the graveyard were closer together than our categories like to admit.

Apellis and the Boy He Wanted

The most remarkable curse tablet from Greek Sicily – and one of the most extraordinary documents of ancient magic anywhere in the world – is the so-called Apellis tablet, ISic030019, excavated from Gela and dated to 475–450 BCE.35

It begins with a single word: “Prayer.”36

That opening matters. Apellis presents the tablet not as dirty work but as petition, as if he were asking the gods to ratify what he wants done.

The full translation reads:

Prayer: Apellis, for love of Eunikos, prays that no one be taken more seriously or be more popular than Eunikos, but all should praise and admire him both willingly and unwillingly. For love of Eunikos I mark down all the khoragoi so that they be ineffectual both in word and in deed, along with their sons and fathers, and so that they fail both in the contest and outside the contests. Whoever does not leave him with me – I mark down Kaledias to keep him apart from Apellis, along with all those in between there and here. I mark down Sosias, the one from the shop of the Alkiadai, because of his love of Melanthios. Pyrias, Mysskelos, Damophantos and their khoragos I mark down, along with their sons and fathers, and all the others who arrive here. May no one be taken more seriously than Eunikos either among men or women. As this lead does, so may my spells against those men draw away their honour. For Eunikos may there be victory always, everywhere. On the lead for love of Eunikos I write.37

Apellis does something unusual: he names himself. He wants Eunikos praised, admired, victorious, and left with him.37 Love is not background here. It is the engine of the whole spell.38

His targets are chorus-leaders and their circles, the men who financed and managed festival performance.39 That matters because the tablet opens a small window onto civic life in Gela: choral competition, public prestige, and the social world that might have drawn poets like Aeschylus westward.40

Apellis wants Eunikos to win. He wants every rival chorus-leader to fail – “both in word and in deed,” both in the contest and outside it, along with their sons and fathers. The curse is generational. It does not merely target the competitors themselves but extends to their entire families, as if the taint of failure should radiate outward through kinship networks.

Then comes a personal note. Kaledias is named specifically: Apellis wants to “keep him apart from Apellis,” suggesting that Kaledias is a rival for Eunikos’s affections. The jealousy is naked. Sosias is named next, identified with extraordinary social precision as “the one from the shop of the Alkiadai” – the establishment of the Alkiadai family, probably a commercial operation where Sosias worked or gathered.41 Sosias is cursed “because of his love of Melanthios” – that is, because Sosias loves another young man, Melanthios, and this love somehow threatens the Apellis-Eunikos bond. Perhaps Sosias is trying to draw Eunikos away from Apellis and toward Melanthios. Perhaps the erotic rivalries overlap with the competitive rivalries of the choral circuit.

Then comes the tactile heart of the spell: “As this lead does, so may my spells against those men draw away their honour.” The metal is doing part of the work. Apellis writes on heaviness and asks heaviness to spread.

Business on the Back of the Spell

One of the most tantalizing questions in Sicilian epigraphy is whether the Apellis of the curse tablet is the same Apellis who appears in a commercial document from Gela, ISic030016, dated to 500–475 BCE – one generation earlier than the curse tablet.42

The commercial document is a lead letter recording a business transaction. It reads, in fragmentary form:

Myskon, son of Damis son of Kobetos […] Anthemokritos bade Apellis offer security […] said […] was; he came upon […] of the Leontines […] Myskon sitting along the avenue […] came not having the money, the price of the cattle; he did not buy […] went to Empedokles, son of Mnasimachos […] let […] become liable to act as surety.43

This is a mundane commercial text. Apellis is asked to stand as surety – a financial guarantor – in a cattle transaction. Myskon, sitting on the avenue, did not have the money. Empedokles son of Mnasimachos was consulted. The transaction apparently fell through.

The names overlap. The commercial letter mentions Apellis and Myskon. The curse tablet names Apellis and Mysskelos – the latter a variant that may or may not represent the same person.44 The dates are close: the letter dates to 500–475, the curse tablet to 475–450. If the same Apellis appears in both, then we have a man who was a known commercial figure in Gela – someone trusted enough to be asked to stand surety for a cattle deal – who also engaged in magical cursing on behalf of his beloved. The co-existence of these two documents, on the same material (lead), from the same city, within the same generation, is one of the most vivid illustrations we possess of how thoroughly magic was woven into the fabric of ordinary Greek civic and commercial life.

Peter Wilson has suggested an even tighter connection: the commercial text and the curse may stand on opposite faces of the same recycled sheet of lead.45 If so, the object moved from the world of surety and cattle prices straight into the world of erotic rivalry and public humiliation. Business paper became sorcery without changing material.

The Lawsuit City

Selinus – Selinous in Greek, modern Selinunte – was one of the westernmost Greek cities in Sicily, founded around 628 BCE by colonists from Megara Hyblaea.46 It stood on the frontier between the Greek and Phoenician zones of influence, within sight of the Phoenician stronghold at Motya across the bay. Its position was precarious. In 409 BCE, the Carthaginian general Hannibal Mago sacked the city, killing, according to ancient sources, sixteen thousand of its inhabitants.47

Before that catastrophe, Selinus was a prosperous and ambitious city. Its temples – designated by modern scholars with letters, since their ancient dedications are mostly unknown – rank among the largest in the Greek world. Temple G, probably dedicated to Apollo, measured over 110 metres in length.48 The city’s legal and religious sophistication is attested by the famous lex sacra of Selinus, a lead tablet from the fifth century BCE that prescribes purification rituals for the community.49

The twenty-two curse tablets from Selinus are concentrated in the early fifth century BCE. Most were found in the western necropolis, some from the sand layer overlying the campo di stele, a sacred area associated with the sanctuary of Zeus Meilichios.50 Their content is predominantly judicial: the tablets target opponents in lawsuits. This is “litigation magic” – the use of curses to hamstring one’s adversary before, during, or after a legal proceeding.

One Selinus tablet targets a man named Chamis with terrible economy: “Chamis, I hold you, and with illness’s turning-point, for failure.”51 The wording pins him at the crisis point of disease, the moment when a body either rallies or slips.52 The curser is not simply asking for sickness. He is asking for suspension at the worst possible threshold.

The medical precision is striking. Whoever wrote it knew how much fear lived inside the idea of a crisis unresolved.

Who Wrote for Hire

Who made these tablets? The question divides scholars.

One view holds that most early Sicilian curse tablets were self-service productions. The technology was simple: anyone who could read and write could scratch names on a scrap of lead. The formulae were minimal – in many cases, nothing more than a list of names, with no invocation, no ritual language, no elaborate theology. The Kamarina name-list tablets, with their bare catalogues of targets, suggest an almost industrial simplicity: get a piece of lead, scratch the name, fold it, bury it.53

The counter-argument points to the formulaic consistency of the tablets. The Kamarina tablets use recognizably standardized language: gegrabatai epi dyspragiai (“inscribed for failure”), anaimatoi eston (“may they be bloodless”).54 The Selinus tablets employ a coherent vocabulary of binding and restraining. The Apellis tablet displays not only formulaic competence but genuine literary skill – the structure of the prayer, the deployment of the sympathetic-magic formula, the careful social identification of the targets. This level of sophistication suggests professional composition, even if the client was the one who actually scratched the letters.

The verbs of writing that appear on the tablets – engrapho (“I inscribe”), katagrapho (“I write down”), apographo (“I enrol”) – have been analysed as evidence for a distinctively literate practice of cursing that is rooted in the technology of writing itself.55 These are not the verbs of oral incantation; they are the verbs of documentation, of registration, of bureaucratic record-keeping. To curse someone by “enrolling” them on lead is to use the mechanisms of the state against them – to turn the technology of civic administration into a weapon.

Sicilian curse tablets from 500–450 BCE exhibit textual features that ground them firmly in the realm of writing rather than speech: columnar layouts, abbreviations, scribal symbols, and the kind of spatial organization found in administrative lists rather than in spontaneous composition.56 This suggests that the people who composed these texts – whether the clients themselves or hired specialists – were literate individuals familiar with the conventions of written documentation. In a society where literacy was far from universal, this is itself a significant marker of social class.

The Messana name-list tablet shows the minimalist end of the practice.57 Just names. No gods, no plea, no explanation. In that form, writing the name and burying it was enough. The lead was expected to understand.

When the Scripts Got Longer

Five centuries after the Kamarina tablets, the practice of cursing on lead was still alive in Sicily – but it had changed almost beyond recognition.

The defixio from Messana designated ISic030075, corpus ID 47186, dates to the first or second century CE. It is not a simple name-list. It is a fully elaborated ritual text, invoking multiple deities by name and prescribing specific actions for the spirits of the dead.

The translation, reconstructed from the Greek, reads:

I bind down, Nymphikos, I deposit her with you, Lord of Hades, for eternal possession of her… and Kore and the Erinyes, so that from her heights she may be brought low, and you will lead her away most swiftly, and down into the earth the Erinyes shall carry the little human being. I bind down, Nymphikos, I deposit her with you, Hermes, Guide of All, so that you may lead her down into the earth most swiftly. Take from her a sacrificial offering on the thirtieth day and run, so that you may shut her up beneath the earth, and among humans no longer allow this other Medusa to go about.58

The target is a woman. She is compared to Medusa – the Gorgon whose gaze turned men to stone. The curser wants her dragged down into the earth by the Erinyes (the Furies), deposited with Hades for “eternal possession,” and shut away from the world of the living. The reference to a “sacrificial offering on the thirtieth day” – hierion triakadin – reveals that the depositor of this tablet was expected to return to the gravesite thirty days later to perform a follow-up ritual, probably an offering to the dead.59

This no longer feels like self-service magic. The invoked powers, the ritual timetable, and the language all suggest someone practiced in this work, someone who could sell not just a curse but a whole sequence of actions.

Across the centuries the technology barely changes: lead, stylus, grave. What changes is the density of the script around it, the cost of expertise, the number of gods summoned in to help.

The Map of the Trade

If we map the known curse tablets from ancient Sicily, a pattern emerges.60

The earliest tablets – sixth century to early fifth century BCE – cluster in the Greek cities of the south and west: Selinus, Gela, Kamarina, and possibly Monte Casasia in the interior.61 These are the frontier cities, the ones closest to the Phoenician zone and to the indigenous Sikel populations of the interior. They are also the cities with the most competitive civic cultures, the ones where colonial Greek communities were still establishing themselves, jostling for position, and working out the social hierarchies that would define their civic lives.

By the fifth and fourth centuries, curse tablets appear across a wider swathe of the island. The Kamarina tablets, over a hundred strong, dominate the corpus numerically. But individual tablets or small groups have been found at other sites across Sicily. Lilybaeum (modern Marsala), on the western tip of the island, produced two curse tablets that provide the only Sicilian examples of the verb katadeo – “I bind down” – the classical term for curse-making.62 Morgantina, an inland site with both Greek and indigenous occupation, yielded ten curse tablets from a pit in the sanctuary of the Chthonic Deities, dating to around 100 BCE. Four of these tablets target the same woman – a slave named Venusta – using nearly identical wording, suggesting either a particularly determined client or a professional who produced multiple tablets for the same commission.63

The map suggests a habit that was never just local. It spread with cities, sanctuaries, and rivalries, and perhaps with the people who sold words for hire.

Civic Lists, Same Metal

We must not lose sight of the non-magical lead tablets from Kamarina, because they provide the social context without which the curse tablets cannot be fully understood.

Between 480 and 450 BCE, the citizens of Kamarina produced a remarkable series of lead documents recording the names of individuals, their fathers’ names, and their phratry affiliations.64 More than 130 of these tablets survive in the corpus, each typically recording a single name: “Aischylos son of Aristios, fourteenth phratry.” “Antisthenes son of Parmenon, fourteenth phratry.” “Herakleidas son of Archonidas, fourth phratry.” “Silanos son of Ithaigeues, fourteenth phratry.” “Daikrates son of Lykiskos, fifth phratry.”65

The phratries – kinship-based subdivisions of the citizen body – were fundamental to the political and social organization of Greek cities. At Kamarina, the tablets attest at least fourteen named phratries, each identified by an ordinal number: first, fourth, fifth, eighth, ninth, eleventh, twelfth, fourteenth.66 Some tablets add financial notations: dekalitron – “ten litrai” – perhaps a registration fee, a tax assessment, or a record of property qualification.67

The names on the phratry tablets overlap with the names on the curse tablets. Melanthios appears on both the Kamarina curse tablet (8420) and the Apellis tablet from Gela (8373). Sosias appears on both the Apellis tablet and the Kamarina name-list defixio ISic030031, which lists “Sosias son of Archonidas” and “Sosias son of Archias” among a catalogue of targets including Menon, Echecratides, Archedamos, Aristodamos, Charipos, Archethales, Chiron, Herakleidas, and Archytas.68 The name Geloios – “the Geloan” or “the one from Gela” – appears on the curse tablet and perhaps betrays the target’s origin in the neighbouring city. Some of these are common names; the overlap may be coincidental. But in a small colonial community where a few hundred families constituted the entire citizen body, the probability of genuine connections is high.

The phratry tablets reveal a city already in the habit of sorting men into named groups, recording affiliation, and fixing identity onto lead. In that setting, the step from list to curse was not large.

Before and After

The oldest lead inscription from Sicily – and possibly the oldest lead tablet in the Greek world – was found at Monte Casasia, an inland site in the territory between Kamarina and Syracuse. It has been dated to the first half of the sixth century BCE, making it roughly contemporary with the earliest Greek curse tablets from anywhere.69

The text is fragmentary and heavily debated. What survives reads, in translation:

[…] from the garden, driving […] by name Tetragos […] windswept shore […] of waters […] blessed is he who […] these things throughout his house and in his mind holds […] of the blessed ones along the wagon-road […] the voice […] subdue, and master the unwilling by force […].70

The language is remarkable. “Blessed is he who” (olbios ho) is a formula associated with mystery religions – particularly the Orphic and Eleusinian traditions, where initiates were promised blessedness in the afterlife.71 “Subdue, and master the unwilling by force” (damnameneu damason de kakos aekontas anankiai) is a command formula that belongs squarely to the world of magic – the language of compelling, of forcing, of overcoming resistance through supernatural power.72

Is it a curse, a hymn, a mystery text, or some mixture of all three? The fragment refuses to settle.6970 That uncertainty is part of the point. Very early on, lead in Sicily was already hosting language that slid between prayer, coercion, and poetry.

At the other end of the timeline stands a fragment from Hybla Heraea (modern Ragusa), in the southeastern corner of the island, dated to the fourth or fifth century CE – nearly a millennium after the Kamarina tablets.73

The text is brief and damaged:

[…] come out, devil! Adonai […] Sabaoth […] ARAKIA […].74

Exelthe diabole – “come out, devil” – is an exorcism formula. Adonai and Sabaoth are Jewish divine names, widely adopted in the syncretic magical tradition of late antiquity. The combination of Jewish divine names with a Greek exorcism command is typical of the late antique magical koine – a shared vocabulary of power-words drawn from multiple religious traditions and deployed by practitioners who served clients regardless of their faith.75

This fragment from Hybla shows the practice still alive after a millennium. Gods change, languages blend, formulas mutate, but the medium and the instinct remain: write command into lead, place it where the dead or the divine might act on it.

What People Paid For

Who commissioned these tablets? For whom were they made? And against whom were they directed?

The surviving evidence suggests several broad categories of motive.76

Competition. The Apellis tablet targets rivals in choregic competition – an agonistic context where prestige, patronage, and public honour were at stake. Competitive cursing is well attested in Athens, where curse tablets have been found targeting rival craftsmen, rival litigants, rival athletes, and rival performers.77 In Sicily, the colonial context may have intensified the competitive pressures. In a newly founded city, social hierarchies were not inherited from ancient tradition but actively constructed through public performance, wealth display, and institutional achievement. The stakes of a choral competition in fifth-century Gela were not merely artistic; they were existential. To win was to establish your faction, your patron, your beloved as dominant. To lose was to concede that dominance to someone else.

Litigation. The Selinus tablets target opponents in lawsuits. This is the most common category of Greek curse tablets across the entire Mediterranean. A litigant who feared losing his case might commission a curse to silence the opposing speaker, confuse the opposing witnesses, or sway the judges.78 The Chamis tablet – “I hold you in the crisis of illness so that you cannot achieve your objectives” – may be a litigation curse disguised as a medical curse, aimed at preventing Chamis from appearing in court or performing effectively if he did.

Erotic rivalry. Apellis curses Kaledias to keep him away from Eunikos. Sosias is cursed because of his love for Melanthios. The erotic dimension of the Apellis tablet is inseparable from the competitive dimension: love, patronage, and public performance were intertwined in a world where a wealthy man’s attachment to a young performer was both a personal passion and a civic investment.

Commerce. The presence of the Apellis commercial letter – recording a cattle transaction involving surety and payment disputes – alongside the curse tablet suggests that commercial rivalries could also motivate cursing. If your business competitor was thriving and you were not, a curse tablet offered a way to level the field that did not require you to improve your own products.

Personal hatred. The Messana tablet against the unnamed woman compared to Medusa is pure malice. The curser wants the target dragged to the underworld by the Furies, shut beneath the earth, removed from the world of the living. The language of this tablet is not competitive or strategic; it is visceral. Someone hated this woman with a hatred that demanded supernatural intervention.

What the Lead Kept

There is something deeply human in these objects. A man sits in the dark, near a grave, scratching names on a thin sheet of lead. He folds the sheet. He drives a nail through it. He pushes it into the earth, into the gap between the stones of a burial, or drops it down a well, or leaves it at the threshold of a chthonic sanctuary. Then he walks away, back to the city, back to the agora, back to the competition or the courtroom or the shop, carrying with him the knowledge that somewhere beneath the ground, a piece of lead bears the names of his enemies, and the dead know those names.

Did it work? The question is not answerable in the terms the ancients would have understood. The curse tablets do not come with follow-up reports. We do not know whether Kerdon failed, whether Chamis remained sick, whether Eunikos won his competition, whether the woman from Messana was brought low. What we know is that people kept making these tablets for over a thousand years – from the sixth century BCE to the fifth century CE and beyond – across the entire Mediterranean world, in Greek, Latin, Aramaic, Hebrew, Coptic, and demotic Egyptian.79 The practice was not marginal. It was not confined to the ignorant or the desperate. It was a technology of spiritual agency, available to anyone who could afford a scrap of lead and a visit to the cemetery.

The more than 1,500 curse tablets that have been recovered from the ancient Mediterranean world represent, as John Gager has argued, a dimension of classical life that was previously inaccessible – the world of private religion, personal anxiety, and individual agency in the face of uncontrollable circumstances.80 The official religion of the Greek polis – its temples, its festivals, its public sacrifices – addressed the community as a whole. The curse tablets addressed the individual. They were private rituals for private problems: the rival who was too successful, the lover who was straying, the opponent who might win the case, the enemy who would not die.

In Greek Sicily, where colonial communities were building new cities on contested ground, where Greek met Phoenician and Sikel in a landscape of overlapping gods and unstable hierarchies, the curse tablet was not a curiosity. It was one more instrument for surviving rivalry. Assembly, lawsuit, festival, market, grave: all were places where advantage could be made or lost. The lead tablet belonged to that same world.

Bloodless, wretched, all of them.

Somewhere beneath the soil of Kamarina, the lead remembers.
