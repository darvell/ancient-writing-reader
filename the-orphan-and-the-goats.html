Family, Property & Survival

The Orphan and the Goats

Aethiops, Sakaon, and the Slow Collapse of Theadelphia, ca. 280-332 CE

Theadelphia Sakaon Aethiops goats orphans village administration Late Roman Egypt petitions livestock Arsinoite nome

20 primary documents · 22 notes

Sakaon's house

Sakaon first appears securely in 310, in a census declaration sent to Ulpius Alexander, the census official for the Heptanomia.2 He was forty-five then. The clerk noted the identifying scar on his left shin, because Egyptian documents liked bodies to be as legible as names.2 He lived in Theadelphia in the Fayum, in his own house, and he swore that he was concealing nobody.2

The declaration is crowded with kin. Sakaon lists himself, his sixteen-year-old son Aeil, two brothers, a nephew, two more kinsmen, and their sons.2 Nine taxable persons in all.2 He also says that nobody had been living with him “for a long time at all” μηδενός μοι συνοικοῦντος ἐκ πολλοῦ χρόνου.2 That is one of those papyrological phrases that becomes sharper the longer you look at it. The house is full of related men, but the household is unstable enough that the declaration has to insist on who counts and who does not.

Sakaon could not write. A certain Konnaros wrote for him “as he is illiterate” ὑπὲρ αὐτοῦ ἀγραμμάτου.2 That subscription trails behind him for decades. Other people keep taking his words down. They write for him when he buys, borrows, petitions, and swears. He knew the system well enough to use it constantly; he did not control the pen.

He was already buying animals. In 299, one year before the census, a man named Dioskorammon sold him a white she-donkey.5 The contract included the kind of practical clause no literary source bothers with: Sakaon was allowed to keep the animal’s foal until it was weaned, for five months, and then had to restore it to the seller.5 It is a small village bargain, but it tells you what Sakaon’s world ran on. Animals were capital, tools, offspring, and collateral all at once.

What goats were worth

The orphan petition never gives the size of Aethiops’s flock. It does not need to. Everybody reading it already knew what goats were worth.

The Sakaon archive preserves that value from the side. In 318 Sakaon himself petitioned after thieves took sixteen goats from a grazing field in the district of Bernikis Aigialou.3 Ten years later, in 328, a lease from Theadelphia sets out the normal terms for hiring ten mature female goats for two years.6 The rent was steep and brutally clear. The lessee owed yearly offspring, five male kids and five female, “equal, without reckoning, and without risk” ἐξ ἴσου ἀνυπόλογα καὶ ἀκίνδυνα.6 He had to do all work connected with the animals at the proper times, pay the tax on the goats himself, and at the end return the original ten alive.6

That one contract says more about village livestock than a page of explanation could. Goats were assessed. Goats were taxed. Goats were productive enough that the rent on ten of them was not cash but ten kids a year. And the owner still expected the breeding females back alive.

There is an older comparison in the same documentary orbit. Around 280, a widow named Aurelia Artemis from nearby Thraso petitioned because the village dekaprotos Syrion had persuaded her husband Ganis to pasture his flock and then, when the husband died, took over sixty goats and sheep for himself.4 Artemis says he rushed in while the body was still laid out, using local power to strip “the property of my young children” τὰ τῶν νηπίων μου τέκνα.4 That papyrus is not about Sakaon or Aethiops, but it belongs to the same countryside and the same moral weather. A dead man leaves animals behind; powerful kin or neighbors move fast; widows and minors run to the governor.

That is why Aethiops’s petition is so tense even though the object at stake sounds small to modern ears. A grandaunt was not trying to take a few sentimental goats. She was moving on the one piece of patrimony that could still multiply.

Sixteen goats in the night

Sakaon knew exactly how quickly a flock could vanish.

On 7 September 318 he submitted a petition to the pagus official Olympius.3 Sakaon says that his tenant was grazing the livestock of a man named Rufus, a former primipilarius, in the field of Bernikis Aigialou, when some “evildoers” κακοῦργοι came by night and carried off sixteen goats.3 Sakaon investigated, identified two suspects living on rural estates, and begged the official to summon them, restore what had been taken, and grant the proper legal redress.3

This is one of the clearest moments in the archive when Sakaon steps out from under taxes and appears as a man who knows animals. He is leasing, grazing, tracking, and detecting. The petition is competent. He names names. He knows which field mattered, whose animals they were, where the suspects lived, and how theft should be processed.3

It also tells us that theft was a normal village risk. The goats were not taken in a market dispute or a formal seizure. They were stolen at night from pasture. A flock in the Fayum had to survive disease, tax, rent, and reproduction, but first it had to survive the dark.

Read beside Aethiops’s petition, the episode sharpens. When the orphan says Annous has fixed her eyes on his father’s animals, Sakaon does not have to imagine what it means to lose a flock. He has already stood in the same bureaucracy complaining that sixteen goats vanished between dusk and morning.13

A marriage turned into a custody battle

The archive also shows why Sakaon may have been the right man, or at least the available man, to guard an orphan from his own kin. His family had already learned how quickly private agreements could collapse once money, remarriage, and village office entered the room.

In 312 a man named Melas petitioned the prefect about a marriage he had arranged for his son Zoilos.7 The girl, Taeus, had been promised from childhood. After her mother’s death, Melas says, Sakaon took another wife, a woman named Kamoutis, and Melas cared for the girl “as if for my own daughter” καθάπερ ἰδίας θυγατρός.7 He then called together friends, performed all the usual marriage business, and married her to his son.7

At that point, Sakaon allegedly tried to break the match under pressure from Kamoutis. He seized the girl back and kept her in his own house.7 Melas’s petition says he even had to borrow money from a tribune to satisfy a demand connected with the marriage settlement, only to discover that Kamoutis now wanted the same girl married instead to her own nephew Sarmates.7

The case is a tangle, and the damaged lines leave some details uncertain. But the overall shape is unmistakable. A girl with value in marriage becomes the center of a struggle among a father, a stepmother, a prospective father-in-law, and rival male claimants. The document keeps circling the same things: payment, custody, and the danger of violence if the arrangement breaks down.7

Melas also slips in another line that matters. Zoilos, the young man whose marriage is being obstructed, is at that moment serving as sitologos, a village grain official, in an already depleted village and paying no small sum each year into the sacred treasury.7 Village office and family crisis are sitting right on top of each other. That pairing comes up again and again in Sakaon’s papers. Nobody in Theadelphia handled one burden at a time.

Village office meant paying first

The dullest texts in the Sakaon archive are often the most revealing. They show what it meant to be one of the men who had to keep Theadelphia functioning after richer or luckier people had already slipped away.

By 312 Sakaon was serving as komarches, village headman, and appears in receipts for the cash value of grain.8 By 319 and 320 he is part of the team of sitologoi, the grain collectors, delivering wheat and barley from Theadelphia for soldiers and for state bakeries.910 One receipt records 43 artabas of clean wheat for soldiers under a praepositus.9 Another string of receipts records barley, then 111 artabas of wheat at the city harbor, then 51 more, then 92 artabas delivered to bakeries, and then another 9 artabas besides.10

This is the administrative texture of the village. Grain went out in measured batches. Names were written beside each installment. Somebody had to advance transport, show up, sign, and keep the numbers straight.

It was never just grain. In September 324 Sakaon and his fellow komarch receive a receipt for the “price of purple” τιμῆς πορφύρας, three talents and 900 denarii, charged to Theadelphia.11 Two days later another official acknowledges the village share of half a worker for the sanctuary of Hermes at Memphis, plus three months of fodder for the worker’s donkey.12 By December the village is paying the wages of its allotted worker for the bakeries of Memphis.13 In June 325 another receipt records the share due from Theadelphia for a laborer and a carpenter dispatched to the alabaster quarries.14 In August 327 the village is paying for a builder and a carpenter, then for bakery workers tied to Alexandria.15

These are formulaic scraps, but their accumulation is the story. Sakaon’s village had to send grain, pay cash conversions, support workers, feed animals, and meet levies that reached Memphis, the quarries, and Alexandria. A villager like Sakaon did not experience empire as a law code or an emperor’s portrait. He experienced it as the next receipt.

Borrowing against the next season

The same years show Sakaon trying to keep himself liquid.

In 321 a woman from the Hermouthiake quarter leased from him about twenty arourai of grain land near Theadelphia on a half-share arrangement.16 Sakaon, as landowner, was to provide seed advances and draft animals from irrigation through harvest; the tenant was to do all the year’s work and would keep half the crop free of tax and transport charges, while the public dues and annona remained Sakaon’s responsibility.16 It is a practical arrangement, but it also tells us he had fields and obligations enough to require subleasing.

In 325 someone from the Hermopolite nome wanted to lease thirty grain arourai from Sakaon near Bubastos.17 In 328 he was still letting out goats.6 The archive keeps catching him in the middle rank: not poor enough to have nothing to lease, not secure enough to stop moving property around.

Then comes the debt. On 1 October 328 Sakaon acknowledged receiving 340 talents of silver capital from a former kosmetes and city councillor, money to be used for two months and repaid by the fourth of Choiak, with interest if he missed the deadline.18 The contract gave the lender recourse not only against Sakaon personally but against all his property, “as from a legal judgment” καθάπερ ἐκ δίκης.18

Three hundred and forty talents is the kind of figure that startles because it sounds too large and too abstract. But the point is simple enough. Sakaon, who had fields, goats, and official experience, still needed emergency cash. The village man who turned up as Aethiops’s guardian was also the village man signing short-term loans under the hand of literate others.

Three men left

The most famous text in the archive comes a little later, in 331 or 332, and it makes every earlier petition look more desperate in retrospect.19

Sakaon, Heron, and Kanaoug petitioned the prefect as representatives of the community of Theadelphia.19 “We are three persons in the village,” they say, “who pay taxes on behalf of the whole village” τρεῖς ἐσμεν … οἵτινες εἰσφέρομεν ὑπὲρ ὅλης τῆς κώμης.19 The burden still stood on 500 arourai, much of it not even being ploughed, and on a roll of twenty-five men including treasury employees.19 The village, they say, had been reduced to severe distress.

So they went searching for their fellow villagers. They found five of them with their families on the estate of Eulogius in the Oxyrhynchite nome.19 The landlord, a vine-dresser, and a cultivator refused even to let them approach the door, driving them back with violence.19 They found three more in the Cynopolite nome holding more than a hundred arourai of crown land.19 The petition asks the prefect to have the runaways returned “with their families” so that the three remaining taxpayers can continue standing in the village.19

If you want one text that explains the pressure behind the rest of the archive, this is it. Theadelphia was not simply poor. It was hollowing out. Every office became harder because there were fewer people left to distribute it across. Every surviving family became easier to squeeze. Every death made the remaining property more tempting.

When Aethiops called himself an orphan, he was not speaking from inside a stable village order. He was speaking from inside the kind of place where three men were trying to hold the tax roll together with their bare hands.

Why Annous moved when she did

Aethiops’s petition is sharpest when it asks why Annous waited.1 While the boy’s father was alive, he says, she had made no complaint at all. She had not stirred over anything. Only after his father’s death did she suddenly attack.1 The argument is legal, but the force of it is emotional. She treated the death as windfall.

The papyrus even pauses to sneer at her age. Annous was over sixty, it says, and had spent her life in peace until now.1 That is not neutral description. It is litigation. A boy and his guardian are trying to convince the governor that an old woman has no plausible preexisting claim and has awakened too late to be believed.

There is another detail in the petition that matters, though it remains slightly opaque. Aethiops says his father had performed three sitologiai, three terms or sets of obligations connected with grain collection, and adds that Annous is liable in connection with them.1 The exact legal relation is difficult to reconstruct from this damaged and formulaic Greek. What can be said securely is that the case was not about goats alone. Grain office, liability, and family property were tangled together in the background.

That helps explain why the petition asks not for damages but for intervention, for the woman’s aggression to be cut off before it goes further.1 Aethiops and Sakaon were trying to stop a seizure in motion.

Fifteen years later, when the grain register of Theadelphia was written up for the tenth indiction, heirs keep surfacing beside the quantities owed.20 Among them appear the heirs of Annous.20 That line does not prove anything specific about her claim against Aethiops. It does show that she belonged to the class of village people whose property and obligations survived them in paperwork. By 336, Theadelphia was full of dead names still paying through heirs.20

What became of the flock

We do not know whether Aethiops kept his goats. The archive preserves the petition and the governor’s forwarding note, not the result.1 That is normal in papyrology and maddening in narrative. We usually get the cry for help, not the answer.

But the Sakaon archive lets us say more than we otherwise could. We know what the flock meant. We know what it meant to lose sixteen goats in the night.3 We know that widows and minors in the same district could see animals taken from the deathbed itself.4 We know the rent on ten breeding females, the tax burden on village livestock, and the way office, debt, and kinship pressed down on the same few households.691018

We also know that the violence did not stop. In 342, a man called Sakaon son of Satabous, almost certainly the same Sakaon by then much older, complained that a man named Heron son of Sakaon had seized eighty-two sheep while Sakaon was trying to meet a grain obligation.21 Another copy of the same complaint says the assault was violent and lawless.22 If that identification is right, then the archive that began with Sakaon’s kin-packed census and reached Aethiops’s orphan petition ended with Sakaon’s own son raiding his flock. Even if the familial identification were wrong, the pattern would remain the same. In Theadelphia, tax pressure and family predation kept converging on animals.

That is the real scale of Aethiops’s case. A boy went to law over goats. Underneath that small fact lay a whole village economy in which goats were rent-bearing assets, taxable objects, stolen goods, dowry leverage, and the fastest thing to snatch when a household lost its strongest adult.

An orphan in a failing village

The paperwork does not care about Aethiops’s face. It does not tell us how old he was, only that he was under age.1 It does not tell us whether he spoke confidently or wept or let Sakaon do most of the talking. It does not tell us whether Annous had ever held him as a baby before deciding his father’s death had left something worth taking.

What it does preserve is the scale of the loss. In a healthier place, an orphan’s patrimony might have included enough land, tenants, and reserves to absorb a predatory relative. In the Theadelphia of these documents, survival rested on much thinner margins. A white she-donkey mattered.5 Ten leased goats mattered.6 Forty-three artabas sent to soldiers mattered.9 Three men left in the village mattered most of all.19

So the governor received yet another petition from the Fayum. Another villager saying he had been wronged. Another request that the state do, just this once, what the village no longer could do for itself.

The grand phrase in the opening line says that governors defend wronged orphans.1 The archive around that line tells a different story. By the time Aethiops reached the governor’s file, his world had already failed several times before the reed pen touched papyrus: when both parents died, when the flock became vulnerable, when kin saw opportunity, and when a village had been reduced so far that the only shield left was a guardian who could not write his own name.
