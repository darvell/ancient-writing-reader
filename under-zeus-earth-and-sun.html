The Letter

Chairemon toi agoranomoi chairein. Dos eleutherosin Euphrosynei doulei…

Chaeremon to the agoranomus, greeting. Grant freedom to Euphrosyne, a slave, aged about thirty-five years, born in her owner’s house of the slave Demetrous. She is being set at liberty under Zeus, Earth, and Sun, by ransom, by her mistress Aloine, daughter of Komon son of Dionysius, of Oxyrhynchus, under the wardship of Komon, the son of Aloine’s deceased brother Dioscorus. The price paid is ten drachmae of coined silver and ten talents, three thousand drachmae of copper.4

That is the whole document. It takes less than a minute to read. It closes with the standard farewell – erroso, “be well” – and the date, written twice. At the bottom, in what may be the same hand or a second one, the endorsement: “Chaeremon, register it. Phaophi 19.”5

The document is catalogued as P.Oxy. 1.48. It ended up at Harrow School, in England. It sits today in the Vaughan Library, a long way from the agoranomus’s office where it was filed nineteen centuries ago.6 Grenfell and Hunt gave it a short note. They translated the Greek. They moved on to the next papyrus.

The form repays lingering. It is a letter from Chaeremon to an unnamed agoranomus, using the epistolary convention of the period: name of sender, name of recipient, chairein (greetings). The agoranomus in Roman Egypt was not a judge or a magistrate in the political sense but a notary, a market official who registered legal transactions, authenticated documents, and maintained the archives of civic life.7 Multiple agoranomi served simultaneously in a city the size of Oxyrhynchus. The one who received Chaeremon’s letter is not named. He was a functionary whose job was to stamp the paper and file it.

And Chaeremon? The name itself means “rejoice” – chairein, the same verb that opens the letter. Chaeremon to the agoranomus, chairein. The man whose name meant joy wrote the letter that freed a woman whose name meant joy. This is likely coincidence. Both names were common. But the coincidence sits there, in the Greek, waiting for someone to notice.

A Name That Means Joy

Euphrosyne comes from eu (good, well) and phren (mind, heart). It means something like “good cheer,” or “mirth,” or simply “joy.”8

It is also the name of one of the Three Graces – the Charites – daughters of Zeus and the Oceanid Eurynome. Aglaea, Splendor. Thalia, Good Cheer. Euphrosyne, Joy. In Hesiod’s telling, they attended Aphrodite. They danced at divine weddings. They were the embodiment of everything graceful and delightful in the world.9

It was also, as it happens, a common name for slaves.

The name of a Grace, a goddess of beauty and delight, was given to a piece of property.

This is one of the small cruelties the ancient world embedded in its language. Slaves in the Greco-Roman world typically bore a single name, without the patronymic that identified free persons – no “daughter of” or “son of” to anchor them in a family line.10 And the names themselves often followed a pattern of aspirational beauty: Plousia, “Rich.” Agathe, “Good.” Chresimus, “Useful.” Euphrosyne, “Joy.”11 Whether these names were chosen by the slave’s mother or assigned by the owner is usually impossible to determine. Perhaps Demetrous, laboring through childbirth in the house of Aloine’s family sometime around 51 CE, named her daughter Joy. Perhaps the family did, the way one might name a new horse or a dog – choosing a word that pleased the ear, that projected the qualities desired in the creature. The mythological resonance – a Grace, an attendant of Aphrodite, a daughter of Zeus – may have been intentional. It may have been irrelevant. We do not know.

The name survives in the papyrus record exactly once: on the day it was entered into the notary’s files alongside the word doulei – slave.12

The name was not unique to women. A document from Oxyrhynchus dated to 175 CE records the examination of slaves belonging to a Roman citizen named Trunnia, including one named Euphrosynus – the male form of the same name. He, too, was certified as “born in the house.”13 The popularity of the name across genders and across a century tells us something. It was a name that owners kept reaching for. Joy was a quality the slaveholding household wished to project, if not to feel.

Born in the House

The Greek is precise: oikogenei ek doules Demetroutos – “born in the house, of the slave Demetrous.”14

The word oikogenes – from oikos, house, and genos, birth – marks a specific legal and social category. Euphrosyne was not purchased at market. She was not captured in war. She was not a foundling scooped from the gutter and raised as property, though that too was common enough in Roman Egypt.15 She was born inside the household, the daughter of a woman who was already enslaved there. The Latin equivalent is verna – a home-born slave, raised alongside the master’s own children, speaking their language, knowing their customs, woven into the fabric of the household from the first day of life.16

The alternative routes into slavery were harsher. A document from Oxyrhynchus, dated to just two years before Euphrosyne’s birth, preserves a dispute that illuminates the casual brutality of the system. A man named Syrus “entrusted to the keeping of my wife Saraeus… a boy foundling named Heraclas, whom he had picked up from the gutter, to be nursed.”17 The foundling died. Syrus then attempted to seize Saraeus’s own freeborn son Apion and carry him off into slavery as a replacement. The foundling had been picked up from the gutter, nursed at a stranger’s breast, and dead before he could speak; his death became a pretext to enslave another child. The boundaries between free and slave, between legitimate child-rearing and human trafficking, were porous in ways that the legal categories fail to capture.

Euphrosyne, at least, was spared this. She was born into a known household, to a known mother.

House-born slaves occupied a distinct and paradoxical position in the hierarchy of unfreedom. They were, by most accounts, more valued than slaves purchased from the open market.18 They were more likely to be trained in skilled occupations – weaving, cooking, personal attendance. They were more likely to be permitted to form families of their own, in the legal non-marriage that Romans called contubernium.19 And they were, disproportionately, the ones who eventually got freed. The proportion of oikogeneis in manumission documents is higher than their share of the slave population would predict, suggesting that owners felt some degree of obligation toward those who had grown up under their roof.20

A house-born slave was still property. A document from the Oxyrhynchite nome, dated to the mid-third century CE, preserves a dispute over the ownership of a house-born slave family spanning three generations: Taaphunchis the slave, her daughter Thaesis, and Thaesis’s children Apollonius and Harseis. The grandmother had been inherited, the daughter registered in the house-to-house census, the grandchildren claimed by competing heirs. Three generations of human beings, their bodies and labor passed down like furniture.21

And a late antique document – fifth or sixth century, from the Christian era – records the flight of five house-born slaves described as “rope-makers, thin in body.” Their owner’s instructions were blunt: “Make every effort everywhere to track them down and have them shackled over their entire body and sent to me.”22 In the ancient mind there was no contradiction between being “born in the house” and being ordered back in chains.

Euphrosyne was born around 51 CE, in the first decade of Nero’s reign, into this world. Her mother Demetrous was already there. The name Demetrous derives from Demeter, the goddess of grain, of harvest, of the earth’s fertility.23 It was one of the commonest women’s names in Greco-Roman Egypt, appearing dozens of times in the Oxyrhynchus papyri alone. A Demetrous daughter of Ammonius cedes land in 100 CE.24 Multiple women named Demetrous appear in a weaving account from 35 CE.25 None of these can be identified with certainty as Euphrosyne’s mother. The name is too common. The silence around slave women is too deep.

But the weaving account is suggestive. Weaving was among the most common occupations for enslaved women in Roman Egypt, and a household that kept a female slave named Demetrous might well have put her to work at the loom.26 If so, Euphrosyne likely grew up to the sound of the shuttle, learning the craft from her mother’s hands before she was old enough to understand what it meant to be someone else’s property.

The Mistress

Aloine, daughter of Komon, son of Dionysius, of the city of Oxyrhynchus.27

That is all we know. The name Aloine is not common in the papyri. We cannot trace her in any other document. She does not appear as a party to a sale, a loan, a lease, a marriage, a will. She emerges from the vast silence of the unrecorded for exactly one moment – the moment she freed Euphrosyne – and then vanishes again.

But the document tells us several things about her.

She was a free woman of Oxyrhynchus, identified by patronymic in the manner of the propertied class: Aloine, daughter of Komon, son of Dionysius. The three-generation naming identifies her family and locates them in the city’s social geography. Her father Komon, her grandfather Dionysius – these were names of the Greek-speaking elite of the Oxyrhynchite nome, the men who appeared in census registers and property declarations, who paid the reduced twelve-drachma poll tax reserved for residents of the metropolis.28

She owned at least one slave, and probably more. The document mentions Euphrosyne and her mother Demetrous, and where there are two enslaved women spanning two generations, there were likely others. This was a household of some substance.

She needed a guardian. As a woman in Roman Egypt, Aloine could not execute a legal transaction without a kyrios – a male guardian who lent his authority to the act.29 Her brother Dioscorus, who would normally have served, was dead. The document says so with a single participle: metellachotos – “the deceased,” from metallasso, a standard euphemism for death in documentary papyri.30 So the guardianship passed to the next generation: Komon, son of Dioscorus, Aloine’s nephew. The family genealogy encoded in this arrangement is worth pausing over:

Komon son of Dionysius (grandfather) had at least two children: Dioscorus (son, now dead) and Aloine (daughter). Dioscorus had a son also named Komon, following the common Greek-Egyptian practice of naming a grandson after the paternal grandfather – papponymy.31 This young Komon now stood as legal guardian to his aunt Aloine – a woman perhaps twenty or thirty years his senior – because the law required a male hand on every document, and the male hands available were limited.

The guardianship arrangement reveals something else. Aloine’s husband, if she had one, is absent. No husband is named. This may mean she was unmarried, or widowed, or divorced. It may simply mean the husband was not the appropriate guardian for this particular transaction. But the silence is notable. In a document built of names and relationships, the missing husband is a gap the reader cannot help but notice.

The Price of Freedom

Argyriou episemou drachmon deka kai chalkou talanton deka trischilion.

Ten drachmae of coined silver, and ten talents, three thousand drachmae of copper.32

These numbers require translation. Roman Egypt operated on a bimetallic system in which silver drachmae and copper drachmae coexisted at a fluctuating but roughly stable exchange rate. In the first century CE, one silver drachma was worth approximately 500 copper drachmae.33 Ten talents of copper equaled 60,000 copper drachmae; add the 3,000 copper drachmae, and the copper payment totals 63,000 – roughly equivalent to 126 silver drachmae. Combined with the ten silver drachmae, the total comes to approximately 136 silver drachmae.34

Was this a high price or a low one?

A slave sale from the same period and the same city – written in the same format, by a man also named Chaeremon, to the same office of the agoranomus – records the sale of a twenty-three-year-old slave woman named Plousia (also called Kale Phasis) for ten talents, five thousand drachmae in bronze.35 Plousia’s price was somewhat higher than Euphrosyne’s ransom. She was twelve years younger and being sold on the open market rather than freed by a willing owner.

Five years after Euphrosyne’s manumission, in 91 CE, the slave Apollonous was freed in Oxyrhynchus for 200 drachmae of imperial silver coin plus copper talents.36 She was twenty-six years old. Her price was higher – consistent with her younger age.

By the early second century, slave prices had risen significantly. In 129 CE, a twenty-five-year-old slave named Dioscorous was sold for 1,200 silver drachmae – the document noting, with the chilling precision of a warranty, that the slave was “free from blemish except epilepsy and marks of punishment.”37 By 180 CE, the sales tax alone on a slave named Plution came to 52 drachmae, suggesting a sale price far higher still.38

Euphrosyne’s ransom of roughly 136 silver drachmae was, then, modest, perhaps below market value for a skilled household slave in her mid-thirties. Ransom prices for house-born slaves were sometimes set below market rate as a gesture of goodwill by the owner, especially for long-serving members of the household.39 Aloine may have set a price that was possible to pay rather than one that reflected Euphrosyne’s full economic value. The lower figure may also reflect what the market would bear for a thirty-five-year-old woman whose productive years were, by ancient reckoning, more than half behind her. Even so, 136 silver drachmae was a serious sum: far more than a laborer could gather quickly, and enough to turn freedom into a financial problem rather than a simple act of kindness.

The document does not say who paid. The freedom was purchased epi lytrois – “by ransom.”40 The Greek lytra means ransom or redemption price. Euphrosyne may have paid from savings, the peculium that some slaves accumulated from tips, small earnings, or portions of wages their owners allowed them to keep.41 A third party may have paid, as in the manumission of Apollonous in 91 CE, where the ransom is explicitly supplied by Heraclas son of Tryphon.42 Or Aloine may have treated the ransom as a legal formality and effectively paid it herself. The papyrus does not let us choose among those possibilities.

The Gods of Manumission

The manumission was performed hypo Dia Gen Helion – “under Zeus, Earth, and Sun.”43

Three cosmic witnesses enclosed the visible world: the sky-father, the ground beneath, the light above. To call them as witnesses was to make the act of freeing irrevocable in a way that no human authority alone could guarantee. Zeus could be invoked, Earth could be sworn upon, and the Sun saw everything. Against those three, no mortal’s change of heart could prevail.

The formula was standard in Egyptian manumissions. Five years later, the brothers Achilleus and Sarapas used the same words when they freed Apollonous: “have set free under sanction of Zeus, Earth, and Sun (hypo Dia Gen Helion) the third part which they jointly own of the slave.”44 The Apollonous document adds a detail absent from Euphrosyne’s: “the deed being drawn up in the street” – en tei plateiai – the manumission performed publicly, outdoors, visible to passersby.45 Whether Euphrosyne’s freedom was similarly declared in the open air, we do not know.

The formula’s roots ran deep. At Delphi, for centuries, slaves had been fictitiously “sold” to Apollo – the god “purchasing” the slave with the slave’s own money, thereby making the freedom a divine act that no human could undo.46 In Chersonesos on the Black Sea, the civic oath began with the same triad: “I swear by Zeus, Ge, Helios, Parthenos…”47 The Egyptian formula served the same function through the same gods. In a world without constitutions or bills of rights, where a freed slave’s liberty depended on the continuing goodwill of a former owner and the enforcement capacity of local officials, the invocation of Zeus, Earth, and Sun was the closest thing to a guarantee. It was theology doing the work of law.

Chaeremon and the Notary

The name Chaeremon appears frequently in Oxyrhynchus documents of this period. In P.Oxy. 75.5051, a near-contemporary text from Domitian’s reign, a “Chaeremon” writes to the agoranomus in identical format to register a slave sale.48 In P.Oxy. 2.243, dated to 79 CE, a “Chaeremon son of Chaeremon, of the Maronian deme” writes to the agoranomus about a mortgage.49 In P.Oxy. 7.1028, from June 86 CE – four months before Euphrosyne’s freedom, in the same city, in the same year – a “Chaeremon son of Onnophris” appears in a status declaration.50

Whether these Chaeremons are the same man, or different men sharing a common name, we cannot determine. But the pattern suggests a figure embedded in the notarial system – an intermediary, a facilitator, perhaps a nominee of the agoranomeion office itself, the person whose job it was to write the letters that made legal transactions happen. Euphrosyne’s freedom passed through his hands. He was the mechanism, not the motive.

The agoranomus himself is unnamed. He processed mortgage registrations during Domitian’s reign – the same office, the same years, the same procedural machinery.51 A loan repayment document from year 3 of Domitian, three years before Euphrosyne’s freedom, shows the same infrastructure at work.52 A debt acknowledgment from the very year of the manumission, 86-87 CE, preserves the economic texture of the moment.53 These were the documents that surrounded Euphrosyne’s freedom on the shelves of the agoranomos’s office – mortgages, debts, land leases, sales. Her liberation was filed among them, one transaction among many, extraordinary to her and routine to the clerk.

Thirty-Five Years

Hos eton le – “aged about thirty-five.”54

The qualifier hos (“about”) is standard in documentary papyri, reflecting the approximate nature of age reporting in antiquity. But take the number at face value. If Euphrosyne was approximately thirty-five in October 86 CE, she was born around 51 CE. Claudius was emperor. Nero was about to be.

What did thirty-five years as property look like?

The papyri sketch the answer in scattered fragments. A wet-nursing contract from 187 CE records a slave woman named Sarapias nursing a free child named Helena for two years, for wages of four hundred drachmae.55 The slave did the labor. The slave’s owner collected the pay. Sarapias herself received nothing directly. A house-born slave registered at age thirteen – the age at which owners were required to declare their slaves for the tax rolls – appears in a document from 122 CE.56 A slave named Chaerammon was apprenticed to learn shorthand in 155 CE, his master paying 120 silver drachmae in three installments for the training.57 These were investments in human property. The skills were the slave’s. The returns were the owner’s.

The wills of Oxyrhynchus dispose of slaves alongside furniture. A will from before 99 CE – Euphrosyne’s own generation – gives the surviving spouse “the power to sell all or any of the slaves belonging to himself or to the one of us who first dies, and with the purchase money to defray the expenses of the funeral and burial of the body.”58 Slaves sold to pay for coffins.

And the children. Under Roman law, the status of a child born to an enslaved woman followed the mother: partus sequitur ventrem. The father’s identity was legally irrelevant.59 A sale document from around 252 CE records a slave woman named Tereus, about twenty-one, sold “together with her male nursling child.”60 Mother and infant, sold as a unit. The document tells us that the ancient world acknowledged the mother-child bond – it was more efficient to sell them as a pair – while simultaneously treating both as movable goods.

If Euphrosyne had borne children during her thirty-five years of slavery, those children would have been Aloine’s property. They would not have been freed by Euphrosyne’s manumission. They would have remained in the house.

We do not know if Euphrosyne had children. We do not know if she left anyone behind.

The Companion Case

Five years after Euphrosyne’s manumission, in August 91 CE, another slave was freed in Oxyrhynchus under nearly identical circumstances. The document is P.Oxy. 4.722, and the parallels are striking.61

The slave was named Apollonous. She was about twenty-six years old. She was freed “under sanction of Zeus, Earth, and Sun” – the same formula, the same gods. The ransom was 200 drachmae of imperial silver coin plus copper talents. The transaction took place before three agoranomi called Psammis.

But the differences are equally revealing.

Apollonous had been freed in stages. She had been co-owned, and the other two-thirds of her ownership had already been liberated by prior transactions. The brothers Achilleus and Sarapas freed the remaining third. This “partial manumission” was a known practice – a slave could be jointly owned by multiple parties, and each party could free their share independently.62 The result was a person who was two-thirds free and one-third enslaved, a legal absurdity that the system apparently managed without embarrassment.

The ransom was paid by a third party: Heraclas son of Tryphon, “aged about 31, of middle height, fair, having a long face and a scar above his right knee.”63 The physical description is standard for agoranomus documents – height, complexion, face shape, distinguishing scars – a kind of ancient passport photo rendered in words. His relationship to Apollonous is not specified. A partner. A kinsman. A patron. We cannot say.

And then there are the physical descriptions. The Apollonous document describes every participant: Achilleus, “aged about 20 years, of middle height, fair, having a long face and a scar on the middle of his forehead.” Sarapas, similar description, “a scar on his left…” Apollonous herself: “of middle height, fair, having a long face and a scar on the right foot.”64

Euphrosyne’s document includes none of this. No height, no complexion, no scars. The difference is formal, not personal – P.Oxy. 1.48 takes the form of a letter, while P.Oxy. 4.722 is a face-to-face registration before the notary. But the effect is poignant. We know what Apollonous looked like. We will never know what Euphrosyne looked like. She exists only as a name, an age, a legal status, and a price.

What Freedom Meant

On October 16, 86 CE, Euphrosyne became an apeleuthera – a freedwoman.65

The word carried weight. An apeleutheros or apeleuthera was a person who had been freed from slavery – a status distinct from both slave (doulos/doule) and freeborn (eleutheros/eleuthera). In Roman legal terminology, the equivalent was libertus/liberta – a freedperson who carried the macula servitutis, the “stain of slavery,” for life.66 Freedom was real but marked. You were free, but everyone who read your name in a document would know what you had been.

Freedpersons identified themselves, in any legal document, by reference to their former owner. The formula was permanent: “Serenus, freedman of Apollonianus son of Sarapion, of Oxyrhynchus.”67 The patron’s name attached to the freedperson like a second patronymic, a permanent marker of origin. Euphrosyne would have been “Euphrosyne, freedwoman of Aloine daughter of Komon.” The name told her story. It told it forever.

Some manumissions included a paramone clause – an obligation for the freed person to remain with the former owner for a set period, continuing to serve.68 No paramone clause is visible in Euphrosyne’s document, though the text has lacunae. If she was free to go, she went. If she was obliged to stay, we do not know for how long.

But the Oxyrhynchus papyri show what freedom could become. Some freedpeople stayed close to the households that had owned them. Some entered trades. Some handled land, loans, and legal business in their own names. A freedwoman named Dionysous later declared her son Ptollis eligible for the twelve-drachma tax class, which meant that whatever had marked her did not pass to the next generation.73 Another freedwoman, Aphroditous also called Demarous, appears in a land sale after being both freed and adopted by her former master.72 Those are not happy endings in any simple sense. They do show that manumission could open a path into property, litigation, kinship, and civic life that slavery had closed.

The Weavers and the Slaves

While Euphrosyne stepped into freedom, the machinery of slavery in Oxyrhynchus continued without interruption.

Within the same decade, perhaps within the same year, the slave trade carried on. The sale of Plousia – a twenty-three-year-old slave woman, also called Kale Phasis – was registered through the same office, using the same format, by a man with the same name.76 “Chaeremon to the agoranomus, greetings. Register the sale.” The same clerk. The same pen. The same formulae.

Plousia had two names – Plousia (“Rich”) and Kale Phasis – which may suggest a slave who had been traded or renamed, passed from one owner to another with a new identity layered over the old.77 Her buyer was Harthoonis, also known as Theon. Her seller was Serapous, daughter of Zoilus son of Theon. The price was ten talents, five thousand drachmae in bronze.

An apprenticeship contract from 155 CE records a slave named Chaerammon being placed with a teacher to learn shorthand – the master paying 120 silver drachmae in three installments.78 The first payment up front. The second “when the student learned the system.” The third when he could write “fluently in every respect and read faultlessly.” A slave trained for skilled labor. The investment was in the owner’s property, not in a person’s future. If Chaerammon was ever freed, we do not know.

And a gymnasium selection document from 94-95 CE – eight years after Euphrosyne’s manumission – records the city’s elite sorting their sons into privileged tax categories.79 A world of status and pedigree from which Euphrosyne, as a freedwoman, was excluded but around which she lived. The Roman citizen woman Julia Heracla, who appears in an Oxyrhynchus document from 95 CE ceding property to her daughter, operated in a different legal universe entirely – Roman citizenship conferred capacities that neither Greek law nor freed status could match.80

A property registration from 90 CE cites the decree of the praefect Mettius Rufus, who had ordered a comprehensive survey of property ownership.81 A land cession from 83-84 CE shows the administrative machinery tightening its grip on the province.82 A loan from the last day of 81 CE records the financial networks of early Domitian.83 A dowry return from 96 CE – the year of Domitian’s assassination – preserves the domestic economy of the same era.84 This was the Oxyrhynchus Euphrosyne knew: a city of paper, of ledgers, of officials who filed and stamped and moved on.

What the Record Does Not Say

Euphrosyne’s document tells us only a few firm things: she was about thirty-five, she was house-born, her mother was Demetrous, her mistress was Aloine, the price was paid, and on 16 October 86 CE the agoranomus was asked to register her freedom.65 It does not tell us who paid the ransom, whether Demetrous lived to see it, whether Euphrosyne stayed in the household under some further obligation, or whether she walked out that same day and never came back.

After that date she disappears. No second papyrus picks her up. No marriage contract names her. No loan, lease, or petition preserves the next step. The best we can say is that other freedpeople in Oxyrhynchus sometimes entered work, property, and family life as people with claims of their own. Some stayed near the households that had owned them. Some married. Some handled business in their own names while carrying the former owner’s name with them. Euphrosyne may have done the same. She may not have. The sheet does not follow her.

What it does preserve is the crossing itself. For thirty-five years she had been written down as property, house-born and inheritable. Then Chaeremon wrote a different instruction to the agoranomus: grant freedom to Euphrosyne. Zeus, Earth, and Sun were named. The price was counted out. The endorsement was added. That was enough to change her legal life, even if it was not enough to tell us the rest of it.

Erroso. Farewell.
