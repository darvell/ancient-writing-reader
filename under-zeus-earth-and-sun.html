Under Zeus, Earth, and Sun

Euphrosyne of Oxyrhynchus -- 86 CE

27 primary documents · 70 notes

On the nineteenth day of the month Phaophi, in the sixth year of the Emperor Caesar Domitianus Augustus Germanicus – which is to say, October 16, 86 CE – a man named Chaeremon wrote a short letter to the agoranomus of Oxyrhynchus. It contained an instruction, a name, a price, and three gods.

The instruction: grant freedom. The name: Euphrosyne. The price: ten drachmae of coined silver and ten talents, three thousand drachmae of copper. The gods: Zeus, Earth, and Sun.1

The letter was not long. Twenty lines of Greek on papyrus, give or take, in the formulaic language of the notarial office. It named the slave, her age, her mother, her mistress, the mistress’s guardian, the legal mechanism, the sum paid. It closed with the standard farewell – erroso, “be well” – and the date, written twice. At the bottom, in what may be the same hand or a second one, the endorsement: “Chaeremon, register it. Phaophi 19.”2

That is the whole document. It takes less than a minute to read. And it is the only surviving evidence that a woman named Euphrosyne – Euphrosyne, Joy – ever existed.

I. The Letter

Chairemon toi agoranomoi chairein. Dos eleutherosin Euphrosynei doulei…

Chaeremon to the agoranomus, greeting. Grant freedom to Euphrosyne, a slave, aged about thirty-five years, born in her owner’s house of the slave Demetrous. She is being set at liberty under Zeus, Earth, and Sun, by ransom, by her mistress Aloine, daughter of Komon son of Dionysius, of Oxyrhynchus, under the wardship of Komon, the son of Aloine’s deceased brother Dioscorus. The price paid is ten drachmae of coined silver and ten talents, three thousand drachmae of copper.3

The document is catalogued as P.Oxy. 1.48. It was discovered in 1897 by Bernard Grenfell and Arthur Hunt, two young Oxford scholars who had come to the ancient rubbish heaps of Oxyrhynchus – modern el-Bahnasa, a town in Middle Egypt about 160 kilometers south of Cairo – and found, buried in the sand, the discarded paperwork of an entire civilization.4 Thousands of papyri. Tax receipts, love letters, petitions to the emperor, Homer fragments, Sappho, Euripides, shopping lists, divorce papers, slave sales, loan agreements, census returns. Among them: Euphrosyne’s freedom.

The papyrus ended up at Harrow School, in England. It sits today in the Vaughan Library, a long way from the agoranomus’s office where it was filed nineteen centuries ago.5 It was published in 1898, in the first volume of The Oxyrhynchus Papyri, the series that would eventually run to more than eighty volumes and transform the study of the ancient world. Grenfell and Hunt gave it a short note. They translated the Greek. They moved on to the next papyrus.

But the document repays lingering.

Notice the form. This is not a decree. It is not a proclamation. It is a letter – from Chaeremon to an unnamed agoranomus, using the epistolary convention of the period: name of sender, name of recipient, chairein (greetings). The agoranomus in Roman Egypt was not a judge or a magistrate in the political sense but a notary – a market official who registered legal transactions, authenticated documents, and maintained the archives of civic life.[^5a] Multiple agoranomi served simultaneously in a city the size of Oxyrhynchus. The one who received Chaeremon’s letter is not named. He was a functionary. His job was to stamp the paper and file it.

And Chaeremon? The name itself means “rejoice” – chairein, the same verb that opens the letter. Chaeremon to the agoranomus, chairein. The man whose name meant joy wrote the letter that freed a woman whose name meant joy. This is likely coincidence. Both names were common. But the coincidence sits there, in the Greek, waiting for someone to notice.

The name appears frequently in Oxyrhynchus documents of this period. In P.Oxy. 75.5051, a near-contemporary text from Domitian’s reign, a “Chaeremon” writes to the agoranomus in identical format to register a slave sale.[^5b] In P.Oxy. 2.243, dated to 79 CE, a “Chaeremon son of Chaeremon, of the Maronian deme” writes to the agoranomus about a mortgage.[^5c] Whether these are the same man, or different men sharing a common name, we cannot determine. But the pattern suggests a figure embedded in the notarial system – an intermediary, a facilitator, perhaps a nominee of the agoranomeion office itself, the person whose job it was to write the letters that made legal transactions happen. Euphrosyne’s freedom passed through his hands. He was the mechanism, not the motive.

II. A Name That Means Joy

Start with the name. Euphrosyne – Εὐφροσύνη – from eu (good, well) and phren (mind, heart). It means something like “good cheer,” or “mirth,” or simply “joy.”6

It is also the name of one of the Three Graces – the Charites – daughters of Zeus and the Oceanid Eurynome. Aglaea, Splendor. Thalia, Good Cheer. Euphrosyne, Joy. In Hesiod’s telling, they attended Aphrodite. They danced at divine weddings. They were the embodiment of everything graceful and delightful in the world.7

It was also, as it happens, a common name for slaves.

A Grace. A goddess of beauty and delight. Given to a piece of property.

This is one of the small cruelties the ancient world embedded in its language. Slaves in the Greco-Roman world typically bore a single name, without the patronymic that identified free persons – no “daughter of” or “son of” to anchor them in a family line.8 And the names themselves often followed a pattern of aspirational beauty: Plousia, “Rich.” Agathe, “Good.” Chresimus, “Useful.” Euphrosyne, “Joy.”9 Whether these names were chosen by the slave’s mother or assigned by the owner is usually impossible to determine. Perhaps Demetrous, laboring through childbirth in the house of Aloine’s family sometime around 51 CE, named her daughter Joy. Perhaps the family did, the way one might name a new horse or a dog – choosing a word that pleased the ear, that projected the qualities desired in the creature. The mythological resonance – a Grace, an attendant of Aphrodite, a daughter of Zeus – may have been intentional. It may have been irrelevant. We do not know.

What we know is that the name survived in the papyrus record exactly once: on the day it was entered into the notary’s files alongside the word doulei – slave.10

We should note that the name was not unique to women. A document from Oxyrhynchus dated to 175 CE records the examination of slaves belonging to a Roman citizen named Trunnia, including one named Euphrosynus – the male form of the same name. He, too, was certified as “born in the house.”11 The popularity of the name across genders and across a century tells us something. It was a name that owners kept reaching for. Joy was a quality the slaveholding household wished to project, if not to feel.

III. Born in the House

The Greek is precise: oikogenei ek doules Demetroutos – “born in the house, of the slave Demetrous.”12

The word oikogenes (οἰκογενής) – from oikos, house, and genos, birth – marks a specific legal and social category. Euphrosyne was not purchased at market. She was not captured in war. She was not a foundling scooped from the gutter and raised as property, though that too was common enough in Roman Egypt.13 She was born inside the household, the daughter of a woman who was already enslaved there. The Latin equivalent is verna – a home-born slave, raised alongside the master’s own children, speaking their language, knowing their customs, woven into the fabric of the household from the first day of life.14

The alternative routes into slavery in Roman Egypt were harsher. A document from Oxyrhynchus, dated to just two years before Euphrosyne’s birth, preserves a dispute that illuminates the casual brutality of the system. A man named Syrus “entrusted to the keeping of my wife Saraeus… a boy foundling named Heraclas, whom he had picked up from the gutter, to be nursed.”[^14a] The foundling died. Syrus then attempted to seize Saraeus’s own freeborn son Apion and carry him off into slavery as a replacement. Picked up from the gutter. Nursed at a stranger’s breast. Dead before he could speak. And his death used as a pretext to enslave another child. The boundaries between free and slave, between legitimate child-rearing and human trafficking, were porous in ways that the legal categories fail to capture.

Euphrosyne, at least, was spared this. She was born into a known household, to a known mother. But the knowledge cuts both ways.

This mattered. House-born slaves occupied a distinct and paradoxical position in the hierarchy of unfreedom. They were, by most accounts, more valued than slaves purchased from the open market.15 They were more likely to be trained in skilled occupations – weaving, cooking, personal attendance. They were more likely to be permitted to form families of their own, in the legal non-marriage that Romans called contubernium.16 And they were, disproportionately, the ones who eventually got freed. The proportion of oikogeneis in manumission documents is higher than their share of the slave population would predict, suggesting that owners felt some degree of obligation – or at least preference – toward those who had grown up under their roof.17

But let us not sentimentalize. A house-born slave was still property. A document from the Oxyrhynchite nome, dated to the mid-third century CE, preserves a dispute over the ownership of a house-born slave family spanning three generations: Taaphunchis the slave, her daughter Thaesis, and Thaesis’s children Apollonius and Harseis. The grandmother had been inherited, the daughter registered in the house-to-house census, the grandchildren claimed by competing heirs. Three generations of human beings, their bodies and their labor passed down like furniture.18

And a late antique document – fifth or sixth century, from the Christian era – records the flight of five house-born slaves described as “rope-makers, thin in body.” Their owner’s instructions were blunt: “Make every effort everywhere to track them down and have them shackled over their entire body and sent to me.”19 Born in the house. Shackled over their entire body. There was no contradiction, in the ancient mind, between these two facts.

Euphrosyne was born around 51 CE, in the first decade of Nero’s reign, into this world. Her mother Demetrous was already there. The name Demetrous – Δημητροῦς – derives from Demeter, the goddess of grain, of harvest, of the earth’s fertility.20 It was one of the commonest women’s names in Greco-Roman Egypt, appearing dozens of times in the Oxyrhynchus papyri alone. A Demetrous daughter of Ammonius cedes land in 100 CE.21 Multiple women named Demetrous appear in a weaving account from 35 CE.22 None of these can be identified with certainty as Euphrosyne’s mother. The name is too common. The silence around slave women is too deep.

But the weaving account is suggestive. Weaving was among the most common occupations for enslaved women in Roman Egypt, and a household that kept a female slave named Demetrous might well have put her to work at the loom.23 If so, Euphrosyne likely grew up to the sound of the shuttle, learning the craft from her mother’s hands before she was old enough to understand what it meant to be someone else’s property.

IV. The Mistress

Aloine, daughter of Komon, son of Dionysius, of the city of Oxyrhynchus.24

That is all we know. The name Aloine (Ἀλοίνη) is not common in the papyri. We cannot trace her in any other document. She does not appear as a party to a sale, a loan, a lease, a marriage, a will. She emerges from the vast silence of the unrecorded for exactly one moment – the moment she freed Euphrosyne – and then vanishes again.

But the document tells us several things about her.

She was a free woman of Oxyrhynchus, identified by patronymic in the manner of the propertied class: Aloine, daughter of Komon, son of Dionysius. The three-generation naming identifies her family and locates them in the city’s social geography. Her father Komon, her grandfather Dionysius – these were names of the Greek-speaking elite of the Oxyrhynchite nome, the men who appeared in census registers and property declarations, who paid the reduced twelve-drachma poll tax reserved for residents of the metropolis.25

She owned at least one slave, and probably more. The document mentions Euphrosyne and her mother Demetrous, and where there are two enslaved women spanning two generations, there were likely others. This was a household of some substance.

She needed a guardian. As a woman in Roman Egypt, Aloine could not execute a legal transaction without a kyrios – a male guardian who lent his authority to the act.26 Her brother Dioscorus, who would normally have served, was dead. The document says so with a single participle: metellachotos – “the deceased.”27 So the guardianship passed to the next generation: Komon, son of Dioscorus, Aloine’s nephew. The family genealogy encoded in this arrangement is worth pausing over:

Komon son of Dionysius (grandfather) had at least two children: Dioscorus (son, now dead) and Aloine (daughter). Dioscorus had a son also named Komon, following the common Greek-Egyptian practice of naming a grandson after the paternal grandfather.28 This young Komon now stood as legal guardian to his aunt Aloine – a woman perhaps twenty or thirty years his senior – because the law required a male hand on every document, and the male hands available were limited.

The guardianship arrangement reveals something else. Aloine’s husband, if she had one, is absent. No husband is named. This may mean she was unmarried, or widowed, or divorced. It may simply mean the husband was not the appropriate guardian for this particular transaction. But the silence is notable. In a document built of names and relationships, the missing husband is a gap the reader cannot help but notice.

V. The Price of Freedom

Argyriou episemou drachmon deka kai chalkou talanton deka trischilion.

Ten drachmae of coined silver, and ten talents, three thousand drachmae of copper.29

The dual currency requires explanation. Roman Egypt in the first century CE operated on a bimetallic system in which most ordinary transactions were denominated in copper (bronze) drachmae, while silver drachmae served as the prestige currency for official accounts and large-value contracts. The two circulated at an exchange rate that fluctuated but hovered in the range of approximately 400 to 500 copper drachmae to one silver drachma.30

Ten talents and three thousand drachmae of copper – a talent being 6,000 drachmae – works out to 63,000 copper drachmae. At a standard conversion rate of roughly 500:1, that is equivalent to about 126 silver drachmae. Add the ten silver drachmae paid directly, and the total comes to approximately 136 silver drachmae.31

Was that a lot? Was it a little?

Consider the comparisons. A near-contemporary document from Oxyrhynchus – P.Oxy. 75.5051, dated to Domitian’s reign (81-96 CE) and addressed, notably, by a “Chaeremon” to the agoranomus in the same formulaic style – records the sale of a slave named Plousia, about twenty-three years old, for ten talents, five thousand drachmae of bronze.32 That is 65,000 copper drachmae – slightly more than Euphrosyne’s ransom, for a woman twelve years younger. Five years after Euphrosyne’s manumission, in 91 CE, the ransom for a twenty-six-year-old slave named Apollonous included 200 drachmae of imperial silver plus copper talents – a sum that likely exceeded Euphrosyne’s price.33 And by 129 CE, the slave Dioscorous, twenty-five years old, was sold for 1,200 silver drachmae – nearly ten times what Euphrosyne cost.34

The Dioscorous document carries a detail worth noting: the slave was sold as “free from blemish except epilepsy and marks of punishment.”[^34a] Standard disclaimer language. The buyer was being warned: this person may seize, and this person has been beaten. The marks of punishment were visible enough to require disclosure. They were, in effect, part of the product description. No such disclaimer attaches to Euphrosyne’s document, but then no physical description of any kind attaches to it. We do not know whether Euphrosyne bore marks of punishment. We do not know whether she had ever been beaten. The document was not a bill of sale, and so the buyer’s protections did not apply.

The price of a slave, like the price of anything, depended on age, health, skills, and the state of the market. Euphrosyne at thirty-five was past her most economically productive years in the harsh calculus of ancient slavery. A younger slave commanded a higher price. The fact that Euphrosyne’s ransom was lower than typical sale prices for younger slaves may reflect her age. It may also reflect a gesture of goodwill from Aloine – a discounted price for a woman who had served the household since birth.35

The deeper question is: who paid?

The document says the manumission was epi lytrois – “by ransom.”36 The Greek word lytra means ransom, redemption price. But the document does not say who provided the money. It could have been Euphrosyne herself, out of savings accumulated over decades – the peculium, the small fund that some slaves were permitted to set aside from earnings or tips. Thirty-five years is a long time to save. A woman spinning wool or weaving cloth, selling her products with her mistress’s permission, keeping a fraction of the proceeds, coin by copper coin, year after year. It is possible. It is perhaps the most hopeful reading.

Or the money could have come from elsewhere. A partner, a lover, a relative on the outside. In the manumission of Apollonous in 91 CE, the ransom was explicitly paid by a third party: Heraclas son of Tryphon, a man of about thirty-one, whose relationship to the slave woman is unspecified in the document but whose willingness to pay 200 silver drachmae for her freedom speaks louder than any label.37 Was there a Heraclas in Euphrosyne’s life? Someone who had saved, or borrowed, or sold something to raise the funds? The document is silent. And perhaps Aloine herself reduced the price. Perhaps the ransom was a formality – a legal requirement that a sum be named and paid, even if the amount was less than the market would have demanded. Owners sometimes freed slaves at below-market prices, particularly long-serving oikogeneis, for reasons the documents gesture at but never fully explain. Sentiment, perhaps. Or religious obligation. Or simple exhaustion with the relationship. We do not know.

We do not know. The document, as always, tells us only what the notary needed to record.

VI. The Gods of Manumission

Hypo Dia Gen Helion.

Under Zeus, Earth, and Sun.38

Three witnesses. Not human witnesses – divine ones. The manumission was performed under the sanction of the cosmic triad: the king of the gods, the earth beneath, the sun above. Together they encompassed all of reality. There was nowhere to hide from their gaze, no corner of the world where a broken oath could go unnoticed. Zeus saw all from Olympus. The Earth felt every footstep. The Sun illuminated every face.

The formula was not unique to Euphrosyne’s document. It appears across manumission papyri from Roman Egypt with the regularity of a legal standard. Five years later and in the same city, when two brothers named Achilleus and Sarapas freed a slave named Apollonous, they did so “under sanction of Zeus, Earth, and Sun.”39 The phrase had the force of ritual. It made the act sacred. And by making it sacred, it made it irrevocable.

This was the point. The divine invocation served a legal function as precise as any notarial stamp. At Delphi, in mainland Greece, the great sanctuary of Apollo had been processing slave manumissions for centuries through a mechanism of fictitious sale: the slave was “sold” to the god, who “purchased” the slave’s freedom with the slave’s own money, so that no mortal could claim the transaction was reversible. The god held the title. The slave walked free.40 In Egypt, the formula hypo Dia Gen Helion accomplished the same thing by different means. The gods were not purchasers but witnesses – and before such witnesses, no human power could undo what had been done.

There is something worth pausing over in the choice of gods. Zeus, the ruler, the god of oaths and justice – the same Zeus whose name opened every civic oath in the Greek world, from the citizen’s pledge at Chersonesos (“I swear by Zeus, Ge, Helios, Parthenos…”) to the market transactions of provincial Egypt.41 Earth, the goddess beneath all things, inescapable, the ground on which all oaths are sworn and to which all bodies return. And Sun, the all-seeing, who crosses the sky each day and witnesses everything that occurs below. These are not the gods of a particular temple or a particular city. They are the gods of the cosmos itself. Euphrosyne’s freedom was registered not merely in the archives of the agoranomus but, the formula insisted, in the structure of the universe.

Whether Euphrosyne believed this is another thing the document does not say. But belief is perhaps beside the point. The formula was not a confession of faith. It was a legal technology. It worked whether you believed in Zeus or not, the same way a notarized signature works whether you believe in the authority of the state. The gods were the mechanism by which irreversibility was achieved. They were the lock on the door that could not be reopened.

And yet there is something in the formula that transcends the merely legal. Hypo Dia Gen Helion. Under the sky-god, the ground, the light. These are not abstract powers. They are the physical world itself – the sky overhead, the dirt underfoot, the sun on your face. When Euphrosyne walked out of whatever room the transaction took place in, she walked under the same Zeus, on the same Earth, in the same Sun that had just been named as guarantors of her freedom. The formula mapped the act onto the world. From this moment forward, the entire visible cosmos was her witness.

VII. Thirty-Five Years

She was about thirty-five. The document says hos eton le – “aged about thirty-five years” – the hos (“about”) a standard qualifier, since exact birth dates were rarely known even for free persons, let alone for slaves.42

Thirty-five years. Count them backward from the autumn day in the notary’s office.

Born around 51 CE, in the household of Aloine’s family. The emperor was Claudius, soon to be Nero. Egypt was a Roman province under a prefect appointed by the emperor, administered by a bureaucracy that ran on papyrus and taxed everything that moved. Oxyrhynchus was a middling city in the Oxyrhynchite nome, its economy fueled by agriculture – the irrigation channels of the Bahr Yussef, the wheat and flax fields of the valley – and by the textile industry that employed a significant portion of its population, free and slave alike.43

What did Euphrosyne do for those thirty-five years? The document does not say. But we can triangulate.

House-born female slaves in Roman Egypt worked at the tasks the household required.44 Weaving was the most common occupation – the looms were set up in the house or in workshops attached to it, and women spun, wove, and finished textiles for both household use and sale. A weaving account from the Fayum, dated to 35 CE, lists multiple women working cloth, their names and outputs carefully tracked.45 Personal attendance was another common role: dressing the mistress, carrying her things, accompanying her to the baths or to market. Wet-nursing was another possibility – a document from 187 CE records a slave named Sarapias who nursed a free child named Helena for two years, her owner collecting the wages of 400 drachmae while the slave herself received nothing directly.46

Cooking, cleaning, carrying water, grinding grain, tending children. The work of the ancient household was physical, repetitive, and never finished. Euphrosyne would have known the interior of Aloine’s house – its mudbrick walls, its courtyard, its storerooms, its loom – better than any other space in the world. It was the only world she had.

Consider what it meant to grow up in a house you could never leave by choice. To know every crack in the plaster, every stain on the courtyard floor, the angle of the afternoon light through the doorway, the sound of the mistress’s sandals on the stairs – and to know that none of it belonged to you. That you belonged to it. Euphrosyne would have watched Aloine’s brother Dioscorus age, sicken perhaps, and die – the man whose son would one day stand as guardian for the act of her own liberation. She would have watched the nephew Komon grow from boy to man. She may have nursed him. She may have woven his first tunic. The intimacies of the household were real, but they were asymmetrical. Aloine could sell Euphrosyne. Euphrosyne could not walk out the door.

A will from Oxyrhynchus, dated to before 99 CE – roughly contemporary with Euphrosyne’s lifetime – contains a provision that captures the calculus of domestic slavery with brutal clarity: the survivor of a married couple is given “the power to sell all or any of the slaves belonging to himself or to the one of us who first dies, and with the purchase money to defray the expenses of the funeral and burial of the body.”[^47a] Slaves as funeral funds. The body of one person liquidated to bury another. This was the legal framework within which Euphrosyne lived for thirty-five years.

And there is a silence here that should not pass without remark. The document names Euphrosyne’s mother, Demetrous, but not her father. This is standard for slaves: the father is legally irrelevant, because a slave cannot contract a marriage, and a child’s status follows the mother’s.47 Euphrosyne’s father could have been another slave in the household. He could have been a free man. He could have been the master himself – or a member of the master’s family. The law did not care, and the document did not record it. Euphrosyne existed, legally, as the product of one woman – her mother – and one household – her owner’s. Everything else was invisible.

The world outside the house was not invisible, however. In the same year Euphrosyne was freed – year six of Domitian, 86 CE – a woman in Oxyrhynchus was filing a declaration of her son Chaeremon’s eligibility for the twelve-drachma tax class.[^47b] Debt acknowledgments were being signed.[^47c] Mortgage registrations were being processed through the same agoranomus’s office that would handle Euphrosyne’s manumission.[^47d] The machinery of the city ground on. Euphrosyne moved through it as property for thirty-five years, and then, on a single day, as a person.

VIII. The Companion Case

On August 29, 91 CE – five years and ten months after Euphrosyne walked out of the notary’s office – another slave was freed in Oxyrhynchus. Her name was Apollonous. She was about twenty-six years old.48

The document, P.Oxy. 4.722, is longer and more detailed than Euphrosyne’s, and its differences illuminate as much as its parallels.

First, the parallels. Same city. Same reign – Domitian, year ten. Same sacred formula: “under sanction of Zeus, Earth, and Sun.”49 Same mechanism: ransom. Same office: the agoranomus, here named Psammis. The legal architecture is identical. The manumission machine of first-century Oxyrhynchus processed both women through the same gears.

Now the differences. Apollonous was not freed all at once. The document specifies that Achilleus and Sarapas, two brothers, are freeing “the third part which they jointly own of the slave who has been freed as regards the other two-thirds.”50 Apollonous had been owned in shares – a common arrangement in Roman Egypt, where property including human property was routinely divided among heirs. Two-thirds of her had already been freed by some prior transaction. The remaining third was now being released. She had achieved her freedom in stages, like a debt being paid down in installments.

The ransom was paid not by Apollonous but by a third party: “Heraclas son of Tryphon son of…, his mother being Taonnophris, of the said city, aged about 31.”51 The document gives Heraclas’s physical description in the standard agoranomus format: middle height, fair, long face, scar above the right knee. We know what he looked like. We do not know what he was to Apollonous – husband, lover, brother, friend, patron. But he paid 200 drachmae of silver plus copper talents for the last third of a woman’s freedom. The relationship, whatever it was, had a price, and he met it.

One more difference, and it is striking. The Apollonous document includes physical descriptions of everyone involved: the two brothers who freed her (“middle height, fair, long face, scar on the middle of his forehead”… “scar on his left…”), Apollonous herself (“middle height, fair, long face, scar on the right foot”), and Heraclas.52 These were standard for agoranomus documents that were drawn up in person, where the notary could see the parties and record their identifying marks. The detail is almost clinical: height, complexion, face shape, location of scars. It reads like a police description, and it functioned as one – these details existed to prevent fraud, to ensure that the person claiming freedom was the person the document described.

Euphrosyne’s document includes no physical description. We do not know if she was tall or short, fair or dark, scarred or unscarred. The letter format – Chaeremon writing to the agoranomus rather than all parties appearing before him – may explain the omission. Or it may not. Either way, Euphrosyne is faceless in the record. Her name, her age, her mother, her price. Nothing more.

There is a further detail in the Apollonous document that deserves attention. The deed was “drawn up in the street” – en tei plateiai, as opposed to in the agoranomus’s office.[^52a] Why in the street? Perhaps the parties gathered informally, near the notary’s station but not inside it. Perhaps the formality of the office was unnecessary or unavailable. The street was a public space, and public spaces were where transactions acquired their binding force – where witnesses could see and remember. In Oxyrhynchus, freedom was not proclaimed from a temple or a throne. It was registered in an office or announced in a street.

IX. What Freedom Meant

The manumission was irrevocable – the gods had witnessed it. But freedom in the Roman world was not a binary switch, flipped from slavery to full personhood in a single moment. It was a threshold, and the ground on the other side was uneven.

After manumission, Euphrosyne became an apeleuthera – a freedwoman.53 The Greek term apeleutheros (ἀπελεύθερος) and its Latin equivalent libertus/liberta designated a specific legal category: a person who had once been enslaved and was now free, but who carried the macula servitutis – the stain of slavery – for the rest of her life.54 A freedperson was not the same as a freeborn person. She occupied an intermediate position in the Roman social hierarchy: above the enslaved, below the ingenui, the freeborn.

In practical terms, this meant several things. A freedperson typically owed her former owner obsequium – deference, respect, a degree of continued allegiance. She might owe operae – services, days of labor. She would identify herself in legal documents by reference to her former owner: “Euphrosyne, freedwoman of Aloine,” just as, nearly a century later, a man named Serenus would identify himself as “Serenus, freedman of Apollonianus son of Sarapion, of Oxyrhynchus.”55

Many manumission documents included a paramone clause – an obligation for the freed person to remain with the former owner for a set period, continuing to serve, a kind of probationary freedom that modern scholars have debated whether to call “conditional liberty” or something closer to a continued servitude under a different name.56 No paramone clause is visible in Euphrosyne’s document. But the text has lacunae – gaps where the papyrus is damaged or illegible – and we cannot be certain what was lost.

What could a freedwoman do in Roman Egypt? The papyri from Oxyrhynchus provide a range of answers that is broader than one might expect.

Consider Serenus, freedman of Apollonianus. By 178 CE, roughly a century after Euphrosyne’s manumission, Serenus was operating as a creditor. He had lent 900 silver drachmae – a substantial sum, representing years of a common laborer’s wages – to a woman named Sarapias from the Small Oasis, the loan secured by her slave, also named Sarapias.57 The terms were precise: interest at the rate of one drachma per mina per month, the capital to be repaid by a fixed date. When the borrower defaulted, Serenus did not accept the loss. He petitioned the archidicastes – the chief judge, the superintendent of the courts – through the strategos of the Oxyrhynchite nome, requesting that legal notice be served on Sarapias, or if she was dead, on her heirs, or if they were minors, on their guardians. The chain of contingencies is remarkable. Serenus, a freedman, was thinking like a lawyer. He knew the system. He knew how to use it.

And there is an irony embedded in the document that should not pass without comment. The loan was secured by a slave. Serenus, a man who had himself been enslaved, had become the kind of person who accepted human beings as collateral. The freedman who had once been property now held property rights over another person’s body. The Roman world did not see this as a contradiction. It was merely the market working as designed.

A freedman lending money, owning property, navigating the Roman legal system, petitioning officials across nome boundaries. The stain of slavery had not prevented him from accumulating wealth and exercising legal rights.

Or consider Dioscorus, freedman of Sarapion, who in 128-129 CE applied for a license to practice as a river-worker in Oxyrhynchus.58 A freedman starting a new trade, registering with the city authorities, entering the economy as an independent agent. Or Ophelas, freedman of a woman named Apia, who in 139 CE leased three arouras of state land for five years.59 A freedman farming government land. Or Dionysous, a freedwoman who in 133 CE declared her son Ptollis eligible for the twelve-drachma tax class – the reduced poll tax reserved for residents of the metropolis whose parents on both sides were inhabitants of the city.60 A freedwoman’s son, gaining access to the privileged tax class. The stain fading, one generation at a time.

The most remarkable case is Aphroditous, a freedwoman of Epicrates. Not only did she accumulate significant property – six and two-thirds arouras of catoecic land, sold for 1,000 silver drachmae – but she had been adopted by her former master. “Aphroditous also called Demarous, freedwoman of Epicrates son of Epicrates son of Heraclides, of Oxyrhynchus, by adoption daughter of Epicrates and of his wife Thaisous…”61 Read that identification again. Freedwoman of Epicrates. By adoption, daughter of Epicrates and his wife. The same man appears in two roles: former owner and adoptive father. The woman who was once his property became, by legal fiction, his child. She had two names – Aphroditous and Demarous – and two identities, one rooted in her servile past and one in her adoptive present. She owned land. She sold it for a thousand silver drachmae. A slave who became a daughter who became a landowner. The legal system allowed it. Whether it happened often, or whether Aphroditous was exceptional, we cannot say. But the possibility existed. It was thinkable. And for Euphrosyne, stepping out of the notary’s office at thirty-five, perhaps it was thinkable too.

And there were limits. A freedman named Abascantus could serve as a guardian of minors – a position of trust.62 But a freedwoman still needed her own kyrios. Dionysous the freedwoman declared her son’s status “with her guardian Eudaemon.”63 Freedom did not erase gender. The same legal architecture that had required Aloine to have a male guardian for the act of freeing Euphrosyne would have constrained Euphrosyne herself in every legal transaction she might undertake as a free woman.

X. The Weavers and the Slaves

Let us return, for a moment, to Demetrous.

Euphrosyne’s mother appears in the manumission document as a single word and a genitive case: ek doules Demetroutos – “of the slave Demetrous.”64 She is the origin of Euphrosyne, the body from which the manumitted woman emerged, and beyond that the document has nothing to say about her. Was she alive in 86 CE? Was she still enslaved? Had she been freed before her daughter, or was she still Aloine’s property when Euphrosyne walked out? The silence is total.

But there is one thread to pull.

A weaving account from Tebtunis, dated to December 35 CE and published as Tyche 28.123, records multiple women named Demetrous working as weavers.65 The account tracks output – who wove what, how much, when – in the manner of a workshop ledger. The women’s names appear without patronymics, which could mean they were slaves, or simply that the record-keeper did not bother with full identification. The account is from the Fayum, not from Oxyrhynchus, and it is dated roughly fifteen years before Euphrosyne’s birth. No connection to Euphrosyne’s mother can be established.

And yet the document tells us something about the world Demetrous inhabited. Weaving was women’s work in Roman Egypt, and it was particularly the work of enslaved women. The household loom was the site where slave labor and domestic economy intersected most directly. A slave woman who could weave produced not only the household’s textiles – tunics, cloaks, bedding, sacks – but surplus goods for sale. She was an economic asset whose value could be measured in finished cloth.

If Demetrous was a weaver, she spent her days at the loom in Aloine’s house, producing fabric that Aloine sold or used. Euphrosyne would have grown up watching her mother work. She would have learned the craft. And if she was permitted to keep any of the proceeds from her own labor – even a fraction, a few copper coins per bolt of cloth – then the ransom money might represent not a windfall from an outside benefactor but the slow, patient accumulation of thirty-five years of saving, thread by thread, coin by coin.

Perhaps. We may imagine it. But we do not know.

What we can say is that textile production was the economic backbone of many Oxyrhynchite households, and that enslaved women were central to it. A slave who could weave was worth more than one who could not. A slave who could weave well was worth more still. And a household that produced surplus textiles had income – income from which, in some cases, the slave herself might be allowed to retain a portion. The peculium was not a legal right but a customary allowance, a pragmatic concession by owners who understood that a slave with something to hope for worked harder than one with nothing. If Demetrous wove cloth for Aloine’s household, and if Euphrosyne learned the trade from her mother, then the loom was both the instrument of their captivity and the means – perhaps – of Euphrosyne’s eventual escape from it.

There is a late antique Christian manumission from Cologne, dated to 589 CE, in which a monk named Victor frees his home-born slave Menas “from any chain of slavery, in order to find… remission and redemption of all my sins.”[^65a] Five centuries after Euphrosyne, the same institution of house-born slavery persisted, but the rationale for manumission had changed. Victor freed Menas not by ransom but for the salvation of his own soul. The gods had changed. The chains had not.

XI. What the Record Does Not Say

There is a discipline required when working with ancient documents, and it is the discipline of acknowledging silence. The papyri tell us what someone needed to record, for purposes that were their own, at a moment that has passed. Everything else is inference, analogy, and the careful deployment of “perhaps.”

The historian of the ancient world is accustomed to this discipline. Most people who ever lived left no written trace at all. The vast majority of the enslaved – numbering in the millions across the centuries of Greco-Roman civilization – exist only as generic categories in legal codes, as items in property inventories, as problems in philosophical dialogues. To have a name in the record is already exceptional. To have a name, an age, a mother’s name, a city, and a date – as Euphrosyne has – is to be, by the standards of ancient slavery, extraordinarily well documented. And yet how little that documentation tells us.

Euphrosyne’s manumission document tells us: - That she existed. - That she was freed on October 16, 86 CE. - That she was about thirty-five years old. - That she was born in the household of Aloine’s family. - That her mother was a slave named Demetrous. - That she was freed by ransom, under divine sanction. - That the price was paid.

It does not tell us: - What she looked like. - What work she did. - Whether she was literate. - Whether she had children. - Whether she had a partner. - Who paid the ransom. - Whether she owed paramone obligations. - Whether she stayed in Oxyrhynchus. - Whether she ever saw Aloine again. - Whether Demetrous was alive to witness her daughter’s freedom. - What happened next.

After October 16, 86 CE, Euphrosyne disappears from the record. She does not appear in any other surviving papyrus from Oxyrhynchus or anywhere else. No lease, no loan, no marriage contract, no tax receipt, no petition, no will. This is not unusual – we possess perhaps one percent of one percent of the documents that were written in Roman Egypt, and the survival of any individual’s paperwork is a matter of extraordinary luck. Thousands of people who lived full, consequential lives in Oxyrhynchus left no trace at all. But the effect is stark. One document. One moment. Then nothing.

The world she entered as a free woman was the same world she had always inhabited, seen now from a different legal position. The streets of Oxyrhynchus were the same streets. The market was the same market. The agoranomus’s office, where her freedom was registered, processed mortgage registrations and debt acknowledgments and slave sales and property transfers in the same month, the same year – P.Oxy. 75.5052 records a debt acknowledgment from the very same year as Euphrosyne’s manumission.66 The bureaucratic machinery did not pause. Chaeremon, or a Chaeremon, wrote to the agoranomus about a slave sale in the same format, the same style, the same era.67 Life went on.

Did Euphrosyne marry? She could now contract a legal marriage – something impossible for a slave. Did she have children? If so, they would be ingenui, freeborn, uncontaminated by the stain of slavery, eligible to participate in the city’s institutions in ways their mother never could. A freedwoman named Dionysous managed exactly this: her son Ptollis was declared eligible for the twelve-drachma tax class, the mark of metropolitan respectability.68 Euphrosyne could have done the same – if she married, if she bore children after her manumission, if she stayed in the city, if she lived long enough.

Did she prosper? She could own property now. She could lease land. She could lend money. She could, like Aphroditous the freedwoman, accumulate arouras of farmland and sell them for silver.69 Or she could remain poor – a freedwoman without connections, without capital, without the support of a patron, navigating the economy of a provincial Egyptian city on her own. Freedom was a legal threshold, not an economic guarantee.

Did she return to weaving? It would have been the skill she knew best. But now the proceeds would be hers. Or mostly hers – depending on what obligations she still owed Aloine, if any.

There is one more possibility the document leaves open, and it is the darkest. The sale of a slave woman and her nursing child in Oxyrhynchus, recorded around 252 CE, lists the pair as a unit: “the female slave belonging to him named Tereus, aged about 21… together with her male nursling child.”[^69a] Mother and infant, sold together. The document tells us that the ancient world acknowledged the mother-child bond – it was more efficient to sell them as a pair – while simultaneously treating both as movable goods. If Euphrosyne had borne children during her years of slavery, those children would have been Aloine’s property. They would not have been freed by Euphrosyne’s manumission. They would have remained in the house.

We do not know if Euphrosyne had children. We do not know if she left anyone behind. The document does not say. But the possibility should sit with us as we read the closing formula – erroso, farewell – and imagine what it felt like to walk out that door.

We do not know. The papyrus at Harrow School is silent on all of it.

What we can say is this.

On a day in October, in the sixth year of an emperor who would be murdered ten years later and whose very name the Senate would attempt to erase from public memory, a woman walked into a notary’s office in a city that no longer exists, in a province that no longer bears its ancient name, and was declared free.70

The transaction was recorded on a strip of plant fiber. It was filed, stored, eventually discarded – thrown into the rubbish heap where, eighteen centuries later, two Englishmen found it and carried it to a school for boys in the London suburbs. The papyrus survived because the climate of Middle Egypt is very dry and because ancient Oxyrhynchus did not burn its trash.

The woman’s name was Euphrosyne. It meant Joy. She was the daughter of a woman named after the goddess of grain, born into a household she did not choose, raised in servitude she could not refuse. For thirty-five years she was property – counted, valued, perhaps loved in the complicated and corrupted way that owners sometimes loved the people they owned, but property nonetheless. And then one day she was not.

The price was counted out in two currencies – silver for the official record, copper for the actual payment. The gods were named: the king of heaven, the earth beneath her feet, the sun above her head. The notary made his endorsement. The date was written twice. The papyrus was filed, and eventually discarded, and eventually found.

Three gods witnessed her freedom. One woman lived it.

Erroso. Farewell.
