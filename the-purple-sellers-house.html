The Purple-Seller's House

Psates son of Abraamios, the Village of This, 592--616 CE

Late Antique Thinis Marriage & Family Craft & Labor Women Property & Land Religion Love & Desire Children

33 primary documents · 111 notes

Three gold coins, less one keration.

That was the price of a dilapidated house, a cell, and a patch of bare land in the village of This, in the Thinite nome of Upper Egypt, on the fourteenth day of June in the year 592 CE.1 The sellers were a husband and wife. The buyer was their son. The property sat on a lane named after dead people – “the lane of the heirs of Pachot” – beside an oil-press belonging to other dead people, the heirs of Pachareabe.2 Its boundaries ran, as the document puts it, “from the ground to the sky.”3

The husband’s name was Psates son of Abraamios. His mother was called Tmouthes. His trade was purple – porphyropoles, purple-seller – a word that links him, across a millennium of Greek, to Lydia of Thyatira in the Acts of the Apostles.4 His wife was Maria daughter of Philamon, whose mother was Taniskene. They had come from the city of Panopolis – modern Akhmim, one of the great textile centres of the ancient world – and they had lived in This “for a long time,” the document says, using a phrase that suggests decades rather than years.5

Neither of them could write. A deacon named Psates son of Papnouthios held the pen for them. “I wrote on their behalf at their request,” the subscription reads.6

And so two illiterate purple-sellers transferred a crumbling house to their eldest son, and a deacon’s handwriting became the only record of the transaction. Seventeen centuries later, we have the papyrus. We have the names. We do not have the conversation that preceded the signing – the family argument, the calculation, the worry about the other children who would inherit nothing from this particular house. That conversation lives in the document’s silences, and in what happened next.

I. The Village on the Edge of Memory

This – Thinis in Greek, Tjenu in Egyptian, Tin in Coptic – was among the oldest named places in the world.7 Ancient tradition held it as the first capital of a united Egypt, the seat of Menes, the king who brought together the Two Lands around 3100 BCE. Manetho, the Ptolemaic-era priest who compiled the Egyptian king-lists, placed the first two dynasties at This. By the time Psates arrived there, probably in the 560s or 570s, This had been declining for millennia. The documents call it a kome, a village, part of the Thinite nome within the Theban province.8

The exact site has never been identified with certainty. Scholars place it near Abydos and modern Girga, in the Nile Valley of Upper Egypt.9 The famous temple of Osiris at Abydos was close. The sacred landscape was dense.

But a village in the Byzantine Nile Valley was not a desolate place. The papyri from This reveal oil-pressers and builders, copper-workers and ironworkers, priests and deacons, professional notaries who kept the legal machinery turning.10 There were lanes named after families and professions. There was a functioning property market – houses bought, sold, inherited, consolidated. There was a church, and the church was everywhere: its deacons wrote the contracts, its priests witnessed the sales, its saints named the streets.

Other documents from the Thinite nome and the wider region fill in the texture. An oath involving a priest named Polychronios dates to around the same period.11 A land sale in the nearby Antaiopolite nome, involving a dealer in copper ware, dates to 610-612 CE.12 An agricultural lease for flax cultivation in the area survives from the late sixth or early seventh century.13 A building inscription of Bishop Tobias provides evidence of episcopal activity, perhaps in the seventh century.14 These are the neighbours of Psates’s story, the fragments that surround his archive without quite touching it.

II. Why They Left Panopolis

Psates and Maria came from Panopolis. The document says so twice: they are “from the city of Panopolis” – apo poleos Panon – “but now for a long time resident in the village of This.”15

Panopolis – modern Akhmim – was famous for its textiles. Strabo mentions its linen-weaving and stone-cutting as ancient industries.16 Archaeological excavations have recovered elaborately woven textiles from the fifth through seventh centuries, including a tunic with Dionysian motifs in tapestry weave and numerous Coptic textiles from the Akhmim cemeteries.17 This was the textile capital of Upper Egypt, perhaps of all Egypt.

Why would a purple-seller leave? The documents do not say. Perhaps the market in Panopolis was crowded – too many weavers, too many sellers, margins thinning. Perhaps Psates calculated that a smaller settlement, hungry for imported colour and cloth, offered better returns. Perhaps he married Maria and her connections pointed south. We do not know. What we know is that by 592, a purple-seller from the textile capital had transplanted himself to an ancient village, bought property from two local men named Viktor son of Psaios and Senouthes son of Phaliout, raised three children, and now, probably elderly, was transferring a house to his eldest son.18

The family kept property in both places. After Psates and Maria died, the inheritance division of 600 CE would reveal “two parental house-plots in the city of Panopolis on the lane of the holy place of Apa Kollouthos.”19 They had not burned their bridges. They were migrants, not refugees.

III. The Colour of Money

What did a porphyropoles actually sell?

The word means “purple-seller,” but by the sixth century in Egypt, “purple” was less a single colour than a spectrum of reds, violets, and crimson-browns produced by multiple methods and materials.20 The ancient Tyrian purple – the famous dye extracted from the hypobranchial glands of Murex brandaris sea snails, requiring thousands of animals for a single gram – was still known but increasingly rare and fabulously expensive.21 Egyptian textile workers had long since developed cheaper plant-based alternatives: alkanet roots (Alkanna tinctoria) combined with lime and vinegar, pomegranate bark mixed with alkanet, lichen-based dyes such as orchil or orcein.22

A Greek papyrus codex from the late third or early fourth century, now in Leiden, preserves five recipes for creating purple dye without sea snails – practical workshop manuals that democratised what had once been a near-monopoly of the Phoenician coast.23 By Psates’s time, purple-dyed textiles were worn by men, women, and children across the social spectrum of Roman Egypt.

An inventory of purple garments from the sixth century lists the variety in breathtaking detail: full garments “of purple,” turban-veils (maphoria) with circular designs, tunic-veils (sticharomaphoria) dyed pentabaphos porphyras – “five-dyed purple” – meaning the cloth had been immersed in the dye bath five separate times for a richer, more saturated colour.24 Another document, a letter from the same century, describes “Antiochene maphoria with descending patterns” – imported or imitation-imported veils with fashionable Syrian designs.25

A seventh-century letter records someone requesting purchases of “a tremissis worth of red-brown, and one of crimson-red, and one of purple-red” – three distinct shades, each bought in quantities pegged to a gold coin.26 This was the market Psates served. Not the imperial court, but the village economy where a farmer’s wife might want a veil with purple decoration for a feast day, or a priest might need a new vestment, or a couple celebrating a wedding might commission something fine.

IV. The Sale

The document itself – P.Paris 21bis – is a standard deed of perpetual sale, a prasis, following a formula that had been used in Egypt for centuries.27 But its details capture a family at a particular moment.

The opening identifies the sellers with scrupulous fullness. “The Aurelii Psates son of Abraamios, whose mother is Tmouthes, purple-seller, and his wife Maria, daughter of Philamon, whose mother is Taniskene.” The title Aurelius, which every free citizen in the empire had carried since the Constitutio Antoniniana of 212 CE, had by the late sixth century become a conventional prefix, drained of specific meaning.28 More telling is the joint identification. Maria is not an appendage. She is a co-seller, a co-owner. The property belongs to both of them – to diapheron hemin, “the property belonging to us” – and both act as principals in the sale.29

The buyer is “Aurelius Pachymios, our most sweet son” – ho glykytatos hemon huios – “himself also a purple-seller, from the same village.”30 The trade had passed from father to son. The endearment glykytatos is formulaic – Byzantine documents routinely use it for family members – but the fact that Psates and Maria are selling to their own child, rather than to a stranger, gives the formula a warmth it does not always carry.

What are they selling? “A dilapidated house and a cell together with the bare land” – oikian katestrammenen kai kellin hama toi psilo topoi.31 The house is old, in poor repair. The “cell” (kellis) was a small room or outbuilding – perhaps a workshop, perhaps a storage space, perhaps sleeping quarters for a servant or worker. The bare land was an adjoining plot where a demolished structure may once have stood.

The seller stipulated that the sale was made “of our own free will and voluntary decision and guileless conscience and unwavering judgment and unrepentant choice, with firm and right mind.”32 This cascade of synonyms was standard legal Greek – the more words piled on, the harder to challenge. But behind the formula lay a real anxiety. Parents selling to a child, in a society where inheritance was the norm, needed to make the voluntary nature of the transaction unimpeachable. Other children might object later. They did not.

V. The Assessment

The price was not pulled from the air. It was set by a professional: “upon the assessment of Senouthes the builder from the same village.”33

This is a remarkable detail. Rather than negotiating between themselves – which, within a family, might have looked suspicious to outsiders who could later challenge the sale – Psates and Maria brought in an independent appraiser. Senouthes the oikodomos, the builder and mason, would have known the village’s property values as intimately as anyone. He had probably built or repaired half the houses on the lane. His assessment of three nomismata less one keration was binding on both parties: “the price mutually agreed upon and approved.”34

The gold nomisma – the Byzantine solidus, the most stable currency in the late antique world – was divided into twenty-four keratia (carats).35 Three nomismata less one keration meant roughly 71 keratia, or about 2.96 nomismata. The precision mattered. In a village economy, a single keration represented real purchasing power.

How did this compare? A house in the village of Aphrodite, sold in October 615 CE, fetched only two-thirds of a nomisma – but the property was described as “old, dilapidated,” and the seller, Eudoxia, was disposing of what may have been a share rather than the whole.36 Another house sale from the sixth century brought about four and two-thirds nomismata.37 When Pachymios himself bought a complete house in This in 616, the price was only thirteen keratia – about half a nomisma.38 Prices varied enormously. The condition of the building, the location, the desperation of the seller, the eagerness of the buyer – all shaped the number.

The payment was made in cash, “by hand-to-hand payment from the house, in full count.”39 The phrase suggests the gold was physically counted out on the spot, coin by coin, inside the house being sold. Perhaps the last act of ownership was receiving the price within the walls.

VI. The Guarantee

The legal armature of the sale was thorough. Psates and Maria guaranteed the property “against everyone who may make a claim, whether on account of our name or public obligation or private debt or anything else whatsoever at any time, of necessity.”40 If anyone challenged Pachymios’s ownership – if a forgotten creditor emerged, if a prior owner surfaced with a competing claim, if a tax obligation attached to the property – the sellers and their heirs were obligated to defend the sale at their own expense.

The penalty for failure was ruinous: repayment of the purchase price in double, plus all costs Pachymios had spent on improvements, plus “two simple gold pieces” as a further fine – and the sale would remain valid regardless.41 This was standard contract language, but standard language encoded real risks. Property disputes in Byzantine Egypt were common enough that every sale document anticipated them.

The earlier purchases from Viktor son of Psaios and Senouthes son of Phaliout – the men from whom Psates had originally acquired the property – were physically handed over to Pachymios as proof of the chain of title. “The two old sale documents,” the deed specifies, “are also deposited with you by us.”42 A buyer in this world did not merely receive a house. He received an archive. The documents were the title.

The witnesses were all clergy: Psates son of Papnouthios, deacon and scribe; Besatas son of Elias, deacon; Athanasios son of Paamios, priest.43 A professional notary, the symboilaiographos Ioulios, handled the formal drafting.44 The church and the law, together, guaranteed the transaction. In a village where literacy was concentrated among the clergy, deacons and priests functioned as the notarial class – scribes, witnesses, guarantors of good faith in transactions whose monetary value could be modest but whose social stakes were enormous.

VII. Death and Division

We do not know when Psates died. We do not know when Maria died.

We know only that by the twentieth of Epeiph in the eighteenth year of the emperor Maurice – 14 July 600 CE – both were gone.45 The document that tells us this, P.Paris 20, opens with a phrase that carries the full weight of Coptic Christian convention: “their blessed parents Psates and Maria having reached the end of their lives” – ton makaritōn autōn goneōn eis to biou telos peripesontōn.46 Makaritōn. Blessed. Departed.

What the document reveals is a family larger than the 592 sale had suggested. There were three children. Pachymios, the purple-seller, who had bought the house. Ioannes, “himself also a purple-seller, his full brother” – kai autos porphyropoles, homognēsios autou adelphos.47 And Tarsene, “another full sister of theirs, a widow” – allē homognēsia autōn adelphē, chēra.48

All three had gathered to divide what their parents had left. The estate was described as “small” – elachistē hypostasis.49 This may be a legal convention, a way of pre-empting tax assessments by understating the total. Or it may be the unvarnished truth. Purple-selling in a village was not a path to great wealth. It was a path to modest respectability.

The division was equal. Each child received one-third of “all the small estate left by them, movable, immovable, and self-moving property, from the most precious to the least valuable item.”50

The phrase “self-moving property” – autokineta – covers livestock. It may also cover people. Two individuals are named as shared assets: “Chariston the copper-worker” (orichalkeus) and “another Chariston the ironworker” (siderurgos).51 Whether these were enslaved persons, debt-bonded workers, or long-term contracted labourers whose service contracts were transferable is impossible to determine with certainty. The document lists them alongside houses and goods as property to be divided. Two metalworkers bearing the same name, practising different crafts, held as shared assets by three siblings – the detail resists easy interpretation. We should note it and not pretend to know more than the papyrus tells us.

VIII. The Settlement Among Siblings

But the house in This – the one sold to Pachymios in 592 – required special treatment. It had already been transferred. It was Pachymios’s property, purchased and paid for, not part of the parental estate. Ioannes and Tarsene explicitly confirmed this: they “have no claim against their most beloved other brother Pachoumis, nor against his heirs, regarding the house sold to him by their said parents Psates and Maria while they were still alive.”52

The earlier sale document was cited, its validity acknowledged. And then came the penalty clause: if either sibling challenged Pachymios’s ownership, the challenger would pay twelve gold nomismata.53

Twelve nomismata. Four times the purchase price. This was not a gentle reminder. It was a clause designed to make litigation more expensive than the house was worth.

Now we can see why Psates and Maria sold rather than gave. A gift could be challenged by other heirs. A sale, at a fair price assessed by an independent builder, witnessed by clergy, notarised by a professional, and backed by the chain of title from two prior owners, was nearly unassailable. The parents had structured the transaction precisely to protect Pachymios from his siblings. Perhaps they knew their children well. Perhaps they knew the world.

What remained in common, undivided, was listed: “two parental house-plots in the city of Panopolis on the lane of the holy place of Apa Kollouthos,” and “another parental house in the village of This,” and the two Charistons.54 These would be divided later, in a separate arrangement that does not survive.

The inheritance document itself was drawn up by a different notary, Dioskoros – not the same person as the Dioskoros who would later work for Pachymios as a hired purple-worker, but a professional symboilaiographos bearing one of the most common names in Egypt.55

IX. Three Emperors

Between the 592 sale and the last document in the archive (616 CE), the Roman world convulsed. The family’s papyri span three emperors.

The 592 sale opens: “In the reign and consulship of our most divine lord of the inhabited world, Flavius Mauricius, the eternal Augustus and Imperator, year ten.”56 Maurice was a capable Cappadocian general who had restored the Sasanian shah Khosrow II to his throne and won a favourable peace. His fiscal austerity, however, enraged the army.57 In November 602, the general Phocas led a mutiny. Maurice and his six sons were executed in one of the most notorious political murders of late antiquity. Phocas seized the throne.58

The 600 inheritance division still dates itself by Maurice’s reign: “year eighteen.”59 By January 605, when Pachymios hired a purple-worker, the dating formula read: “In the reign of our most serene lord Flavius Phocas, the eternal Augustus and Imperator, year three.”60 The wine loan of October 606 used the same formula, still under Phocas.61 But by November 613, when Kallinikos borrowed from Pachymios for the second time, the emperor was Heraclius, who had overthrown and killed Phocas in 610.62

Three emperors. Two violent coups, each involving the murder of the predecessor. And in the village of This, the purple-seller went on buying houses, hiring workers, and lending money. The documents register the changes in their dating formulas and nowhere else.

The invocation formulas also shifted. Under Maurice: the secular “in the reign and consulship.”63 Under Phocas and into the early years of Heraclius: “In the name of the Lord and Master Jesus Christ our God and Saviour.”64 By 613 and 616: “In the name of the holy and consubstantial Trinity, Father and Son and Holy Spirit.”65 Whether these reflect evolving theological emphases in Egyptian Christianity, changes in imperial chancery practice filtering down to provincial notaries, or simply the preferences of successive village scribes, they trace a thread of doctrinal development running through the paperwork of daily life.

The murder of an emperor in Constantinople – nearly two thousand kilometres away – changed the formula at the top of the page. It changed nothing at the bottom.

X. The Workshop

By 605, Pachymios was running a real operation.

The contract with Dioskoros son of Harsynios, preserved as SB 1.4503, reveals a workshop with multiple employees.66 Dioskoros is identified as a “hired purple-worker” – misthios porphyropoles – and he is contracting “to serve in the craft alongside your other hired workers” – synergazesthai sou tois allois misthiois.67 The plural matters. Pachymios had staff.

The terms were precise. Two years of service, reckoned from the fifteenth of Tybi in the tenth indiction. Wages: nineteen artabai of grain over the full two years – nine in the first year, ten in the second – plus food provided by Pachymios.68 Since one artaba of wheat was roughly equivalent to about one-third to one-half a nomisma in this period, Dioskoros’s annual wages came to approximately three to five nomismata – comparable to the price of the house Psates had sold in 592.69

A house for two years’ labour. That was the scale of the village economy.

The slight increase in the second year – nine artabai rising to ten – may reflect expected growth in the worker’s productivity, or it may simply be a negotiated sweetener. If Dioskoros left early, he owed Pachymios one gold nomisma as a penalty. If Pachymios expelled him before the term ended, Pachymios owed the full wages regardless.70 The symmetry of the penalty clause speaks to the balance of power. This was not a master dictating to a slave. It was a contract between a workshop owner and a skilled worker, each with obligations and each with recourse if the other defaulted.

Dioskoros’s mother Tibellas co-signed the contract – perhaps as guarantor, perhaps because Dioskoros was young and still under parental authority.71 A comparable contract from Antinoopolis, dated 569 CE, records a pattern weaver from Lykopolis entering into a similar service agreement – the same structure of fixed-term employment across different trades.72

The workshop itself is invisible. We have no description, no inventory, no floor plan. But we can infer its outlines. Purple-dyeing required vats for the dye baths, a supply of water, fuel for heating, and raw materials – alkanet roots or lichen, mordants like alum, textiles to dye. A workshop with multiple hired workers was not a one-room affair.

XI. The Patron and the Debtor

Pachymios was not only a dyer and a seller. He was a moneylender.

In October 606, a man named Kallinikos son of Senouthes borrowed from him. Kallinikos was a “hired worker” – misthios – from the village, and his need was straightforward: “for my own necessary needs,” the document says.73 The amount was one gold nomisma less one-half keration, weighed “by the weight of the leaves” – phylloph – a local weight standard.74 The repayment was not in gold but in wine: seventy-five koloba of “unadulterated new wine,” each containing seven xestai (about 3.5 litres), to be delivered “at the vineyard of Lynochois, at the time of the vintage, God willing.”75

Seventy-five koloba at seven xestai each amounts to roughly 262 litres of wine. That was the price of one gold coin, less a fraction, in the economy of early seventh-century This.

One of the witnesses to this transaction was a priest named “Ioannes son of Abraamios, from This.”76 The name is arresting. Abraamios was Psates’s father. Could this Ioannes be Pachymios’s own brother – the “Ioannes, himself also a purple-seller” named in the 600 inheritance division? He would have needed to become a priest in the intervening six years, which is not impossible but cannot be confirmed. Or this could be a different Ioannes son of a different Abraamios, bearing common names in a village where a few naming pools circulated endlessly. We note the coincidence and move on.

Seven years later, in November 613, Kallinikos borrowed again.77 Same debtor. Same creditor. Same formula. Same village. The amount was smaller this time – one-third of a nomisma less one-quarter keration – and the repayment correspondingly modest: twenty-five koloba of wine.78 The ratio held. Wine for gold, year after year, the vineyard paying for the needs of winter.

This was not predatory lending. It was the patron-client economy of a late antique village, where a man with capital extended credit to a man without it, and the repayment came from the land at harvest time. Whether the embedded interest rate was fair, exploitative, or irrelevant to the social relationship between the two men, we cannot determine from the documents alone.

XII. The Second House

In June 616 – twenty-four years after his parents sold him a dilapidated house – Pachymios bought another one.79

The sellers were women. Pyras daughter of Besatas, whose mother was Maria, acted also on behalf of her absent sister Rose. Antheria daughter of Senouthes, whose mother was Kyra, joined as co-seller.80 The property was “a complete house with all its rights, situated in the same village of This, on the lane called that of the phylake” – the guard-house – “having come to us by inheritance.”81

The price was thirteen gold keratia. About half a nomisma. A fraction of what his parents’ property had cost.82 Either this was a smaller house, or the sellers were in financial difficulty, or both.

But the detail that matters is the boundary description. The house’s eastern boundary is given as “the house of Pebos son of Horos and the house of the purchaser Pachymios.”83 Pachymios was buying the house next door. This was not a random acquisition. It was consolidation – a systematic expansion of his holdings, property by property, lane by lane.

In the southern boundary, a priest’s house. In the northern boundary, a public lane. In the west, the house of one Monathios son of Pamidos. And to the east: Pachymios. The village was filling in around him.

Among the witnesses was “Elias son of Besatas, priest.”84 In 592, a deacon named Besatas son of Elias had witnessed the parents’ sale. Now, twenty-four years later, someone who may be that deacon’s son – promoted from deacon to priest over the intervening generation – witnesses a sale to the deacon’s original client’s son. The village turned in on itself. The same families served the same functions across decades.

Another witness was “Mousaios son of Papnouthios, priest.”85 In 592, the scribe who wrote on behalf of Psates and Maria was “Psates son of Papnouthios, deacon.” Perhaps related. Perhaps not. In a village of limited naming stock, patterns repeat without implying connection.

The notary for this sale, and for the other late documents in the archive, was a man named Paulos – the third notary to serve This in the period covered by the family’s papers, after Ioulios (592) and Dioskoros (600).86

XIII. Women in the Archive

Maria daughter of Philamon is a co-seller, not a consenting spouse standing behind her husband. She owns and she disposes. Under late Roman and Byzantine law in Egypt, both spouses could hold and transfer property jointly, and Maria exercised this right with full legal standing.87 Compare Eudoxia in the village of Aphrodite, who in 615 sold a house “with the consent and agreement of her husband Anoufios” – a formula that foregrounds the husband’s permission rather than the wife’s ownership.88 Maria’s document uses a different construction. Both sellers are named equally, both act as principals, both are illiterate and both need a scribe to write on their behalf. The disability is shared. The authority is shared.

Tarsene, the widowed sister, received her one-third share of the inheritance without recorded objection or qualification.89 A widow in Byzantine Egypt occupied a particular legal position: freed from the requirement of a male guardian (kyrios) that constrained unmarried and sometimes married women, she could act independently in most transactions.

In the 616 sale, the sellers are again women: Pyras, acting for herself and her sister Rose, and Antheria.90 They had inherited the property and were disposing of it on their own authority. These were not passive actors. They chose to sell, they chose the buyer, they signed the deed.

Elsewhere in the corpus, women appear in similar roles. A woman named Amaresia, described as a trader, appears in a document from Antinoopolis in 569 CE.91 A couple elsewhere owes a decorated cape and dress to an official – textiles as currency, as obligation, as the medium through which late antique economic life was conducted.92

XIV. The Coptic World

The family’s Panopolis property sat on “the lane of the holy place of Apa Kollouthos.”93 This places them in the physical and spiritual landscape of Coptic Christianity as precisely as any theological statement.

Kollouthos was a physician-saint from the Theban region, martyred during the Diocletianic persecutions of the early fourth century. His cult spread rapidly through Upper Egypt.94 A lane named after his holy place would have been a devotional landmark and a property address all at once. To live on the lane of Apa Kollouthos was to live in the shadow of Coptic sanctity.

The will of Flavius Theodoros from nearby Antinoopolis, dated 567 CE, names the monastery of Apa Senouthes in the Panopolite nome as heir – the famous White Monastery of Shenoute, one of the greatest institutions of Egyptian monasticism, located near Sohag, close to Panopolis.95 This confirms the dense monastic landscape surrounding the family’s home city.

The clergy who populated the family’s documents – deacons writing contracts, priests witnessing sales – were not merely pious bystanders. They were the functional literate class in a village where most people could not write. Comparable documents from across Egypt show the same pattern. A surety document with presbyter and deacon witnesses from the early seventh century.96 A debt acknowledgment involving the priest Psais, from 549 CE.97 Priest Jakob’s rent debt in the same year.98 A Coptic-Greek bilingual debt document with a deacon witness from the late sixth century.99 The church was the social infrastructure.

XV. The Economy of Grain and Wine and Gold

Egypt paid 2.6 million solidi per year to Constantinople under Justinian, and the papyri from this period show no signs of monetary recession.100 The economy was highly monetised. But payments in kind remained common, especially for wages and debts.

Dioskoros the purple-worker was paid in grain: nineteen artabai over two years.101 Kallinikos repaid his loans in wine: seventy-five koloba one year, twenty-five another.102 The oil-pressers of nearby Aphrodite dealt in oil.103 Bakers dealt in bread.104 Gold was the unit of account, the standard against which everything else was measured, but the actual medium of exchange was often the product itself.

A payment to a coppersmith named Viktor, from the early seventh century, shows the metalworking economy.105 A weaver paying house-rent in a seventh-century document reminds us that textile workers were tenants as well as artisans.106 A debt cancellation from the village of Aphrodite, dated 515 CE, shows the same network of credit and obligation that connected Pachymios to Kallinikos.107

The family’s story played out within this economy. Psates brought his skill from Panopolis. Pachymios turned it into a workshop. The workshop paid its workers in grain, its creditors in gold, and its debtors repaid in wine. Every transaction was documented, witnessed, notarised. The paper was the proof. Without it, you might lose the house.

XVI. What We Know and What We Imagine

Here is what the documents allow us to reconstruct.

Sometime around the middle of the sixth century, a purple-seller named Psates left the textile city of Panopolis with his wife Maria and settled in the ancient village of This. They built a business, bought property, raised three children. By 592, probably elderly, they sold a dilapidated house to their eldest son for a fair price assessed by a local builder, with the explicit purpose – we can now see – of removing that property from the future inheritance pool and protecting Pachymios’s claim.

Both parents died before July 600. Their three children – two purple-sellers and a widow – divided the estate equally, confirmed the earlier sale with a crushing penalty clause, and left certain properties undivided for future resolution.

Pachymios prospered. He ran a workshop with multiple hired workers, contracted them for multi-year terms, paid them in grain, and fed them at his table. He lent gold to local workers and received wine at harvest. He bought property systematically, consolidating his holdings over at least twenty-four years. He lived through three emperors and two coups. He appears in the record for the last time in June 616, still buying, still building, still identified as porphyropoles from Panopolis, “now by the providence of God residing in the same village of This.”108

Pronoia theou. By the providence of God. The phrase appears only in the 616 document, not in any of the earlier ones. Perhaps it was the notary Paulos’s preferred formula. Perhaps Pachymios, now an older man, had developed a sharper sense of divine grace. The Sasanian Persians would invade Egypt in 619, only three years after his last attested transaction.109 The Arab conquest would follow in 641-642.110 Whether Pachymios lived to see either catastrophe, we do not know.

Here is what we imagine. Perhaps Psates’s hands were permanently stained. Purple dye does not wash off easily; the Leiden recipes describe processes involving heating, soaking, and repeated dipping that leave the dyer’s fingers marked for months.111 Perhaps the house on the lane of the heirs of Pachot smelled of the workshop – the sharp mineral tang of alum mordant, the earthy sweetness of alkanet root, the sourness of old vinegar used to fix the dye. We may imagine. But we should say when we are imagining.

The documents give us names, dates, prices, boundaries, penalties. They give us a deacon’s handwriting and a notary’s seal. They do not give us the colour of anyone’s hands. They do not tell us whether Psates was a good father, whether Maria was happy, whether Tarsene grieved her husband or her parents more, whether Pachymios paid Dioskoros fairly, whether Kallinikos resented his debts or was grateful for the credit.

What the documents do, relentlessly, is show a family that kept its records. Sale documents passed from seller to buyer. Old deeds were deposited with the new owner. Inheritance settlements cited earlier contracts by name. For twenty-four years, the paper trail accumulated: six documents that survive, and surely dozens more that do not.

Psates and Maria could not write. But they understood the power of the written word well enough to have every transaction recorded, witnessed, notarised, and preserved. The deacon held the pen. The purple-seller held the house.
