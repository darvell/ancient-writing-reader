Because I Am a Greek

Ptolemaios, Son of Glaukias --- The Serapeum at Memphis, c. 172--145 BCE

37 primary documents · 117 notes

They came on the eleventh of Phaophi, in the nineteenth year of the reign of Ptolemy Philometor — November, by our reckoning, of 163 BCE.1 The sweepers and the bakers, the men whose hands were calloused from temple service, who scrubbed the sacred floors and kneaded the offering bread, who descended daily into the Anoubieion to perform their rotation of duties.2 With them came a doctor named Archebios and a cloak-seller called Mys, and others whose names the victim never learned.3 They forced their way into the Astartieion, the small chapel of the Semitic goddess Astarte nestled inside the great Serapeum complex at Memphis, and they went looking for a man.

The man they found was Ptolemaios, son of Glaukias.

He was Macedonian. He had not left this building in ten years.

They wanted to drag him out — exspasai, to strip and humiliate him.4 This was not the first time. They had tried before, he wrote afterward, during the rebellion — ouses apostaseos — and for a specific reason: para to Hellena me einai.5

Because I am a Greek.

Ptolemaios saw what was happening. He recognized the look of men who had lost all sense — aponoenoemenous, he called them, a word that carries the weight of recklessness tipping into violence.6 He barricaded himself inside his chamber. But his agent Harmais was not so lucky. They found Harmais on the dromos, the great processional avenue lined with limestone sphinxes that led up to the temple, and they threw him to the ground and beat him with bronze scrapers.7

Chalkois xystersin. Bronze files, the kind used for smoothing metal or scraping hides. Repurposed, in a moment of fury, as weapons.8

Afterward, from behind his locked door, Ptolemaios sat down and composed a petition. He addressed it to Dionysios, one of the king’s philoi and strategos of the nome, in careful, practiced Greek. He described what had happened. He asked that Dionysios write to Menedemos, the official stationed in the Anoubieion, ordering the attackers to make restitution — and if they refused, to have them sent to the strategos himself, “so that you may treat them in accordance with your hatred of wrongdoing.”9

The petition was filed. A clerk endorsed it. Four different hands added notes to the papyrus — date, subject, forwarding instructions.10 The bureaucracy ground forward. Whether Ptolemaios ever received justice, the papyri do not say.

What they do say — what over one hundred and twenty documents found scattered across the Serapeum complex say — is that this man lived inside the temple for at least twenty years, and perhaps longer, held there by something he understood as a divine command, unable to leave, writing petition after petition into what increasingly looks like silence.11

I. The Tenth Year

By the time the sweepers broke in, Ptolemaios had already spent a decade confined to the Astartieion. He dated his katoche — his religious detention — to approximately October of 172 BCE.12 In every petition he filed, he marked the count: “now for the ninth year,” “now for the tenth year,” “now for the eleventh year,” as though the accumulation of years itself constituted an argument, as though duration were evidence of legitimacy or, perhaps, of suffering.13

The Greek word katoche (from katechein, to hold fast, to detain) described his condition, and the condition of others like him in the Serapeum.14 A katochos — one who is “held” — was not a prisoner in any legal sense. Ptolemaios received visitors. He handled money, managed accounts, wrote and received letters, adopted a girl, took in two orphaned sisters, directed his younger brother’s education.15 He was, in many ways, an active participant in the world. But he could not cross the threshold of the sacred precinct. He states this again and again in language that admits no ambiguity: ou dynamai exelthein, I cannot leave.16

What held him? The scholarly literature has debated this for over a century. Some have argued that katoche was a form of voluntary religious dedication, a vow made to the god Sarapis in exchange for healing or revelation.17 Others see it as a kind of temple asylum that calcified into permanence. Still others understand it as divine possession in the most literal sense — the god seized the man and would not let go.18 The evidence from Ptolemaios’s own archive suggests something more nuanced, and more disturbing. One petition, written on behalf of the twin sisters he looked after, states that Ptolemaios’s care for them was kata prostagma tou theou — “by command of the god.”19 The god had spoken, perhaps through a dream, perhaps through the mediation of a priest, and Ptolemaios obeyed. Whether he had once entered the temple freely, whether there had been a moment of choice before the door closed behind him, we cannot say. By the time the archive begins in earnest, around 164 BCE, the question is moot. Ptolemaios is inside. He has been inside for years. He lives in a pastophorion, a priestly chamber within the Astartieion, and the world comes to him only in fragments — letters, ration deliveries, the sound of violence on the dromos.20

He was not alone in his condition. A man named Harmais — perhaps the same Harmais who was beaten, perhaps a different one, as the name was common — also petitioned from katoche, describing himself as being in his fifth year.21 Apollonios, Ptolemaios’s own younger brother, eventually entered katoche alongside him; by 158 BCE, a petition records Apollonios as having been detained for fifteen years, which would place his entry around 172 BCE, the same year as Ptolemaios.22 The Serapeum, it seems, collected people. It drew them in, held them, and did not easily let them go.

II. The Serapeum

To understand what it meant to be held in this place, one must try to see the place itself.

The Serapeum stood at Saqqara, the vast necropolis that served as the city of the dead for Memphis, the ancient capital of Lower Egypt.23 Memphis itself lay where the Nile delta begins to fan out from the narrow valley, a city of perhaps a hundred thousand people in the second century BCE, still a great metropolis even as Alexandria had overtaken it in political importance.24 The necropolis stretched along the desert escarpment to the west, above the floodplain, a landscape of sand and carved stone and monumental silence. The Step Pyramid of Djoser, already two and a half millennia old, loomed over everything.25

The Serapeum complex — to mega Sarapieion, the great Serapeum, as the papyri consistently call it — was not a single building but a sprawl of interconnected temples, chapels, galleries, courtyards, and administrative structures.26 At its heart lay the cult of the Apis bull, the living manifestation of the god Ptah, chief deity of Memphis. When an Apis bull died, it became Osorapis — Osiris-Apis — and was embalmed and buried in enormous granite sarcophagi in underground galleries carved deep into the bedrock.27 The Hellenistic god Serapis, after whom the temple was named, was himself a fusion of Osorapis with Greek divine attributes, a deliberate creation of the early Ptolemaic dynasty meant to bridge the religious worlds of their Greek and Egyptian subjects.28

The dromos, the processional avenue where Harmais was beaten, was a grand affair — a long stone-paved road lined on both sides with sphinxes, leading up from the cultivated land toward the temple entrance.29 Auguste Mariette, the French archaeologist who rediscovered the Serapeum in 1850–51, first noticed the heads of sphinxes protruding from the sand and followed them like a trail of bread crumbs to the temple itself.30 It was in the chambers and corridors of this complex that he found the papyri — Ptolemaios’s papyri, along with those of other katochoi and temple functionaries — scattered and buried, waiting nearly two thousand years to be read.

Within the Serapeum precinct stood several subsidiary temples: the Anoubieion, dedicated to Anubis, the jackal-headed god of embalming, with its own staff and administration;31 the Asklepieion, where the Greek healing god Asklepios was identified with the deified Egyptian architect Imhotep;32 and the Astartieion, the chapel of the Semitic goddess Astarte, where Ptolemaios lived.33 That a Macedonian man should spend his life in a chapel dedicated to a Phoenician goddess inside an Egyptian temple complex overseen by a Greek administration tells you something about the religious landscape of Ptolemaic Egypt — layered, syncretic, pragmatic, and strange.

III. A Macedonian in Egypt

Ptolemaios identified himself, in every petition, as Makedon — Macedonian.34 This was not merely an ethnic designation. It was a legal and social status. The Macedonians formed the apex of the Ptolemaic settler hierarchy, descendants (or claimed descendants) of the soldiers who had followed Alexander the Great into Egypt and stayed.35 They were katoikoi, military settlers granted land allotments (kleroi) in exchange for military service, constituting a privileged class above ordinary Greek settlers and far above the native Egyptian population.36

Ptolemaios’s father, Glaukias, held a kleros at a village called Psichis in the Heracleopolite nome, a district in Middle Egypt along the Nile.37 It was a modest holding, by the standards of Macedonian settlers. Glaukias had four sons: Ptolemaios, Hippalos, Sarapion, and Apollonios, the youngest, sometimes called paidarion — the child.38 The family was not wealthy, but they were Greek-speaking, literate, and legally privileged. They were, in the social taxonomy of Ptolemaic Egypt, the ruling class.

How Ptolemaios came to the Serapeum, and why, the papyri do not directly explain. He entered katoche around 172 BCE, when Ptolemy VI Philometor had been on the throne for roughly eight years.39 We may imagine him as a young man — though we do not know his age — perhaps visiting the temple for an incubation dream, as many did, seeking divine guidance or healing. Perhaps something happened during that visit. A dream. A command. A priest’s interpretation that changed the course of his life. By the time we encounter him in the documents, the question of origin has been overtaken by the fact of duration.

Glaukias died around 161 BCE, at Psichis, and Ptolemaios could not attend him.40 “My father departed to the gods,” he wrote in one petition, and then immediately pivoted to the practical consequences: he could not leave the temple to settle the estate, and his brothers were being harassed by local officials.41 In another petition, he described how neighbors at Psichis — Hesperos, Ataios, and Polemon — had demolished the family home and carried off building materials worth twenty talents, “showing contempt because I cannot leave the temple.”42 He asked the king to intervene. He asked his brother Apollonios to represent him. He asked for the twenty talents to “be brought to me here and I may have what I need and not waste away from hunger.”43

The phrase is striking. Not waste away from hunger. A Macedonian katoikos, a member of the settler elite, begging the king for the proceeds of his own demolished house so that he could eat. Katoche, whatever spiritual dignity it conferred, was materially ruinous.

And yet he stayed.

IV. The Sweepers and the Bakers

The attack on Ptolemaios in November 163 BCE was not an isolated incident. It was a symptom.

The kallyntai — the sweepers, the temple cleaners — and the artokopoi — the bakers who produced the sacred bread offerings — were Egyptian. They were the manual laborers of the temple economy, performing service rotations (ephemereiai) in the Serapeum and the Anoubieion.44 They occupied the lowest tier of the temple hierarchy, below the priests, below the pastophoroi (chamber-keepers), below even the katochoi who sat in their locked rooms and wrote petitions. And they knew what Ptolemaios was. He was Greek. He was Macedonian. He was one of the conquerors.

The context matters enormously. In the 160s BCE, Egypt was convulsing. The Sixth Syrian War had brought a Seleucid army to the gates of Alexandria in 168 BCE; it took a Roman ultimatum, the famous “Day of Eleusis,” to force Antiochus IV to withdraw.45 The royal family was fracturing — Ptolemy VI Philometor and his younger brother Ptolemy VIII Euergetes II, later known as Physcon, fought bitterly for the throne, with periods of joint rule, exile, and reconciliation.46 And in Upper Egypt, a major native rebellion, sometimes called the revolt of Dionysios Petosarapis, had flared in the 160s BCE, a continuation of decades of Egyptian resistance to Greek rule.47

Ptolemaios himself named this context. The sweepers had tried to expel him before, he wrote, ouses apostaseos — “there being a rebellion.”48 The violence against him was not personal. It was political, ethnic, structural. The temple, which housed Egyptians and Greeks together in an uneasy cohabitation ordered by syncretic theology and Ptolemaic administration, became a microcosm of the tensions rending the kingdom.

There was another incident, perhaps slightly earlier. Ptolemaios, the man appointed by the archiphylakites in the Anoubieion, and a man called Amosis, agent of the archiereus (high priest), arrived at the Astartieion with temple police — phylakitai — claiming they had information that weapons were hidden inside.49 They searched the entire building. They found nothing. But the search itself was a message: the authorities suspected — or wanted Ptolemaios to know they suspected — that the Greek enclave within the Serapeum might be harboring arms. In the expanded version of this petition, addressed directly to the king and queen, Ptolemaios added that the raiders had stolen property from the deposits of other enkatochoi (fellow detainees) and ransacked to adyton tes theas — the inner sanctum of the goddess Astarte herself.50

A Greek man. A Semitic goddess. An Egyptian temple. The lines of identity and loyalty in the Serapeum were not simple.

V. Harmais on the Dromos

The dromos would have been busy that day, as most days. The processional avenue was not merely a ceremonial route; it was the main artery of the temple complex, a thoroughfare where pilgrims, priests, vendors, petitioners, and temple servants moved between the various shrines and administrative buildings.51 Harmais, Ptolemaios’s agent — the man who ran errands for him, carried messages, perhaps purchased supplies in the markets of Memphis below — was on the dromos when the mob found him.

They knocked him down. They beat him with bronze scrapers.

The detail is precise in the way that details are precise when a man has been turning the event over in his mind, composing and recomposing the petition that will carry his only hope of redress. Chalkois xystersin. Not fists. Not sticks. Bronze instruments, tools of craft repurposed as weapons.52 The petition does not say how badly Harmais was hurt. It does not say whether he cried out, whether anyone intervened, whether the sphinxes stared down with their limestone indifference. It says what it needs to say to make a legal case: there was an assault, it was unprovoked, it was part of a pattern, and the strategos should act.

Harmais appears throughout the archive, a figure both essential and peripheral. His name is Egyptian — Har-mais, “Horus is born” — and his role as Ptolemaios’s agent suggests the kind of cross-ethnic relationship that the Serapeum both produced and strained.53 Financial accounts record payments to Harmais: 400 drachmas here, 600 drachmas there.54 He was not a slave but a paid associate, someone who moved freely through the spaces Ptolemaios could not enter. He was, in a sense, Ptolemaios’s body in the world.

And it was his body that absorbed the blows.

VI. The Twin Sisters

Among the papyri in Ptolemaios’s archive, a disproportionate number concern not Ptolemaios himself but two Egyptian girls: Thaues and Taous.55

They were twins. Their names shift across the documents — Tagetes, Tages, Tautes, Taus — the fluid transliteration of Egyptian names into Greek script producing a small chaos of variants.56 Their story, told and retold across multiple petitions, is one of the most harrowing in the archive.

Their mother, Nephorys, had abandoned their father and taken up with a soldier named Philippos, son of Sogenos, stationed at Memphis.57 This was not merely an affair. Philippos attempted to murder the twins’ father. The father escaped by leaping into the river and swimming to an island, where a passing boat picked him up and carried him to Herakleopolis.58 There, the petition says, he “died of grief.”59 His body was eventually recovered by his brothers and brought back to the necropolis at Memphis, but Nephorys — his estranged wife, his would-be killer’s lover — had not dared to bury him.60

Nephorys, meanwhile, sold half the family house for seven talents and collected rent of fifteen hundred drachmas monthly on the rest, giving nothing to the twins.61 The girls were expelled. Starving, they made their way up to the Serapeum, to a man their father had known — “a certain Ptolemaios, one of those in katoche in the great Serapieion, who was a friend of our father.”62 Ptolemaios took them in. He fed them.

Then something happened that bound their fates to the temple permanently. The sacred Apis bull Ta-Renenutet II died on April 6, 164 BCE.63 In the ancient ritual of mourning, modeled on the myth of Isis and Nephthys weeping over the body of Osiris, a pair of twin sisters was required to perform lamentations over the dead bull, which in death became Osorapis.64 Thaues and Taous were selected. “When the mourning of Apis occurred,” the petition reads, “they bring us down to mourn the god.”65

The twins became, in effect, ritual mourners. They poured libations to Osorapis on behalf of the king and queen.66 For this service, they were entitled to an annual stipend from the royal treasury: one metretes of sesame oil, one metretes of castor oil, baked loaves, and grain.67 It was not much. And it rarely arrived on time.

The bureaucratic saga of the twins’ rations fills dozens of papyri. Petition after petition, addressed to the hypo-dioiketes Sarapion, to the epimelete Mennides, to the king and queen themselves, pleading for the delivery of oil that was owed, bread that had not come, payments that were months or years in arrears.68 Ptolemaios, who could not leave his chamber to pursue the matter in person, appointed Demetrios, son of Sosos, a Cretan, as his legal representative.69 The receipts and memoranda show the slow, grinding process: Mennides writes to Theon about measuring oil; Theon issues an order; a receipt is generated for two metretai of sesame oil received on December 26, 162 BCE.70 One imagines the relief was temporary.

The twins’ half-brother Panchrates, Nephorys’s son, made things worse. He stole their oil rations and their money and fled to his mother.71

And through all of this, there was Ptolemaios. Unable to leave. Unable to protect them except through words on papyrus. The most remarkable petition states that the god himself — to theion — had commanded Ptolemaios to look after the twins continuously, “so that we should lack none of the necessities.”72 It adds a detail that catches in the throat: “Most people who do not know say, on account of the care he exercises over us, that he is our father.”73

He was not their father. But “not maintaining this by ancestral custom, but by command of the god,” he had become something like one.74

VII. Dreams in the Temple

On the twenty-first of June, 158 BCE, Ptolemaios was recording his dreams.75

The papyrus known as UPZ 1.77 preserves a small dream diary, a collection of night visions that Ptolemaios set down with the care of a man who believed that dreams were messages from the divine and that the messages mattered.76 They are strange. They are intimate. They resist easy interpretation, which is perhaps the point.

One dream belonged to Tages, one of the twins. She dreamed she was walking down through the quarter, counting nine houses. She wanted to turn back. Someone said, “Yes, it is time to go.” She replied, “It is late for me.”77

It is late for me. A girl’s dream, recorded by a man who had been in the temple for fourteen years. Whether the lateness was hers or his, or both, the diary does not explain.

Ptolemaios dreamed of the twins as well. He saw Tages “with a beautiful voice, sweeter in tone and in good condition.” He saw Taous “laughing, and her foot large and clean.”78 The specificity of the details — the voice, the foot — has the quality of genuine dream memory, that odd precision that clings to fragments while the narrative dissolves.

Other dreams were darker. In one, two men stood before Ptolemaios and said: “Ptolemaios, take the money of blood.” They counted out a hundred drachmas for him and gave Tagetes a purse full of stater coins, saying: “Here, the money of blood.” Ptolemaios replied: “She has more money than I do.”79 The image is disquieting. Blood money. The dreamer counting, comparing, envying — even in sleep, the economics of scarcity at the Serapeum intruded.

In still another dream, recorded under year 23 of the king’s reign, Ptolemaios called upon the greatest Ammon to come from the north. Then he imagined himself as a cow in labor. Someone seized the cow and threw her down, plunged a hand into the womb, and extracted a bull.80 The Apis symbolism is unmistakable — the birth of the sacred bull from suffering, the divine emerging violently from the mortal body. Whether Ptolemaios saw himself as the vessel or the witness, the dream speaks of pain and expectation and the nearness of the god.

A professional dream interpreter named Nechthonbes appears in a later document, interpreting dreams about “the twins and myself.”81 The Serapeum was, among other things, a place of incubation — pilgrims came to sleep within the sacred precinct and receive divine communications through dreams. That Ptolemaios, a permanent resident, should have developed a rich dream life, and should have taken the trouble to record it, suggests that dream interpretation was not merely an incidental feature of his confinement but perhaps one of its purposes.82 The god had seized him. The god spoke to him in his sleep. The writing down was itself an act of devotion, or of desperate attention.

Later, Apollonios would write to Ptolemaios from outside the temple: “I am having wicked dreams; I see Menedemos pursuing me.”83 Even correspondence brought dreams, as though the Serapeum’s atmosphere of divine communication leaked out through the papyrus itself.

VIII. Letters from Outside

The world beyond the temple walls reached Ptolemaios in handwriting.

In September of 161 BCE, a man named Lysimachos wrote to “Ptolemaios, the twins, and Apollonios” — addressing the entire household as though it were a family unit, which by then it was.84 “If you are well… I too am in good health. Know that since I left you…” The formula is standard, the letter conventional, but its address is revealing. Lysimachos had visited. He had sat in the pastophorion, shared a meal perhaps, and now he was writing back from wherever he had gone, maintaining the connection.

From a man named Dionysios came a letter of wounded dignity: “Such liberality, not vulgarity, have I displayed to all people, but especially to you…”85 Something had gone wrong between them. The letter does not survive completely enough to say what. But the tone — defensive, aggrieved, slightly pompous — suggests a friendship under strain.

The most revealing letters came from Ptolemaios’s family. His brother Sarapion wrote in August of 153 BCE to announce his marriage: “I have contracted marriage with the daughter of Hesperos. I intend to bring her in during the month of Mesore.”86 The news arrived at the Serapeum as news from another planet might — a brother marrying, building a life, while Ptolemaios remained inside. The name Hesperos is notable; a man by that name was among the neighbors who had demolished the family home at Psichis years earlier.87 Whether this was the same Hesperos, and whether the marriage represented reconciliation or merely the complicated entanglements of village life, we can only wonder.

The youngest brother, Apollonios, wrote the most. He addressed Ptolemaios as patri — “father” — a term of respect and perhaps of genuine filial feeling, since Ptolemaios had raised him after Glaukias’s death.88 His letters are practical, affectionate, occasionally tense. In October of 154 BCE: “Send me (information about) how much Peteusorapis has and from what date, likewise Kottabos, likewise Chentosneuseus.”89 In July of 159 BCE: an accounting of money, the sale of a linen cloth, the sale of a garment.90 In August of 152 BCE, the letter about the wicked dreams, followed by anxious questions about “Apollonios and yourself.”91 And then, in September of 152 BCE, a note edged with resentment or shame: “I swear by Sarapis, if I were not somewhat embarrassed, you would not see my face…”92

What had Apollonios done? What was the embarrassment? The archive preserves the letter but not the context. We are left with a fragment of emotion, detached from its cause, floating like all the other fragments in the Serapeum’s documentary debris.

One letter stands apart. It is not addressed to Ptolemaios but was found among his papers, and it celebrates a different kind of crossing. Someone writes to someone else: “Learning that you are studying Egyptian letters, I rejoiced for you and myself, because now at least when you come to the city you will teach the children at the practice of Phalou…etes the enema-doctor, and you will have provision for your old age.”93 The passage has been much discussed. It suggests that someone in or near the Serapeum milieu was learning to read demotic Egyptian — that the boundary between Greek and Egyptian culture, so violently enforced by the sweepers and bakers, was also being quietly eroded by education.94

IX. Oil and Bread

The material life of the Serapeum can be reconstructed, in surprising detail, from the financial accounts in the archive. These are not literary documents. They are lists, receipts, tallies. They have the flat eloquence of numbers.

Water arrived daily, measured in keramia — clay jars.95 Clothing was purchased and recorded: linen (sindonas), dyed goods (bapta), tunics. The accounts from 161 BCE list costs for each item.96 The twins’ clothing expenses for 160 BCE were tracked separately, as a charge against their official stipend.97

The household expense account for the twins from January 159 BCE is a remarkable document, a daily shopping list that reads like a menu: cakes, garlic, lettuce, pomegranates, nuts, radishes, beer (zyton), goose meat, salt, honey, soda (nitron), sycamore beer, sesame cakes.98 The diet was varied, even pleasant. There were payments to the plagiophylakes — “side-guards,” perhaps security personnel — and a separate notation: “Harmais’ account: 400 drachmas.”99

Money flowed through the temple household in small, carefully recorded increments. Apollonios’s accounts show payments to individuals with Egyptian names — Petesochos, Patorso, Orbek, Petenetis — alongside payments to figures from the Greek community.100 A payment of 2,200 drachmas to Apollonios himself is recorded in March 160 BCE.101 The castor oil distributions were measured in kotylai — cups, roughly a quarter of a liter each.102 Bronze vessels were inventoried.103

And then there are the expenses for the mourning of the Mnevis bull, the sacred bull of Heliopolis, in March of 158 BCE: grain, throne-covers, pure bread, salt, soda.104 The death of a sacred animal set the entire temple apparatus in motion, and the archive captures a sliver of the resulting expenditure.

What emerges from these accounts is a picture of a household that was modest but not destitute, dependent on institutional rations that arrived erratically and on family support that came when it could. Ptolemaios was not starving — not usually — but he was always close enough to want that the threat of it shaped his rhetoric. “So that I may not waste away from hunger” was not, perhaps, pure hyperbole.105

X. Twenty Years

The archive’s main body runs from the mid-160s to the early 150s BCE, with the bulk of the documents concentrated between 164 and 158 BCE.106 But the span of Ptolemaios’s confinement was far longer.

If he entered katoche in October 172 BCE, as the chronology suggests, and if the latest document mentioning him — a letter from Paniskos dated February 8, 145 BCE — found him still at the Serapeum, then Ptolemaios spent at least twenty-seven years inside the temple complex.107

Twenty-seven years. A man could enter in his twenties and emerge, if he emerged at all, in his fifties. Empires could rise and fall. The Serapeum’s daily rhythms — the morning purifications, the evening offerings, the slow accretion of sand against the dromos sphinxes — would have become the entire architecture of a life.

In 158 BCE, someone — perhaps Ptolemaios himself, perhaps Apollonios — scrawled a note that survived among the archive: “I am despondent (oligopsycho) about my katoche and about Tagetes. Where shall we live?”108 The word oligopsycho means something like “small-souled,” “faint-hearted,” “losing heart.” It is the opposite of megalopsychia, the Aristotelian virtue of great-souledness. It is a word for the feeling of being diminished.109

Where shall we live. The question is devastating in its simplicity. For a man who had lived in the same building for fourteen years, and who would apparently continue to live there for at least another thirteen, the question “where shall we live” suggests that even after all that time, the situation felt provisional. The temple was not a home. It was a holding.

What kept him? The divine command, presumably — kata prostagma tou theou.110 But also, perhaps, the practical impossibility of leaving. His family property was destroyed. His father was dead. His brothers were scattered. The twins depended on him. His legal identity had become inextricable from his status as katochos; every petition, every official interaction, began with the formula: “Ptolemaios, son of Glaukias, Macedonian, of those in katoche in the great Serapieion.”111 To leave would be to become no one.

His brother Apollonios’s trajectory is instructive. Apollonios entered katoche alongside Ptolemaios, apparently around the same time, and by 158 BCE had been confined for fifteen years.112 He was the younger brother, the paidarion, the one who had been sent to study, who wrote letters, who managed the family’s affairs outside. And then he too was inside, held by the same god, in the same building. The archive contains both their petitions, sometimes in the same papyrus.113 Two brothers, sealed together in the Astartieion, growing old in the service of Sarapis.

XI. What the Record Does Not Say

The archive does not end. It stops.

The last datable document that mentions Ptolemaios is the letter from Paniskos, dated February 8, 145 BCE, concerning the administration of contracts in the Thebaid.114 It is an administrative matter, routine, bureaucratic. It does not say: “And this is the last letter Ptolemaios will receive.” It does not say: “The old man is dying.” It does not say anything at all about what happened next.

Did Ptolemaios leave the Serapeum? Did he die there? Was there a moment when the god released him, when the katoche lifted like a fever breaking? Or did he simply grow old in his pastophorion, the petitions growing fewer, the dreams growing stranger, until one day Harmais came to the chamber door and received no answer?

We do not know.

The papyri were found scattered across the Serapeum complex by Mariette’s workmen in the early 1850s. They had been separated, mixed with other documents, dispersed. Today they are spread across Europe: roughly sixty in the Louvre, thirty in the British Library, fifteen in the National Museum of Antiquities at Leiden, with others in the Vatican Library, the Hermitage, and elsewhere.115 Ulrich Wilcken gathered them together in his monumental Urkunden der Ptolemaerzeit in 1927, reconstructing the archive from its fragments.116 But reconstruction is not recovery. The silences remain.

A late document in the collection, dated to 98 BCE — more than fifty years after the main archive ends — is a copy of a letter by Petesis, the chief embalmer of Osorapis.117 It was found in the same location as Ptolemaios’s papers. Whether it indicates continued use of the Astartieion, or merely the archaeological accident of documents from different periods settling into the same stratum, is impossible to say.

What we are left with is an archive that is also a life, or the traces of a life. Over one hundred and twenty documents testify to the existence of a man who was held inside a temple by a god he could not refuse, who was beaten because he was Greek, who raised two orphaned girls because the god told him to, who dreamed of blood money and laboring cows and the laughter of children, who wrote petition after petition to officials who endorsed them and filed them and did not always act. A man who, somewhere in the middle of his confinement, in a moment of what sounds like despair, asked the question that the archive will never answer:

Where shall we live?

Perhaps it is enough that he asked. Perhaps it is enough that someone wrote it down, on a scrap of papyrus, in the pastophorion of the Astartieion, in the great Serapeum at Memphis, in the desert above the Nile, in the tenth year, in the fifteenth year, in the twentieth year of being held by a god who would not let go.
