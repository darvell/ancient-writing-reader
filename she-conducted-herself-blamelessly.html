She Conducted Herself Blamelessly

Syra, Daughter of Theon --- Oxyrhynchus, c. 20--50 CE

Domestic Violence Dowry Law Women's Legal Agency Weaving 1st Century CE Oxyrhynchus

I. The Petition

Let her speak. The full text, translated from the Greek, reads:

I married Sarapion, bringing him by cession a dowry amounting to 200 drachmae of silver. As he was destitute of means I received him into my parents’ house, and I for my part conducted myself blamelessly in all respects. But Sarapion, having squandered my dowry as he pleased, continually ill-treated and insulted me, using violence towards me, and depriving me of the necessaries of life; finally he deserted me, leaving me in a state of destitution. I therefore beg you to order him to be brought before you, in order that he may be compelled perforce to pay back my dowry increased by half its amount. This petition is without prejudice to any other claims which I have or may have against him.3

Every sentence earns its place. The petition follows the standard structure of Graeco-Egyptian legal complaints — addressee, identification of the petitioner, narrative of the wrong, request for remedy, reservation of rights — but within that structure, Syra’s account is relentless. She does not wander. She does not plead for sympathy. She builds her case: she brought money into the marriage; she behaved well; he did not. She wants compensation.

The document is catalogued as P.Oxy. 2.281. It was published in 1899 by Bernard Grenfell and Arthur Hunt as part of the second volume of The Oxyrhynchus Papyri, the vast trove of documents excavated from an ancient garbage heap at Bahnasa, Egypt.4 No date survives on the papyrus itself, but Grenfell and Hunt placed it in the early first century CE based on the handwriting and the name of the archidikastes Heraclides.5

What Syra could not have known — what would not have mattered to her — is that her discarded petition would outlast the court that received it, the city where she lived, and the empire that governed her.

II. The Woman and the City

Oxyrhynchus sat on the Bahr Yusuf, a channel of the Nile in what the Romans called the Heptanomia, the region of Middle Egypt.6 By Syra’s time it was a significant provincial capital, a Greek-speaking metropolis in a landscape still deeply Egyptian. The city had a gymnasium, a hippodrome, temples to both Greek and Egyptian gods, a Serapeum where contracts were notarized, and a population that mixed Greek settlers, Egyptian natives, and the ambiguous category called “Persians of the Epigone” — descendants of Persian-era military colonists whose ethnic label had long since become a legal fiction.7

It was also a city of weavers. The papyri from Oxyrhynchus record a dense community of artisan textile workers — gerdioi, as they called themselves — organized by quarter, taxed by trade, and vulnerable to the predations of local officials.8 They lived in named neighborhoods: the quarter of Gymnasium Square, the quarter of Thoëris Square. They bought and sold looms, apprenticed their sons to master weavers, and haggled over the price of linen thread.9 We know all this because they left behind their contracts, their tax receipts, their letters, and their complaints. The desert air preserved what the archivists discarded.

Syra’s father was named Theon.10 Her husband, Sarapion. These were among the most common names in the Oxyrhynchite onomasticon — Theon appears in dozens of papyri from the city, Sarapion in dozens more.11 The names tell us that the family moved in the Greek-speaking stratum of Oxyrhynchite society, though “Greek” in this context meant something fluid: people who wrote in Greek, registered their contracts in Greek, petitioned in Greek, and lived in a city where the temples still honored Osiris and Isis alongside Serapis and Heracles.

Of Syra herself, her petition is all we have. Her name, Syra, means “the Syrian woman” — perhaps an echo of ancestral origins, perhaps simply a common name by the first century.12 She is identified only as “daughter of Theon,” with no ethnic designation, no neighborhood, no trade. We know she had parents with a house. We know she had a dowry. We know, from the precision of her legal language, that she or her scribe understood exactly how the courts worked. Beyond that, we are guessing.

But one detail in the petition is extraordinary, and scholars have noted it: Syra appears to act without a male guardian.13

In Graeco-Egyptian law, women typically required a kyrios — a male guardian — to conduct legal business. Marriage contracts, property sales, petitions: almost all of them name the woman’s kyrios, whether a father, husband, brother, or son.14 Thaësis, who amicably divorced her husband in 96 CE, acted “with her guardian her step-father Onnophris.”15 Sieapis, who registered property around 76 CE, used “her son Ariston son of Diogenes as guardian.”16 Even Julia Heracla, a Roman citizen woman of substantial means, needed “the guardian assigned to her by the instructions issued by Gaius Septimius Vegetus, the late praefect.”17

Syra names no guardian. She petitions alone. Perhaps the guardian’s name appeared in a lost portion of the papyrus. Perhaps the requirement was not strictly enforced for petitions, as opposed to contracts. Perhaps she had obtained the ius liberorum, the “right of three children,” which freed women from the guardianship requirement.18 We cannot know. But the absence is striking, and it adds to the impression of a woman acting on her own authority in a system that usually insisted she could not.

III. A Destitute Man Enters the House

The marriage, as Syra describes it, inverted the expected order.

In a conventional Oxyrhynchite marriage, the wife moved to the husband’s household. The contracts are explicit: the couple would “live together,” the husband would “supply the bride with necessaries in proportion to his means,” and if they separated, the husband would return the dowry within a stipulated number of days.19 The assumption was that the husband had means. He had a house, or access to one. He had a trade, or a farm, or a business. The wife contributed her dowry — cash, jewelry, clothing, sometimes land — and the husband contributed the household.

Sarapion had nothing.

“As he was destitute of means,” Syra writes, “I received him into my parents’ house.”20 The Greek is pointed: liton pantelos onta — “being utterly bare,” “completely without resources.” The word litos can mean simple, plain, unadorned, but here, paired with pantelos (completely, utterly), it means stripped down to nothing.21 Sarapion came to the marriage empty-handed.

Why would Syra’s family agree to this? We can only speculate. Perhaps Sarapion came from a respectable family that had fallen on hard times. Perhaps the match was arranged for social reasons that had nothing to do with money — a connection between families, a favor owed, an expectation that the young man would find his feet. Perhaps there were fewer eligible men than eligible women in the weaving quarters of Oxyrhynchus, where military levies and labor corvées drained the male population.22 Tryphon son of Dionysius, the best-documented weaver in the city, was himself released from military service around 52 CE due to cataracts and poor eyesight — his body requisitioned by the state and returned only when it was damaged.23

Or perhaps Syra’s parents simply misjudged. They took in a man who seemed capable of improvement and watched him consume their daughter’s future instead.

The phrase “my parents’ house” — ta ton goneon mou oiketeria — suggests that Syra’s parents were still alive when the marriage began, or at least that the family home was still identified with them.24 This house was perhaps the family’s most significant asset after the dowry itself. In the cramped quarters of Oxyrhynchus, where a woman could report a burglary and name her weaver neighbor as the prime suspect because “my house is easy of access from the house of Heras,” domestic space was both intimate and porous.25 Sarapion would have lived among Syra’s relatives, eating their food, sleeping under their roof, while contributing nothing.

IV. The Price of 200 Drachmae

The dowry was 200 silver drachmae, given “by cession” — kata synchoresis — meaning it was registered through the official notarial process, probably at the agoranomos’s office or through the public bank at the Serapeum.26 This was not an informal arrangement. It was documented, witnessed, and legally binding.

What did 200 drachmae mean?

A weaver’s loom — the essential tool of the artisan trade in Oxyrhynchus — cost 20 drachmae.27 Syra’s dowry could have bought ten looms. A linen tunic, the kind of garment a weaver might produce and wear, cost 8 drachmae.28 Her dowry equaled twenty-five tunics. A corrupt tax collector named Apollophanes extorted 24 drachmae from a weaver over six months, seizing clothing off his back and demanding monthly payments — and the weaver thought this worth petitioning the strategus about.29 Syra’s dowry was more than eight times that extortion.

These comparisons place her family squarely in the artisan class: not wealthy, but not poor. Not the class that brought dowries of 4,100 drachmae, as the family of Thais daughter of Sarapion did when she married in 127 CE.30 Not the class that could absorb the loss of 800 drachmae, as Ammonarion’s family did when her husband died and his heirs negotiated a settlement.31 But not destitute, either. Not the class that had nothing to bring.

For comparison, consider Saraeus, daughter of Apion, who married the weaver Tryphon in 37 CE — almost exactly Syra’s period. Saraeus brought a dowry of 72 drachmae: 40 in cash, 20 in the value of a pair of gold earrings, and 12 for a milk-white robe.32 Syra’s dowry was nearly three times Saraeus’s. The difference suggests that Syra’s family, whatever their trade, had somewhat more capital than a typical weaver’s household, or that they invested more heavily in their daughter’s marriage — perhaps precisely because the groom brought so little.

In a household where the weaver’s daily breakfast cost an obol, where beer cost another obol, where leeks and bread for children were counted in half-obols, 200 drachmae represented years of careful accumulation.33 The household accounts from a roughly contemporary Oxyrhynchite family — P.Oxy. 4.736, dated to around the turn of the era — record expenses that rarely exceeded a few drachmae per week.34 Pure bread for the children: half an obol. Milk for the children: half an obol. Garlands for a birthday celebration: two obols. At that rate of expenditure, 200 drachmae was a fortune.

And Sarapion squandered it.

V. The Squandering

“Having squandered my dowry as he pleased” — katachresamenos tei phernei eis hon ebouleto logon — is a phrase that carries both legal weight and personal bitterness.35 The verb katachraomai means to use up, to consume, to waste. The qualifying phrase eis hon ebouleto logon — “to whatever purpose he wished” — makes it worse. He did not lose the money to misfortune. He spent it as he saw fit, without regard for her or for the legal obligations that bound him.

Those obligations were real. Marriage contracts from Oxyrhynchus routinely included clauses restricting the husband’s use of the dowry and his own property. The elaborate contract of 127 CE stipulates that “it shall not be lawful for the husband to sell or mortgage or otherwise dispose of his property without the consent of the bride.”36 A contract from the reign of Domitian (81–96 CE) explicitly prohibits the husband from ill-treating his wife — mede kakouchein auten — using the same root word that Syra uses in her petition.37 Even when a marriage was contracted “without a written agreement” — agraphos, as Tryphon’s second marriage apparently was — the dowry and its return obligations remained legally enforceable.38

What did Sarapion spend the money on? The papyrus does not say. Perhaps drink. Perhaps gambling. Perhaps bad investments, or debts he had concealed before the marriage, or the kind of slow hemorrhage of cash that happens when a man without discipline has access to capital for the first time. We may imagine — though we cannot know — a progression familiar from other eras: the early extravagances justified as necessities, the tightening of household resources as the capital dwindled, the growing tension between a wife who watched her inheritance disappear and a husband who resented her watching.

The legal consequence was clear. If the dowry was gone and the marriage ended, Sarapion owed Syra not merely the 200 drachmae he had consumed, but that amount “increased by half” — syn hemioliai — for a total of 300 drachmae.39 The hemiolion, the fifty-percent penalty, was standard in marriage documents and loan agreements alike. It appears in Tryphon’s marriage settlement with Saraeus: “If I do not repay in accordance with the above terms I will forfeit to you the said sum with the addition of half its amount.”40 It appears in the 127 CE contract of Thais: the husband will return the dowry “or forfeit this amount increased by one half.”41 It appears in loan documents filed through the archidikastes’s office.42 The hemiolion was not a punishment imposed by the court; it was a contractual penalty triggered by the husband’s failure to return the dowry when required. Syra was not asking for extraordinary relief. She was asking for what the law already promised her.

VI. The Violence

The petition’s central accusation comes in a cascade of Greek participles, each one escalating:

ou dieleipen kakouchon me kai hybrizon kai tas cheiras epipheron kai ton anankaion endee kathistasv43

“He did not cease ill-treating me and insulting me and laying hands on me and making me destitute of the necessaries of life.”

Four charges. Four distinct legal wrongs. It is worth pausing on each.

Kakouchon — “ill-treating.” The verb kakoucheo appears in marriage contracts as explicitly prohibited conduct. When a contract says mede kakouchein auten — “nor to ill-treat her” — it establishes a standard that, if breached, gives the wife grounds for separation and dowry recovery.44 Syra’s use of the word is not incidental. She is invoking a contractual norm.

Hybrizon — “insulting.” But hybris in Greek carries far more weight than the English word “insult.” It means an outrage against a person’s honor and dignity, a deliberate humiliation, an act that treats another human being as less than they are.45 In Athenian law, hybris was a prosecutable offense. In the Graeco-Egyptian context, it signaled a violation of the social compact between husband and wife — the understanding that marriage, whatever its economic dimensions, was also a relationship of mutual respect. The marriage contracts spell this out: “let both live blamelessly together.”46

Tas cheiras epipheron — literally, “laying on hands,” “bringing his hands against me.” This is unambiguous. Physical violence.47 The phrase is a standard legal formula for assault, but its physicality cuts through the legalism. Hands on her body. Not once — the imperfect participle and the phrase ou dieleipen (“he did not cease”) indicate repeated, ongoing violence.48

Ton anankaion endee kathistasv — “making me destitute of the necessaries of life.” The anankaia — necessaries — were what marriage contracts obligated the husband to provide: food, clothing, oil, the basic requirements of existence.49 Contract after contract uses the formula: “the husband shall supply the bride with necessaries in proportion to his means.”50 By depriving Syra of these, Sarapion violated one of the most fundamental terms of the marital relationship. That he did so after squandering her dowry — after consuming the very capital she had brought to sustain the household — adds a particular cruelty. He ate her future and then starved her present.

The accumulation is deliberate. Syra’s scribe — or Syra herself, if she composed the petition — understood that each term carried specific legal resonance. Together, they constitute an overwhelming case: not a single incident, but a pattern of sustained abuse and neglect. The Greek syntax reinforces this: the four participles are strung together without pause, each one adding to the last, building toward the final blow.

Hysteron de enkateleipe me lithen kathestosan.

“Finally he deserted me, leaving me in a state of destitution.”51

The word litos appears again. It was used earlier to describe Sarapion when he entered the marriage: liton pantelos onta, “utterly destitute.” Now it describes Syra: lithen kathestosan, “in a state of destitution.” The symmetry is devastating, and it is surely intentional. He came with nothing. He left her with nothing. The poverty that was his when he arrived had become hers when he departed.

VII. The Weavers of Gymnasium Square

Around the same time Syra was composing her petition — or within a decade of it — another resident of Oxyrhynchus named Sarapion, son of Theon, was filing his own complaint. He was a weaver, gerdios, living in the quarter of Gymnasium Square.52

The coincidence of names is arresting. Syra is “daughter of Theon.” This weaver is “son of Theon.” Both documents date to the mid-first century CE. Both emerge from the same city. The possibility that this Sarapion is Syra’s brother — that we have, by the accident of preservation, documents from two members of the same family — is tantalizing.53

The weaver Sarapion’s complaint is addressed to Tiberius Claudius Pasion, the strategus of the Oxyrhynchite nome, and it concerns a man named Apollophanes, a former collector of the trade tax on weavers. Apollophanes, Sarapion says, “using great violence seized from me a linen tunic which I was wearing, worth 8 drachmae.”54 He also extorted additional payments: four drachmae at once, and then two drachmae each month for six months, totaling 24 drachmae.55 The weaver wants the strategus to proceed against the tax collector.

Sarapion son of Theon was not the only weaver victimized by Apollophanes. Another petition from the same period, filed by Alexandrus son of Apollonius, a weaver living in the quarter of Thoëris Square, accuses the same former tax collector of extorting 16 drachmae.56 The two petitions were published consecutively — P.Oxy. 2.284 and 2.285 — and they paint a picture of systematic exploitation: a tax collector who had left office but continued to shake down the artisan community, seizing garments, demanding cash, confident that weavers lacked the social standing to resist.

If Sarapion son of Theon was Syra’s brother, then the family portrait sharpens considerably. A household of modest artisans. A sister married off with a dowry that represented a serious investment — 200 drachmae, nearly ten times what the tax collector extorted from the brother over six months. A brother harassed by corrupt officials, stripped of the tunic off his back. A brother-in-law who consumed the family’s capital and then vanished. These are people living at the edge, not of starvation, but of respectability — the narrow margin where a loom purchase, a bad marriage, or a predatory tax collector could tip the balance.

We cannot confirm the connection. Theon was a common name. Sarapion was a common name. The weaver Sarapion son of Theon may have been no relation to Syra daughter of Theon at all. But the documents sit side by side in the published volumes, and the temptation to read them together is strong.57

VIII. The Mirror Image

The most illuminating parallel to Syra’s petition comes from a man whose archive is one of the treasures of Oxyrhynchite papyrology: Tryphon son of Dionysius, weaver.58

Tryphon’s life is documented across more than a dozen papyri spanning from the 20s to the 70s CE — precisely Syra’s period. He was, like the hypothetical brother Sarapion, a weaver. He lived in Oxyrhynchus. He married twice. His first marriage ended badly. His second marriage left a paper trail of contracts, purchases, and family arrangements that lets us reconstruct, in unusual detail, the life of an artisan household.

It is his first marriage that mirrors Syra’s story — but from the other side.

Sometime between 29 and 37 CE, Tryphon petitioned the strategus Alexandrus about his first wife, Demetrous:

I married Demetrous, daughter of Heraclides, and I for my part provided for my wife in a manner that exceeded my resources. But she became dissatisfied with our union, and finally left the house carrying off property belonging to me… I beg, therefore, that she be brought before you in order that she may receive her deserts, and return to me my property.59

The structural parallels with Syra’s petition are remarkable. Both open with the same formula: “I married [name].” Both claim blameless conduct on the petitioner’s part. Both accuse the spouse of wrongdoing. Both ask that the spouse be summoned before the court. Both close with the identical reservation: “This petition is without prejudice to the other claims which I have or may have against…”60

But the substance is reversed. Tryphon is the abandoned husband; Syra is the abandoned wife. Tryphon claims his wife left voluntarily, taking his property; Syra claims her husband deserted her after taking hers. Tryphon says he provided “in a manner that exceeded my resources”; Syra says Sarapion squandered her dowry and deprived her of necessities. The same legal template serves opposite narratives.

What happened between Tryphon and Demetrous? The petition gives few details beyond the property theft. But we know that Tryphon remarried — in 37 CE, he married Saraeus, daughter of Apion, who brought a dowry of 72 drachmae.61 This second marriage appears to have been more stable: Tryphon and Saraeus had a son, Thoënis, whom Tryphon later apprenticed to another weaver.62 But the dowry was modest — less than half of Syra’s 200 drachmae — suggesting that Tryphon’s resources had not recovered from whatever Demetrous had taken.

The Tryphon archive opens other windows. In 44 CE, Tryphon’s mother Thamounion reported to the local authorities that Tryphon’s older son — also named Thoönis, from the first marriage — had disappeared.63 The son “has no trade,” Thamounion said, and “possesses no means.”64 The language is strikingly similar to Syra’s description of Sarapion: liton pantelos onta, utterly destitute. The registration was necessary because the family would otherwise be liable for the missing son’s taxes. A young man with no trade and no resources had simply walked away from Oxyrhynchus, leaving his grandmother to manage the paperwork of his absence.

In 52 CE, Tryphon himself was released from compulsory military or labor service on account of “cataract and shortness of sight.”65 The examination took place in Alexandria — a journey of some two hundred kilometers. A weaver whose livelihood depended on his eyesight, declared unfit by the Roman authorities, returned to Oxyrhynchus diminished. Two years later, in 54 CE, he bought a new loom for 20 drachmae.66 Life continued.

These are the textures of ordinary existence in first-century Oxyrhynchus. Marriages that failed. Sons who vanished. Eyes that clouded. Tax collectors who stole the shirt off a man’s back. Women who petitioned the highest courts for the return of what was theirs. The lives are small in the way that all lives are small when measured against the scale of empire. But the documents make them vivid, and Syra’s petition sits among them like a voice in a crowd — distinct, but part of the same conversation.

IX. Before Heraclides, Priest and Chief Justice

Syra did not petition the local strategus, as Tryphon did, or the village officials, as many Oxyrhynchites did. She addressed her complaint to the archidikastes himself — the chief justice of all Egypt, based in Alexandria, charged with supervising every court in the province.67

The title is formulaic but revealing: “Heraclides, priest, chief justice, superintendent of the chrematistae and the other courts.”68 Every archidikastes bore this compound title. We find it attached to different names across the centuries: Theon, who handled a dowry settlement after a husband’s death;69 Pallas, who oversaw a loan agreement in 81 CE;70 Aurelius Gaius, also called Harpocration, who registered a property sale in 267 CE.71 The office endured. The formula never changed. It was, in its way, as stable as anything in Roman Egypt.

The chrematistae, whom the archidikastes supervised, were originally one of three court systems established by the Ptolemies.72 Ptolemy II Philadelphus had created a tripartite judicial structure: the dikasteria for Greek disputes under Greek law, the laokritai for Egyptian disputes under Egyptian law, and the chrematistai — literally, “those who handle business” — for royal and administrative matters.73 Over time, the chrematistae absorbed the other courts. They were originally itinerant, traveling on circuit like English assize judges, but the volume of cases made them stationary.74 By the Roman period, the archidikastes in Alexandria coordinated a judicial system that reached into every nome of the province, handling everything from marriage disputes to property registrations to debt collections.

For Syra to petition the archidikastes rather than the strategus suggests either that her case had particular complexity, that local remedies had already failed, or that the archidikastes was the appropriate jurisdiction for disputes arising from registered marriage agreements.75 The dowry had been conveyed kata synchoresis — through the official notarial process — and the archidikastes oversaw the registration system that gave such documents their force.

The petition asks Heraclides to “order him to be brought before you” — syntaxai katastesai auton epi se.76 This is not a metaphor. The coercive power of the archidikastes’s office included the authority to compel appearance, and failure to appear could result in arrest and detention.77 A loan document from 81 CE, filed through the archidikastes Pallas, specifies that the debtor is “liable to arrest and detention until she pays in full.”78 Syra was invoking the full apparatus of state coercion against a man who had taken her money, beaten her, and disappeared.

Whether that apparatus responded, we do not know.

X. What the Women of Oxyrhynchus Could Do

Syra was not the only woman of Roman Egypt to take her case to court. The papyri record a long tradition of female petitioners, from the Ptolemaic period through the Roman, exercising legal rights that were, by the standards of the ancient Mediterranean, remarkably extensive.

The most famous case is that of Dionysia, daughter of Chaeremon, whose lengthy legal battle against her own father is preserved in P.Oxy. 2.237.79 Chaeremon attempted to dissolve Dionysia’s marriage and reclaim her dowry. Dionysia fought back through multiple courts and across years, citing legal precedents and the opinions of jurists. The record preserves an extraordinary moment: a prefect’s ruling that “the decision depends upon the question, with whom the wife wishes to live.”80 A lawyer’s opinion is also cited: “Since Dionysia has been given away by her father in marriage, she is no longer in his power.”81

These are not abstract principles. They are decisions applied to specific women in specific disputes, and they establish that married women in Roman Egypt had real legal autonomy — the right to remain in a marriage their fathers wanted to dissolve, the right to petition for the return of property, the right to appear (usually with a guardian, but sometimes without) before the highest courts in the province.

The legal framework that protected these rights was rooted in older traditions. Under Ptolemaic law, Egyptian women could inherit, purchase and sell property, make contracts, borrow and lend goods, and initiate court cases.82 Greek women in Egypt operated under somewhat different norms, but the practical effect was similar: women were economic actors with enforceable rights. The Roman period complicated this picture by more strictly enforcing the requirement of a male guardian, but even this restriction had significant exceptions. Women who obtained the ius liberorum — the “right of three children,” a privilege granted under Augustan marriage legislation — could act without a guardian entirely.83 P.Oxy. 12.1475 records a woman “acting without a guardian by the ius liberorum.”84

Syra’s petition, then, was not anomalous. It was part of a documented tradition. Women in Oxyrhynchus registered property, concluded marriage contracts, dissolved marriages, filed complaints about theft and violence, and demanded the enforcement of their financial rights.85 What distinguishes Syra’s petition is its combination of claims — abuse, squandering, and desertion all in one document — and the apparent absence of a guardian. She was, as far as the surviving text shows, acting alone.

XI. What the Record Does Not Say

The papyrus ends. There is no verdict, no subsequent filing, no record of Sarapion’s appearance before Heraclides or anyone else. We do not know whether the archidikastes issued a summons. We do not know whether Sarapion was found, or whether he had left Oxyrhynchus entirely — as Tryphon’s son Thoönis left, as so many men of the artisan class seem to have left, absorbed into the migrant labor of the Nile valley or the anonymity of Alexandria.

The papyrus itself was found in a rubbish heap. This is where virtually all the Oxyrhynchus papyri were found — in the municipal garbage dumps that accumulated over centuries on the desert edge of the city.86 The dry climate preserved what moisture would have destroyed. But the fact that Syra’s petition ended up in the trash raises its own questions. Was this a draft that was superseded by a fair copy? A duplicate retained for personal records and then discarded? A petition that was filed, processed, and disposed of by the court system? Or a complaint that was never sent at all — written in anger and then abandoned, the way so many letters to the powerful are composed and never mailed?

There is no way to know. The silence is total.

What we can say is this: sometime in the first half of the first century CE, a woman in a provincial Egyptian city marshaled the resources — intellectual, linguistic, financial — to compose a legal petition of considerable sophistication. She addressed it to the highest judicial authority available to her. She stated her case in terms that precisely tracked the language of enforceable marriage contracts. She asked for a specific remedy — the return of her dowry with the standard penalty — and reserved her right to pursue additional claims. She did all this in a society that generally required women to act through male intermediaries, and she appears to have done it alone.

The system she appealed to was real. The courts existed. The law she invoked was enforceable. Other women, in other cases, obtained the relief they sought. Amicable divorces returned dowries to wives who had brought them.87 Settlements were negotiated through the archidikastes’s office.88 Fathers who tried to reclaim their married daughters were told by prefects that the decision belonged to the wife.89

But we do not know whether the system worked for Syra. We do not know whether she got her 300 drachmae. We do not know whether Sarapion was dragged before Heraclides, priest and chief justice and superintendent of the chrematistae and the other courts, and compelled to answer for what he had done. We do not know whether Syra returned to her parents’ house, poorer than before, and resumed the life that the marriage had interrupted. We do not know whether she married again. We do not know when she died, or where, or how.

We know only that she spoke. That she said: I conducted myself blamelessly. He did not. He squandered what was mine. He beat me. He left me with nothing. I want it back.

And that someone, at some point, threw her words away.
