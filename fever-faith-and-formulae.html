The Doctor's Complaint

The Hippocratic treatise On the Sacred Disease is one of the most celebrated texts in the history of medicine.6 Written in the late fifth century BCE, probably by a physician associated with the medical school on Cos, it takes aim at a specific target: the healers who treat epilepsy – the “sacred disease” – with religious rituals rather than physical remedies.

The author is furious. These practitioners, he writes, are “magicians, purifiers, charlatans, and quacks” who “conceal themselves behind superstition” and call the disease sacred “in order that their utter ignorance might not be manifest.”7 Their methods are elaborate: they prohibit patients from wearing black clothing, from lying on goatskins, from putting one foot on top of the other. They forbid certain foods. They attribute nighttime terrors to the attacks of Hekate and the assaults of heroes.8 They purify the sick with blood and other substances, then hide the materials of purification – some buried in the earth, some cast into the sea, some carried to the mountains “where no one shall touch them or tread upon them.”9

The Hippocratic author insists that epilepsy has natural causes. It originates in the brain. It involves phlegm blocking the passages of air. It can be observed in goats, which “are most frequently seized” by the condition; if you cut open the head of an afflicted goat, “you will find the brain moist and full of dropsy and foul-smelling, and in this way you will know that it is not a god who damages the body, but disease.”10

And then he delivers his manifesto: “Whoever is acquainted with such a change in men, and can render a man humid and dry, hot and cold by regimen, could also cure this disease, if he recognizes the proper season for administering his remedies, without minding purifications, spells, and all other illiberal practices of a like kind.”11

This passage has been read for twenty-five centuries as the founding declaration of rational medicine – the moment when science parted from superstition, when the physician replaced the sorcerer, when observation triumphed over incantation. It is a magnificent polemic. It is also a spectacular failure.

The purifiers and spell-casters did not go away. They were still operating when the Hippocratic author was writing. They were still operating five hundred years later. They were still operating a thousand years later. They are, in various forms, still operating now. The Sacred Disease is not the record of a victory. It is the record of a protest against a practice that was winning.

What the Fever Knew

To understand why magical healing persisted, you must first understand what it was fighting. The ancient Mediterranean was a world of fevers.

Malaria, in its various forms, was endemic across the lowland plains, river deltas, and coastal marshes of Greece, Italy, North Africa, and Egypt.12 The disease, caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted through mosquito bites, produces fever patterns so distinctive that ancient physicians built an entire nosological system around them. The quotidian fever (amphimerinos) strikes every day. The tertian fever (tritaios) strikes every other day – a forty-eight-hour cycle. The quartan fever (tetartaios) strikes every third day – a seventy-two-hour cycle.13 These patterns correspond, in modern understanding, to the reproductive cycles of different Plasmodium species: P. falciparum for the dangerous daily fevers, P. vivax for the tertian pattern, P. malariae for the quartan.14

Hippocrates knew these patterns intimately. In the Epidemics, he tracks fevers through individual patients with clinical precision: “In this constitution during winter began paralyses which attacked many, a few of whom quickly died. In fact, the disease was generally epidemic.”15 His Aphorisms correlate fever types with seasons: “Of summer, certain of these, and continued, ardent, and tertian fevers”; “Of autumn, most of the summer, quartan, and irregular fevers, enlarged spleen, dropsy.”16 The enlarged spleen is a classic sign of chronic malaria – the organ swelling as it filters infected red blood cells.17

Galen, writing in the second century CE, systematised the classification further. Each fever type was caused by a specific humor: quotidian fever by phlegm, tertian fever by yellow bile, quartan fever by black bile.18 His framework would dominate medical thinking for fifteen centuries. More importantly for our story, his fever categories passed directly from the physician’s consulting room into the amulet-maker’s workshop. The same technical vocabulary appears in both Hippocratic case notes and magical amulets – the same words, amphimerinos, tritaios, tetartaios, doing double duty in two supposedly incompatible systems of thought.

This is not a coincidence. It is the central fact of ancient medical magic.

The Disease List as Weapon and Shield

Before we reach the healing amulets, we should pause at a tomb in Attica.

Sometime in the Roman imperial period – the inscription is undated, but its language and formulae suggest the second or third century CE – someone commissioned a curse to protect a burial monument, a heroon, from desecration.19 The text invokes the chthonic gods by name: Plouton, Demeter, Persephone, the Erinyes. It warns that anyone who strips the ornaments or removes the marble revetment will suffer a comprehensive catalogue of disasters: “May the earth not be walkable for him nor the sea navigable, but may he be uprooted, root and branch.” And then it gets specific: “He shall experience all evils – shivering, fever, quartan ague, elephantiasis, and whatever evils and sufferings befall humans.”20

A companion inscription, made by a woman named Antonia Sokratike for her “sweetest husband” Antiochos Synesios, uses an almost identical formula.21 The same diseases, in the same order: phrike, shivering; pyretos, fever; tetartaios, quartan ague; elephas, elephantiasis. The two inscriptions are so similar that they must derive from a common template – a professional curse-writer’s handbook of standard formulae.22

What is striking about these disease lists is their precision. They are not vague imprecations (“may evil befall you”). They name specific conditions that the ancient Mediterranean world recognised and feared. Shivering and fever are the hallmarks of malaria. Quartan ague, with its relentless seventy-two-hour cycle, was particularly dreaded because it could persist for years, debilitating without killing, reducing its victims to chronic invalids.23 Elephantiasis – the grotesque swelling of the limbs caused by lymphatic filariasis, another mosquito-borne disease – was the most visible and terrifying of all, transforming human skin into something resembling an elephant’s hide.24

The curse-writers knew what people feared most. And the amulet-makers, working from the same vocabulary, the same tradition, sometimes perhaps the same workshops, knew what they needed to promise protection against.

The Technology of the Amulet

Strip away the theological content of any ancient healing amulet and what remains is a technology – a standardised procedure with identifiable components, as consistent in its structure as a legal contract or a medical prescription. Roy Kotansky, who spent decades cataloguing inscribed Greek magical amulets on gold, silver, copper, and bronze lamellae, demonstrated that these objects followed patterns as rigorous as any professional tradition.25

The components are:

First, the invocation. The practitioner calls upon a supernatural power. In pagan amulets, this might be Asklepios, Apollo, Isis, or Serapis. In Gnostic amulets, it is IAO (a Greek rendering of the Hebrew divine name YHWH), Abrasax, or Sabaoth. In Christian amulets, it is Christ, the Holy Spirit, the Virgin Mary, or the saints and archangels. In the post-conquest Ahmed amulet, it is Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, and other angelic beings.26 The power invoked changes with the religion of the client. The structural position of the invocation does not.

Second, the identification of the patient. The sick person is named, usually with a matronymic – identification through the mother rather than the father. Arian. Joannia whom Anastasia also called Euphemia bore. Vibius whom Gennaia bore. Ahmed son of Mariam.27 The matronymic is standard in magical texts because, as the practitioners understood, maternity is certain in a way that paternity is not. The mother’s name anchors the spell to the correct individual with maximum precision. This is the same logic that governed ancient legal identification, where slaves were identified by their mothers.28

Third, the disease specification. The amulet names the condition to be cured. Not “illness” in general, but specific diseases: daily fever, nightly fever, slight fever; quotidian, tertian, quartan; fever and burning and sufferings and mania and weeping. The specificity matters. The amulet must know what it is fighting. A generic healing request would be like a legal contract without terms – unenforceable.29

Fourth, the command. The supernatural power is ordered to act: guard and preserve, chase from her and put to flight, bring out the fever, deliver from every evil. The language is imperative. The practitioner does not beg. The practitioner commands, or at minimum petitions with the authority of divine names that compel obedience.30

Fifth, the urgency formula. Many amulets end with a demand for immediate action: “now now, quickly quickly” – ede ede, tachy tachy. The Greek phrase tachy tachy appears across centuries of magical texts, from pagan love spells to Christian healing amulets to post-Islamic Coptic charms.31 Its persistence is remarkable. It is the one element that never changes, regardless of the religion, language, or century of the document.

Taken together, those five elements – invocation, identification, specification, command, urgency – constitute the technology of the healing amulet. It is as stable as a sonnet form or a legal pleading. Gods rise and fall. Empires crumble. The formula endures.

The Gnostic Amulet for Arian

Return now to Oxyrhynchus in the fourth century. The amulet for Arian survives as P.Oxy. inv. 2065, a papyrus fragment catalogued in the Digital Corpus of Literary Papyri as DCLP 65.64394.32

The text that survives is the second half of the amulet – the beginning is lost. What remains is the heart of the charm:

…that you may guard and preserve Arian from the daily fever-chill and from the nightly fever-chill and from the slight fever, and do not allow these to approach Arian at all, according to your will first and according to her faith, for she is a servant of the living God, and that your name may be glorified forever…33

Below this petition, the amulet displays a cruciform arrangement of sacred names: “Jesus, Father, Son, Mother, Christ” – and, at the sides, “Abrasax” and “Arian.”34

Several features demand attention.

The fever classification is medically specific. The text distinguishes between epimerinos phrikos (the daily fever-chill), kathimerinos phrikos (a variant term for the daily pattern), nykterinos phrikos (the nightly fever-chill), and leptoriguon or leptopyretion (the slight or mild fever).35 These are not random words. They reflect a systematic attempt to categorise the different ways malaria and other febrile illnesses present – the sweating that comes in the day, the shivering that comes at night, the low-grade persistent fever that never quite breaks. The amulet-maker knew his diseases the way a doctor knew his diseases. He may, in fact, have been a doctor – or at least someone trained alongside doctors.

The phrase “according to your will first and according to her faith” places the amulet squarely in a Christian theological framework. Healing depends on God’s will and the patient’s faith – a formulation that would satisfy any bishop.36 Yet the presence of Abrasax in the cruciform arrangement complicates the picture. Abrasax (or Abraxas) was a figure from Gnostic cosmology, particularly associated with the teachings of Basilides in second-century Alexandria. The name’s numerical value in Greek (A=1, B=2, R=100, A=1, X=60, A=1, S=200 = 365) made it a symbol of totality, encompassing the full year and all its powers.37 By the fourth century, Abrasax had migrated from Gnostic theology into the general vocabulary of magical power – a vox magica whose original meaning was less important than its perceived efficacy.

The woman Arian is called doule, “handmaid” or “servant” of the living God – a standard Christian self-designation.38 She was probably an ordinary Egyptian Christian, not a Gnostic initiate. The amulet-maker drew from whatever traditions promised power. Theological consistency was not the point. Results were the point.

The Christian Amulet for Joannia

A century later, still at Oxyrhynchus, another woman fell ill with fever. Her name was Joannia, and her mother was a woman known by two names: Anastasia, also called Euphemia.39

The amulet made for Joannia is one of the most complete surviving examples of Christian magical healing. Published as P.Oxy. 8.1151, it has been studied since Arthur Hunt and Bernard Grenfell first edited it in 1911.40 It is a masterpiece of the genre.

The text opens with an exorcism:

Fly, hateful spirit! Christ pursues thee; the Son of God and the Holy Spirit have outstripped thee.41

The verb is imperative – pheuge, flee. The disease is conceived as a spirit, a demonic presence that must be driven out. This demonological understanding of illness was common in late antiquity, shared by Christians and pagans alike.42 The innovation of the Joannia amulet is the identification of the pursuing power: not Asklepios or Isis, but Christ, pursued and flanked by the Son of God and the Holy Spirit. The Trinity hunts the fever-demon.

Then the amulet invokes a specific healing precedent:

O God of the sheep-pool, deliver from every evil thy handmaid Joannia whom Anastasia also called Euphemia bare.43

The “sheep-pool” is the Pool of Bethesda from the Gospel of John (5:2-9), where Jesus healed a man who had been infirm for thirty-eight years.44 The amulet-maker is grounding his authority in a specific biblical miracle – not a general claim about Christ’s power, but a precise textual reference that his client could verify. This is citation as magical technology.

Then comes the Gospel of John itself, quoted verbatim:

In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. All things were made by him and without him was not anything made that hath been made.45

The opening of John’s Gospel was the single most common biblical text used in ancient amulets, appearing on papyrus, parchment, gems, and metal lamellae across the Mediterranean and beyond.46 Its use was not primarily devotional. The words themselves were understood to carry power – a creative, cosmogonic power that spoke the universe into existence and could therefore speak disease out of a body. The Logos that made all things could unmake a fever.

Then the formal medical petition:

O Lord Christ, Son and Word of the living God, who healedst every sickness and every infirmity, heal and regard thy handmaid Joannia whom Anastasia also called Euphemia bare, chase from her and put to flight all fevers and every kind of chill, quotidian, tertian, and quartan, and every evil.47

Here is the disease specification, precise and exhaustive: pyretos, fever; rhigos, chill; amphimerinos, quotidian (daily); tritaios, tertian (every other day); tetartaios, quartan (every third day). The Greek terminology is identical to what appears in Hippocratic and Galenic medical texts.48 The amulet-maker who wrote this was literate in medicine as well as in theology.

The phrase “the living God” – tou theou tou zontos – appears in both the Arian amulet and the Joannia amulet, separated by about a century.49 It is a formula with deep Jewish and Christian roots, but in the context of healing amulets it carries a specific connotation: the God who is alive has power over life and death, over sickness and health. A dead god cannot heal. The living God can.

Finally, the intercessions:

Pray through the intercession of our lady the mother of God and the glorious archangels and Saint John, the glorious apostle and evangelist and divine, and Saint Serenus and Saint Philoxenus and Saint Victor and Saint Justus and all the Saints.50

The presence of local Egyptian saints alongside the universal figures of Mary and the archangels suggests that the amulet-maker drew from both the universal church and the local cult.51 Saints Serenus, Philoxenus, Victor, and Justus were venerated in Egypt; their names lent the amulet the authority of local heavenly patrons who knew the landscape, the diseases, the needs of their community.52 Scholars have noted that the amulet’s use of nomina sacra (abbreviated sacred names), its liturgical cadences, and its structural resemblance to Christian prayer formulae indicate that it was likely produced by a member of the clergy – a priest or monk who doubled as a healer.53

This is not a marginal practice. This is a church-produced document. The official religion had not replaced magical healing. It had absorbed it.

Christ Saves Vibius

Between the Arian amulet and the Joannia amulet in time, and belonging to the same tradition in form, stands a third document that deserves attention: the amulet for Vibius, preserved as P.Batav. 20.54

This sixth-century papyrus from Egypt opens with a creedal formula rendered as a series of staccato declarations, each marked with a cross:

Christ was proclaimed in advance; Christ appeared; Christ suffered; Christ died; Christ was raised; Christ was taken up; Christ reigns.55

Seven statements, moving from prophecy through incarnation, passion, death, resurrection, and ascension to the present tense of cosmic sovereignty. This is a miniature Christological creed, compressed to its barest elements. And then, without transition, the creed becomes a healing command:

Christ saves Vibius, whom Gennaia bore, from all fever and from all shivering, daily, quotidian, now now, quickly, quickly.56

The name Vibius is Latin – unusual in a Greek papyrus from Egypt, suggesting a client of Italian or Roman provincial origin.57 The matronymic Gennaia is Greek. The fever terminology is again precise: pyretos (fever), phrike (shivering), kathimerinos (daily), amphimerinos (quotidian). And the urgency formula closes the text: “now now, quickly, quickly” – the same words that appear on the Ahmed amulet three centuries later, the same words that had been closing magical texts for half a millennium before Vibius was born.58

The Vibius amulet is structurally brilliant. By embedding the healing command within a creedal declaration, it transforms the entire history of salvation into a therapeutic event. Christ did not merely die and rise for the redemption of the world in general. Christ died and rose so that Vibius, specifically Vibius, son of Gennaia, could be freed from his fever, right now, today.

The Hippocratic Paradox

Let us return to the fifth century BCE, to the physician who wanted to abolish all this.

The paradox of the Sacred Disease is that its author, while attacking magical healers, shares more with them than he admits. His argument that epilepsy originates in the brain – correct, as it happens, though for wrong reasons – does not lead to any effective treatment. “Whoever is acquainted with such a change in men, and can render a man humid and dry, hot and cold by regimen,” he writes, “could also cure this disease.”59 The remedy is dietary regimen: adjustments in food, drink, and lifestyle designed to rebalance the bodily humors.

Did this work? For epilepsy, no. The Hippocratic dietary approach to epilepsy was no more effective than the purifications it replaced.60 The purifiers offered dramatic ritual, personal attention, dietary restrictions, and the comfort of a supernatural explanation. The Hippocratic physician offered less dramatic but equally ineffective dietary adjustments, less personal attention, and the cold comfort of a natural explanation. The patient, facing seizures that neither could cure, had no rational basis for preferring one over the other.

The Aphorisms are candid about the limits of treatment: “Those cases of epilepsy which come on before puberty may undergo a change; but those which come on after twenty-five years of age, for the most part terminate in death.”61 Against malaria, the Hippocratic physicians were somewhat more effective – they could observe and predict the fever patterns, and quinine-containing plants may have found their way into some regimens – but they had no cure.62 Against elephantiasis, they had nothing.

The magical healers had no cure either. But they offered something the physicians often did not: a personal relationship with the patient, a dramatic narrative of combat between good and evil, and a tangible object – the amulet itself, worn against the skin, carried in a pouch, placed under a pillow – that the patient could touch and hold as evidence that someone was fighting for them.63

The slave markets tell the real story. A papyrus from 125-126 CE records the sale of two slaves “captured by the spear” – prisoners of war – with a standard commercial guarantee: “healthy according to the edict, free from sacred disease and physical defect.”64 Nearly six centuries after Hippocrates declared that epilepsy was not sacred, the Roman legal system still used the term hiera nosos – “sacred disease” – in its commercial contracts.65 The rationalist revolution had changed the vocabulary of the consulting room. It had not changed the vocabulary of the marketplace, or the fear that lay behind it.

The Economics of Healing

Who made these amulets? What did they charge? How did the industry work?

The evidence is scattered but suggestive. The materials themselves ranged from cheap to expensive. Papyrus and parchment were affordable – a small strip cost a fraction of a day’s wages.66 Metal lamellae in gold or silver were luxury goods; Kotansky’s catalogue of inscribed gold, silver, copper, and bronze amulets shows that wealthier clients could commission durable objects that would last indefinitely.67 Gemstones with engraved magical images and inscriptions – the so-called “magical gems” studied by Simone Michel and others – were among the most expensive amulets available, requiring the skills of a trained gem-cutter as well as a literate practitioner.68

The practitioners themselves occupied an ambiguous social position. The Shepherd of Hermas, a second-century Christian text, offers a vivid image: “Sorcerers carry their charms in boxes.”69 This is a portrait of an itinerant professional – a person who moves from client to client, carrying a portable toolkit of prepared amulets, ingredients, and reference texts. The magical handbooks preserved in the Greek Magical Papyri – the collections of tested recipes, formulary templates with blank spaces marked “NN” for the client’s name – are the reference manuals of such professionals.70

The Didache, an early Christian teaching document, lists “enchanters, astrologers, and magicians” alongside standard sins, suggesting that these were common enough to require specific prohibition.71 The fact that Christian authorities repeatedly condemned magical healing – and that Christian clergy repeatedly produced it – indicates a market that was too profitable and too popular to suppress.

The cost of professional medicine provides context. In Athens of the fourth century BCE, twelve drachmas would buy a dose of hemlock – the execution drug.72 A physician’s consultation was more expensive than a curse tablet or a healing amulet, which required only cheap lead, papyrus, or a strip of parchment.73 For the poor, for slaves, for women without independent resources, for anyone who could not afford a physician or who had already tried one and found no relief, the amulet-maker offered an accessible alternative.

And the amulet-maker offered something else: choice. Physicians belonged to theoretical schools with fixed doctrines. Amulet-makers were eclectic by nature. If invoking IAO did not work, they could try Michael. If Michael failed, they could add Abrasax. If the Greek approach proved insufficient, they could incorporate Coptic, Hebrew, or Aramaic formulae. The syncretic flexibility of magical healing – the willingness to draw power from any available source – was a competitive advantage that no single medical or religious tradition could match.

The Words That Cannot Be Spoken

At the heart of many healing amulets lie the voces magicae – the “magical words” that are pronounceable but semantically opaque.74 ABLANATHANALBA. AKRAMMACHAMARI. SESENGENBARPHARANGES. IAO SABAOTH ADONAI. These sequences appear across centuries of magical texts, from second-century BCE Egypt to tenth-century CE amulets, with striking consistency.

ABLANATHANALBA is a palindrome – it reads the same forwards and backwards.75 This formal property was itself understood as magical: a word that cannot be reversed cannot be undone. The palindrome traps the power it names, holding it in an endless loop of self-reference. When inscribed on an amulet, the palindromic word creates a sealed circuit of supernatural energy around the patient.

The vowel sequences – AEIOUOO, or the full seven-vowel series AEHIOYO – represent the seven planets, the seven musical notes, and the seven aspects of the divine name.76 When recited aloud, they produce a continuous, humming sound that could be sustained for long periods, creating an acoustic environment of sacred sound. The amulet for Ahmed son of Mariam includes the vowel sequence AEHIOYO twice, flanking the names of the archangels.77

Some voces magicae are demonstrably derived from identifiable languages. IAO is the Greek transliteration of the Hebrew Tetragrammaton YHWH. SABAOTH is the Hebrew tsevaot, “hosts” or “armies.” ADONAI is Hebrew for “my Lord.” MICHAEL, GABRIEL, RAPHAEL are Hebrew-derived names meaning “Who is like God,” “God is my strength,” and “God heals.”78 The last of these – Raphael, “God heals” – makes his presence in healing amulets etymologically inevitable.

But many voces magicae resist identification. AKRAMMACHAMARI, SESENGENBARPHARANGES, EONEBUYOTH – these may be corrupted Egyptian, Aramaic, or Persian, or they may be deliberate coinages, constructed to sound foreign and therefore powerful.79 The foreignness was the point. A word that the patient could not understand came from a world the patient could not access – the world of divine power, where the ordinary rules of language did not apply. To speak an incomprehensible word was to speak in the language of the gods.

The Gnostic practitioner who made the Arian amulet, the Christian cleric who made the Joannia amulet, and the Coptic practitioner who made the Ahmed amulet all drew from this shared lexicon of power-words. They were competitors who used the same vocabulary. They were rival denominations of the same faith – the faith that language, properly deployed, could intervene in the physical world.

After the Conquest

In 641 CE, the Arab general Amr ibn al-As completed the conquest of Egypt. The Byzantine administration collapsed. The Greek language, which had been the administrative and literary language of Egypt for nearly a millennium since Alexander’s conquest, began its long retreat.80 Coptic, the final stage of the Egyptian language written in a modified Greek alphabet, remained the language of the Christian majority. Arabic, the language of the new rulers, gradually spread through administration, commerce, and conversion.

The healing amulet industry adapted. It had survived the transition from pharaonic to Ptolemaic Egypt, from Ptolemaic to Roman, from pagan to Christian. It would survive the transition to Islam.

The amulet for Ahmed son of Mariam is the most striking evidence.81 Preserved as P.Heidelberg inv. Kopt. 544b, it is a small parchment sheet, 7.3 centimetres by 6.5 centimetres, folded into a tight packet approximately 1.1 by 2.5 centimetres – designed to be worn on the body, probably in a pouch hung around the neck or tucked into clothing.82 Hans Quecke published it in 1963. Recent scholarship at the Coptic Magical Papyri project at the University of Wurzburg has dated it to approximately the tenth or eleventh century CE, based on palaeographic comparison with similar manuscripts in the Heidelberg collection.83

The text opens with eight and a half lines of voces magicae and angelic names: Michael, Gabriel, Raphael, Souriel, Zarathiel, Zedeki, Anael, Yoel, and others.84 The angelic names all end in -el, the Hebrew suffix meaning “God” – a feature of Eastern Mediterranean magical tradition that persisted regardless of the practitioner’s or client’s religious affiliation.85 Then the petition:

I beseech you… that you may bring out the fever and the burning and the sufferings and the evil things and the mania and the weeping from Ahmed son of Mariam, in the hour that he wears you. Yea, yea! Quickly, quickly!86

The client’s name is Arabic: Ahmad, one of the most common Muslim male names, meaning “most praised.”87 His mother’s name is Mariam – the Arabic form of Mary, shared by Jews, Christians, and Muslims alike. The amulet is written in Coptic, with some Greek loanwords (including mania and the verb phoreo, “to wear”). The practitioner was almost certainly a Coptic Christian. The client was almost certainly a Muslim.

This is not as surprising as it might seem. Research on the Coptic Magical Papyri has documented extensive cross-communal magical practice in medieval Egypt.88 An eighth-century monastery contained an amulet inscribed with a Muslim prayer. Contemporaneous Coptic letters describe Christians sending amulets attributed to a Muslim sage to heal a woman from sickness. A tenth-century curse from the Cairo Genizah – the famous repository of Jewish documents – begins with the Islamic basmala (“In the name of Allah, the All-Beneficent, the All-Merciful”), then continues in Arabic written in Coptic script and in Coptic language, and was found in a Jewish synagogue.89

The boundaries between religions were, in practice, far more porous than their theologians would have preferred. When your child had a fever, you did not consult a systematic theology. You consulted whoever had a reputation for results.

The Persistence of the Formula

Let us place the documents side by side.

Arian’s amulet, fourth century: “Guard and preserve Arian from the daily fever-chill and from the nightly fever-chill and from the slight fever.”90

Joannia’s amulet, fifth century: “Chase from her and put to flight all fevers and every kind of chill, quotidian, tertian, and quartan, and every evil.”91

Vibius’s amulet, sixth century: “Christ saves Vibius, whom Gennaia bore, from all fever and from all shivering, daily, quotidian, now now, quickly, quickly.”92

Ahmed’s amulet, tenth or eleventh century: “Bring out the fever and the burning and the sufferings and the evil things and the mania and the weeping from Ahmed son of Mariam, in the hour that he wears you.”93

The Attic tomb curse, second or third century: “He shall experience all evils – shivering, fever, quartan ague, elephantiasis.”94

Five documents, spanning perhaps eight hundred years. Four are protective amulets designed to heal. One is a curse designed to harm. But they share the same technical vocabulary, the same understanding of disease as a catalogue of specific afflictions, the same conviction that supernatural power can be directed at a named individual to produce a specific physical effect.

The gods summoned to intervene change: Abrasax and the living God; Christ and the Holy Spirit and the saints; the archangels Michael, Gabriel, and Raphael; Plouton and Demeter and the Erinyes. But the structure of the appeal does not change. Name the power. Name the patient. Name the disease. Command the cure. Demand speed.

This persistence is not an accident or a coincidence. It reflects the fundamental truth that the diseases did not change. Malaria was the same in the fourth century BCE and the tenth century CE. The fever came in the same patterns. The shivering came at the same hours. The quartan ague returned every seventy-two hours, as regular as a calendar. No theology, no philosophy, no medical revolution altered the biological reality of Plasmodium parasites reproducing in human blood cells.

And the psychological needs of the sick did not change either. A person with a fever that would not break, lying in a darkened room in Oxyrhynchus or Attica or post-conquest Cairo, needed the same things in every century: the assurance that someone powerful was paying attention to their specific case; the comfort of a tangible object that represented that attention; the hope that this time, this god, this formula, this practitioner would succeed where others had failed.

Hippocrates was right that epilepsy was a disease of the brain, not a divine punishment. He was right that the purifiers and spell-casters were offering treatments with no biological mechanism of action. But he was wrong about something more fundamental. He assumed that once people understood the natural causes of disease, they would abandon supernatural remedies. He assumed that knowledge would change behavior. Twenty-five centuries of evidence suggest otherwise.

The formula endured because it worked – not in the sense that it cured diseases (it did not), but in the sense that it did something that rational medicine often failed to do. It named the patient. It acknowledged the specific suffering. It promised that the universe was paying attention. It demanded action, right now, quickly, quickly.

In the end, that may have been enough.
