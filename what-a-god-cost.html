What a God Cost

Reading One Temple Account from Roman Egypt

The First Charge

The largest figure near the top is for oversight of priests: 5,500 drachmas.1 Before robes, incense, or bread, the account places supervision. Priests had to be watched, registered, and charged for. Another papyrus from Theadelphia makes the same point from the side. In 104-105 CE, Pekysis son of Satabous paid 12 silver drachmas, plus surcharges, as admission tax of priests.10 Priestly service did not float free of taxation. Entry into sacred office could itself generate a receipt.10

The same opening section turns from people to places. Two temple enclosures at Nilopolis, one of Isis Nepherses and one of Isis Nephremmis, carry a charge of 2,100 drachmas plus surcharge.1 Then comes the village secretary’s fee, 95 drachmas plus surcharge.1 A shrine needed walls, staff, and a paper trail. This account gives all three on the same page. The gods may have occupied the center of the cult, but the first entries belong to the people who administered access to them.

Oil and Offices

Next come the oil presses of Socnopaeus at Socnopaiou Nesos: 142 drachmas 2 1/2 obols, plus surcharge.1 The number is smaller, but the line matters because it shows a temple world tied to production as well as consumption. Oil was needed constantly for lamps. A late first-century BCE oath from Oxyrhynchus shows four lamplighters binding themselves and their property to supply the proper oil for the daily lamps in the shrines of Sarapis, Isis, and Thoeris.2 The Louvre account does not describe the lamps themselves, but once the presses appear, the burden behind them is easy to see. A god’s room had to stay lit.12

After the presses, the papyrus names offices. For the prophet, lesones, and image-bearer of Socnopaeus and Enoupis at Nilopolis: 64 drachmas plus surcharge.1 For the prophet of the very great god Suchus: 344 drachmas.1 The account does not stop to explain what each office did. It assumes that the reader knows. Other documents supply the missing edges. In 216 CE, Aurelios Pakysis described himself as priest and stolistes of the first-rank temple at Soknopaiou Nesos.6 Titles like prophet, image-bearer, and stolistes belonged to a real hierarchy of service around the gods, and this papyrus lets us watch some of that hierarchy acquire a price.16 The line is terse, but the labor behind it was not. Someone had to open the rooms, dress the image, process offerings, and keep the sequence of ritual acts from breaking down.

Dressing the God

Then the account turns from offices to things placed directly before the divine image. Three fine linen robes for the gods: 300 drachmas total.1 The line is short, but it is enough. Divine images were clothed, and clothed repeatedly. Linen had to be bought, stored, and replaced. A second-century BCE petition by Poregebthis, priest and warden of a temple of Isis, gives a harsher glimpse of what such goods could mean. After an assault, he listed among the losses and sacred valuables a linen garment worth 2,000 drachmas, together with money and bronze equipment.7 Temple cloth in Egypt was not decorative padding. It was costly property that had to exist in usable condition.7 A robe on an image sounds ceremonial. In the accounts it looks like recurring expense, vulnerable to theft, damage, and wear like any other valuable textile.

Smoke and Scent

Below the robes come the aromatics. Kyphi and other expenses: 500 drachmas.1 Kyphi for the god Harpocrates: 96 drachmas.1 Sacrifice and incense on the birthday of the deified Augusti: 40 drachmas.1 Myrrh and balsam for the gods on three days: 60 drachmas.1

The temple interior had to smell right as well as look right. Kyphi was a compounded perfume, not a generic pinch of incense.8 Myrrh and balsam belonged to the same expensive world of imported or closely handled substances.18 A petition about the taxed trade in juniper resin shows how easily sacred needs could spill into disputes over sale and monopoly.9 Aromatics were burnable offerings, but they were also commodities moving through taxed channels.9 The account reminds us that scent had to be purchased the same way grain and linen did. A pleasing ritual atmosphere depended on supply.

Bread Every Day

The bottom of the account is heavier than the top because it shifts from cash to grain. Baking for the officiating priests of the five tribes of the great god Socnopaeus is reckoned at one artaba of wheat daily, for 365 artabas over a year before the festival additions are counted.1 Then feast days raise the burden. Seven days here, eight there, nineteen days for the birthday of Socnopaeus, nine for the marriage of Isis Nepherses, and other blocks in which four artabas a day are needed.1

Here the account comes closest to showing the rhythm of an actual year. Priests had to eat on ordinary days, then eat more on the days when the gods required more ceremony. A temple could not survive on piety alone; it needed a grain base stable enough to carry both routine and festival.1 An account of sacred land belonging to Isis of Taposiris Aquila, with money and corn reckoned against it, shows the agrarian side of the same problem. Sacred land was measured, classified, and taxed as land because temple life had to be fed from somewhere.3 By the time the reader reaches the bottom of the Louvre papyrus, the temple has become legible as a daily consumer of bread. That is one of the hardest truths the sheet preserves. Gods could be honored only if men were fed often enough to keep serving them.

The five tribes matter here because they turn ritual into schedule. One artaba each day keeps ordinary service going. Festival blocks push the number up because more priests are present, more hands are needed, and the god’s calendar cannot be honored on ordinary rations. The rise from daily bread to feast-day surplus is where the account stops being a list and becomes a year.

What the Papyrus Leaves Off

The sheet is still only a sheet. It does not tell us who held the stylus, who checked the calculations, or whether the year had been easy or strained. It does not list every animal, field, storehouse, and vessel standing behind these charges. Other scraps help with that missing background. A mid-third-century BCE letter about the cows of Isis and Osiris shows sacred livestock kept as productive property.12 An inventory of offerings from 213-216 CE lists gold lamps, silver objects, robes, statues, and even daily oil supplied to some village shrines.11 Temples accumulated objects, animals, and land, but this account reminds us that accumulation was not the same thing as readiness. Stored wealth still had to be converted into light, cloth, scent, and bread.11112

What a God Cost

Read in order, P.Louvre 1.4 gives a plain answer. A god cost supervision of priests, fees to scribes, enclosures that carried charges, oil and the means to secure it, named cult offices, robes, kyphi, incense, myrrh, balsam, and bread every day of the year with more bread on feast days.1

The other papers only sharpen the same point. A temple needed enough order that the lamps could burn every day, enough land and income that priests could keep eating, enough cloth and scent that the god could keep appearing as a god, and enough agreement with officials that the whole arrangement would continue to be recognized.1231011

That is what this damaged account preserves. Not theology. Not a hymn. A year of obligations, written down before they were forgotten.
