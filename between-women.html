Between Women

Contracts, Houses, Letters, and the Thin Archive of Female Attachment in Roman Egypt

Isidora and Didyma

One woman, Isidora, gives an infant into the care of another woman, Didyma.1 The contract survives in Greek. It is explicit, detailed, and unsentimental. Didyma agrees to nurse the baby in her own home for sixteen months, receiving a monthly wage. She must keep her milk pure. She must not sleep with a man. She must not become pregnant. She must not nurse another child. She must present the infant whenever Isidora asks to see it. She is responsible for the belongings sent with the child as well as for the child itself.1

This is a labor contract. It is also an intimacy contract.

The point of the restrictions is clear. If Didyma becomes pregnant, her milk will change and her household’s priorities will change with it. If she sleeps with a man, she risks pregnancy. If she suckles another child, she divides the nourishment for which Isidora is paying. The contract attempts to police not only Didyma’s work but the uses of her body.1

That makes the text unusually revealing. A wet-nursing contract turns one woman’s body into the place where another woman’s future is temporarily lodged. The child is the formal subject, but the relation that animates the papyrus is between the two women: one entrusting, the other carrying, feeding, waiting for inspection, and promising bodily exclusivity under penalty.1

There is no reason to call this erotic. There is every reason to call it intimate.

Contracts like this existed because infant mortality was high, mothers died in childbirth, milk failed, households broke, and urban families regularly relied on substitute nursing arrangements.6 But the contract does more than show a market in milk. It shows how a woman’s household could become, for a fixed time, the extension of another woman’s maternal power. Didyma is not merely an employee. She becomes the bodily environment of Isidora’s child. The arrangement is paid, watched, and hedged with sanctions because it matters too much to leave to trust alone.1

This is one place to linger: not because it proves romance, but because it shows one woman binding another to her through need, flesh, and repeated inspection. The relation is legal because the relation is already emotionally dangerous.

The House at Cercemunis

Another document, from 76 CE, is simpler and therefore, in its way, almost as suggestive.2 Sieapis, daughter of Sarapion son of Apollonios, filed a property return in the village of Cercemunis. The house, she says, belongs to me and to my sister, Thais.2

That is almost the whole story the state cared to record.

Yet a jointly held house is not a small thing. The Roman Egyptian archive names women constantly as wives, mothers, daughters, guardians’ dependents, and dowry recipients. It names them somewhat less often as co-holders of a residence in their own right. A shared house means shared interest, shared risk, shared taxation, and probably shared domestic strategy. It means that whatever else happened in the emotional lives of Sieapis and Thais, they were materially tied together enough to appear as a unit before the officials of the record system.2

That unit matters because the documentary archive is biased toward exactly such moments of material legibility. If two women built their lives around one another in a way that did not affect taxes, inheritance, guardianship, nursing, or household property, the papyri would have almost no reason to preserve the relation at all. Shared property is one of the few channels through which female attachment becomes visible.25

So a house is not proof of a love affair. But it is the kind of infrastructure within which attachment could harden into a life.

To My Sister, Mistress Dionysia

The letters are thinner and, because of that, often more charged.

In one late third-century letter from Oxyrhynchus, Pathermouthis writes to my sister, mistress Dionysia.3 The contents are administrative. She cannot come in person; Dionysia is to see whether us can be registered, and if so, to register us, me and Patas.3 It is a small note about bureaucratic necessity.

But the letter presupposes a great deal. Dionysia can stand in for the absent writer. She can navigate official procedure. She is trusted with a matter serious enough to require prompt registration, and the absent woman assumes not only practical competence but loyalty.3

The address itself is conventional. Sister in Egyptian letters can mean literal sibling, metaphorical kin, spouse, or simply an intimate term of address shaped by family language.5 The word cannot bear more than the evidence allows, but it should not be flattened either. To address another woman as my sister, mistress Dionysia is to place oneself in a hierarchy of affection and dependence that the language of kinship is expected to carry.35

This is where queer history in the papyri becomes most difficult. The same formulas can veil and reveal at once. A woman may call another sister because she is her sister. Or because that is how female friends speak. Or because kinship language is the only safe public idiom available for intimacy. We do not get to decide among these possibilities from a single line. We only get to notice that the archive is built in such a way that intimate female relations, whatever their exact character, are most likely to survive under names that neutralize them.35

Eirene and Asphaleia

Another fourth-century note is even smaller.4 Eirene writes to my sister Asphaleia, very many greetings. She sends greetings to their parents and brothers. Maria greets you. Then comes the request: buy a little cumin, and more of it again if possible.4

At first glance the papyrus looks trivial, almost unserious. But ancient intimacy often survives exactly in the form of errands. Someone knows whom to ask. Someone knows which spice is wanted. Someone carries the chain of greetings from one household to another. A woman’s place in another woman’s life is proved not through confession but through assumption: of course you can do this for me; of course I write to you; of course Maria’s greeting will matter to you; of course our households remain entangled enough that cumin belongs in the same letter as kinship.4

No one would call this a love letter. Yet it belongs to the emotional infrastructure of ordinary life, and that infrastructure is how stronger attachments persist. The archive almost never preserves female interiority directly. It preserves circulation: goods, names, favors, child-care, legal representation, greetings. To read for women is to read those currents.45

What the Archive Shows

Set beside one another, the documents show women acting as sisters, household partners, property co-holders, substitute mothers, trusted correspondents, and caretakers of one another’s legal vulnerability.1234 What they do not show easily is lovers.

That is partly because of survival. Ancient letters are lost at random. It is partly because of genre. Legal documents preserve obligations, not longing. And it is partly because strong female bonds could be socially coded as kinship, service, or domestic cooperation even when they carried more feeling than those terms suggest.57

To ask whether any one of these women really loved another woman in the erotic sense is therefore both necessary and impossible. Necessary, because otherwise queer people disappear into neutral language forever. Impossible, because the papyri were not written for us and did not imagine our categories.7

The better response is to stay with the density of the ties that do survive.

The Closest We Can Honestly Come

If I had to choose one relation from this small archive as the strongest candidate for a fuller story, I would choose Isidora and Didyma.1

Not because the contract proves they were lovers. It does not. But because it creates the most demanding and embodied relation between two named women that this group of documents gives us. The arrangement is prolonged. It is domestic. It is invasive of the nurse’s private life. It depends on repeated contact. It is built on an unstable mixture of wage, trust, surveillance, and bodily dependency.1

Such an arrangement could harden into affection, resentment, loyalty, jealousy, grief, or some combination of them. Very little of that would ever be allowed onto papyrus. The legal form screens precisely the emotional content it makes possible. That is why the document matters. It tells us where to look for female attachment in a hostile archive: not in declarations, but in arrangements so intimate that they required law.1

The house of Sieapis and Thais belongs next to it, then the letters of Dionysia and Asphaleia.234 Together they do not prove a female love affair. Together they do prove that women in Roman Egypt built lives through one another with enough intensity to enter contracts, hold property, exchange necessity, and bear one another’s domestic burdens.

That may not be the story a modern reader first asks for. But it may be closer to the truth of what the papyri can really give us.

Limits of the Record

Did Sieapis and Thais live together in the house they registered?2 Probably, but the text does not say. Did Dionysia and the woman who wrote to her call each other sisters literally, metaphorically, or tactically?3 We do not know. Did Eirene’s cumin note belong to a network of ordinary kinship or to an attachment more personal and private than the surviving formula lets us see?4 Again, we do not know.

And Isidora and Didyma? We know the terms, the money, the milk, the restrictions, the penalties. We do not know whether the infant lived. We do not know whether the women ever liked one another. We do not know whether trust grew between them, or whether each month of the contract merely sharpened suspicion. We do not know whether the child’s mother returned often, or whether Didyma’s house became for a time the center of a life that officially remained someone else’s.1

That uncertainty is not a defect in the story. It is the story.

For women in the ancient archive, especially in the search for queer history, silence is not empty. It is structural. The documents that survive were designed to record exactly those parts of a relation that could be enforced, taxed, inherited, or disputed. Everything else had to live between the lines, or in letters that no one saved, or in households that disappeared without legal conflict.

This is not a story of proven female same-sex love. It is a story about how close we can honestly come.
