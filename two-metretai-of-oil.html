Two Metretai of Oil

A Small Debt Case in the Herakleopolite, 153 BCE

Oil Trade Debt Petitions Credit Herakleopolite Nome Ptolemaic Egypt

3 primary documents · 5 notes

The size of the quarrel

Oil moved through every level of life in Hellenistic Egypt. It lit lamps, entered food, mattered to bathing, and circulated through official distribution as well as private sale.23 But this petition is not about the whole oil economy. It is about what happens when one delivery does not get paid for.

The text is damaged after the amount, but the drift is plain. Dioskourides says the money had been demanded repeatedly and still had not arrived.1 He asks the official to have Petophois put under security until payment is forced.1 That is the part worth lingering on. The debt was small enough to fit into a few lines. It was serious enough that the creditor wanted the debtor bound over until the money appeared.

This is what petty commerce looked like once trust failed. A sale became a petition. Repeated requests became documentary proof that patience had run out.

Credit at close range

Ptolemaic petitions from the Fayum and neighboring districts show the same pattern again and again. Someone is owed grain, fodder, rent, or cash; the debtor delays; the creditor writes upward.45 One man from Magdola complains that another owes him the price of fodder and will not pay.4 Another, from Ammonias, says a cavalryman still has not handed over fifteen artabas of barley promised from the harvest.5 The amounts and commodities change. The structure does not.

That wider paperwork makes the oil case easier to read. Dioskourides was not doing anything strange or theatrical. He was using the normal machinery for collection when private pressure had failed.

The surviving address matters too. He petitions a commander and phrourarch, also named Dioskourides.1 Commercial life did not sit apart from the coercive arm of the state. If a debtor held out, the seller could try to bring official force down beside the account.

What two metretai can show

It would be easy to treat this as a minor quarrel, and in one sense it is. Nobody dies. No estate is at stake. No one claims to have been beaten or dispossessed. One man says another still owes him the price of oil.

That is exactly why the papyrus matters. It catches the ordinary scale at which ancient trade actually broke down. Most people did not live inside spectacular fortunes. They lived among deliveries, promises, harvests, credit, and delay. A missing payment on two metretai of oil could be enough to send a man to a scribe.

The petition survives in damaged form, but its pressure is intact. A seller has waited, demanded, waited again, and finally written the whole thing into the file. By then the oil was long gone. What remained was the debt, the irritation, and the hope that an official order might do what repeated asking had not.

Notes

  1. Corpus ID 137048, P.Diosk. 3, before 23 January 153 BCE (year uncertain), Herakleopolite. Petition of Dioskourides son of Pakemis against Petophois son of Petonouris. The surviving translation preserves the key sequence: “I am being wronged” ἀδικοῦμαι; the debt for “the price of two metretai of oil” τιμὴν ἐλαίου μετρητῶν β; the amount of two talents and four thousand bronze; and the request that the debtor be “put under security” until payment is made.
  2. Oil appears constantly in Ptolemaic administrative and commercial papers, including distribution and accounting texts from the Arsinoite and neighboring nomes. For one local account context, see Corpus ID 10350, P.Lille 1.9, a third-century BCE memorandum concerning oil measures.
  3. On the place of oil in the Ptolemaic economy, see Dorothy J. Thompson, Memphis under the Ptolemies, and related work on state-controlled staples, taxation, and household consumption.
  4. Corpus ID 40196, P.Enteux. 36, 27 February 221 BCE, Magdola. Petition in which a creditor says he is being wronged because another man still owes him the price of fodder and does not pay even after repeated demands.
  5. Corpus ID 28445, P.Enteux. 46, 27 February 221 BCE, Ammonias. Petition in which Theonides says Seuthes owes him fifteen artabas of barley and has still not paid after the agreed time passed.
