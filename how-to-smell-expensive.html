How to Smell Expensive

Perfume Sellers, Barber Shops, Bath Costs, and the Infrastructure of Being Well Kept in Roman Egypt

Roman Egypt Perfume Unguent Barbers Baths Consumer Culture Retail Grooming Oxyrhynchus Arsinoite Nome

35 primary documents · 48 notes

1. Smell Could Be Leased Like Any Other Business

In P.Fay. 93, Sarapion negotiates for a share in Castor’s perfume-selling and scent-making enterprise.1 The language is unusually revealing because it treats fragrance as a divisible stream of rights and income. The share has proportions. The rent is monthly. The term is fixed. A world of bodily intimacy appears in the hard grammar of contract.

The crucial exclusion of agorai and festivals gives the document its wider significance.1 Those occasions mattered enough to be withheld. Whether that meant higher volume, better margins, or simple strategic control, the economic point is the same: scent sales were not flat across the calendar.

The lease looks less isolated once other perfumery titles are set beside it. Earlier Ptolemaic records preserve perfumery terms (μυρεψικῆς, μυρεψικῇ) tied to perfumers and perfumery work.78 A first-century BCE text clusters the verbs “to prepare perfumes” and “to make unguents” (μυρεψεῖν, μυροποιεῖν), making manufacture rather than mere sale explicit.9 Later documents name the seller himself as a perfume seller (μυροπώλης, μυροπώλῃ).101112131415

By the time the lease of 161 CE appears, then, the documentary language is already thick. Perfume retail is not a stray luxury habit. It has occupational labels, workshop language, and a contractible share structure.17891011

2. The Perfume Seller Was a Recognizable Person in the Archive

Some papyri mention the trade almost casually, and that casualness is important.

A Zenon letter tells the recipient to send for the perfume seller (τὸν μυροπώλην) and hear what he has to say.11 Later texts from late antique Egypt address or identify a perfume seller (μυροπώλης) directly.12131415 In other words, the perfume seller is legible enough in documentary life to function like any other specialist: someone one summons, pays, leases from, or deals with by title.

That matters because it shifts perfume out of the realm of literary fantasy. Roman writers loved to turn scent into moral commentary. The papyri do something duller and more useful. They show perfumers and perfume sellers as ordinary economic actors.

The same is true of aromatic goods (ἀρωματικά). Those appear in late antique commercial lists and correspondence as a recognizable category.1617 To write that term is already to assume a buyer, a seller, a stock, and a way of distinguishing scented goods from the rest of commerce.

3. Buying Grooming Goods Looked Like Any Other Shopping Run

A late third-century order from Oxyrhynchus gives us the consumer end of the trade.2 Achillis is told to buy good perfume, powder, combs, incense, oil, papyrus, sauce, and other things in one breathless sequence.2 The note is crowded, interrupted, and practical.

That disorder is exactly what makes it valuable. Perfume is not purchased in a hermetically sealed luxury zone. It travels in the same shopping errand as writing materials, condiments, furniture, and wine.2 Grooming is woven into provisioning, not separated from it.

The order also preserves an important material truth: looking good meant assembling several things at once. Not just perfume, but powder. Not just powder, but combs. Not just combs, but oil and incense.2 Grooming in the papyri looks less like one product than like a kit.

That pattern is visible elsewhere. Clothing lists and inventories preserve shawls, belts, veils, headbands, towels, and bath gear.18192021 A note asks for handsome linen and a sash before the recipient comes to the festival.22 Another fragment speaks of garments sent for the public festival (πρὸς τὴν πανήγυριν).23 These are not all beauty texts in a narrow sense. They are the material world in which public appearance had to be assembled.

4. Unguent Required Containers, Measures, and Trust

Scented goods did not float free. They had packaging.

One letter sends four cotylae of unguent as part of a gift package.3 Another asks for four unguent containers to be sent quickly.4 Still others preserve the word perfume box or unguent container (μυροθήκη).242526

This is one of the nicest details in the archive because it shows the hardware of smell. Someone expected scented liquids or semi-liquids to travel in recognisable vessels. Someone could ask for several at once. Someone else knew what sort of object was meant.4242526

That does not require a grand manufacturing system. It does require a market stable enough that both scent and its container could be ordered by name.

The gift letter with the four cotylae matters for another reason too.3 It shows unguent moving not only through shops but through kin and friendship networks. Good smell, like good food, could be sent.

5. Oils and Aromatics Sat Inside Wider Commodity Markets

Perfume economies were never just about perfume.

The documents keep pushing outward from the finished scented product to the raw and semi-raw substances that fed it. There are aromatic goods (ἀρωματικά) as a commercial class.1617 There are oil-sellers in dispute.2728 There is a tax-connected text tied to an oil manufacturer.29 There is alum administration, useful because controlled substances and finishing materials circulated through monitored channels.30 A desert letter mentions myrrh and pepper.31 An ostracon from the second century BCE lists prices for oil together with myrrh and cinnamon (σμύρνα, κιννάμωμον).32

Used carefully, these texts show the outer ring of the grooming economy. Taken together, they place perfume and grooming beside markets in oil, resins, spices, minerals, and containers, even when the links are not explicit in every case.272829303132

Even an undated remedy for eye disease belongs on the edge of this story.33 It mixes ingredients like pepper, gum, cadmia, psimythion, and opobalsam. That is not a vanity recipe. But it reminds us how porous the boundary could be between cosmetic, medicinal, and aromatic substances. The same goods moved across different bodily uses.33

6. Barbering Was a Trade with Equipment and Shops

The papyri are surprisingly good on barbers.

One letter from the mid-third century CE orders Italian wood and planks for the making of barber's equipment.5 It is an important correction to the literary stereotype of the barber as mere chatter and razor. The document suggests capital outlay: tools, fittings, or furniture substantial enough to justify estate timber.5

A very different group of Ptolemaic texts reveals the barber from below. One memorandum gives directions to the barber’s shop (κουρεῖον) in Hermopolis.34 Another expense note includes payment “to the barber” (κουρεῖ) for shaving.35 A second-century BCE account does the same in passing.36

These are tiny records, but they are exactly what we need. They show that barbering had a place, a vocabulary, and a price.

A later Oxyrhynchite petition preserves the verb in a more threatening register: “I will cut your hair” (τὴν τρίχα σου κουρεύσω).37 That is not a shop receipt, but it proves how socially legible cutting and managing hair remained. Hair, in other words, was not invisible maintenance. It was a public condition.

7. Baths Belonged to the Same World of Maintenance

Barbers and perfume sellers did not exhaust the infrastructure of looking well kept. Baths belonged to it too.

Ptolemaic accounts preserve repeated tiny expenses “for the bath” (βαλανεῖ).38394041 Another Zenon text concerns the bathhouse at Philadelphia directly (τὸ βαλανεῖον).42 An imperial account records expenditure on a newly built bathhouse.43 A later property document includes the purchase of a bath establishment (λουτρόν ἤτοι βαλανεῖον).44

These are not luxury manifestos. They are stronger than that because they are ordinary. They show the bath as a routinized expense, a building type, and a managed space.

The later estate archive reinforces the same point. One letter orders that the bathhouse be heated in every possible way before expected arrivals.45 Another records fuel apportioned for the baths of the great gymnasium.46

Taken together, these texts make a simple argument. Grooming in Roman Egypt did not end with perfume. It extended into washing, heating, fuel, attendants, and the built environment of the bath.

8. Festivals Intensified the Need to Appear

The lease of the perfume business reserves out markets and festivals.1 Other documents help explain why that clause mattered.

Festival lists include garlands, oil, sweet wine, and greenery.47 Letters tell people to bring good linen and a sash and come to the festival.22 Another refers to the night-festival of Isis.48 A late fragment speaks of garments for the public festival (πανήγυρις).23

None of these texts says outright that festival crowds bought more perfume. We should not force them to say more than they do. What they do show is that feast days were moments of concentrated public appearance. Bodies went out among other bodies. Clothes, oil, and ornament mattered more because visibility mattered more.122234748

That makes the exclusion clause in P.Fay. 93 much easier to understand. If there were days on which many people needed to look, smell, and seem more than ordinarily presentable, a perfume seller might well want to hold those days back from the ordinary division of profits.1

9. Looking Good Was a Small but Real Infrastructure

The larger claim has to be made with care.

The papyri do not prove a coherent, empire-wide beauty industry. They do not let us reconstruct the exact margins of a perfumer, or the exact design of the barber’s apparatus, or the precise contents of every unguent jar.1524

What they do preserve, repeatedly, is a chain of maintenance. A share in a perfume-selling business (μυροπώλικη) can be leased.1 Perfume sellers (Μυροπῶλαι) and perfumers appear by title.789101112131415 A household orders perfume, powder, combs, and oil.2 Unguent travels in measured quantities and recognizable containers.34242526 Oil-sellers, aromatics, myrrh, cinnamon, and alum connect the trade to larger commodity systems.1617272829303132 Barbers have shops, prices, and equipment.5343536 Baths consume money, fuel, buildings, and labor.383940414243444546

That is already enough to change the scale of the subject. Looking expensive in Roman Egypt was not just personal taste. It rested on retailers, containers, bathhouses, shop labor, timber, oils, spices, and the ordinary errands of other people.

The archive keeps catching beauty where it touches money. That is why it feels so concrete.
