Between Women

Isidora, Didyma, and the Child in the Nurse's House

The Contract

Isidora handed her baby to Didyma and had the terms written down.1

The child would stay in Didyma’s house for sixteen months.1 Didyma would feed the baby with her own body, keep the child alive, keep safe the clothes and other items that came with it, and produce the infant whenever Isidora wanted to see it.1 For that she would be paid a monthly wage.1

Then the contract closes in.

Didyma is not to sleep with a man.1 She is not to become pregnant.1 She is not to nurse another child at the same time.1 If the arrangement breaks down, the papyrus already knows how the penalty will be calculated.1

In Didyma's House

The document is about a baby, but its pressure falls on a grown woman.

Wet nursing in Roman Egypt was paid work, and families wrote contracts because an infant could die, a nurse could disappear, or another pregnancy could alter the whole bargain.2 Isidora did not merely hire food for her child. She hired a house, a body, a schedule of visits, and months of vigilance. The clothes went with the infant and had to come back.1 Access to the child stayed with the mother.1 The nurse’s sex life became part of the contract because sex might lead to pregnancy, and pregnancy might change the milk.12

That is what gives the papyrus its force. Didyma is being paid, but she is also being watched. Isidora is surrendering daily care, but she is not surrendering control. For sixteen months one woman’s household is opened to another woman’s demands.

The paper does not tell us the age of the baby, whether Isidora had other children, whether Didyma had recently given birth herself, or what the rooms in that house looked like. It does tell us what both women thought could go wrong. Milk could be divided. Clothing could be lost. Access to the child could become difficult. A woman’s own body could stop serving the agreement she had signed.

What the contract does not say

No one writes down whether the women liked each other.

That silence is ordinary. Documents of Roman Egypt preserve what can be enforced, measured, paid, and checked.23 They are much better at wages and penalties than at trust. Even so, this contract lets more feeling through than it means to. A mother does not pay for the right to inspect a child unless distance has become dangerous. A nurse does not accept rules about sex, pregnancy, and exclusive milk unless the money matters or the need does.

The baby disappears into the legal language. Isidora and Didyma do not. One woman leaves the child in another woman’s rooms. The other agrees to reorganize her body and household around that child for sixteen months. That is the relationship the papyrus preserves: intimate, paid, unequal, and impossible to run without cooperation.

We do not know whether the infant lived. We do not know whether Didyma kept the bargain easily, or whether Isidora came often, or whether the two women ended the term in gratitude, resentment, or relief. The contract was not made to preserve any of that. It was made to hold one fragile arrangement together long enough for a child to survive it.1
