The Defeat

In 494 BCE, after six years of revolt, the Persian Empire crushed the Ionian Greeks. The naval battle of Lade, off the coast of Miletus, decided the matter. The Ionians, divided by internal treachery, were overwhelmed. What followed was systematic.

Herodotus, writing half a century later, describes the aftermath with a precision that suggests he considered the details important enough to record exactly:

When they had gained mastery over the cities, they chose out the most handsome boys and castrated them, making them eunuchs instead of men, and they carried the fairest maidens away to the king; they did all this, and they burnt the cities with their temples. Thus three times had the Ionians been enslaved, first by the Lydians and now twice in a row by the Persians.3

Herodotus does not soften the scene. The most beautiful boys are cut; the most beautiful girls are carried off. The symmetry tells you how empire thought. Beauty was not an ornament of the conquered. It was loot. The boys would end up in Persian households and palaces; the girls in the king’s harem. Both became resources.

This was not exceptional. Herodotus records that the annual tribute from Babylon and Assyria to the Persian king included “five hundred castrated boys” alongside a thousand talents of silver.4 The boys were a line item in the imperial budget, listed between precious metals and livestock. The scale implies an industry. Five hundred per year, from a single satrapy. Across the empire, the numbers must have been staggering.

Who held the children down? Who made the cuts? Which boys bled out and vanished before they ever reached the court? Herodotus does not say. He does, however, preserve the story of one man who made a trade of it.

His name was Panionius, and he came from the island of Chios. Herodotus describes his trade with cold disgust:

He would procure beautiful boys and castrate and take them to Sardis and Ephesus where he sold them for large sums of money. For the barbarians value eunuchs more than perfect men, by reason of the full trust that they have in them.5

There is the paradox again, embedded casually in the ethnographic aside. The “barbarians” – Persians, Lydians, the peoples of the Near East – valued eunuchs more than intact men. Why? Because they trusted them more. Eunuchs, stripped of the capacity to found rival dynasties, posed no threat to the king’s succession. Their loyalty could be total because their ambition had nowhere else to go.

Panionius was a Greek profiting from a Persian demand. He was a middleman in a supply chain that began with kidnapping and ended in royal courts. Among his many victims was a boy from the town of Pedasa in Caria, near Halicarnassus. His name was Hermotimus.

Hermotimus survived the operation. He was sold to the Persian court. And there, against all probability, he rose. Herodotus tells us that “as time went on, he stood higher in Xerxes’ favour than any other eunuch.”6 By the time of Xerxes’ invasion of Greece in 480 BCE, Hermotimus was powerful enough to be entrusted with the guardianship of the king’s sons.

And then came the encounter. While Xerxes was preparing his expedition at Sardis, Hermotimus found Panionius. The man who had castrated him decades earlier was now living in the region with his wife and four sons.

Hermotimus invited Panionius to visit, speaking kindly, promising rewards. When the slave-trader arrived with his entire family, Hermotimus laid bare his intentions:

With these words of reproach, he brought Panionius’ sons before him and compelled him to castrate all four of them – his own children; this Panionius was compelled to do. When he had done this, the sons were compelled to castrate their father in turn.7

Herodotus calls this “the fullest vengeance for wrong done to him than had any man whom we know.” The sentence carries an unmistakable note of admiration. Hermotimus had not merely punished Panionius. He had made him experience what he had inflicted – made him perform it with his own hands, on his own sons, and then receive it from them. The symmetry is terrible. It is also, in Herodotus’s telling, just.

What Hermotimus’s story reveals, beyond the personal drama, is a commercial infrastructure of castration that was widespread enough to support specialists like Panionius. The trade ran from Greek islands to Persian courts. It was driven by demand, not ideology. The Persians did not castrate boys because they believed in a theological principle. They castrated boys because they had discovered, through centuries of imperial administration, that castrated men made excellent bureaucrats, guardians, and confidants. The eunuch was a technology of governance.

The King's Servants

Xenophon understood this. His Cyropaedia, written around 370 BCE, is not quite history and not quite fiction – it is an idealised portrait of Cyrus the Great as the model ruler, using Persian institutions to explore Greek questions about leadership. In Book VII, he turns to the question of why Cyrus chose eunuchs for his personal attendants, and his analysis is the most sustained ancient reflection on the eunuch’s social position that survives.

Xenophon’s argument proceeds in stages. First, the premise of exclusion:

Those who had children or congenial wives or sweethearts, such he believed were by nature constrained to love them best. But as he observed that eunuchs were not susceptible to any such affections, he thought that they would esteem most highly those who were in the best position to make them rich and to stand by them.8

In Xenophon’s analysis, the eunuch is built out of deprivation. No children. No wife. No lover. No household of his own pulling against the master. Strip those bonds away and, Xenophon argues, loyalty rushes into the empty space.

Then, the acknowledgement of contempt:

Inasmuch as eunuchs are objects of contempt to the rest of mankind, for this reason, if for no other, they need a master who will be their patron; for there is no man who would not think that he had a right to take advantage of a eunuch at every opportunity unless there were some higher power to prevent his doing so.9

This is the cold part of Xenophon’s argument. He is not pausing to condemn the contempt. He is treating it as a fact of the world: a eunuch is someone anyone might exploit unless a stronger man stands over him. Vulnerability becomes policy. The master’s protection purchases the servant’s total dependence.

Then, the counter-argument against the most obvious objection – that eunuchs are weak:

He drew this conclusion also from the case of other animals: for instance, vicious horses, when gelded, stop biting and prancing about, to be sure, but are none the less fit for service in war; and bulls, when castrated, lose somewhat of their high spirit and unruliness but are not deprived of their strength or capacity for work.10

The comparison to animals is brutal, but it shows how the argument worked. Castration, for Xenophon, did not erase usefulness. It redirected it. The gelded horse still carries a rider. The bull still pulls. The eunuch still fights, rides, hunts, and serves.11

And finally, the practical conclusion:

And if it is thought with some justice that they are inferior in bodily strength, yet on the field of battle steel makes the weak equal to the strong. Recognizing these facts, he selected eunuchs for every post of personal service to him, from the door-keepers up.12

Weapons are the great equaliser. A eunuch with a sword is as dangerous as any man. Cyrus, in Xenophon’s telling, staffed his entire personal household with eunuchs – doorkeepers, guards, attendants, administrators. The eunuch was not a marginal figure in the Persian court. He was the foundation of the king’s security.

Xenophon even stages the theory as a death scene. A noblewoman orders her eunuchs to stand back while she mourns her husband; when she kills herself, the three eunuchs kill themselves where she had posted them.13 In Xenophon’s world, perfect dependence ends in perfect loyalty.

The Female Sickness

Not all ancient gender non-conformity was produced by the knife. Herodotus records a category of people among the Scythians who defied the binary through what he understood as divine punishment.

Scythian warriors plunder a temple of Aphrodite at Ascalon, and the goddess answers by afflicting them and their descendants with what Herodotus calls “the female sickness.”14 Travelers, he says, could still see among the Scythians the people known as Enarees.15

The Enarees were not eunuchs in the Persian sense – they had not been surgically castrated. They were, in Greek understanding, “hermaphrodites” – people who lived as something other than fully male, possibly adopting female dress and social roles.16 Modern scholars have debated what the Enarees actually were: people with intersex conditions, transgender individuals in Scythian society, ritual specialists who adopted a gender-liminal role, or some combination of these. The ancient sources do not permit certainty.

What is clear is their function. They divined. Herodotus imagines them braiding and unbraiding strips of lime bark with their fingers while they prophesied.17 In his account, the people set apart by gender were also the people thought able to see farther than everyone else.

That link between sacred power and gender crossing would return, louder and bloodier, in the cult of Cybele.

The Day of Blood

In the spring, the liturgical calendar of the Roman Empire included a week of festivals that disturbed even the most cosmopolitan observers.

On March 15, the festival of Canna intrat marked the beginning: a procession of reed-bearers recalled the myth of Attis, the beautiful Phrygian boy beloved by the goddess Cybele, who was driven mad and castrated himself beneath a pine tree. On March 22, the Arbor intrat: a pine tree, wrapped in woollen bands and decorated with violets, was carried into the temple of the Magna Mater on the Palatine Hill, symbolising the dead Attis. On March 24, the Dies Sanguinis, the Day of Blood: the Galli, the castrated priests of Cybele, slashed themselves with knives and whipped themselves in a frenzy of mourning. And on this day, inspired by the music of drums and pipes and the frenzy of the crowd, new initiates performed the irrevocable act.18

Lucian of Samosata, writing in the second century CE about the similar rites at Hierapolis in Syria, describes the moment:

Any young man who has resolved on this action strips off his clothes, and with a loud shout bursts into the midst of the crowd, and picks up a sword from a number which I suppose have been kept ready for many years for this purpose. He takes it and castrates himself and then runs wild through the city, bearing in his hands what he has cut off. He casts it into any house at will, and from this house he receives women’s raiment and ornaments.19

The act was public, irreversible, and identity-making. After the cut, the Gallus put on women’s clothing, let his hair grow, wore cosmetics, and belonged to the goddess.20 The ritual did not merely wound the body. It reassigned the person.

Pausanias preserves the myth that undergirded the ritual. Zeus, falling asleep, let his seed fall upon the ground, and from it grew a hermaphroditic demon called Agdistis, “with two sexual organs, male and female.” The gods, fearing Agdistis, “cut off the male organ.” From the severed member grew an almond tree. A daughter of the river Sangarius ate the fruit and conceived Attis, a boy of more-than-human beauty. “Agdistis fell in love with him.” When Attis was about to marry a mortal princess, Agdistis appeared at the wedding in a jealous rage. “Attis went mad and cut off his genitals, as also did he who was giving him his daughter in marriage.”21

The myth is dense with meaning. Agdistis is a being of both sexes whose male organ is removed by the gods – castration as cosmic order. Attis, born from the removed organ, is castrated in turn by love and madness. The Galli who re-enacted this myth on the Day of Blood were not simply performing a ritual. They were entering a story. They were becoming Attis – dying to masculinity, being reborn as something else, something that belonged wholly to the divine feminine.

Pausanias preserves another version too: Attis was a eunuch from birth.22 In that telling there is no frantic cut beneath a tree, only a life that begins already outside the usual categories. The myth itself keeps shifting, as if ancient audiences could not decide whether such a body had to be inflicted or might simply exist.

The connection between Cybele, Dionysus, and gender transgression runs deep in Greek literature. Euripides’ Bacchae, written around 405 BCE, opens with a choral ode that links the two cults explicitly:

Blessed is he who, being fortunate and knowing the rites of the gods, keeps his life pure and has his soul initiated into the Bacchic revels, dancing in inspired frenzy over the mountains with holy purifications, and who, revering the mysteries of great mother Kybele, brandishing the thyrsos, garlanded with ivy, serves Dionysus.23

The Bacchae presents Dionysus himself as a figure of gender ambiguity. When Pentheus, the king of Thebes, confronts the god, he sees a being with long curls, pale skin, and a feminine face – a figure who defies the categories that Pentheus is desperate to enforce. The god responds by dressing Pentheus in women’s clothing, transforming the hyper-masculine king into a spectacle of femininity before leading him to his death.24

The play’s central terror is not violence but dissolution – the dissolution of the boundary between male and female, between civilised and wild, between human and divine. Dionysiac religion, like the cult of Cybele, proposed that the sacred could be reached through the abandonment of ordinary gender. The maenad who dances on the mountain has left her loom and her husband’s house. The Gallus who castrates himself has left the world of men entirely. Both have crossed over into a space where the ordinary rules do not apply.

But the Greek city never sat easily with this crossing. In Euripides, sacred gender disorder is both ecstasy and terror. The same play that praises the rites ends with a king torn apart.

The Favorite

If Hermotimus represents the eunuch as avenger, and the Galli represent the eunuch as sacred vessel, the figure of Bagoas represents the eunuch as political actor – and as object of desire.

Bagoas was a Persian eunuch who served first in the court of Darius III and then, after the Persian king’s defeat and murder in 330 BCE, passed into the household of Alexander the Great. The historical record is fragmentary and contested. Curtius Rufus, writing in the first century CE, gives Bagoas a substantial role in Alexander’s court, describing him as the king’s favourite and depicting him as a figure of considerable political influence.25

Plutarch provides the most vivid scene. At a festival, Bagoas competed in song and dance and won the prize:

All in his festal array, he passed through the theatre and took his seat by Alexander’s side; at sight of which the Macedonians clapped their hands and loudly bade the king kiss the victor, until at last he threw his arms about him and kissed him tenderly.26

A eunuch, in women’s costume after a dance performance, sitting beside the most powerful man in the world, kissed in public at the crowd’s demand. The scene is at once political theatre and genuine intimacy. The Macedonians are not scandalised; they are delighted. They demand the kiss. The eunuch’s beauty, his performance, his relationship with the king – all of this is visible, acknowledged, even celebrated.

But other sources tell a darker story of eunuch power at Alexander’s court. When the Persian satrap Orxines refused to pay Bagoas respect, declaring that “it was not Persian custom to pay respect to men used as women,” Bagoas manoeuvred to have him accused of plundering the tomb of Cyrus the Great. Orxines was executed.27 The eunuch’s revenge operated through the channels of court politics, not through the crude symmetry of Hermotimus’s vengeance. Bagoas weaponised his intimacy with power. The man who called him a whore ended up dead.

Athenaeus preserves the gossiping version of the same story: Alexander had taken up Persian habits and was too much under Bagoas’s influence.28 The court eunuch becomes, at once, servant, lover, and explanation for imperial decadence.

The State's Ledger

The Roman Empire, inheriting the traditions of both Greece and the Near East, developed a comprehensive legal system for managing bodies that did not fit the binary. The most revealing document is the Gnomon of the Idios Logos, a papyrus from Roman Egypt preserving administrative regulations compiled after 149 CE.

The Gnomon regulated inheritance, marriage, and property transfer across the complex social hierarchy of Roman Egypt – Romans, Alexandrian citizens, Egyptians, freedmen, and foreigners. Buried among its provisions is a clause that reveals how the state classified eunuchs:

Of registered eunuchs and the impotent, after death, if they die intestate, the total is confiscated; if they make a will, two thirds; but one third goes to whomever they may bequeath among those of the same class.29

Read that again. If a eunuch died without a will, the Roman state seized everything he owned. If he made a will, the state still took two-thirds. He could leave only one-third, and only to people “of the same class” – meaning, presumably, other eunuchs or freedmen of similar status.

The provision is devastating in its implications. Eunuchs existed in Roman Egypt – they were numerous enough to require registration and regulation. They could own property. They could accumulate wealth. But the state treated their property as essentially forfeit. The logic is clear: eunuchs have no legitimate heirs. They cannot marry. They cannot produce children. When they die, their wealth has nowhere natural to go. So the state takes it.

Compare this to the Gnomon’s treatment of other categories. Childless Romans who were unmarried could not inherit from other Romans, but they were not stripped of their own property at death.30 Egyptian women who married foreigners faced reduced inheritance rights, but their children could inherit from both parents.31 The eunuch was in a category by himself – penalised not for what he had done but for what he was.

The Gnomon also required eunuchs to register. Somewhere in Roman Egypt there had to be lists: names, locations, property, bodies reduced to taxable categories.32 This is the paperwork side of contempt. Not insult, not theater, but inventory.

The regulation also hints at inspection, denunciation, and proof. Someone had to decide who counted. Someone had to look.

Not all eunuchs accepted their assigned place in the social order. In the second century CE, a man named Favorinus of Arles became one of the most celebrated intellectuals in the Roman Empire – and he was a eunuch.

Favorinus was born around 80 CE in Gaul. Ancient sources describe him variously as a eunuch and as a hermaphrodite – Philostratus used the latter term, while his rival Polemo of Laodicea described him as “a eunuch born without testicles.”33 Modern scholars have suggested he may have had an intersex condition. Whatever the biological reality, Favorinus lived as a man, competed as a man, and excelled in the most masculine of Roman cultural arenas: the rhetorical display.

Maud Gleason has shown how Favorinus’s feud with Polemo exposes Roman masculinity as something performed and policed rather than merely possessed.34 Polemo reads bodies like indictments: gait, voice, eyes, posture, hands. He wants gender deviance to be visible at a glance.35

Favorinus, by Polemo’s analysis, should have been disqualified from public life. His body was wrong. His voice was wrong. His face was beardless. But Favorinus was not only a successful orator; he was famous for boasting of three paradoxes in his life: “that, being a Gaul, he spoke Greek; that, being a eunuch, he had been tried for adultery; and that, having quarrelled with an emperor, he was still alive.”36 The adultery charge is significant. Someone – a husband, a rival – accused this beardless, high-voiced eunuch of sleeping with a woman. The charge may have been fabricated, but the fact that it was brought at all suggests that Favorinus’s gender presentation did not prevent him from being perceived as a sexual agent.

Favorinus’s career shows the categories straining against a real life. A eunuch could still become famous, desirable, dangerous, and hard to classify.

Three Kinds of Eunuchs

Into this world of Persian court eunuchs, Greek philosophical analysis, Roman legal regulation, and Gallic ecstatic self-castration, a Galilean teacher introduced a new taxonomy.

The Gospel of Matthew, composed in the late first century CE, records Jesus speaking about marriage and divorce. When his disciples protest that the strictness of his teaching makes it “not expedient to marry,” he responds:

For there are eunuchs who were born that way from their mother’s womb, and there are eunuchs who were made eunuchs by men; and there are eunuchs who made themselves eunuchs for the Kingdom of Heaven’s sake. He who is able to receive it, let him receive it.37

Three categories. Born eunuchs – those with congenital conditions that prevented reproduction, the people whom Pausanias’s tradition called “eunuchs from birth.” Made eunuchs – the victims of Panionius, the boys chosen by Persian generals for their beauty, the vast apparatus of forced castration that supplied the courts of the Near East. And voluntary eunuchs – those who chose celibacy or, in some interpretations, self-castration for spiritual reasons. The third category would prove explosive. Early Christian writers debated for centuries whether Jesus was speaking metaphorically of sexual abstinence or literally endorsing self-castration. The church father Origen, according to later tradition, took the passage literally and castrated himself, though scholars have debated whether this story is historical.38

What is striking about the Matthean passage is its inclusiveness. All three categories of eunuch are acknowledged without condemnation. The born eunuch is not cursed. The made eunuch is not pitied. The voluntary eunuch is praised. “He who is able to receive it, let him receive it” – the phrase implies that the eunuch’s condition, far from being a mark of contempt, can be a path to spiritual achievement.

The Ethiopian eunuch of Acts 8 embodies this transformation. He is “a eunuch of great authority under Candace, queen of the Ethiopians, who was over all her treasure” – a court eunuch of the traditional kind, powerful, wealthy, and sexually ambiguous.39 He has travelled to Jerusalem to worship, suggesting Jewish sympathies, but as a eunuch he would have been excluded from the Temple congregation under Deuteronomy 23:1. He sits in his chariot reading Isaiah – specifically the passage about the suffering servant who “was led as a sheep to the slaughter” and whose “generation” – whose posterity, whose descendants – is cut off.40

The eunuch, reading about a man cut off from descendants, asks for baptism and is not refused.3940 The story turns a body marked for exclusion into one welcomed without argument.

What the Knife Cost

We should not romanticise. For every Hermotimus who rose to power, for every Galli priest who found meaning in self-castration, for every Bagoas who kissed the king, there were thousands whose stories ended in the surgery itself.

Pre-modern castration was dangerous. The ancient sources do not dwell on mortality rates, but modern medical history suggests that the death rate for pre-pubescent castration in the absence of anaesthesia and antisepsis could be as high as one in four or one in three.41 The boys chosen by Persian generals after the fall of Ionia – chosen for their beauty, which is to say for their youth and health – did not all survive the selection that was meant to preserve them.

The distinction between forced and voluntary castration matters enormously. The Ionian boys had no choice. The five hundred castrated boys sent annually from Babylon to the Persian king had no choice. The victims of Panionius had no choice. Their castration was an act of violence imposed by conquering armies, slave traders, and imperial administrators. It was motivated by the same logic that drove the taking of the “fairest maidens” – the logic of extraction, of turning human bodies into useful commodities.

The Galli’s self-castration was something entirely different. It was an act of religious commitment, undertaken in ecstasy, at the climax of days of fasting, dancing, and ritual mourning. It was witnessed by crowds. It was celebrated. The new Gallus, running through the streets with his severed genitals in his hand, throwing them into a house to receive women’s clothing in return, was performing a public transformation of identity.42 He was dying to one self and being born to another.

But the voluntariness of the Galli’s act should not be overstated. Lucian’s description suggests that the decision was made in a state of extreme emotional and physical arousal, after days of fasting, flagellation, and ecstatic music. Ancient writers debated what counted as free choice under those conditions. Roman law, increasingly hostile to voluntary castration, would eventually criminalise the practice under Hadrian and his successors.43 The Lex Cornelia de sicariis was extended to cover anyone who castrated another person, with severe penalties. The state drew a line: the body belonged not only to the individual but to the community, and the community had an interest in preserving it intact.

Xenophon’s Cyrus, in yet another episode of the Cyropaedia, encounters the aftermath of castration used as a weapon of personal cruelty rather than imperial policy. Gobryas, a Babylonian noble allied with Cyrus, tells the story of his friend’s son, who was castrated by the Assyrian prince simply because the prince’s concubine had praised the young man’s looks:

He had him seized and castrated; and the occasion, so some people said, was simply because his concubine had praised his friend, remarking how handsome he was.44

A compliment. A woman remarks on a young man’s beauty, and a prince answers by cutting that beauty out of the social world. Castration here is not administration or devotion. It is naked ownership.

The ancient world generated multiple terms for people who did not fit the binary, and the terms were not interchangeable. The eunoukhos was a castrated male – the word literally means “bed-guardian,” from eune (bed) and ekhein (to keep). The kinaidos or Latin cinaedus was a man who was perceived as feminine in deportment and desire, whether or not he was castrated – a category closer to what modern scholars would call gender non-conformity. The hermaphroditos was a person with ambiguous genitalia. The androgynos was a man-woman, a being of mixed gender. The Gallus was a castrated priest. The Enaree was a Scythian seer struck by the goddess with “the female sickness.”

Each term carried different social meanings and different consequences. The eunoukhos was despised but useful. The kinaidos was despised and useless – a figure of pure contempt in Roman invective, representing the failure of masculine self-control.45 The hermaphroditos was a prodigy – a sign from the gods, sometimes ominous, sometimes wondrous. The Gallus was sacred and abject simultaneously – a priest of a state-recognised cult who was nonetheless forbidden from inheriting property under Roman law and excluded from full citizenship.

The Roman physiognomists mapped these categories onto the body with obsessive precision. Polemo’s treatise catalogues the physical signs of the kinaidos: a loose gait, a tilted head, wandering eyes, a soft voice, fingers held apart, knees touching. These signs were understood as legible – as evidence of an inner condition that the body could not conceal.46 Masculinity was not merely a matter of genitalia. It was a matter of performance, and the performance was constantly scrutinised.

This is what Gleason means by “making men.” In the Roman world, masculinity had to be continuously performed. The people who failed that performance, or were said to fail it, exposed how unstable the category always was.

The Paradox

Across a thousand years of evidence, the pattern changes less than one might expect. Xenophon calls eunuchs contemptible. Roman invective made the cinaedus a stock figure of degradation. Physiognomists wrote whole catalogues of bodily failure. Yet courts kept eunuchs close. Persian kings trusted them. Roman emperors relied on chamberlains. The cult of Cybele, adopted by Rome in 204 BCE, depended on castrated priests for its rites.47 Even an insult aimed at Demetrius Poliorcetes drew its force from the fact that eunuchs could hold serious office: the royal treasurer, the gazophylax, was often one of them.48

That combination of contempt and dependence runs through the record. Bagoas at Alexander’s court, Hermotimus taking revenge, eunuchs moving close to the king in the courts of Artaxerxes II: each story carries unease about people treated as degraded and yet placed near power. In other settings the same bodies could be marked as sacred. The Enarees prophesied. The Galli served the Great Mother. The eunuch of the Gospel of Matthew could make himself a eunuch “for the Kingdom of Heaven’s sake.”

The ancient world never settled these meanings into a stable category. Eunuchs could be mocked, trusted, feared, employed, taxed, and revered, sometimes within the same political order. Xenophon’s line captures the tension as clearly as anything in the archive: “Eunuchs are objects of contempt to the rest of mankind,” followed almost at once by the claim that a eunuch might surpass all others in loyalty to his master.49
