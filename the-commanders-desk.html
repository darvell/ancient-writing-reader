The Commander's Desk

Flavius Abinnaeus, Praefectus Alae, Dionysias, 340--351 CE

Roman Army Village Policing Petitions Desert Frontier 4th Century CE Fayum

57 primary documents · 67 notes

The Road to the Fortress

Flavius Abinnaeus arrived at Dionysias with thirty-three years of military service behind him and a letter from the emperor in his hand.

He had enlisted around 303 CE as a ducenarius – a junior cavalry officer – in the vexillatio Parthosagittariorum, a unit of Parthian-style mounted archers stationed at Diospolis, the ancient city of Thebes, deep in Upper Egypt.4 He was probably Syrian by birth. The name Abinnaeus is Semitic, and the Parthian archer units drew heavily from the eastern provinces. He was likely a Christian from the start; his later correspondence is saturated with the language of the church. In 303, when Diocletian’s Great Persecution was reaching its height, that would have been a dangerous thing to be.5

For three decades he served in the south. We know almost nothing about those years. A soldier’s life in the Egyptian garrisons was not the Roman legionary experience of popular imagination – no Hadrian’s Wall, no Rhine frontier charges, no barbarian sieges. It was garrison duty in a landscape of temples and canals, punctuated by the occasional threat from the Blemmyes, a Nubian people who raided Egypt’s southern border with enough regularity to keep the army busy but not enough to make anyone famous.6

Then, around 336, something changed. The comes Senecio – a high military official in the Egyptian command – selected Abinnaeus to escort a delegation of Blemmye ambassadors to Constantinople.7 This was not a small assignment. The Blemmyes had been raiding the Thebaid for generations, and their ambassadors were traveling to the capital of the empire to negotiate with the emperor himself. Abinnaeus was the man trusted to get them there.

He arrived in Constantinople in July 336, in time for Constantine’s tricennalia – the thirtieth anniversary of the emperor’s accession, a spectacular celebration of imperial power.8 There, at the court, he was promoted to the rank of protector – a title that placed him in the emperor’s personal military household, a stepping stone to higher command. The old cavalryman from Thebes had entered the inner circle.

What followed was a period of service that the archive sketches in fragments. He escorted the Blemmye ambassadors home. He spent three years in the Eastern Desert – the harsh, mineral-rich wasteland between the Nile and the Red Sea, where quarries and caravan routes required military protection.9 Then he was assigned to bring recruits from the Thebaid to the court of Constantius II at Hierapolis, probably for the planned campaign against Persia. And there, at Hierapolis, the emperor gave him his appointment: praefectus of the Ala Quinta Praelectorum, the Fifth Cavalry Wing of the Select, stationed at the fortress of Dionysias in the Arsinoite nome.10

An imperial letter of appointment. A cavalry command. A mudbrick fortress at the edge of the desert.

He could not have known what he was walking into.

The Fortress

Dionysias – modern Qasr Qarun – stood at the western tip of the Fayum, where the cultivated land gave way to sand and the caravan route to the Bahariya Oasis began. The fortress itself was Diocletianic: built in the late third century, a rectangle of roughly 90 by 80 meters, with square towers at the corners and semicircular towers along the walls.11 It was mudbrick, like everything in the Fayum. The desert preserved it well enough that its outline can still be traced.

The unit Abinnaeus commanded, the Ala Quinta Praelectorum, was a cavalry wing – in theory, five hundred horsemen, though late Roman units rarely maintained their paper strength.12 The Notitia Dignitatum, the imperial register of offices and units compiled a generation later, lists the unit at Dionysias under the command of the dux Aegypti, the military duke of Egypt.13 Abinnaeus reported to the dux. The dux reported to the emperor. Between the emperor and the shepherds of Theoxenis, the chain of command was shorter than you might think.

The fortress sat at a crossroads. To the east lay the canal-laced farmland of the Fayum, the villages of Hermopolis and Philadelphia and Philagris and Narmouthis, each with its own rhythms of grain and tax and complaint. To the west lay the desert and the oasis route. To the south, Lake Qarun – what remained of the ancient Lake Moeris – spread its shrinking waters across the basin floor. Dionysias controlled the passage between the settled world and the empty one.

But Abinnaeus’s duties had almost nothing to do with the desert.

The Duties of a Praefectus

The Roman military manual prescribed the commander’s routine: keep the troops in camp, maintain discipline, bring the soldiers out for training, keep the keys of the gates, go round the guards, attend the soldiers’ mealtimes and sample the food, inspect the sick quarters, punish offences, hear complaints.14 This was the official version. The archive tells a different story.

Day after day, year after year, what came across Abinnaeus’s desk was not military intelligence or tactical planning. It was petitions. Complaints from villagers about theft. Reports from subordinates about missing soldiers. Letters from friends asking favors. Demands from superiors asking for tax enforcement. Requests from monks asking to borrow hunting nets.

The petitions alone would have consumed a man. They followed a rigid formula, as standardized as a modern police report, and they arrived in a steady stream.15 The address was always the same: Phlaouiō Abinnaiō eparchō eilēs stratiōtōn kastrōn Dionysiadis – “To Flavius Abinnaeus, prefect of the cavalry of the soldiers of the camp of Dionysias.”16 The petitioner identified himself: name, father’s name, village, and – crucially – status. Veteran. Soldier’s wife. Soldier’s daughter-in-law. Deacon. Landowner. The narrative followed: what happened, when, who did it, how the petitioner found out. Then the request: arrest the suspects, compel them to confess, and forward the case to the dux. Always the dux. Because Abinnaeus could arrest, but he could not judge. He was the first link in a chain that led upward to a man in Alexandria or wherever the duke happened to be.

The formula never varied. But the crimes did.

The Sheep-Shearing Summer

The summer of 346 was bad for sheep.

On the fifth of Epeiph – June 29 – Aurelius Aboul, son of Dionysius, from the village of Hermopolis, filed the first petition. His eleven sheep had been shorn in the night. Not stolen. Shorn. The criminals had crept into his fold, cut the wool from eleven animals in the dark, and vanished.17

Aboul had investigated on his own and learned the identity of the ringleader: Paul. A soldier. One of Abinnaeus’s own men.18

The accomplices were worse. Peter, son of Sarapion, and his brother Melas – also a soldier – and Apion, son of Horion. Apion was the village irenarch, the peace officer, the man whose job it was to prevent exactly this kind of crime.19 The cop was in the gang.

Aboul added a postscript: “On another occasion the same persons drove off pigs of mine to the number of six.”20 The same gang. The same pattern. Not a one-time crime but a pattern of organized theft, involving soldiers and the village police, operating with apparent impunity in the territory Abinnaeus was supposed to control.

Six days later, on the eleventh of Epeiph, Aurelia Maria arrived with her petition about the nine shorn sheep and three carried off alive from Theoxenis.21 Different village. Different criminals – Ioannes and Eleias, identified by the field-guards. But the same crime, the same season, the same appeal to the same commander.

Later that year, sometime around the turn of December and January, another petitioner – his name is damaged, the son of Heron, from the village of Hermopolis – reported that ten pigs had been driven off “a considerable time ago.”22 He had tracked down one of the suspects himself, a man named Zoilos son of Diogenes, by searching village to village until he found him.23

And at some point around the same period, Aurelius Ouranios – himself a praepositus, a military officer, a man who outranked most of the soldiers in the camp – reported that while he was walking around his field in Theoxenis, three brothers led by Petros son of Agaon had driven off his sheep, seized his fodder, and when he confronted them, beaten him nearly to death with clubs.24

A military officer. Beaten with clubs. On his own land.

The archive does not record Abinnaeus’s response to any of these petitions. We do not know whether he sent soldiers, whether arrests were made, whether the cases reached the dux. What we know is that the petitions survived in his files. He kept them. Perhaps that is all he could do.

The Night Brigands

The phrase appears again and again: lēstrikō tropō – “in the manner of brigands.”25 It was a legal term, a formula that elevated a theft or assault from a civil dispute to a criminal matter worthy of the dux’s attention. But it also described a reality. The crimes in Abinnaeus’s archive happened at night. They happened by stealth. They happened to people in their homes.

On the twenty-fifth of Pauni, 343, Flavius Priscus – an honorably discharged veteran – and Alia, wife of the soldier Heron, were robbed. Priscus was away from home. Criminals came at night, tunneled into his house, and took everything that belonged to him and to Alia.26 The Greek is precise: they dioruxantes – dug through – the wall. Mudbrick houses could be breached with a shovel and an hour’s work. Priscus asked Abinnaeus to arrest the village dēmosioi – the public servants, the local officials responsible for security – and compel them to hand over the perpetrators.27 When the police cannot protect you, you arrest the police.

On the first of May 346, Flavius Aunes – another honorably discharged veteran, another landowner in Hermopolis – reported the same crime: house broken into at night, lēstrikō tropō, everything stolen.28 The same solution: arrest the irenarch and the village police, compel them to identify the criminals.

On the twenty-sixth of August 346, Aurelia Ataris – daughter of the veteran Melas, a landowner in Hermopolis – told a worse story. She had gone to collect a debt. Poleion, together with Apion the son of the irenarch Horion and Poleion’s sister Kyriake, had locked her in a house and beaten her so severely that she “threw herself down from above” to escape.29 She now “lies near death” – parameneō klinērei.30 The son of the peace officer, again. The same family, perhaps, as the Apion son of Horion who had helped shear Aurelius Aboul’s sheep.

And on the thirtieth of July 346, Aurelius Anteus from Narmouthis addressed a more general complaint to Abinnaeus. He wrote about tas epi tōn topōn ginomenas harpagas kai pleonexias hypo tōn dynatōterōn anthrōpōn – “the seizures and greedy acts happening in our area by the more powerful men.”31 Not a specific crime. A condition. The strong preying on the weak, and the commander of the local garrison as the only authority anyone could turn to.

The Commander Under Pressure

Abinnaeus was not invulnerable. The archive contains a letter that makes this clear.

Chairemon – perhaps a member of the city council of Arsinoe, perhaps a local notable – wrote to Abinnaeus in language that would have made any modern official reach for a lawyer: “You are not doing well acting in this way, but you risk being arrested as a criminal.”32

The charge was specific. Abinnaeus had withdrawn soldiers from the village of Theoxenis. In their absence, a house had been robbed and animals driven off. Chairemon warned him: if you do not send the soldiers back, the city council will report you to the dux.33

A cavalry commander, threatened with arrest. Not by the barbarians, not by the enemy, but by the civilian authorities whose villages he was supposed to protect. The letter reveals the tension at the heart of Abinnaeus’s position. He commanded soldiers, but he served civilians. He had military authority, but he was answerable to a civilian chain that ran from the village councils through the dux to the emperor. When he pulled his men out of a village – for training, for another assignment, for any reason – the village was exposed, and the villagers knew exactly whom to blame.

We do not know why Abinnaeus had withdrawn the soldiers. Perhaps he was short-staffed. The archive suggests he was. One report describes a subordinate spending three days in the village of Karanis trying to extract a single recruit, only to have the entire village refuse to let anyone leave. They detained the irenarch. They offered two nomismata – gold coins – and fifty talents of silver as a bribe or a bargain, anything to keep their men.34 Recruiting was a war of attrition fought village by village, and the villages were winning.

Another report catalogs the problem of absenteeism. Didymus could not come because he was in financial difficulty, having borrowed three gold coins from soldiers. Gerontios was found at the village of his wife. Theodoros was found detained somewhere.35 The soldiers of the Ala Quinta Praelectorum were not, it seems, spending their time in the fortress. They were scattered across the Fayum, entangled in debts and marriages and local disputes, as rooted in village life as the civilians they were supposed to control.

The Christians and the Commander

Abinnaeus moved in a world where Christianity was no longer persecuted but not yet settled. Constantine had legalized the faith in 313. His son Constantius II, who reigned during Abinnaeus’s command at Dionysias, was an Arian – a follower of the theological position that Christ was subordinate to God the Father, not co-equal with him. The bishops of Egypt, led by Athanasius of Alexandria, were overwhelmingly Nicene – believers in the full divinity of Christ. The conflict between these positions was not abstract. It determined who held church offices, who controlled church property, and who was exiled or restored by imperial order.36

Abinnaeus was almost certainly a Nicene Christian. His correspondents addressed him in the language of the church. The monk Apa Mios called him “beloved brother” and wrote “in the Lord God” with prayers for divine protection.37 A priest named Kaor, the “papas” of Hermopolis, addressed him as “my lord brother Abinnaeus” and sent greetings to the children.38 An anonymous correspondent quoted scripture – “whoever gives a cup of water to one of these little ones shall not lose his reward” – and asked Abinnaeus to protect a widow’s son who had been enlisted for military service.39

In 344, the comes Valacius dismissed Abinnaeus from his command.40 The archive does not give the reason explicitly. But Valacius was known as a supporter of the Arian party and a persecutor of Athanasius’s followers. The timing is suggestive. Perhaps Abinnaeus was dismissed for his religious loyalties. Perhaps it was a matter of administrative convenience. We do not know. What we know is that he appealed.

He traveled to the imperial court. On the way, the prytanis of Arsinoe – Aurelius Eulogius son of Andrew, the president of the city senate – gave him a power of attorney, commissioning Abinnaeus to obtain an official appointment as exactor (tax collector) while he was at court.41 The document is dated to the first of February, 345. Even in disgrace, even dismissed from his command, Abinnaeus was the kind of man the city elite trusted with their business at court. A man with connections. A man who knew how to navigate the imperial bureaucracy.

By the first of May 346, he was back.42 The petitions resumed. The sheep were shorn. The pigs were stolen. The commander was at his desk.

The Letters

Not everything on the desk was a complaint. Some of it was the texture of ordinary life – the favors and requests and greetings that connected a garrison commander to the world beyond his walls.

Apa Mios, the monk, wrote at least three letters to Abinnaeus. In one, he asked to borrow hunting nets – signa lina – because gazelles were destroying his crops, and he sent a pig skin as a gift.43 In another, he wrote about sending wine jars carried by two or three camels. He asked Abinnaeus to pay the camel-drivers immediately because it was a feast day and they needed to return quickly. He also mentioned sending dough for bread.44 The monk was not a hermit in a cave. He was a farmer and trader, connected to military patronage, exchanging goods across the Fayum by camel caravan.

Aetis – possibly Abinnaeus’s property manager – wrote about accounts that no one had paid, about hay that could not be found (Peeieou and Eulogeios said they had none but could substitute wheat), about a vineyard that had been planted on land the villagers claimed was communal, and about sacks needed for loading grain.45 The letter is a snapshot of the small, grinding logistics of a landowner’s life in the Fayum: collect the debts, find the hay, resolve the boundary dispute, load the grain.

Klematios wrote asking Abinnaeus to grant a soldier named Ision leave to attend to Klematios’s business affairs. The justification was a chain of family connections: Ision was the father-in-law of Timothy the veteran, who was the brother of Paul the cornicularius – the staff officer – of the camp.46 Everyone was connected to everyone. Every favor request came with a genealogy.

And then there was the letter from the mother of Moses.

She did not give her own name. She was “the mother of Moses,” and she addressed Abinnaeus as “my master and patron, praepositus of the camp of Dionysias.”47 She reported that Heron had been sent away with the “barbarians” – frontier service, perhaps, or escort duty with foreign troops. She asked for a few days’ leave for her servant Athioein. And then this sentence, which has survived seventeen centuries in the sand:

Hai gar pente hēmerai holos eniautous eisin – “For the five days are a whole year.”48

Five days felt like a year. A small, precise measure of loneliness, dictated by a woman whose name we do not know, addressed to a military commander who may or may not have granted the request. The papyrus does not tell us. It tells us only that she asked, and that she ended with the formula that recurs through the archive like a prayer: “After God, we have no helper but you.”49

The Foster-Child and the Deacon

Two letters from the margins of Abinnaeus’s household illuminate the patron-client relationships that structured late Roman society as thoroughly as any law.

Palas addressed Abinnaeus as “master of my soul and my ruler, my praepositus.”50 He was Abinnaeus’s threptos – his foster-child, a dependent raised in the commander’s household, bound by ties of obligation and affection that were not quite family and not quite servitude. Palas asked for a few sheep for his wife, “so she too may have a little income.” He closed with a pledge: “I am your slave and will not leave you.”51 The word “slave” – doulos – was not literal. It was the language of dependence, the vocabulary of a man who owed everything to his patron and knew it.

At the other end of the social spectrum, the last dated document in the entire archive – the eleventh of February, 351 – comes from Aurelius Heron, a deacon of the catholic church in the village of Berenikis.52 A man named Euporos, son of Hermias, from Philagris, had broken into his house lēstrikō tropō and seized all his clothing.53 The deacon could produce evidence. He wanted Abinnaeus to arrest the thief and send the case to the dux.

The formula is identical to every other petition in the archive. The crime is identical. The language is identical. But the petitioner identified himself as diakonos tēs katholikēs ekklēsias – “deacon of the catholic church.”54 In the year 351, in a village in the Fayum, a Christian clergyman was robbed of his clothes and turned to a cavalry commander for justice, using exactly the same procedure that any shepherd or veteran would use. The church had not replaced the state. It had simply added another category of petitioner to the queue.

The Economy of the Desk

Buried among the petitions and letters are the administrative lists – the grain accounts, the tax assessments, the military rosters – that reveal the economic infrastructure of Abinnaeus’s command.

A grain account from the village of Andromachis lists names and amounts in artabas: Euporos, Kyrillos, Helles, Agathos, Sarmates, Melas, and dozens of others, each owing a specific quantity of wheat or barley.55 A barley collection account records the eispraksis krithēs – “collection of barley” – assessed on villagers by name and amount in talents.56 A boat-tax assessment lists the naubia – levies for river transport – by individual and amount.57

A wine account is more revealing. It lists distributions of spathia and keramia – wine measures and jars – to soldiers, veterans, and officials. It names Kampol, Kannas, Hatres, Polion the decurion, and “the praepositus himself” – Abinnaeus.58 The commander received his allocation alongside his men. The account also records a pig and fleeces, mentions Libyans, a recruit named Melas, and a veteran named Paul son of Kapiton. Wine, wool, and pork: the currency of garrison life.

A military roster lists soldiers by name and rank: the decurions Moses and Paesis, and rank-and-file men with Egyptian, Greek, and Roman names – Petronios, Neilammon, Hatres – alongside their grain allocations.59 The roster is thin. The ala was supposed to be five hundred strong. The lists suggest something considerably smaller.

And there is one commercial transaction that survives: Flavius Elias, a soldier of the camp of Dionysias, sold cattle to Flavius Abinnaeus.60 The commander was buying livestock from his own soldiers. The line between military authority and private commerce was, at best, blurry.

The Dismissal, the Appeal, the Return

The interruption of Abinnaeus’s command in 344 is the most dramatic episode in the archive, but the archive itself treats it obliquely. We have no document recording the dismissal. We have no copy of the appeal. What we have are the documents from the edges: the power of attorney from the prytanis, dated to the period of exile, which tells us Abinnaeus was traveling to the imperial court; and the resumption of petitions in 346, which tells us he came back.61

The comes Valacius, who dismissed him, was a figure of some notoriety in the ecclesiastical sources. Athanasius of Alexandria, in his polemical writings, names Valacius as a persecutor of Christians – specifically, of those who supported the Nicene creed against the Arian position favored by Constantius II.62 If Abinnaeus was a Nicene sympathizer – and his correspondence with monks and priests suggests he was – then his dismissal fits a pattern of religious purges in the Egyptian military command during the 340s.

But we should be careful. The archive does not say this. It preserves facts and silences, and the silences are not evidence. Abinnaeus may have been dismissed for religious loyalties, for incompetence, for corruption, or for a personal grievance we cannot recover. The religious explanation is plausible. It is not proven.

What is clear is that Abinnaeus had the resources and the connections to appeal directly to the emperor, and that the appeal succeeded. By 346 he was back at his desk, receiving petitions, and he would remain there until at least February 351 – the date of Aurelius Heron’s petition, the last document in the archive that can be securely dated.63

After that, silence. He retired, probably to Philadelphia, the town where the archive was eventually found.64 Whether he died there, and when, and whether anyone petitioned on his behalf in his old age, we do not know.

What Survived

The Abinnaeus archive was purchased in 1893 – split between the British Museum and the University of Geneva – and published in 1962 by H.I. Bell, V. Martin, E.G. Turner, and D. van Berchem as The Abinnaeus Archive: Papers of a Roman Officer in the Reign of Constantius II.65 Eighty-two documents. A decade of one man’s professional and personal life, preserved because the Fayum is dry and papyrus does not rot in sand.

What does the archive tell us? Very little about battle. There are no tactical assessments and no dispatches about barbarian movements. There is one reference to the Blemmyes, in the career summary at the beginning of Abinnaeus’s service, decades before Dionysias.66 The frontier fortress at the edge of the desert mostly produced paperwork about stolen pigs.

That is the archive’s revelation. The Roman army in fourth-century Egypt was not primarily a fighting force. It was a police force, a tax-collection agency, a dispute-resolution mechanism, and a patronage network. The commander’s desk was piled with petitions from women whose sheep had been shorn, from veterans whose houses had been tunneled into, from deacons whose clothes had been stolen, from monks who needed hunting nets, from foster-children who wanted sheep for their wives.

The Abinnaeus archive captures what empire looked like in one corner of fourth-century Egypt: the daily work of keeping order in a landscape where everyone knew everyone, where the irenarch could be part of the gang, where soldiers drifted away from camp, where villages resisted the recruiters, and where church, state, family, and market pressed against each other in every dispute.

Meta ton theon, allon ouk echomen boēthon ei mē se. After God, we have no helper but you.67

That was the sentence the mother of Moses wrote to the commander of a mudbrick fortress at the end of the irrigated world. It was, perhaps, the truest description of his job that anyone ever composed.
