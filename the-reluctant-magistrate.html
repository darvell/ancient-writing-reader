The Reluctant Magistrate

Achilles of Hermopolis and the Office He Tried to Refuse, 192 CE

Hermopolis Magna Civic Office Liturgies Gymnasium Roman Egypt Commodus

1 primary documents · 7 notes

The offer that trapped him

The surviving minutes begin in noise. Bystanders shouted that Achilles should imitate his father, remembered as a brave man and a man who took office readily.1 Achilles replied with a maneuver rather than a refusal. He offered to undertake the office of exegetes instead, provided he could contribute two talents a year and be released from another burden tied to leased land.1

It was a rich man’s counteroffer. Two talents annually was serious money.4 But it was also a mistake.

Olympiodoros, acting as advocate, answered at once. If Achilles wished to be crowned as exegetes, he could pay the entry deposit; otherwise, by putting himself forward for the greater office he had already shown that he could bear the lesser one now required by the city.1 Achilles protested that he was not able to support the cosmetes.1 The advocate’s answer is the line that survives because it did the work: a man who had offered for the greater office ought not to shrink from the lesser.1

The trap was now public and plain. Achilles had tried to choose the burden that suited him. The city wanted the other one.

The city around him

Hermopolis Magna was large, old, and administratively hungry.5 It had quarters, registries, a gymnasium, temples, and the full social apparatus of a nome capital. In 192 it still lacked the city council that Egyptian metropoleis would receive only a few years later under Septimius Severus.6 Until then, much of civic life depended on liturgical service: wealthy men drafted into offices that were both prestigious and financially dangerous.36

That setting matters because Achilles was not being singled out by a bizarre accident. He was being drawn into a system that needed his money as much as his name. Civic office in Roman Egypt was one of the ways a city converted private wealth into public functioning.3

The office he wanted, exegetes, ranked high and dealt in law and guardianship.6 The office he was being pushed toward, cosmetes, kept a city’s elite youth in motion. Both were burdens. One was easier to accept in public.

The crowning

The meeting moved to the Caesareum, where the current and former cosmetae assembled.1 A man named Diogenes argued that Achilles could not simply nominate himself for exegetes because imperial rules required three exegetae-designate for that office.1 The legal ground kept narrowing.

Then an ex-cosmetes named Aspidas son of Hermas stepped forward and said the words that ended the matter: on his own responsibility, he crowned Achilles as cosmetes.1

That phrase mattered. Aspidas was not offering applause. He was guaranteeing performance. If Achilles failed, the risk could rebound onto the man who had put him forward.7 The city advocate declared that the office was now secured for the city, and the strategos ordered the proceedings entered into the minutes.1

The argument was over.

What the paper keeps

We do not know how he served. We do not know whether he managed the expense well, whether he hated every day of the office, or whether he later became the sort of man whose own name was used to shame a reluctant successor.

The papyrus keeps something harder and more useful than that. It shows a civic system working in the open, with shouting bystanders, legal formulas, appeals to a father’s reputation, and one wealthy man discovering that public honor was something a city could force on him.13

The case survives because someone copied the minutes, filed them, and the copy endured. What remains most vividly in that file is not the dignity of office, but resistance. Achilles tried to choose his own burden. Hermopolis chose for him.

  1. P.Ryl. 2.77 (corpus ID 37841), dated 31 October 192 CE. Minutes from Hermopolis Magna recording Achilles son of Nearchidas also called Cornelius, the crowd’s intervention, Achilles’s offer to serve as exegetes, the advocate’s reply, and the final crowning by Aspidas. [source 37841]
  2. On the office of cosmetes in Roman Egypt and its connection to the ephebes and gymnasium, see A.K. Bowman, “The Town Councils of Roman Egypt,” American Studies in Papyrology 11 (1971), and related discussions of metropolitan liturgies.
  3. Naphtali Lewis, The Compulsory Public Services of Roman Egypt, 2nd ed. (1997), remains the standard account of liturgical office as a financial burden imposed on local elites.
  4. For rough wage comparison in Roman Egypt, see Walter Scheidel, “Real Wages in Roman Egypt,” working paper (2008). The point here is scale: two talents annually was an elite-level commitment, not a token offer.
  5. On Hermopolis Magna as a nome capital with major temples, gymnasium culture, and quarter-based administration, see R.S. Bagnall, Egypt in Late Antiquity (1993), and general topographical discussions of Hermopolis.
  6. Bowman, “The Town Councils of Roman Egypt,” on the pre-council government of Egyptian metropoleis and the later Severan reform.
  7. The language of “crowning” on personal responsibility in P.Ryl. 2.77 indicates more than ceremonial approval; it is a guarantee that the office is secured and will be carried through.
