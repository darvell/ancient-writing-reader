Neoteros, Asklepiades, and Theodora in Egypt, 6 CE-700 CE

The Question at Karanis

That local setting matters because the journey to Alexandria was real work.4 It meant leaving the Fayum, finding transport, carrying the complaint to a larger center, and paying what the move required. Neoteros wanted the god’s answer before he took that risk.

Egyptian oracle questions were usually handled through a recognisable procedure.7 The petitioner wrote a question, or had it written, on a small slip of papyrus. In some shrines both possible answers were prepared in advance, one favorable and one unfavorable. The slips were set before the god. One was selected and returned.8 The chosen scrap could then be kept in the hand, carried home, and shown if needed. A few such slips still survive: folded, stored, lost, or left behind.

How the God Answered

Neoteros’s papyrus may preserve only the question, but it belongs to that world of handled documents. It forces the trouble into a sentence short enough for a god to answer and a petitioner to keep.

The wording is narrow. Neoteros does not ask for justice in general terms. He asks whether he should go to Alexandria. The paper does not solve the case by itself. It fixes the decision in a form that can be returned, carried away, and obeyed.

Asklepiades and Tapetheus

One earlier ticket from the same region shows the same habit at a sharper domestic angle. On 26 April 6 CE, a man named Asklepiades son of Areios addressed the crocodile god Soknopaios with a marriage question.9

If it is not granted to me to marry Tapetheus the daughter of Marres, and she may not become my wife, show it and confirm to me this writing. In the past Tapetheus was the wife of Horion.9

This is a marriage question with names attached all the way through. Asklepiades wants Tapetheus and no one else. Tapetheus had already been the wife of Horion. That may mean widowhood. It may mean divorce. Either way, the old marriage had to be named because it affected the next one, and Asklepiades wanted the god’s ruling before he went farther.

Read beside Neoteros’s ticket, the Asklepiades papyrus shows the same habit at a different pitch. One man asks whether he should spend money and take the risk of going to Alexandria.3 Another asks whether a specific marriage should go forward.9 In both cases the decision has already been cut down to the point where a returned scrap can change what happens next.

The Shrine at Dios

Far from the Fayum, in the Eastern Desert, Roman soldiers used a different but related system. At the praesidium of Dios, a fortified stop on the road from Coptos to Berenike, excavators found a shrine with oracle texts written on ostraca and buried in the podium fill.10 These were not individual questions like Neoteros’s. They were pre-written answers, numbered and ready to be drawn.

That find matters because it preserves not one petitioner’s uncertainty but the shrine’s own stock of answers. The ostraca sat inside a military station on a desert road where men were always waiting for orders, escorts, news, and safe passage. A soldier did not need to compose a fresh complaint or marriage query. He needed a sentence he could draw quickly and act on before the road or the sea turned against him.

Some are practical encouragement:

Open the gates that are easy to loose. You have passable paths. Proceed on the road before you.11

Some are warnings:

What you are attempting in your heart, do not do, knowing that it is not expedient.12

And some speak directly to the fear of travel:

You will see your companion. Do not fear the waves of the sea. Do not suspect the storm. Nothing hard shall befall you.13

The soldiers at Dios did not need to frame a fresh question each time. The shrine had answers waiting. Yet the use stays close to Neoteros’s world. Men on a hard road, far from home, wanted a sentence strong enough to end hesitation. Even at Xeron Pelagos, another desert post, a surviving ticket could carry threat in the label alone: one lot is simply “of a chthonian daimon.”14

The Christian Question

The old habit survived the change of gods. A later Greek oracle question from Egypt, written sometime between 501 and 700 CE, asks:

O God of the Christians, should we give Theodora, your servant, to Joseph?15

The wording is closer to Asklepiades than to Neoteros. Marriage is still the issue. Someone wants the sentence fixed in writing before the household commits itself.

By then Christian lot books circulated in Egypt as well. The best known is the Gospel of the Lots of Mary, a book of pre-written answers to be drawn after prayer.16 Some responses are direct:

Go, fulfill your vow, for this is from God.17

Others delay action:

This matter that you want to do: its time has not yet come.18

Churchmen attacked such practices often enough to show that they continued.1920 The names had changed since Neoteros stood before Soxis Pnepheros. The written answer had not disappeared.

The Unanswered Half

Neoteros’s answer is gone. Asklepiades’s answer is gone. The answer for Theodora and Joseph is gone. The moment before the choice hardens into action is what remains.

Neoteros has not yet boarded the boat to Alexandria. Asklepiades has not yet married Tapetheus. Theodora has not yet been given to Joseph. Each document stops at the same point: the question has been written down, but the future has not yet declared itself.

Neoteros’s scrap stays close because it is small, local, and incomplete. It keeps the expense, the uncertainty, and the trip to Alexandria in view, then breaks off. Perhaps he carried the answer north. Perhaps the god kept him in the Fayum. The archive kept only the question.
