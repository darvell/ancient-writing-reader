The Grain Ledger of Kametis

Kametis son of Psenchonsis, Thebes, c. 104--251 CE

Thebes grain taxes ostraca Roman Egypt fiscal history household economy women and property granaries

77 primary documents · 34 notes

The House That Is Measured in Artabas

The first recoverable traces are in Thebes granary books, not household archives. In one register, a Psenchonsis son of Psenchonsis has 15 artabas measured into the metropolis granary in year 8 of Trajan.4 Two years later, Psenchonsis again appears as a measured person in the same fiscal routine.5 By 119-123 CE, Kametis son of Psenchonsis appears explicitly, in villages and in the metropolis, repeatedly, in small increments tied to named brokers and farmers.6 That sequence gives us the secure core of the dossier: a father generation visible in the granary books, then Kametis himself.

The recurring format matters: almost every payment is mediated through someone else – Kollouthos son of Panameus,7 then through Melanion son of Psenchonsis, then through Esoueris and other intermediaries. This is not necessarily weakness. In a tax world, representation is routine: landholders route obligations through agents, clerks route totals into district ledgers, and accountability is built through signatures on the margin by people like Phil and Apollonios.8 The amounts are often modest by modern standards – one-third, one-half, one and one-half artabas – but each entry is a slice of a recurring annual share.

A Family in the Grain Room

By 125 CE, we can see Kametis and people explicitly linked to him in the documents: Senchonsis daughter of Kametis appears moving 22 1/6 artabas for the upland quarter, while Kametis is elsewhere in the same season for smaller household shares.9 In one list, Thaesis daughter of Kametis daughter of Phaeris daughter of Maieuris appears as a taxpayer in grain accounting as well.10 That is secure evidence that women identified through Kametis entered the same fiscal stream. It is not yet proof of a single continuous household.

Here the identity problem begins. The family names repeat in patterns that may indicate a single line with daughters, a collateral branch, or both. The documents never say this was Kametis’ mother or this was his sister. The most that can be said securely is that women carrying the stem “daughter of Kametis” appear in the same fiscal channels. The step from that pattern to a reconstructed household remains inferential.12

The Quarter Circuit: Agora, Charax, South

The grain records are also territorial. Kametis’ name turns up in delivery notes on behalf of the Metropolis, Charax, Agora, the South quarter, and sometimes “the upland.”13 In Epeiph 122, he is recorded in the villages for three-and-a-half artabas and then paired with signatures for different quarters.14 That territorial spread belongs to the secure dossier. Later Antonine entries place other Kametis variants and branch names such as Pikois, Pikotos, and Psen- forms in the same granary system.15 Those later recurrences may illuminate the same family network, but they no longer identify the same individual with confidence.

Beyond Wheat: Guard Posts, Baths, and Watch Fees

Only a narrow slice of Kametis’ life is direct grain accounting, but the same onomastic cluster appears in taxes on movement and infrastructure. Imouthes and associates collect lookout-post and Agorai taxes from Psenchonsis and his brother for year 18 of Trajan.16 In 118 CE, another collector records bath-tax for Psenchonsis son of Petemenophis.17 In the same year, river-guard post taxes are paid on behalf of Epaphras son of Psenchonsis.18

That trio is revealing at the level of the broader name cluster: the same names appear in grain and non-grain fiscal loops, suggesting an integrated municipal burden rather than a one-off crop incident. It does not prove that Kametis himself paid every such levy. It does show the administrative world in which his dossier belongs.19

The Markets List and the Tax-Farmers

The market-list entries sharpen that picture. At least one long fiscal list records Kametis son of Psenchonsis owing 10 drachmas 3 1/2 obols for the Markets and his daughter Senchonsis owing 1 drachma 5 obols as related branch.20 That format – “of the Markets,” with names grouped and amounts in drachmas/obols – differs from granary entries, but it uses the same naming economy: patronymic chain, amount, witness signature.21 In another market-like context, Panameus and others, many sharing the broader naming network, transact around guard and departure deficits.22 These records extend the administrative setting securely only where Kametis or his daughter are named; beyond that they provide context, not biography.

The practical lesson is that one family may enter the archive under several fiscal genres, and the historian must carry names across genres before asking what any one person “did.”

The Legal Form of Delegation

A recurring element across the records is delegation. Kametis is often in the name, while the farmer, collector, or scribe is someone else. Porieuthes appears through Senchonsis for penalties; Melanion appears as mediator for Esoueris/Kametis-linked entries; Kollouthos appears for large Kametis deliveries.23 In another doc, Amonios certifies house-rental transport in related quarter accounts tied to descendants of this onomastic cluster.24 The documents do not say “agent.” They assume it, because in a tax bureaucracy with many offices, proxies are routine infrastructure.

How Long a Name Travels

If you follow strict patronymics, one might expect only a bounded life span for Kametis son of Psenchonsis. In practice, the name persists in shifted forms across several decades. By the 150s and 160s, records note Kametis linked to branches like Psenchonsis of Pikois, Kametis son of Nonnos, and Kametis son of Pikotos in chaff or quarter entries.25 By the 180s and 190s, Kametis son of Pikois appears in Charax and Agora deliveries, and names like Thaesis and Senchon daughters continue in similar fiscal channels.26 At that point the story has moved from a dossier of one man to a recurrence pattern within a family naming pool.

This is continuity under uncertainty. We cannot prove these later Kametis are the same individual; the safest claim is that a family cluster using the same names appears in the same kinds of obligations over at least two generations.27 Flattening all of them into one person overstates certainty. Separating them absolutely would hide the persistence of the fiscal network.

The Long Tail into the Third Century

The later entries show that the network does not vanish when names become ambiguous. Through 180s records in the Commodus years and into the years of the Severan emperors, people linked to this onomastic set still appear in deliveries, deficits, and shortfall settlements for granaries of Theben.28 A late record still credits Kametis with textile credit language from a master-weaver context and another records Psensenpamonthes son of Kametis in a tax list for drachmas,29 while Psenamounis son of Psenchonsis appears in early Severan granary measurement lists as one of the last stable anchors in the chain.30

This is not one uninterrupted autobiography but a lineage footprint.31 From the archive’s perspective, that matters in its own right: names, kin terms, and recurring obligations survive even when biography does not.

Limits of the Record

The ledgers never open far enough to show us Kametis’ doorway. They do not tell us where his fields sat, whether he was literate, how large his household was, or whether Senchonsis and Thaesis were daughters, nieces, or some collateral branch that happened to inherit the same burdens.32 They do not tell us whether the branch with Kametis son of Pikotos was a cousin or a namesake generation removed. The archive preserves the tax shape before the family shape.33

We do know enough to see a social reality: by the second century, and especially in the secure 119-129 core, Kametis son of Psenchonsis stood within a Theban fiscal environment of grain deliveries, quarter accounts, market tolls, and proxy administration.34 Beyond that core, the archive points to a wider network in which names linked to Kametis continued to appear. The archive rarely gives us voices. It gives us obligations, and those obligations are enough to show that this was not a marginal position.
