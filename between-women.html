In Didyma's House

The child is the formal subject of the agreement, but the force of the document falls on the relation between the two women.1 Isidora must trust Didyma enough to leave the infant in her house. Didyma must accept inspection, waiting, and limits on sex and pregnancy in exchange for pay. The contract treats milk as labor, but it also treats the nurse’s whole domestic life as something the child’s mother may regulate.1

That is what makes the papyrus so revealing. Roman Egyptian documents often show women as daughters in dowry arrangements, wives in marriage receipts, or widows in inheritance disputes. Here one woman’s body is temporarily requisitioned for another woman’s child, and the arrangement has to be precise because too much depends on it.16 If Didyma breaks the terms, the contract can punish her. If Isidora suspects neglect, the contract gives her the right to inspect. It is an intimate relation drafted in the language of enforcement.

Paid care, inspection rights, and bodily discipline all sit on the same sheet of papyrus.1 The contract makes Didyma’s house part of Isidora’s household business. For sixteen months the two women are tied together by feeding, visits, penalties, and the condition of a single child.

The House at Cercemunis

In 76 CE, Sieapis, daughter of Sarapion son of Apollonios, registered property in the village of Cercemunis. The return is a tax document, not a memoir, and in the dry language of declaration Sieapis records that the house belongs to her and to her sister Thais.2

The return is dry. That dryness is the point. Officials wanted taxable facts, not domestic feeling. But a house held jointly by two women is still a strong kind of fact. It means shared liability, shared standing, and probably some form of shared domestic plan. It means taxes, repairs, neighbors, and legal exposure were not arranged around a husband in this case, but around two women named on the same declaration.2 If women built their lives around one another in ways that never touched property, tax, nursing, guardianship, or inheritance, the papyri had little reason to remember them. A jointly held house is one of the ways such closeness enters the record at all.25

That house belongs beside Didyma’s rooms. One papyrus binds two women through milk and visits. Another binds two women through walls, taxes, and title.

To My Sister, Mistress Dionysia

In one late third-century note from Oxyrhynchus, Pathermouthis writes to “my sister, mistress Dionysia.” She cannot come herself; Dionysia is to see whether registration can be done and, if so, to do it for “me and Patas.”3 The texture is all in the formulae: the address, the explanation of absence, the quiet assumption that Dionysia will understand both the errand and the stakes.

That is a small request only if one ignores what it presumes. Dionysia can move in the writer’s place. She can deal with officials. She is expected to act quickly and correctly. The address “sister” may be literal kinship, or a formula of closeness, or both. It cannot safely be made to say more than the letter says. But it does mark a relation strong enough to carry bureaucratic risk and practical trust.35 In these archives, that matters. A missed registration could cost money, delay a claim, or leave a household exposed. Dionysia is the person Pathermouthis trusts to stand in front of that trouble.

Eirene and Asphaleia

Another fourth-century note narrows the scale again.4 Eirene writes to her sister Asphaleia, sends greetings to parents and brothers, adds “Maria greets you,” and asks her to buy cumin, and more if possible.4 That is nearly all the note contains, which is why the note feels real. It assumes shared errands, shared kin, and a household economy close enough that one woman’s spice request can be folded into another woman’s day.

Such letters survive because errands matter. Someone knows whom to ask. Someone expects the request to be carried out. Someone’s greeting is worth passing on. Cumin is a small thing, but it belongs to cooking, care, and repeated household contact; people ask for such purchases from those who are already woven into their daily obligations.4 The archive of women’s relations in Egypt is built from these small acts: registration handled for another household, spices bought for another kitchen, a house held together, a child fed in another woman’s rooms.234

The Papers Together

Taken together, these papers keep women in view as each other’s bodily substitutes, property partners, trusted intermediaries, and routine providers of care.1234

Isidora and Didyma remain at the center because theirs is the most demanding of these ties. Their contract is prolonged, domestic, supervised, and built from wage, dependence, and repeated access to another woman’s house and body. The house at Cercemunis and the letters to Dionysia and Asphaleia belong in the same cluster. Work, kinship, property, errands, and care all kept women moving in and out of one another’s rooms, papers, and obligations.12345

Limits of the Record

Did Sieapis and Thais live together in the house they registered? The return does not say.2 Was Dionysia really a sister, or a friend, or some relation between the two that Greek kinship language flattened into one term? The letter does not say.3 Was Eirene’s cumin request ordinary family traffic, or part of a denser and more personal tie? The note does not say.4

And Isidora and Didyma? We know the money, the term, the restrictions, the inspection rights, and the penalties. We do not know whether the baby lived, whether the women liked one another, or whether the months in Didyma’s house produced trust, resentment, grief, or nothing beyond performance of the contract.1

These papyri were written to preserve what could be enforced, taxed, inherited, or checked. A child is left in Didyma’s house. A house in Cercemunis belongs to two sisters. Dionysia is trusted with a registration. Asphaleia is asked for cumin and whatever else she can send. The feeling, if it survives at all, survives inside those obligations.
