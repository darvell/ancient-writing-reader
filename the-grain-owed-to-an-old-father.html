The Grain Owed to an Old Father

Pappos of Ghoran, Grammar Lessons, and Old-Age Support, 222 BCE

Pappos Ghoran Ptolemaic Egypt old age family conflict petitions grain education Arsinoite nome

8 primary documents · 13 notes

The old man's claim

Pappos does not say simply that he is poor and deserves pity. He frames his case as an exchange with a history.

The surviving Greek says that he taught the boy and grammar, διδάξαντος αὐτὸν … καὶ τὴν γραμματικήν.1 The damaged beginning of the sentence probably once named another skill or field of instruction before the grammar is mentioned.1 Even in broken form, the point is clear enough. Pappos was not presenting himself as a helpless dependent who had happened to beget an ungrateful son. He was presenting himself as the man who had made Strouthos into someone.

That matters because grammar in Ptolemaic Egypt cost labor, time, and ambition. A father who could claim to have taught a son grammar was claiming more than ordinary feeding and rearing. He was claiming the work that turned a child into a person who could function in a written world.3

Then comes old age. The text says Pappos became older, γενομένου πρεσβυτέρου.1 The exact surrounding words are damaged, but the sequence is not. Instruction belonged to the earlier phase of life; maintenance belonged to the later one. Pappos’s grievance is that the second half of the bargain has failed.

One artaba and four drachmas

The arrangement itself had administrative shape. Pappos says that through a man named Dioskourides in Arsinoe, Strouthos was to provide him every month one artaba of wheat and four drachmas.1 This was not merely a household conversation. Somebody outside the immediate household had become the witness or channel through which the obligation was recognized.

That detail is what keeps the case from dissolving into family sentiment. The son was not just morally bound. He had been bound in a form a petition could cite.

What did one artaba mean? Exact purchasing power shifted with place and year, but the scale is legible. It was a serious monthly allotment of grain, enough to keep a single adult alive if managed carefully.24 The four drachmas would not make a man comfortable, but they would keep the grain from having to do everything.

That is why the petition feels so contemporary even across more than two millennia. Old age here is not a philosophical state. It is a ration.

What happens when he sees the boy

The petition gets uglier after the financial failure. Pappos says that Strouthos has not given him any of what was agreed, and that whenever he happens to meet him, the son insults him “with the ugliest abuse” λοιδορεῖ με τὰ αἴσχιστα.1

That line is easy to underestimate because it survives in a damaged text. But it changes the case. We are not dealing with silent neglect. We are dealing with a son who has already decided that refusal will be accompanied by humiliation.

The old man does not give us the exact words. The papyrus is too broken, and perhaps the scribe did not think them worth preserving. But the choice to record the abuse matters. Pappos wanted the king’s officials to see not only arrears, but contempt.

This is one of the things petitions do well. They translate private cruelty into a form a government might act on. The cruelty does not have to be grand. It only has to be made legible.

Ghoran and the petition habit

Pappos came from Ghoran in the Arsinoite nome, a village that survives in the petitions not as a peaceful rural backdrop but as a place where people repeatedly tried to drag local injuries into the royal file.567

One petitioner from Ghoran says he is being prevented from building and asks that the accusations raised through his petition be investigated.5 Another, Iollas, complains that he is being wronged over pasture and land and asks the king to have the strategos compel repayment measured in artabas.6 A third text from nearby Ghoran preserves an orphan saying he is being wronged over a paternal holding and that another man is forcibly carrying away the embankment and pressing him with violence.7

Those are not the same dispute as Pappos’s. They do show the same instinct. When local arrangements broke down, people in this district reached for the petition. They named the king. They named the office they wanted activated. They turned grievance into a portable sheet of papyrus and sent it outward.

That helps explain why Pappos’s complaint does not read like a plea to neighbors or kin. It is already past that point. The household has failed as an enforcement mechanism. So he goes to the royal state.

Royal justice and small domestic economies

Ptolemaic petitions often begin at the highest imaginable level and then narrow quickly into something embarrassingly specific. To king Ptolemy, greetings: my son will not give me my grain.1

That scale shift is the whole genre. The state did not necessarily solve these problems, but it provided a channel in which the small could claim attention from the large.8

Nearby petitions from the same years prove how wide the range could be. A woman from Magdola complains that another woman dragged her from a bathtub in Oxyrhyncha and assaulted her there.9 A woman from Trikomia complains that a bath attendant burned her while she was washing.10 Dionysodoros of Ghoran complains that a prison guard detained him over missing records.11

Put beside them, Pappos’s case does not look trivial. It looks exactly of a piece with a society in which everyday conflict had learned to speak upward.

The father who taught grammar

It is tempting to romanticize the educational detail. One can easily start imagining a father building a son’s future through lessons and then waiting to be repaid in old age. The papyrus does not permit that much softness.

What it does permit is a sharper observation. Pappos understood that teaching was a kind of investment and that age was the point at which that investment should return support.1

That logic is not unique to this petition. Family papers from Egypt keep showing that value moved across generations in ways that were formal, negotiated, and often painful.1213 The household was not a shelter from economics. It was one of the places economics bit hardest.

Pappos says he taught Strouthos grammar because he is defining what the son owes. Feeding a child is ordinary. Teaching him letters and grammar belongs to a stronger claim.

We do not know what Strouthos said

The case survives from one side. We do not have Strouthos’s answer, if he gave one. We do not know whether he denied the agreement, pleaded poverty, accused his father of lying, or simply stayed away.

That missing second voice matters. There are possible complications. The son may have had debts. The official channel through Dioskourides may have broken down. The damaged lines in the middle of the petition may once have qualified the story in ways we can no longer recover.1

But the silence of the other side does not make Pappos’s petition weak. It makes it typical. The archive so often preserves the moment of complaint rather than the outcome that the best ending a historian can usually offer is the limit of what the papyrus allows.

Here the limit is still enough to sting. An old man says: I taught the boy. I taught him grammar. He was supposed to send me wheat and cash every month. Now he gives me nothing and curses me when he sees me.1

That is already a whole domestic tragedy, even before the king’s clerk decided whether to care.
