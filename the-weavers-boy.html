The Weaver's Boy

Learning a Trade in Roman Oxyrhynchus, 1st--3rd Century CE

36 primary documents · 106 notes

The boy’s name was Thoonis. His father handed him over on the eighteenth of September, in the thirteenth year of Nero, to a man named Ptolemaeus, son of Pausirion, weaver – and what the papyrus records is the transaction, not the morning.1 We do not know whether Thoonis cried, or went eagerly, or stared at the loom in the corner of Ptolemaeus’s workshop with the blank incomprehension of a child confronting the machine that will consume his next year. We know that his father, Tryphon, signed the contract – or rather, could not sign it, and had someone sign for him.2 We know the terms. We know the penalties. We know what a drachma was worth and what a day’s absence cost. What we do not know is whether the boy looked back.

But the contract survives. And contracts, for all their dryness, are documents of hope – a father’s hope, specifically, that his son will learn what he knows, and perhaps more than he knows, and carry it forward.

The Greek term for what Thoonis was being apprenticed to learn is gerdiake techne – the weaver’s art.3 Not “trade,” not “skill,” but techne: the same word Aristotle used for the knowledge that distinguishes the craftsman from the laborer, the same word Plato used for the arts that shape civilization.4 The weaver himself was a gerdios, and his professional identity was registered, taxed, regulated, and – when necessary – complained about by the state. A gerdios was not a day laborer. He was a man with a recognized craft, a place in the civic order, a tax category. A weaver’s boy, arriving at a loom in a provincial Egyptian town under the emperor who would burn Rome, was being inducted into an art – and into a social identity that would follow him for the rest of his life.

I. The Contract

P.Oxy. 2.275 is not a long document.5 It fits on a single sheet. The handwriting is competent, professional – this was drawn up by someone who wrote contracts for a living, perhaps in the office of the agoranomos, the notary-magistrate who certified legal transactions in Oxyrhynchus.6 The structure follows a formula that had been standardized across centuries of Egyptian practice: identification of the parties, statement of terms, penalty clauses, date, signatures.

The parties: Tryphon, son of Dionysius, son of Tryphon, his mother being Thamounis, daughter of Onnophris. And Ptolemaeus, weaver, son of Pausirion, son of Ptolemaeus, his mother being Ophelous, daughter of Theon. Both inhabitants of the city of Oxyrhynchus.7 The identifications run three generations deep on both sides. In a society without surnames, identity was genealogy. You were your father and your father’s father, and on your mother’s side the chain ran parallel. The bureaucracy needed to know exactly who you were, because it would tax you, conscript you, and hold you to your contracts for the rest of your life.

The terms: Tryphon apprentices his son Thoonis, whose mother is Saraeus, daughter of Apion, “not yet of age,” to Ptolemaeus for one year.8 The boy is to serve and perform all orders given him by Ptolemaeus “in respect of his weaver’s art in all its branches of which Ptolemaeus has knowledge.”9 One year. This was short – remarkably short. Most weaving apprenticeships in Roman Egypt lasted two to five years, with an average of roughly two and a half.10 A century later, another boy named Thonis would be apprenticed for five years to a master named Heraclas, and the contract would spell out a graduated wage scale that assumed no useful productivity for the first two and a half years.11 One year suggests that Thoonis was not starting from zero. Perhaps his father had already taught him the basics at home – the stance, the rhythm, the way the shuttle moves through the warp threads – and the apprenticeship with Ptolemaeus was meant to finish what Tryphon had begun.

Or perhaps it was all Tryphon could afford.

The financial terms are unusual. Normally the master paid the apprentice wages, which increased as the boy’s skill grew. Here, Tryphon bore the cost of feeding and clothing his son throughout the apprenticeship, but Ptolemaeus paid Tryphon five drachmae per month “on account of victuals” and twelve drachmae at the end “on account of clothing.”12 The arrangement reads less like employment and more like a subsidized education – the master paying toward the cost of maintaining a student who would, for much of the year, be more burden than help. A boy learning to weave did not produce sellable cloth. He produced mistakes. He broke threads. He tangled the warp. He had to be watched and corrected, and every hour spent teaching him was an hour the master was not at his own loom, not producing the fabric that paid his taxes and fed his family.

Then the penalties. If Thoonis misses any days, Tryphon must produce him for an equivalent number of days after the contract period, or forfeit one drachma per day.13 If Tryphon withdraws the boy before the term is complete, the penalty is one hundred drachmae – plus an equal sum to the treasury.14 And if Ptolemaeus fails to instruct the boy thoroughly, he faces the same penalties.15

One hundred drachmae. To a weaver in first-century Oxyrhynchus, this was a staggering sum – roughly five times the total food subsidy Ptolemaeus would pay over the entire year. It was enough to buy five looms.16 It was more than Saraeus’s entire dowry when she married Tryphon three decades earlier.17

The doubling of the penalty – one hundred to the aggrieved party and one hundred to the treasury – was characteristic of Egyptian contract law under Roman rule. It served a double purpose: compensating the victim and punishing the offender, with the state taking its cut. The treasury’s share ensured that the state had an interest in enforcement, that breach of contract was not merely a private dispute but a matter of public concern. A father who pulled his son out of an apprenticeship was not just cheating a master. He was cheating the fisc.

The penalty existed not because anyone expected it to be paid but because its magnitude made breach unthinkable. Both parties were locked in. The boy would learn. The master would teach. The contract – he didaskalia haute kyria – was valid.18

II. The Father's Choice

Why Ptolemaeus?

Tryphon had been a weaver all his life. He had married twice – first Demetrous, then Saraeus, the daughter of Apion, who brought a dowry of seventy-two drachmae to the marriage: forty in cash, twenty in the form of gold earrings, and twelve for a milk-white robe.17 The marriage contract, signed in May of 37 CE in the first year of Caligula, was witnessed at the Serapeum and recorded through the bank of Sarapion, son of Kleandrus – the same neighborhood, the same institutions, the same narrow world of transactions and guarantees that would later frame his son’s apprenticeship. Tryphon was described in the document as a “Persian of the Epigone” – a legal classification that by the first century had nothing to do with Persia and everything to do with an old bureaucratic category inherited from the Ptolemaic military settlement system.105 It conferred certain legal privileges. It said nothing about his ancestry.

Of all the weavers in Oxyrhynchus – and there were many, in a city that possessed what scholars have called “Egypt’s most important weaving industry”19 – why did Tryphon choose this particular master for his son?

The answer lies, probably, in the weaving community itself. Ptolemaeus was the son of Pausirion, and Pausirion was no ordinary weaver. He appears in other documents as a prominent member of the Oxyrhynchus weaving trade, a man who apprenticed at least three of his own sons to other master weavers – a deliberate strategy for broadening their skills and reinforcing the professional networks that held the craft together.20 The weaving families of Oxyrhynchus were bound to each other by decades of such exchanges. Your son learned from my father. My nephew learned from your brother. The system created debts of knowledge that circulated like currency.

Tryphon himself was a weaver, as was his father Dionysius before him, and his grandfather Tryphon before that.21 The craft ran in the blood – or at least in the household, which in Roman Egypt amounted to the same thing. A child growing up in a weaver’s home breathed loom dust, heard the rhythmic clack of the shuttle, watched his father’s hands move across the warp threads from the time he could stand. The loom was not in a separate factory. It was in the house. The house was the factory.

And yet Tryphon did not teach his son himself. The paradox is central to the apprenticeship system: fathers who were masters of their craft sent their sons to learn it from other men. The practice was so common that it must have served purposes beyond mere instruction.22 A boy taught by his own father remains a son – subject to paternal authority, enmeshed in family dynamics, liable to the particular tensions that arise when a parent tries to be a teacher. A boy sent to another master becomes an apprentice – subject to professional discipline, embedded in a workshop hierarchy, accountable to someone whose authority derives not from kinship but from skill. The contract transformed a family relationship into a professional one. It imposed structure on what would otherwise be informal. It gave both parties legal recourse if things went wrong.

There may have been another reason, less comfortable to contemplate. By September of 66 CE, when the contract was signed, Tryphon was approximately fifty-eight years old.23 Fourteen years earlier, he had traveled to Alexandria – a journey of over two hundred kilometers, likely by boat up the canal system and then north along the Nile – to stand before the prefect of Egypt himself and be examined for military service. The prefect’s name was Gnaeus Vergilius Capito, and his title was prefect of Upper and Lower Egypt, the most powerful Roman official in the province.24 Before this man, a weaver from a provincial town presented his eyes for inspection.

The verdict: “Release from service was granted… to Tryphon, son of Dionysius, weaver, suffering from cataract and shortness of sight.”24 Katarakte kai amblyo-pounti. Cataracts. A weaver with cataracts – a man whose livelihood depended on close, precise work with fine threads, on distinguishing the white-violet from the plain white, on counting warp threads and detecting broken ones – was losing his ability to see. The diagnosis was both a reprieve and a sentence. It freed him from military service. It condemned him to a gradual narrowing of his world.

He continued working. The documents show him active for at least another fourteen years after the diagnosis, buying a loom in 54 CE, purchasing half a house in 55 CE, and finally apprenticing his son in 66 CE.25 Cataracts are progressive. In the ancient world, without surgical intervention, they led eventually to blindness. How much could Tryphon see in 66 CE? Enough, apparently, to navigate the legal system, to choose a master for his son, to negotiate terms. But perhaps not enough to weave. Perhaps the apprenticeship was not only an investment in his son’s future but a provision for his own declining years. Thoonis would learn the craft thoroughly, and when Tryphon could no longer see the threads, the boy would take over. The one-year term begins to look different in this light – not a shortcut but an urgency. The father’s eyes were failing. The son needed to learn. There was not much time.

III. Twenty Drachmae for a Loom

Two years before the apprenticeship, on the eighth of August 54 CE, Tryphon bought his loom.26

The document is P.Oxy. 2.264, and it records the sale by a man named Ammonius, son of Ammonius, of a histos gerdiakos – a weaver’s loom – for twenty drachmae of silver.27 The transaction was conducted through the bank of Sarapion, son of Lochus, near the Serapeum, and Ammonius guaranteed the sale “with every guarantee, under penalty of payment to you of the price which I have received from you increased by half its amount, and of the damages.”28

The guarantee clause is worth pausing over. If the sale fell through – if someone else claimed ownership of the loom, or if the loom proved defective – Ammonius would owe Tryphon not just the twenty drachmae but thirty: the price “increased by half its amount.” This was standard boilerplate in Egyptian sale contracts, but it functioned as real insurance. A weaver who spent his savings on a loom and then lost it to a rival claimant would be ruined. The guarantee meant that Ammonius bore that risk, not Tryphon.

Twenty drachmae. What was that worth? It was the same sum Saraeus’s gold earrings were valued at in her marriage contract with Tryphon.29 It was roughly four months of the food subsidy Ptolemaeus would later pay for Thoonis. It was enough to buy several months of grain. A household expense account from the Augustan period records the cost of a weaver’s breakfast at one obol, his beer at one obol, and the cost of weaving a single cloak at one drachma and two obols.92 At that rate, a loom represented roughly fifteen cloaks’ worth of labor. It was not nothing, but it was not a fortune either. A loom was a working tool, not a luxury – the ancient equivalent of buying a used sewing machine or a secondhand truck. What mattered was not its monetary value but its productive capacity: a working loom in the hands of a skilled weaver could generate income for decades.

The loom itself was a specific machine. It measured three weavers’ cubits less two palms – roughly 1.35 meters, or about four and a half feet, in width.30 It came equipped with two rollers (antia), two beams (histopodes), and one epimitron, a supplementary beam or measuring device whose exact function scholars still debate.31 This was a warp-weighted loom, perhaps, or more likely the horizontal ground loom that Egyptian weavers had used for millennia, adapted over the centuries but fundamentally unchanged: a frame that held the warp threads taut while the weaver passed the weft threads through them with a shuttle, building the fabric row by row.

The seller, Ammonius, could not write. “I, Heraclides, son of Dionysius, wrote for him as he was illiterate,” the document notes.32 This was common. Literacy in Roman Egypt was unevenly distributed – perhaps 20 to 30 percent of men in the cities could read and write Greek, far fewer in the villages, and fewer women than men.33 Among craftspeople, illiteracy was routine. The master weaver Heraclas, who a century later would take on the apprentice Thonis for five years, was also illiterate: “I, Thonis also called Morous, son of Harthonis, wrote for him as he was illiterate.”34 Tryphon himself may have been unable to write – his signatures in the archive are consistently written by others.35 A man could be a master of his craft, could run a workshop and negotiate contracts and buy property and apprentice sons, and never write his own name.

The looms themselves were valuable enough to be inherited, divided among heirs, and even pawned. An inventory of a deceased man’s property from the early second century records “a half share in three weavers’ looms which belonged to his father” – three looms suggesting a small workshop, the half share suggesting they had been split between siblings, and the whole entry sitting alongside a long list of items pledged to creditors: a bronze plate pawned for twenty drachmae, a box for ten, a tin flask for four, a tunic for eight.36 The family was in financial difficulty. The looms – the productive assets, the means of livelihood – were what remained unpledged, perhaps because without them there would be no income at all, no way to redeem the pledged items, no way to survive. A loom in pawn was a future foreclosed.

A will from the reign of Hadrian carefully bequeathed a single “weaving rod measuring five cubits” – about 2.3 meters – as a separate, named item, alongside furniture and household goods: a banquet-table of acacia wood, a pallet, a four-footed seat.37 The weaving rod was not listed as part of a loom but as an independent object, valuable enough to merit its own clause in the testament, its own named heir. These were tools with the weight of livelihood. You did not discard them lightly. You passed them down.

IV. The Quarter of Gymnasium Square

Weavers in Oxyrhynchus did not live in one place. The documents scatter them across the city’s quarters – Temgenouthis Square, Gymnasium Square, the Square of Thoeris, the quarter of Poimenike – names that survive only because bureaucrats needed addresses for tax rolls and legal complaints.38 But the clustering is suggestive. Tryphon’s house was near the Serapeum, on a street called Temgenouthis, and when a slave weaver named Apollophanes died in 61 CE, his registration in the tax rolls placed him in “Temgenouthis Square” – the same neighborhood.39 The Serapeum quarter, with its great temple complex and its bank and its notarial offices, appears to have been a center of the weaving trade.

Oxyrhynchus itself was a substantial city – perhaps 25,000 inhabitants or more at its peak in the early second century.40 It sat on a levee above the Bahr Yusuf canal, roughly 160 kilometers south of Cairo, in the fertile strip where the Nile’s western branch fed the Fayum depression. The city had a gymnasium, a hippodrome, several temples, a theater, and the full apparatus of Greco-Roman urban life: magistrates, a city council, tax collectors, notaries, and – crucially for historians – an enormous volume of paperwork. Contracts, petitions, tax receipts, private letters, census declarations, apprenticeship agreements, death notices, marriage documents, property sales – the bureaucracy of daily life generated a flood of papyrus that was eventually dumped in the city’s rubbish heaps, where the dry desert air preserved it for two thousand years.41 The excavations that recovered this material, beginning in the 1890s, have made Oxyrhynchus the single most prolific source of papyri from the ancient world – and the weaving trade, which generated so many of those documents, the best-documented craft in Roman Egypt.

The houses were densely packed. When a woman named Diemous filed a theft complaint in 144 CE, she accused her neighbor, a weaver named Heras, son of Kalathus, and “those working with him,” noting that “my house is easy of access from the house of Heras.”42 The complaint alleged that gold bracelets, a gold figure of the god Bes, and silver bracelets had disappeared from her terrace. The detail about easy access suggests walls shared or abutting, upper floors overlooking each other, the dense urban fabric of a city where workshops and residences were not separate zones but layered together, room by room, floor by floor. A three-storied house like the one Tryphon purchased near the Serapeum would have held the family’s living quarters above and, perhaps, the workshop below – the loom on the ground floor where the light was best, the living and sleeping rooms on the upper stories, the terrace on top where cloth could be hung to dry and neighbors could peer across.43

The city’s geography shaped the weaver’s day. Water for washing wool and linen came from the canal. Yarn-merchants – stemonopoioi – sold the raw materials.44 Fullers, who cleaned and finished the woven cloth, operated their own workshops nearby. Dyers handled the coloring. The chain of textile production ran from raw fiber to finished garment, and each stage had its specialists, its prices, its points of friction. When a man named Troilus wrote to his sister about having a white tunic made, he mentioned the weavers’ wages, the purple dye stored with the tools, and the need for “good measure” and generous coloring – a customer’s demands filtering down to the loom.45

V. The Tax Collector's Violence

The weaving trade was taxed. This is not a remarkable statement – everything in Roman Egypt was taxed – but the specificity of the taxation matters. There was a tax on weaving as such, the gerdiake techne tax, levied on every registered weaver in the city.93 It was collected bimonthly, farmed out to private tax-collectors who purchased the right to collect it and then profited from the difference between what they collected and what they owed the treasury. The system created a class of men whose income depended on squeezing the people they served.

The earliest surviving receipt for this tax dates to 22-21 BCE, when a linen-weaver named Patemnophis paid forty drachmae as his third installment.94 In 17 BCE, two receipts from the same month show a linen-weaver assessed at twenty drachmae and a general weaver at just two – a tenfold disparity that may reflect different scales of production, different sub-specializations, or different rates of extraction.95 By the mid-first century CE, the tax was an established fixture of the weaver’s life, as predictable as the Nile flood and as unwelcome.

But there was a difference between taxation and extortion. Around the year 50 CE – a decade and a half before Tryphon apprenticed Thoonis – a weaver named Sarapion, son of Theon, walked the streets of Oxyrhynchus wearing a linen tunic worth eight drachmae. He lived in the Gymnasium Square quarter. He was, by all evidence, an ordinary craftsman going about his business.46

Then Apollophanes found him.

Apollophanes was the ex-collector of the trade tax on weavers – the gerdiake techne tax, a levy specific to the weaving profession, collected bimonthly by tax-farmers who purchased the right to collect it and then squeezed as much as they could from the men and women who owed it.47 Note the word “ex-collector.” Apollophanes no longer held the office. He had been replaced. But the residual authority – or at least the residual intimidation – of having once been the man who collected the tax was enough.

The word Sarapion used in his petition was bia – violence. Not threat. Not coercion. Violence. “Using great violence,” Apollophanes seized the tunic Sarapion was wearing – literally pulled it off his body in the street – and then extorted four additional drachmae, and then two drachmae each month for six months. Total: twenty-four drachmae in illegal payments, plus the shirt off his back.48

Sarapion petitioned Tiberius Claudius Pasion, the strategos of the Oxyrhynchite nome – the Roman-appointed governor of the district, the highest local authority, the man to whom complaints went when the system itself was the predator. “I therefore beg you to proceed against him as you may think fit,” Sarapion wrote – or rather, had written for him, since the weaver may well have been as illiterate as his colleagues.52 The petition was polite, formulaic, deferential. The violence it described was not.

He was not alone. Another weaver, Alexandrus, son of Apollonius, living in the quarter of the Square of Thoeris, filed a similar complaint against the same Apollophanes. The ex-collector had extorted sixteen drachmae from Alexandrus “among other people” – that phrase, meta allōn, suggesting a pattern so widespread that Alexandrus could not even list all the victims.49

And a third weaver. Dius, son of Peteuris, identified himself as one of “the weavers of the quarter of Poimenike” – the Shepherds’ Quarter – and reported that a different ex-collector, Ammonius, had extorted forty drachmae in one year and another forty in the next, “bit by bit.”50 Eighty drachmae total. Dius specifically noted that the extortion had left him unable to pay his legitimate trade tax – the very tax the collector was supposed to be collecting.51 The system had consumed itself.

Three separate petitions from three separate weavers in three separate quarters of the same city, all within a few years of each other, all describing the same pattern: men who held or had held the authority to collect taxes using that authority – or its residual threat – to steal from the people they were supposed to serve. The petitions were addressed to the strategos, who was expected to “proceed against him as you may think fit.”52 Whether he did, we do not know. The papyri preserve the complaints but not the outcomes. The silence that follows a petition is one of the most common silences in the ancient record, and one of the most frustrating.

What the complaints do reveal is the texture of vulnerability. A weaver was a small operator – one man, one loom, one household – exposed to anyone with a claim to authority. He could not close the door. The tax collector knew where he lived, knew what he owed, knew that the cost of resistance (a trip to Alexandria for a legal hearing, lost days at the loom, the risk of official retaliation) exceeded the cost of paying. The tunic ripped from Sarapion’s body was worth eight drachmae. The cost of justice was incalculable.

And the system could break a man entirely. A later document, from the end of the second century, records cloth-weavers’ tax arrears and lists individuals “who have gone away to unknown places” – the phenomenon scholars call anachoresis, flight from tax obligations.96 When the burden became unbearable, some weavers simply vanished. They left their looms, their houses, their quarters, and disappeared into the countryside or the anonymity of another city. The tax rolls recorded their absence with bureaucratic indifference: “not traced.” A weaver who fled was a weaver who had calculated that the cost of staying exceeded the cost of losing everything. Tryphon, with his cataracts and his house near the Serapeum and his son at the loom, did not flee. But he lived in a world where flight was a possibility that the state had to account for.

VI. What the Boy Learned

The contract says Ptolemaeus was to teach Thoonis “the weaver’s art in all its branches of which Ptolemaeus has knowledge.”53 The phrase – kata ten gerdiakon technen ta kata meros panta – implies subdivisions, specializations, a body of knowledge with internal structure. What were the branches?

We can reconstruct some of them from the material evidence and the documentary record, though the papyri, characteristically, tell us about prices and taxes and disputes and say almost nothing about the work itself.

Linen was the primary textile of Egypt – it had been since the pharaonic period, when temple workshops produced linen of legendary fineness for priestly garments and mummy wrappings.54 The flax plant grew abundantly in the Nile valley, and linen-weaving (linyphia) was a distinct specialization within the broader weaving trade, taxed separately and valued differently. The tax receipts distinguish between the “linen-weavers’ tax” and the general “weavers’ tax,” and the amounts differed dramatically: in 17 BCE, a linen-weaver named Petechonsis was assessed twenty drachmae while a general weaver named Leukis paid only two.55 Whether this reflected higher income, higher status, or simply a different tax rate is impossible to say with certainty, but the disparity is striking.

Wool was increasingly important in the Roman period. The Romans brought their preference for woolen garments to Egypt, and the demand for woolen tunics, cloaks, and blankets expanded the market for wool-weavers alongside the established linen trade.56 A weaver who could work both fibers – who understood the different tensions, the different moisture requirements, the different dyes that each material accepted – was more versatile and more valuable than one who knew only linen or only wool.

The physical labor was repetitive and demanding. The loom held the warp threads taut – stretched vertically on an upright loom or horizontally on a ground loom – and the weaver passed the weft thread through them with a shuttle, row after row, hour after hour, from sunrise to sunset.57 The width of Tryphon’s loom – roughly four and a half feet – determined the maximum width of the fabric he could produce. Wider cloths required wider looms and, sometimes, two weavers working side by side. The rollers and beams controlled the tension of the warp; the epimitron may have measured the cloth as it grew; the heddle mechanism (if the loom had one) allowed the weaver to raise alternate warp threads, creating a shed through which the shuttle could pass quickly.

Patterns were possible. The letter from Troilus to his sister mentions “purple” dye stored with the weaving tools and insists on generous coloring – evidence of patterned or colored weaving, not just plain white cloth.58 Another letter urgently requests matching dyed yarn of a specific shade – “white-violet” – because “the tunic is to be woven immediately.”59 The demand for color-matched yarn suggests that weavers worked to precise specifications, incorporating colored threads into the fabric according to a predetermined design. This was skilled work. A boy learning it would need to understand not only the mechanics of the loom but the aesthetics of the finished product – how colors interacted, how patterns emerged from the intersection of warp and weft, how to achieve the “good measure” that customers demanded.

Thoonis would have started with the simplest tasks: winding bobbins, preparing yarn, cleaning the loom, fetching water. He would have watched Ptolemaeus work before being allowed to touch the loom himself. Then, perhaps, simple weaving – plain cloth, no pattern, low stakes. Mistakes could be unraveled. Broken threads could be repaired. Gradually, over weeks and months, the tasks would grow more complex, the expectations higher, until the boy could produce cloth that Ptolemaeus was willing to sell.

The physical toll should not be understated. A weaver worked from sunrise to sunset – the five-year contract of 183 CE specifies this explicitly.57 That meant roughly twelve hours in summer, somewhat less in winter, sitting or standing at a loom in a room that would have been hot in the Egyptian summer and cold in the delta winter, repeating the same motions hundreds of times per day: pass the shuttle, beat the weft, shift the heddle, pass the shuttle again. The body adapted to the loom or it did not. A boy who could not endure the hours, whose back ached or whose fingers cramped or whose concentration wandered, would produce faulty cloth and earn his master nothing. The contract required the boy to “perform all the orders given him by Ptolemaeus,” and those orders, day after day, would have been the same: sit. Weave. Do it again. Do it better.

A bilingual note from the second century – Greek and Demotic Egyptian, side by side – identifies a weaver named Hierax simply by name and trade: “Hierax, son of Psonsneou, weaver.”97 The persistence of Demotic alongside Greek among the weaving families suggests that many weavers maintained their Egyptian identity even as they operated within the Greek administrative system. Thoonis – an Egyptian name, not a Greek one – may well have spoken both languages in the workshop, using Greek for contracts and Demotic for the daily business of the loom.

One year was not much time. But if Tryphon had already taught his son the basics, one year with Ptolemaeus might have been enough to learn what Tryphon could not teach: a different technique, a different specialization, the particular knowledge of a master whose family had been weaving for generations.

VII. Twenty Holidays a Year

The five-year contract of P.Oxy. 4.725, signed in 183 CE – over a century after Thoonis’s apprenticeship – specifies that the apprentice Thonis was entitled to twenty holidays per year “on account of festivals.”60 No deduction from wages would be made for these days, once the paid phase of the apprenticeship began. But any absences beyond the twenty, whether from “idleness or ill-health or disobedience or any other reason,” had to be made up without pay.61

Twenty days. In a working year of perhaps 350 days (allowing for the festivals themselves), this was roughly six percent of the total – one day off every two and a half weeks, on average, though in practice the festivals were clustered around particular months. What festivals?

The religious calendar of Roman Oxyrhynchus was a palimpsest – layers of Egyptian, Greek, and Roman observances written over each other. The great Egyptian festivals of Isis and Osiris, the Nile flood celebrations, the Ptolemaic dynastic festivals that had outlived the dynasty they commemorated, the Roman imperial holidays – all competed for space in the calendar.62

The Serapeum, the great temple complex near Tryphon’s house, would have hosted many of these observances. Serapis himself was a syncretic deity – part Osiris, part Apis, part Greek invention – and his festivals drew worshippers from across the city. A weaver’s boy, released from the loom for the day, might have watched processions, eaten festival food, stood in crowds outside the temple where his father conducted his banking business and where his grandfather’s cousin had sold them half a house. Or he might have slept. After working sunrise to sunset at a loom, sleep was perhaps the most valuable thing a holiday could offer.

The contract’s precision about holidays – twenty, no more, with make-up days required for any excess – reveals the tension between religious obligation and economic necessity. A workshop that stopped for every minor festival would lose money. A workshop that ignored every festival would offend the gods and the community. Twenty days was the negotiated compromise, written into the contract because both parties needed to know exactly where the line fell.

The make-up clause is unsparing. If the boy exceeded his twenty holidays, whether “from idleness or ill-health or disobedience or any other reason,” his guardian Ischyrion was required to produce him for equivalent days after the contract expired – days during which the boy would work without wages, fed by his guardian, performing all his duties “as aforesaid.”61 Illness was not an excuse. A boy with a fever still owed the time. A boy who refused to come to the workshop still owed the time. The contract did not distinguish between willful absence and misfortune. Time was owed. Time would be repaid.

The boy’s time was not his own. It belonged to the master, except on the days when it belonged to the gods.

VIII. The Graduated Wage

The most revealing feature of the five-year contract is the wage scale. For the first two years and seven months of the third year, Heraclas the master paid nothing for the boy’s labor.63 Then, in the remaining five months of the third year, twelve drachmae per month. In the fourth year, sixteen. In the fifth, twenty-four.64

The progression is a map of learning. For thirty-one months, the boy was an expense – a body that consumed food and space and teaching time while producing nothing of value. Then, gradually, he began to earn. The jump from zero to twelve drachmae marks the moment when his weaving became saleable, when the cloth he produced on the loom could be sold to customers and the master could recoup some of his investment. The subsequent increases – twelve to sixteen to twenty-four – reflect increasing speed, increasing skill, increasing independence. By the fifth year, the apprentice was producing work worth twenty-four drachmae a month to the master, which means the cloth he wove was worth considerably more than that, since the master had to cover his own costs and profit.

Twenty-four drachmae a month was not a laborer’s wage. It was not a master’s income. It was the wage of a young man who was nearly but not quite a professional – still bound by contract, still under the master’s authority, but capable of producing work that met professional standards.

The master’s obligation was stated with a precision that amounted to a guarantee: Heraclas agreed “to instruct the apprentice in the aforesaid art within the period of 5 years as thoroughly as he knows it himself.”11 As thoroughly as he knows it himself. The standard was not mere competence. It was equivalence. The apprentice, at the end of five years, was supposed to know what the master knew. Whether this was achievable – whether any five-year training could truly transfer the accumulated knowledge of a lifetime – is another question. But the contract said it, and the penalty for failure was one hundred drachmae, and the words stood as a legal commitment to the fullest possible transmission of the craft.

After the fifth year, when the contract expired, what happened? The apprentice became a journeyman, perhaps. Or he returned to his family’s workshop. Or he set up on his own, buying a loom – twenty drachmae, the price Tryphon had paid a century earlier, though inflation would have raised it – and joining the rolls of the gerdioi who owed the weaving tax and might, if unlucky, encounter their own Apollophanes.

The clothing provision tells its own story. Heraclas was required to furnish the apprentice with one tunic per year, of increasing value: sixteen drachmae in the first year, then twenty, twenty-four, twenty-eight, and thirty-two.65 The escalation reflects two realities. First, inflation – the gradual erosion of the drachma’s purchasing power across the second century. Second, the boy’s growing body. A tunic that fit a twelve-year-old would not fit a seventeen-year-old. Each year the garment cost more because the apprentice was larger and the money was worth less. The tunics were work clothes – the garments a boy wore at the loom, stained with dye and frayed by the friction of the shuttle. They were not luxuries. They were operating costs, built into the contract alongside the wages and the holidays and the penalty clauses, because a boy who lacked a tunic could not work.

IX. Women at the Loom

The apprenticeship contracts that survive are for boys. But the tax receipts tell a different story.

Among the series of weaver tax receipts spanning the years 141 to 197 CE, at least two were paid by women. One, from 141 CE, records payment by a woman whose name is partially lost – “Ch[…]os daughter of Senpetemenophis.”66 Another, from 188 CE, records payment by “a slave-woman.”67 Both were paying the weavers’ tax, which means both were practicing the weaver’s trade. They were not apprentices. They were not auxiliaries. They were taxpaying professionals, registered in the rolls alongside the men.

The evidence for female weavers in Roman Egypt is thin but real.

Girls were rarely apprenticed – only one documented female apprenticeship contract survives from the region, involving an orphaned girl working off her deceased father’s debts.68 Out of thirty-nine documented apprentices in Roman Egypt, eleven were slaves and the vast majority were male.106 The absence of girls from the formal apprenticeship system did not mean they were absent from the looms. Women’s labor in weaving is more often glimpsed through household accounts and incidental references than through formal contracts. The senate debate of 271–272 CE mentions that “those who undertook the work in question and their wives were unable to spin the yarn” – the wives appear as participants in production, not as bystanders.69 Spinning – the preparation of raw fiber into thread – was traditionally women’s work in the ancient Mediterranean, and the division of labor that assigned spinning to women and weaving to men was widespread, though not absolute.

But the tax receipts complicate this picture. A woman paying the weavers’ tax was not merely spinning. She was weaving – operating a loom, producing cloth, and being assessed for it by the same collectors who assessed the men. The fact that a slave-woman could be individually taxed for weaving suggests that enslaved women operated looms in workshops alongside free craftsmen, producing the same goods under the same fiscal regime.70 The workshop of a master weaver like Heras, son of Kalathus – the man accused of theft by his neighbor Diemous in 144 CE, described as working with “those working with him” – may well have included women among its workers, free and enslaved, though the documents do not say so explicitly.71

What the documents do not say is, as always, as important as what they do. We have no apprenticeship contract for a girl learning to weave. We have no petition from a female weaver complaining of tax extortion. We have no purchase of a loom by a woman. The archive is shaped by what was written down, and what was written down was shaped by what mattered to the men who controlled the bureaucracy. Women wove. They paid taxes. They appear in the record as flickers – a name on a receipt, a phrase in a senate debate – and then vanish. The archive is shaped by what was written down, and what was written down served the state’s need to tax, conscript, and register. Women who wove within the household, producing cloth for family use rather than for sale, would never have appeared in the tax rolls at all. The women who do appear – the taxpaying professionals, the slave-women registered individually – represent only the visible edge of what was probably a much larger reality of female textile labor that the documents, by their very nature, cannot capture.

X. The Senate Debates the Weavers

Two centuries after Thoonis stood at Ptolemaeus’s loom, the city senate of Oxyrhynchus – the boule – sat down to argue about cloth.

The document is P.Oxy. 12.1414, dated to 271–272 CE, and it preserves something extraordinary: the actual minutes of a senate debate, recorded as it happened, with the speakers identified and their words quoted.72 The prytanis – the presiding officer – opens by noting that the senators had previously examined a list of temple requirements and fixed a limit on production, but that the priests had objected, claiming that the workers and their wives “were unable to spin the yarn.”73

Then Septimius Diogenes, a former hypomnematographus and now the city’s legal officer, reports on the economics. The yarn-merchants say the price of weaving-yarn is forty-nine denarii. Eleven denarii had been paid to them from the treasury’s account. The senators respond: “Let the yarn-merchants be content with nineteen denarii besides what was paid to them from the Treasury’s account.”74

The yarn-merchants wanted forty-nine. The treasury had already paid eleven. The senate offered nineteen more – thirty total, against the merchants’ asking price of forty-nine. This was a negotiation, conducted on the senate floor, with the city’s money at stake.

Then a petition from the cloth-weavers was read aloud. The weavers asked for higher pay, “on account of the rise in the value of the materials and in the wages of their workmen.”75 The prytanis agreed: “Let more drachmae be given to the cloth-weavers, making 200 drachmae in all, on account of the rise in value of the materials.”76

The weavers had organized. They had drafted a collective petition. They had presented it to the senate. And they had won – at least partially. The increase to two hundred drachmae acknowledged the reality of inflation, which by the 270s was severe. The monetary system of the Roman Empire was in crisis; the silver content of the coinage had been debased repeatedly; prices were rising in nominal terms even as the real value of the currency collapsed.77 The weavers were not getting richer. They were running to stand still.

But the fact that they could petition the senate at all – that they had the organizational capacity to draft a collective complaint, the standing to have it read aloud in the chamber, and the leverage to extract a concession – says something about the position of weavers in Oxyrhynchus. They were not powerless. They were not invisible. They were a constituency, organized enough to make demands and important enough that the senate had to listen. Two centuries of spinning and weaving had made the textile trade integral to the city’s economy and its identity. The senate needed cloth for the temples. The temples needed linen for the priests. The army needed tunics for the soldiers. The weavers stood at the center of all of it, and they knew it.

This capacity for collective action had deep roots. A document from Euhemeria in the Fayum, dated to the reign of Tiberius (15-36 CE), reveals a weavers’ guild with formal officers – a prostates (head) named Heracles and a grammateus (secretary) named Aphrodisius – who acted as sureties for guild members facing legal claims.98 The guild was not a modern trade union. It did not negotiate wages or organize strikes. But it functioned as a mutual-aid society, binding its members together in networks of obligation and guarantee. When a wool-worker named Paninoutis brought a claim against five weavers of Euhemeria, the guild leaders stepped forward to guarantee the accused men’s appearance. The craft community protected its own.

The senate debate also reveals the quality control that the city imposed on temple linen. Septimius Diogenes proposed that “the cloth-weavers who are to weave the linen of the temple appoint someone to test it” – an inspector, drawn from the weavers’ own ranks, who would certify that the finished product met the required standard.99 The weavers were not merely producers. They were guarantors of quality, answerable to the senate and the temples for the cloth they made. The techne was not just a skill. It was a public trust.

XI. A Three-Storied House by the Serapeum

On the fourth of September, 55 CE, Tryphon stood before the agoranomi Andromachus and Diogenes – the notary-magistrates of Oxyrhynchus – and bought half a three-storied house from his mother’s cousin, Pnepheros, son of Papontos.78

The document, P.Oxy. 1.99, describes both men with the meticulous physical detail that Egyptian legal practice required. Tryphon: about forty-seven years old, medium height, fair-complexioned, with a long face, a slight squint, and a scar on his right wrist.79 Pnepheros: about sixty-five, medium height, fair, long-faced, with a scar above his eyebrow and another on his right knee.80 These were not portraits for aesthetic purposes. They were identification – the ancient equivalent of a photograph on a passport, recorded so that no one could later claim to be someone else.

The house stood near the Serapeum, “in the southern part of the street called Temgenouthis to the west of the lane leading to ‘Shepherds’ Street.’”81 Its boundaries were meticulously recorded: public roads to the south and east, the house of Thamounis (Tryphon’s own mother) to the north, and the house of Tausiris (Pnepheros’s sister) to the west, “separated by a blind alley.”82 The neighborhood was a web of family – Tryphon buying from his mother’s cousin, living next to his mother, separated from the seller’s sister by a narrow lane. The transaction was conducted “in the street,” a detail that suggests the legal proceedings were held outdoors, perhaps at the threshold of the house itself.83

The price was thirty-two talents of copper – a substantial sum in the copper currency standard of Roman Egypt.84 For a weaver, this was a major investment. It was also an act of consolidation: Tryphon was assembling property around his mother’s house, building a family compound in the quarter near the Serapeum where the weavers worked and the bank where he transacted his business operated. A three-storied house was not a cottage. It had multiple rooms, a staircase, entrances and exits, and “appurtenances” – a legal term covering everything from cisterns to roof terraces.85 For half of such a house, a weaver could have a workshop on the ground floor, living quarters above, storage in the third story or on the roof.

This was what a weaver could accumulate over a lifetime of work. Not wealth. Not luxury. But property – solid, inheritable, located in the neighborhood where his trade was practiced and his neighbors were his colleagues. Other weavers, too, accumulated modestly. A land register from the late second or early third century records a linen-weaver named Theoninos owning one and a half arouras of agricultural land – about one acre, a small holding but enough to supplement the income from the loom with grain or flax.100 Another register lists a weaver named Perseus with three and a half arouras – a more substantial plot, suggesting a man who had done well.101 The weaving trade did not make anyone rich, but it could, over a lifetime of steady work, build a modest patrimony.

A census declaration from the Arsinoite nome, dated 189 CE, captures the household of a weaver named Herodes in vivid detail: aged fifty, married to a woman who was also his sister (a common Egyptian practice), with sons who had become goldsmiths, nephews who were rod-makers and laborers, tenants and their families – over twenty people crammed into a fraction of a shared house.102 The diversity of occupations within one household illustrates how craft families hedged their bets, spreading their labor across multiple trades rather than concentrating everything in weaving alone.

Twenty-five years after the house purchase, in 91 CE, a man named Heraclas, son of Tryphon – likely Tryphon’s son or grandson – would participate in a slave manumission, contributing two hundred drachmae toward the ransom.86 The family had prospered, modestly, across the generations. The looms had done their work.

XII. What the Record Does Not Say

Did Thoonis finish his apprenticeship?

The contract says one year. The start date was September 66 CE, so the term would have expired in September 67 CE. The penalty for early withdrawal was one hundred drachmae. The penalty for inadequate instruction was the same. Both parties had powerful incentives to fulfill their obligations.

But we do not know. The contract itself is the last document in the archive that mentions Thoonis by name. He enters the record as a boy, “not yet of age,” handed over to a master weaver, and then the record falls silent.87 Did he become a master himself? Did he take over his father’s loom? Did he weave in the house near the Serapeum, on the street called Temgenouthis, using the loom that Tryphon had bought from the illiterate Ammonius for twenty drachmae?

Perhaps. The patterns of the trade suggest it. Sons of weavers became weavers. The craft was hereditary. The loom was inherited. The house was inherited. The quarter was inherited. Generation after generation, the same families sat at the same looms in the same streets, producing the linen and wool that clothed the city and its temples and its dead.

Or perhaps not. The year 66 CE was not an ordinary year. In Judaea, that same September, the Jewish revolt against Rome was escalating toward the catastrophe that would end with the destruction of the Temple in Jerusalem four years later.88 In Rome, Nero’s reign was entering its final convulsions. In Egypt, the ripple effects of these upheavals would have been felt in tax demands, military requisitions, and the general anxiety that attends the collapse of imperial authority. Sixty years later, in 128 CE, the weavers of a Fayum village called Socnopaei Nesus would be required to produce nineteen tunics for the guards and five white cloaks “for the needs of the soldiers serving in Judaea” – a compulsory liturgia that fell on the craft as a whole.103 A weaver’s boy in Oxyrhynchus might have faced similar demands, or been drafted, or fled, or simply decided that the loom was not for him.

The archive of Tryphon, son of Dionysius, contains roughly thirty documents spanning three decades of a weaver’s life – his military exemption, his loom purchase, his house purchase, his marriage, his son’s apprenticeship.89 It is one of the richest collections of personal documents from the ancient world. And yet it tells us almost nothing about what Tryphon thought, or felt, or feared. It tells us what he bought and what he signed and what his eyes could not see. It tells us the terms of his son’s contract and the penalties for breach. It does not tell us whether the boy was happy.

Consider what else the archive does not say. It does not mention Tryphon’s first wife, Demetrous, except obliquely – she is a shadow behind Saraeus, the second wife, the mother of Thoonis. It does not tell us when Tryphon died, or how, or whether his cataracts progressed to blindness. It does not tell us what happened to the house near the Serapeum, whether Thoonis inherited it or Heraclas or someone else entirely. It does not tell us whether the loom Tryphon bought from Ammonius in 54 CE was still in use in 91 CE, when Heraclas contributed two hundred drachmae to a slave manumission – a sum that suggests the family had money, which suggests the looms were working, which suggests someone was sitting at them, day after day, sunrise to sunset, but who? The papyri do not say.

The apprenticeship system itself – that careful mechanism of contracts and penalties, graduated wages and holiday allowances, mutual obligations and make-up days – was designed to transmit knowledge from one generation to the next. It worked, in the sense that weaving continued in Oxyrhynchus for centuries. But by the late third century, the system was changing. The state was intervening more directly, establishing government weaving-works – gynaecea – where workers served under compulsion, providing sureties to guarantee they would not flee.90 A weaver taking an oath to serve in the government factory at Panopolis swore “by the fortune of our lords Diocletian and Maximian” that he would “go up to Panopolis and serve in the weaving-works that serves the well-being of the most valiant soldiers, and will remain there until released.”91 The language of the oath – remain there until released – is the language of detention, not employment. The independent craftsman of Tryphon’s era, owning his own loom, buying his own house, negotiating his own contracts, was becoming something else: a conscript, bound to a state factory, producing cloth not for the market but for the army.

The distance between Tryphon’s September morning in 66 CE – a father walking his son to a master’s workshop, a contract signed, a future imagined – and the oath at Panopolis two centuries later is the distance between autonomy and compulsion. The craft was the same. The loom was the same. The threads were the same. What changed was who controlled them.

A government letter from the mid-fourth century makes the transformation explicit. An official named Nicon instructs the exactor of the Oxyrhynchite nome to provide military assistance to ensure that “the persons responsible may be compelled to manufacture the clothing in irreproachable materials.”104 Compelled – anagkasthosi. The weavers who had once petitioned the senate for better pay were now compelled by centurions to produce for the state. The guild leaders who had stood surety for their fellows were replaced by officials who used soldiers to enforce compliance. The workshops where a boy like Thoonis had learned his craft under a contract freely entered were becoming factories where men labored under oath, providing sureties against their own flight.

But that was the future. In September of 66 CE, the future had not yet arrived.

A boy named Thoonis stood at a loom in Oxyrhynchus, in the quarter near the Serapeum, in a three-storied house on Temgenouthis street. His father, squinting with cataracts, watched him – or tried to. The shuttle moved. The weft thread crossed the warp. The fabric grew, row by row, in the old way, the way it had always been done. The contract specified one year. One year of sunrise to sunset, of orders obeyed, of threads passed through threads, of a boy becoming – perhaps – a weaver.

The papyrus that recorded the arrangement survived because someone threw it away. It was discarded along with thousands of other documents into the rubbish heaps at the edge of Oxyrhynchus, where the dry desert air preserved what the inhabitants had meant to destroy. Nearly two millennia later, British excavators recovered it, catalogued it, published it. The boy, the father, the master, the loom, the house, the street – all are gone. What remains is the contract, and in the contract, the faintest outline of a life: the terms under which a child learned to make cloth, and the penalties that applied if anyone broke their word.
