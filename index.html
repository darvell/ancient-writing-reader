How to Smell Expensive

Perfume Sellers, Barber Shops, Bath Costs, and the Infrastructure of Being Well Kept in Roman Egypt

A dossier of more than forty papyri shows that grooming in Roman Egypt was not just taste but infrastructure. Perfume sellers and perfumers appear by title, shares in a perfume-selling business (μυροπώλικη) could be leased, markets and festivals were valuable enough to be reserved out, households ordered perfume, powder, combs, and unguent jars, and barbers, baths, oils, and aromatics all left documentary traces. Looking good had a supply chain.

Bloodless and Wretched

The Curse Tablet Industry of Greek Sicily, 550--100 BCE

Between 550 and 100 BCE, the Greek cities of Sicily produced an extraordinary concentration of lead curse tablets — defixiones — scratched with names, folded, pierced with nails, and buried in the graves of the dead. From Kamarina's necropolis at Passo Marinaro to the workshops of Gela, from the sanctuaries of Selinus to the tombs of Messana, ordinary people paid to have their rivals, enemies, and romantic competitors condemned to be 'bloodless and wretched.' The story follows the named individuals on these tablets — Apellis the lover, Eunikos the chorus-boy, Kerdon the cursed, Chamis the sick — through a millennium of Sicilian magic.
