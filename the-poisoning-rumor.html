The Poisoning Rumor

A Rejected Suitor, a Vineyard Sale, and the Paper Trail of a Scandal at Oxyrhynchus

1. What the accusation actually says

The core text is P.Oxy. 3.472, a speech or memorandum from before 10 October 131 CE.1 It survives in rough shape, but the crucial sequence is clear enough. Sarapion is said to have left Hermione’s house without telling anyone that he felt anything strange and without any suspicion at all.1 Only after going out from his own house, or from the household of himself and the son who was to inherit, did he begin saying that he had been poisoned.1

That sequence matters because it tells us where the story did not begin. It did not begin at the threshold of Hermione’s house. It did not begin with immediate symptoms, or at least not with symptoms he chose to mention at once. It began later, after he was back among his own people.1

The defense seizes on the delay. It then adds two alternative explanations. First, Sarapion had reasons to poison himself: he was being destroyed by creditors and had reached a point of desperation.1 Second, if someone else had plotted against him, the likeliest person was his own son, the future heir.1 Neither claim can be proven from this text alone. Both are forensic suggestions. But they are not random suggestions either. The speech presents them as plausible motives, and separate evidence in the dossier does indeed show debt pressure and inheritance stakes.12

Then comes the line that changes the temperature of the case. The speech says that Sarapion was jealous of Hermione, that he called himself her husband, that she did not acknowledge him as such, and that he suffered like a lover and did not want her to outlive him.1 That is not how one writes about a neutral business rival. At the least, the wording makes personal animus and wounded attachment a plausible part of the case.

The same text shows that the poisoning story was entangled from the start with documents and property. There is argument over a missing slave named Smaragdus, over whether a mortgage deed ever existed, over whether a daughter and mother had made a joint agreement for 150 jars from vineyards, and over whether these things had anything at all to do with the accusation.1 In other words, by the time the poisoning rumor reached official form, it was already attached to a much larger dispute.

2. The later petition says the property case was made up

That larger dispute comes into focus in P.Oxy. 3.486, written after 10 October 131 CE.2 Dionysia, daughter of Chaeremon, with mother named as Hermione, petitions the epistrategus while stuck in Alexandria waiting for a case to move.2 Her opponent is Sarapion son of Mnesitheus.2

Dionysia says she had bought a vineyard and grain land from Sarapion’s father years earlier, in the 11th year of Hadrian, paying both the father and one of his creditors and receiving the proper official contract.2 Sarapion then claimed that she held the property only by way of mortgage or security.2 More than that: in the copy of her earlier petition to the prefect embedded in the same papyrus, Dionysia states that Sarapion had accused her mother Hermione of poisoning and had ἐλογοποιήσατο, concocted or fabricated, a claim about property supposedly being withheld from him.2

This second papyrus is the piece that turns a good suspicion into a much stronger dossier. The poisoning accusation is no longer floating by itself. It sits beside a concrete fight over land, loans, mortgages, and creditors.2 Dionysia presents the two matters as connected, even if the precise chain of causation between them cannot now be recovered in full.

It also tells us something about scale. This was serious enough to move from the Oxyrhynchite metropolis to the epistrategus and then the prefect; serious enough to require travel to Alexandria; serious enough that delay could be financially catastrophic while flood damage was destroying property back home.2 This is not neighborhood chatter preserved only in rumor. It is neighborhood chatter that reached the top of the provincial file stack.

3. Hermione's household had money, and that made it vulnerable

The poisoning case would be easier to dismiss as melodrama if Hermione’s household were otherwise invisible. It is not. P.Oxy. 3.472 preserves enough of the defense to show that Hermione had children, that one of them was Dionysia, and that money and produce were moving through formal agreements inside the family.1 The speech says Hermione had three children.1 It says Dionysia begged her mother not to leave her dependent on too little; Hermione then gave her one and a half talents; and Dionysia in return paid an annual provision, connected to 150 jars from vineyard income, so that her mother should not lose that capital during her lifetime.1

That arrangement matters because it makes Hermione legible in exactly the way that could attract both lawsuits and gossip. She was not a shadowy woman in the background of men’s papers. She appears as a woman handling capital, negotiating with a daughter, and sitting at the center of claims about vineyards, mortgages, and provisions.1

Dionysia’s later petition confirms the same material world. The disputed property is vineyard land and grain land. Creditors are explicitly involved. Official contracts and mortgage registration are the terrain on which the fight is being waged.2 The household is not poor, but it is exposed. Wealth in the papyri is rarely calm. It comes wrapped in claims, registries, guaranties, and people who think something ought to have been theirs instead.23

The most economical reading is that Sarapion attached himself to a household worth attaching himself to. When that failed, the accusation remained useful.12

4. The rumor probably began at home, not at Hermione's door

If we ask the simplest version of the question, what did people in Oxyrhynchus think had happened, the answer is easy enough. A man visited Hermione. Later he said he had been poisoned. There was already property litigation. There may have been a missing slave and arguments about deeds. The story could spread quickly.12

If we ask the harder question, what does the evidence actually support, the answer changes.

The strongest point remains the sequence. Sarapion left Hermione’s house without reporting suspicion. He raised the cry of poison only later, after returning to his own side of the dispute.1 That does not prove innocence. It does make the most theatrical version of the story much harder to sustain.

The second strong point is motive. The defense’s alternative motives are not speculative in the abstract; they are anchored in details preserved elsewhere in the dossier. Debt really is present.12 Creditors really are present.2 Inheritance pressure really is present, because the speech explicitly points to the future heir.1 And emotional grievance is present in the blunt language of jealousy and unreciprocated self-styling as husband.1

The third strong point is that Dionysia, in a later petition, treats the poisoning accusation as part of a package of harassment that included an invented property claim.2 That does not mean every element of Sarapion’s complaint was false. It does mean that Hermione’s side understood the poisoning charge as one instrument in a broader struggle rather than as a separate event.

So the best-supported reconstruction is not an actual poisoning scene in Hermione’s house. It is the moment after Sarapion left it: some combination of humiliation inside his own household, already acute debt pressure, and a story that could be told in a way that put danger, blame, and public suspicion on Hermione.12

5. The wider family archive is even messier

A much larger Oxyrhynchite dossier, P.Oxy. 2.237, may preserve another part of the same dispute orbit, and it helps contextualize the poisoning case even if the exact linkage cannot be demonstrated at every point.3 The reason to put it beside P.Oxy. 3.472 and 3.486 is not one shared name alone, but a cluster of overlaps: Dionysia, Chaeremon, maternal property under dispute, mortgage and archive procedure, and the same kind of repeated petitioning through Oxyrhynchite and Alexandrian channels.23 Its surviving Greek is damaged, but the old edition’s translation preserves a long struggle involving Dionysia, her father Chaeremon, her husband Horion, maternal property, mortgage registration, and repeated petitions up and down the chain of command.3

In this text Chaeremon is represented as repeatedly attacking Dionysia over money and possession, trying to reclaim what he had transferred, and even attempting to take her away from her husband’s house against her will.3 Dionysia’s side insists that her rights over the property had already been examined by the strategus on the evidence of the archive keepers, that earlier petitions had established the validity of the registrations, and that her father kept returning with the same matter in altered forms.3

One line in the translation is especially revealing. Chaeremon, the text says, “renewed his attacks” and, once it was no longer possible to proceed effectively against her on the earlier property issue, turned his schemes in another direction.3 Elsewhere the petition calls parts of his campaign a pretext for plotting against her.3 Whether or not every surviving section belongs to exactly the same stage of the family quarrel, the atmosphere is unmistakable: one household, many petitions, endless re-description of the same conflict.

That matters for Sarapion’s accusation because it shows the environment in which it landed. If P.Oxy. 2.237 belongs to the same dispute orbit, Hermione and Dionysia were not living in a quiet documentary world where every dispute had one clean subject. They were in the middle of a social and legal ecosystem in which money, marriage, maternal provision, paternal authority, and archive procedure were all already fused.123

Once that was true, poison could become one more story people told about the same family.

6. Officials in Egypt knew exactly this trick

The most revealing contextual evidence is not even comparative in the usual sense. It sits inside P.Oxy. 2.237 itself, where the petitioner appended copies of earlier legal opinions and decrees.3 One of them, a proclamation of Valerius Eudaemon, says that many debtors, when pressed for payment, try to evade or postpone repayment by threatening to bring a more serious charge.3 Some, the decree says, hope to terrify their creditors into accepting less; others hope that the mere threat of an accusation will make creditors give up their claims.3

That is strikingly close to the logic implied by the Sarapion dossier. P.Oxy. 3.472 says he was being ruined by creditors and had reasons for self-poisoning.1 P.Oxy. 3.486 says his property claim involved land whose price had been paid not only to his father but also to the father’s creditors.2 And P.Oxy. 2.237 preserves a prefectural decree showing that administrators recognized this tactic as a real problem. The decree is contextual rather than evidentiary: it does not prove Sarapion’s intent, but it does make this reading of the dossier more historically plausible.3

No single line says, therefore Sarapion invented the poisoning accusation because of debt. That would be too tidy. What the dossier does give us is stronger than tidiness. It gives us a documented pattern recognized by Roman administrators themselves, and it places Sarapion inside exactly the sort of debt-loaded dispute in which that pattern operated.123

If we are looking for the real story under the rumor, this is where it begins to come into view.

7. Fact in these cases came from inspection, not talk

Some Roman Egyptian records also show what officials could expect when bodily harm was serious enough to prove. In those cases we often see inspection, measurement, witnesses, and medical reporting.4567

A third-century BCE letter from P.Sorb. 1.9 discusses what one man accuses another of and says that certain bodies are said to be available for examination, ἐτασμὸς τῶν κατηγορουμένων σωμάτων.4 A later public doctor from Hermopolis reports formally on injuries under official instruction.5 P.Brem. 2 speaks of inspectors selected from other nomes.6 Petitions for assault or violent attack regularly describe wounds, summon officials, and request written action rather than relying on mood or rumor.789

That comparison does not prove that no poisoning took place. Poison is harder to inspect than a broken lip or visible bruising. What it does show is that the documentary culture had ways of trying to convert accusation into evidence. In Sarapion’s case, by contrast, the surviving emphasis falls elsewhere: not on medical proof, but on sequence, motive, mortgage, missing documents, and who said what after leaving whose house.12

That is the texture of rumor entering law. The paperwork is thick. The proof is thin.

8. The papyri have a whole vocabulary for people doing this to each other

Once we widen out from Hermione’s household, the dossier sits inside a long documentary history of rumor, slander, false accusation, and legal opportunism.

A letter of 115 CE uses φημιζόμενα, “things being reported,” to describe alarming current talk bad enough to keep a sister awake for fear of her brother’s safety.10 Ptolemaic documents warn purchasers not to συκοφαντήσειν or διαβαλεῖν those subject to them.11 Zenon archive letters worry that people may be συκοφαντηθῶσι, falsely harassed or slandered.12 Others complain directly, συκοφαντοῦμαι, “I am being slandered / vexatiously attacked.”13

False witness appears as its own legal category: ψευδομαρτυρίου, ψευδομαρτυρίαι.1415 Accusation itself, κατηγορεῖν, is ordinary documentary language, but the records show how easily it could be paired with pressure, coercion, or procedural maneuver.41617 In late antique petitions defendants complain that charges have been brought against them under various heads, ἐπὶ διαφόροις κεφαλαίοις, and that accusations are being heaped together in court.18

Even casual letters sometimes preserve the same social reflex. One writer jokes or threatens, συκοφαντήσω δέ σοι, “I will bring a vexatious charge against you,” the sort of line that only works if everyone knows the idiom.19 Another private letter complains of an ἐπιβουλή, a plot or scheme, that has succeeded in alienating allies.20

This broader archive does not make Sarapion’s accusation false by itself. It does show that Oxyrhynchus and its documentary neighbors had no shortage of scripts for turning resentment into accusation and accusation into administrative trouble.1011121314151617181920

9. Women with property were especially exposed to this kind of pressure

Hermione was not accused in a vacuum. The papyri are full of women whose money, dowries, land, or contracts become the center of disputes pursued by men through petitions and procedure.321222324

P.Col. 4.83 is one of many Ptolemaic petitions in which debt to a wife becomes the pivot of a larger complaint.21 P.Oxy. 1.70 insists that a written transaction has both trust and truth, πίστιν καὶ ἀλήθειαν.22 Other Oxyrhynchite texts track mortgages, archive deposits, property statements, and disputes over who actually had the right to what had been registered.232425

The embedded legal opinions in P.Oxy. 2.237 are especially important here. They insist that an adult woman may stay with the husband she chooses, that fathers cannot simply use legal pretexts to pull daughters back at will, and that publicly deposited contracts are valid and enforceable.3 Those texts were not copied into Dionysia’s petition by accident. They were copied because family authority, marriage, and money were all being fought over at once.3

Seen against that background, Hermione’s position becomes clearer. She was dangerous to accuse not because she was helpless, but because she was documented. A woman with capital, children, agreements, and archived rights could be attacked precisely through the same paper structures that protected her.123

10. An interpretation

What follows is an interpretation rather than a recoverable transcript of events. The archive does not preserve anybody’s private thoughts. What it does preserve is enough pattern, motive, and sequence to let one reading stand out above the others.123

Sarapion wanted a place in Hermione’s household that Hermione did not give him.1 The speech’s language is too pointed to make that easy to flatten away: jealousy, self-styling as her husband, the pain of a rejected lover.1 At the same time he was entangled in debt and in a family property situation crowded with creditors, mortgages, and inheritance pressure.12 Those two things together, wounded desire and financial exposure, are the real engine of the story.

In that reading, the poisoning accusation was not the beginning of the conflict. It was the most dramatic form the conflict could take. Sarapion left Hermione’s house without saying he suspected anything.1 Only later, back inside his own household, did he begin saying that he had been poisoned.1 That is the moment the social story becomes imaginable: not a woman slipping him poison under everyone’s nose, but a man returning home humiliated, endangered, and in need of a version of events that would put Hermione on the defensive.

Once he said the word poison, the rest of the city could do the work for him. A visit to a woman’s house, a later cry of illness, existing fights over land, a missing slave, talk of deeds and mortgages: that is already enough material for a neighborhood to build a scandal.12 And in Roman Egypt scandal had administrative force. It could become a petition, a counter-petition, a hearing, a delay, an expense, a trip to Alexandria, another season of uncertainty.23

My own reading is that Sarapion’s real grievance was that Hermione and then Dionysia would not let him convert intimacy into leverage.12 He could not become Hermione’s acknowledged man. He could not easily recover the land sale on the terms he wanted. He could not outmatch the archive. On that reading, he reached for the accusation that would travel fastest, frighten hardest, and stain the household most deeply.123

That is why the prefectural decree copied into the wider family dossier matters so much. It shows that Roman administrators already knew this pattern: debtors and litigants tried to stall money claims by escalating them into graver accusations.3 The decree does not name Sarapion. It does something more useful. It tells us that his behavior fits a recognized social script.

So the rumor that probably circulated through Oxyrhynchus was simple: Hermione poisoned a man who had visited her. The interpretation that seems to me to fit the dossier best is harsher and more ordinary. This may have been a property fight and a romantic injury that learned how to speak the language of poison.123

That is why this dossier feels so modern. It is not just about crime. It is about narrative control. Who gets to say what happened after everyone has left the house.
