Because I Am a Greek

Ptolemaios Son of Glaukias, the Great Serapeum at Memphis, 172--152 BCE

Temple Confinement Ethnic Conflict Dreams Greek-Egyptian Relations Ptolemaic Era Memphis

I. The Man Who Could Not Leave

The Great Serapeum at Memphis sat on the desert plateau at Saqqara, overlooking the Nile valley and the white walls of the oldest capital in Egypt. Below it, tunneled into the living rock, were the burial vaults of the sacred Apis bulls – the living incarnation of Ptah, chief god of Memphis. The temple complex sprawled across the sand: the main Serapeum dedicated to Sarapis (the Hellenized Osiris-Apis), the Anoubieion dedicated to Anubis, the Asklepieion dedicated to the healing god, the Astartieion dedicated to the Semitic goddess Astarte. Between them ran the dromos, the sacred way, a corridor of crouching stone sphinxes baking in the sun.3

Ptolemaios son of Glaukias was a Macedonian – not merely Greek, but from the military settler class that had ruled Egypt since Alexander’s successors carved it up in the late fourth century BCE.4 His father Glaukias was a settled soldier in the village of Psichis in the Heracleopolite nome. The family had land and status. When Ptolemaios calls himself Macedonian in his petitions, he is not giving color. He is reminding the officials reading them that he belongs to the ruling people.

And yet this man could not leave a room.

Sometime around October 172 BCE, Ptolemaios entered the Serapeum and became one of the people “held” by the god.5 Sarapis had seized him, and he could not leave. Not would not. Could not. He says so over and over across two decades of petitions.6 He lived in a priestly chamber within the Astartieion, and his world shrank to those walls and the few courtyards he could reach without crossing out of the sacred enclosure.

Was it a vow? A divine command received in a dream? A form of temple asylum that hardened into permanence? Scholars have debated this for a century, and the documents do not resolve it.7 What they do resolve is the lived reality: Ptolemaios was stuck. He depended on others to bring him food, to carry his petitions to the strategos, to represent him in legal proceedings he could never attend. He watched the world through the narrow aperture of papyrus – writing, always writing, to people who could walk through doors he could not.

II. Year Nine: The Girl Who Sought Asylum

Around 164 BCE, in his ninth year of confinement, Ptolemaios wrote to the king and queen about a girl named Herakleia.

She had come to the temple seeking asylum – a common recourse for the vulnerable in Ptolemaic Egypt, where sacred precincts offered a measure of protection that the law outside could not guarantee.8 Ptolemaios had taken her in. He says he adopted her, since he had no children of his own, and she performed services for him because he was in katoche and could not care for himself.9

Then a temple rod-bearer named Zoilos – a rhabdouchos, one of the minor temple police – took a bribe and handed the girl over to soldiers from the garrison at Memphis.10 Ptolemaios, immobile, could do nothing but write. He begged the kings to intervene. The petition mentions, almost in passing, that his father Glaukias had “departed to the gods” – died – and Ptolemaios could not leave the temple even to bury him.11

That phrase deserves a pause. A man’s father dies in a village somewhere in Middle Egypt. The son, confined by divine command, cannot travel to wash the body, cannot perform the rites, cannot stand at the grave. He learns of it by letter and writes to the king about a stolen girl. The grief is buried beneath the legal formula. But it is there.

III. The Revolt and the Raid

The violence against Ptolemaios did not happen in a vacuum. The 160s BCE were a catastrophic decade for Ptolemaic Egypt.

Between 170 and 168 BCE, the Seleucid king Antiochus IV Epiphanes invaded Egypt twice, pushing as far as Memphis itself. Only the intervention of Rome – the famous “Day of Eleusis,” when the Roman envoy Gaius Popillius Laenas drew a circle in the sand around Antiochus and told him to decide before he stepped out of it – forced the Seleucids to withdraw.12 In 164–163 BCE, the Ptolemaic royal family fractured: Ptolemy VI Philometor was briefly expelled from Alexandria by his younger brother Ptolemy VIII Euergetes II, and the two brothers divided the kingdom before an uneasy reconciliation.13

And beneath these dynastic convulsions, a native Egyptian revolt – the apostasis referenced in Ptolemaios’s petition – simmered through the countryside.14 Dionysios Petosarapis led an uprising that expressed, among other things, Egyptian resentment of Greek overlordship. The temple complexes at Memphis, where Greeks and Egyptians lived and worked side by side in an uneasy symbiosis, became pressure points. An Egyptian sweeper with a bronze scraper in his hand and a Greek recluse who could not run – the geometry of the violence writes itself.

Shortly before the beating on Phaophi 11, another raid had already occurred. A man named Ptolemaios (appointed by the archiphylakites in the Anoubieion) and Amosis, agent of the chief priest, arrived at the Astartieion with temple guards, claiming they had intelligence that weapons were hidden inside.15 They searched the entire chapel. They found nothing. But the search itself was the message: you are suspect. You are foreign. We can enter your room whenever we wish.

In a separate petition to the king and queen about the same series of events, Ptolemaios adds a detail absent from the version sent to the strategos. The raiders did not merely search. They ransacked “the inner sanctum of the goddess” – to adyton tes theas – and stole property from the deposits of the other katochoi, the other recluses confined in the temple.16 The Astartieion held not just Ptolemaios but others like him, people who had been “seized” by the divine and could not leave. Their belongings were taken. Ptolemaios, unable to appear in court, asked the king to appoint a Cretan named Demetrios son of Sosos to represent him.17

IV. The Twins

Sometime before 164 BCE, two Egyptian girls arrived at the Serapeum starving.

Their names were Thaues and Taous – the spellings shift across the papyri into Tagetes, Tages, Taus, Thaous, a dozen variants that suggest scribes struggled with Egyptian names rendered into Greek script.18 They were twins. Their story, told across a sequence of petitions that Ptolemaios drafted on their behalf, is one of the most harrowing family narratives to survive from antiquity.

Their mother, a woman named Nephorys, had abandoned their father and taken up with a soldier named Philippos son of Sogenos from the Memphis garrison. Philippos attempted to murder the twins’ father. The man escaped by jumping into the river, swimming to an island, and being picked up by a passing boat.19 He fled to the Heracleopolite nome. There, the petition says with devastating concision, “he died of grief.”20

His brothers brought the body back to the Memphis necropoleis. But Nephorys – the wife who had arranged the murder – would not bury him.21 She sold half the family house for seven talents, collected rent of fifteen hundred drachmas a month from the other half, and gave nothing to her daughters.22

The twins, orphaned and dispossessed, went up to the Serapeum. They went to Ptolemaios.

The petition describes the encounter with a precision that suggests it mattered to everyone involved: “a certain Ptolemaios, one of those in katoche in the great Serapieion, who was a friend of our father, and going up to him we were sustained.”23 He was their father’s friend. He took them in. He fed them.

Why? The most detailed version of the petition, UPZ 1.20, offers an extraordinary answer. Ptolemaios cared for the twins not from personal charity but kata prostagma tou theou – “by command of the god.”24 The deity himself – Sarapis, or perhaps the Apis – had ordered Ptolemaios to look after them. And the god’s command was so thorough, so visible in its daily expression, that strangers who observed Ptolemaios caring for the twins assumed he was their father: “most people who do not know say, on account of the care he exercises over us, that he is our father.”25

A man confined to a temple by divine seizure, caring for two orphaned girls by divine command, mistaken by passersby for their father. The layers of obligation – religious, social, emotional – are impossible to disentangle. Perhaps Ptolemaios did not try.

V. Mourning the Bull

On April 6, 164 BCE, the sacred Apis bull Ta-Renenutet II died.26

This was not a minor event. The death of an Apis bull triggered a seventy-day mourning period modeled on the mythology of Osiris – the same span the Egyptians allotted to the embalming of a pharaoh.27 The dead bull became Osorapis, the Osiris-Apis, and was buried in the underground galleries beneath the dromos with full divine honors. A new pair of twin sisters was needed to perform the ritual mourning, playing the roles of Isis and Nephthys who had wept over Osiris at his death.

Thaues and Taous were chosen.

The twins became ritual functionaries of the state, pouring libations to Osorapis on behalf of King Ptolemy and Queen Cleopatra, “the gods Philometores.”28 In exchange, they were entitled to an annual stipend from the royal treasury: one metretes of sesame oil, one metretes of castor oil, baked loaves from the Asklepieion, and grain.29 The rations were administered through a chain of bureaucrats – the sub-dioiketes Sarapion, the epimelete Mennides, subordinate officials named Theon – and paid through the state bank.

In theory.

In practice, the rations did not arrive. The twins went hungry. And Ptolemaios – their guardian, their stand-in father, the man the god had commanded to look after them – began to write.

VI. The Ration War

What follows in the archive is a bureaucratic siege. Between 163 and 160 BCE, Ptolemaios and the twins produced petition after petition after petition – to the sub-dioiketes, to the epimelete, to the king and queen directly – demanding the rations that the state owed and the priests withheld.30

The sheer volume is staggering. UPZ 1.17: the twins request their annual oil.31 UPZ 1.39 and 1.40: the twins petition Sarapion about delays.32 UPZ 1.41: the twins petition the king about unpaid oil and castor oil for years 20 and 21.33 UPZ 1.44: Ptolemaios and the twins petition together.34 UPZ 1.45: Ptolemaios identifies himself as “son of Glaukias, Macedonian, overseer of the twins in the great Serapieion, being in katoche now already for the eleventh year.”35 UPZ 1.46 through 1.48: multiple versions of the same complaint – the priests have not delivered what the twins are owed.36

And then the administrative trail: Mennides writes to Theon ordering oil delivery.37 Theon orders measurement of the oil.38 Demetrios the Cretan, Ptolemaios’s legal representative, receives the oil on the twins’ behalf.39 Receipts are issued, bank records filed, dossier inventories compiled.40 A note survives listing what the twins should receive from the Asklepieion: “three baked loaves per day from the mourning period until the present day.” And then, in a different hand: “they have not received them.”41

Three loaves a day. That was the promise. The reality was hunger, and paperwork, and Ptolemaios writing draft after draft in his pastophorion, unable to walk to the grain store and demand the bread himself.

VII. The Family in the Village

While Ptolemaios fought for the twins’ rations, his own family was disintegrating.

His father Glaukias had died. His three brothers – Hippalos, Sarapion, and Apollonios – remained in the village of Psichis, and without the protection of an elder brother or a father, they became targets. In his twelfth year of katoche, around 161–160 BCE, Ptolemaios petitioned the king: his brothers were being harassed by local officials, and he begged the royal house to write to Kydias, strategos of the Heracleopolite nome, to protect them.42 He added: “they provide me with bread and sustain me.”43 Without his brothers, he would starve.

By his thirteenth year, the family home at Psichis had been demolished. Neighbors named Hesperos, Ataios, and Polemon had torn the house apart, stealing building materials worth twenty talents, and walled off the courtyard and building site.44 They did it, Ptolemaios wrote, “showing contempt because I cannot leave the temple.”45 A man who cannot walk through his own door cannot defend his own house two hundred kilometers away.

He asked the king to order the neighbors to hand over the property to “Apollonios, my younger brother,” and to repay the twenty talents for the stolen materials, “so that they can be brought to me here and I may have what I need and not waste away from hunger.”46

Hina me limo diaphtharomai. So that I may not be destroyed by hunger. The phrase recurs, in various forms, across the archive. Ptolemaios is always on the edge of starvation. The god holds him; the world forgets to feed him.

VIII. Apollonios

The youngest brother, Apollonios, is the most vivid secondary figure in the archive. He is called paidarion – “the child” – in early documents, and he grew up in Ptolemaios’s shadow, eventually entering the Serapeum himself.47

By 158–157 BCE, a petition reveals that Apollonios had been in katoche in the Astartieion alongside his elder brother for fifteen years – meaning he entered the temple around 172 BCE, the same year as Ptolemaios, when he was perhaps no older than a boy.48 He too was beaten. Three sons of a woman named Teebesis entered his priestly chamber and assaulted him.49 The violence against Greeks in the Serapeum was not limited to Ptolemaios alone.

Apollonios addressed Ptolemaios as “father” – patri – in his letters, a term that may reflect the age gap between them, or Ptolemaios’s role as guardian, or both.50 He ran errands. He handled money. A financial letter from 159 BCE has him accounting to Ptolemaios for silver coins, the sale of a linen cloth, the sale of a garment.51 He is the hands and feet Ptolemaios does not have – the man who walks through doors, who goes to Herakleopolis about the house, who collects debts from Petosiris and Semphtheas and Sarapion.52

And he has nightmares.

In August 152 BCE – one of the last letters in the archive – Apollonios writes to Ptolemaios: “I am having wicked dreams; I see Menedemos pursuing me.”53 Menedemos was the official in the Anoubieion, the man the strategos had appointed to enforce justice in the temple precinct. That he appears in Apollonios’s dreams as a pursuer tells us something about the psychological atmosphere of the Serapeum that no petition can convey.

IX. The Dream Diary

Ptolemaios kept a dream diary.

On a papyrus dated to June 158 BCE, he recorded his own dreams and those of the twins, preserving a sequence of nighttime visions that read like dispatches from a parallel world.54

Tages – one of the twins – dreamed of walking down through a quarter, counting nine houses. She wanted to turn back. “They” – unnamed presences – told her: “Yes, it is time to go.” She replied: “It is late for me.”55 We do not know who “they” are. We do not know what was late. But the dream’s geography – walking down, counting, wanting to turn back, being told it was time – has the texture of a journey that cannot be reversed. We may imagine Ptolemaios sitting in his pastophorion, recording the dream of a girl who dreamed of leaving.

His own dreams are stranger. He dreamed of Tages “with a beautiful voice, sweeter in tone and in good condition,” and of Taous “laughing, and her foot large and clean.”56 These are not erotic dreams; they are dreams of well-being, of the girls healthy and whole, dreams that counter the daily reality of hunger and bureaucratic neglect.

In another dream, two men appeared before him and counted out money: “one hundred drachmas for me, and for Tagetes the twin a purse full of stater coins.” They called it “the money of blood.”57 Ptolemaios’s comment, recorded alongside the dream, is wry: “She has more money than I do.”

And then there is the dream of the cow.

“I imagined in my sleep calling upon the greatest Ammon to come from the north to me… I imagined I was a cow in labour. He seizes the cow and throws her down. He puts his hand into her womb and extracts a bull.”58

The symbolism is unmistakable. The Apis bull was believed to be born of a cow struck by divine lightning – a miraculous conception, a theophany in animal form. Ptolemaios, the Greek recluse in an Egyptian temple, dreamed himself into the central myth of the place that held him captive. He was the cow. The god reached inside him and drew forth something sacred.

A dream interpreter named Nechthonbes was consulted about some of these visions.59 The name is Egyptian. The practice of oneiromancy – dream interpretation at sacred sites – was ancient in Egypt and well attested at the Serapeum, where pilgrims came specifically to sleep within the temple precincts and receive divine messages through incubation.60 Ptolemaios, the Macedonian who could not leave, inhabited the same dream-world as the Egyptian priests who wanted him gone.

X. Despondent

A note survives – brief, undated, perhaps never sent – in which Ptolemaios writes: “I am despondent about my katoche and about Tagetes. Where shall we live?”61

Oligopsycho. The word means “small-souled,” “faint-hearted,” a state of spiritual diminishment that sits somewhere between depression and despair. It is the only moment in the archive where Ptolemaios’s composure breaks entirely. His petitions are formal, structured, controlled – the work of a man who understands bureaucratic rhetoric. This note is something else. It is a cry written on scrap papyrus: I am despondent. Where shall we live.

The question hangs unanswered. The archive does not tell us what happened to Tages. It does not tell us where they lived, or if the food arrived, or if the priests relented. It tells us that he wrote the question down.

XI. The Household Accounts

Between the petitions and the dreams, the archive preserves something unexpected: the daily shopping lists.

UPZ 1.89, dated to January 159 BCE, is a household expense account for the twins. It records purchases of cakes, garlic, lettuce, pomegranates, nuts, radishes, beer – zyton, the Egyptian barley beer – goose meat, salt, honey, soda (nitron, used for cleaning), sycamore beer, and sesame cakes.62 There are payments to “side-guards” – plagiophylakes, perhaps the temple security detail. And at the bottom: “Harmais’ account: four hundred drachmas.”63

Harmais. The same man who was beaten with bronze scrapers on the dromos. He is still there, years later, still serving, still being paid. His name runs through the financial records like a thread: six hundred drachmas here, four hundred there, payments for linen, payments from Sarapis and Horos and Aphrodisios.64

Other accounts document the texture of confinement with relentless specificity. Water supply records count the keramia – clay jars – delivered daily.65 Clothing accounts list sindonas (linen garments), bapta (dyed goods), ekmigeia (wax impressions, perhaps for seals).66 A grain distribution record from August 158 BCE tracks individual allotments.67 An account from March 158 BCE records expenses for the mourning of the Mnevis bull – the sacred bull of Heliopolis, a counterpart to the Apis – including grain, throne-covers, pure bread, salt, and soda.68

Through these lists, we can reconstruct a household. It is not a prison. Ptolemaios eats pomegranates and goose. He drinks beer. He has linen clothes. He handles money, hires servants, keeps accounts. But he cannot walk to the market to buy the pomegranates himself. Everything comes to him through intermediaries, through the slow machinery of delivery and receipt and record-keeping that substitutes for the ordinary freedom of stepping outside.

XII. A Letter About Learning Egyptian

Among the miscellaneous papyri found in the archive is a letter that does not belong to Ptolemaios but illuminates his world from an unexpected angle.

“Learning that you are studying Egyptian letters,” the writer begins, “I rejoiced for you and myself, because now at least when you come to the city you will teach the children at the practice of Phalou…etes the enema-doctor, and you will have provision for your old age.”69

The recipient is unknown. The writer is unknown. But the letter was found in the Serapeum archive, and its subject – a Greek learning to read Egyptian – speaks directly to the bilingual, bicultural milieu in which Ptolemaios lived. The Serapeum was a place where Greek and Egyptian overlapped and interpenetrated, where a Macedonian recluse cared for Egyptian twins by divine command, where an Egyptian dream interpreter named Nechthonbes analyzed a Greek man’s dreams, where someone’s friend was learning demotic and being congratulated for it.

The ethnic hatred that drove the temple sweepers to attack Ptolemaios was real. But it was not the whole story. The archive shows Greeks and Egyptians entangled – commercially, ritually, emotionally – in ways that complicate any simple narrative of colonizer and colonized.

XIII. Sarapion's Wedding

In August 153 BCE, a letter arrived at the Serapeum from Ptolemaios’s brother Sarapion. It contained news of a different kind: “I have contracted marriage with the daughter of Hesperos. I intend to bring her in during the month of Mesore.”70

Hesperos. The name appears elsewhere in the archive – as one of the neighbors who demolished the family home at Psichis and stole twenty talents’ worth of building materials.71 The man who tore down Ptolemaios’s house, or perhaps his son, was now giving his daughter in marriage to Ptolemaios’s brother.

We cannot be certain these are the same Hesperos. The name is not uncommon. But if they are, the marriage represents something more interesting than a love match: a settlement, a reconciliation, a merger of hostile families through the oldest diplomatic technology available. The property dispute may have been resolved not by the strategos or the king but by a wedding.

Ptolemaios, of course, could not attend.

XIV. The Last Letters

The archive thins after 156 BCE. Apollonios writes in October 154 about administrative matters.72 In August 152, he sends the letter about his wicked dreams and Menedemos the pursuer.73 In September of the same year, another letter from Apollonios to Ptolemaios “the father,” strained and defensive: “I swear by Sarapis, if I were not somewhat embarrassed, you would not see my face.”74

What embarrassment? We do not know. The letters grow shorter, more allusive, harder to read. Arabs named Myroulla and Chalbas write to their brother Dakoutes about “an unpleasant incident.”75 Apollonios writes to Sarapion asking for grain: “I have given Ptolemaios the two hundred drachmas.”76 The household is still functioning, still circulating money and food, still tethered to the man in the pastophorion who cannot leave.

And then, in February 145 BCE – seven years after the main archive falls silent – a man named Paniskos writes to Ptolemaios about the administration of Egyptian contracts in the Thebaid.77 It is the latest datable text that mentions Ptolemaios by name. If he was still alive, he was in his late fifties or sixties, and he had been in the temple for twenty-seven years.

XV. The Discovery

The papyri of Ptolemaios son of Glaukias were found by Auguste Mariette in 1850–51.

Mariette had come to Egypt on behalf of the Louvre to purchase Coptic manuscripts. Instead, he noticed the head of a sphinx protruding from the sand at Saqqara and began to dig.78 What he found was the dromos – the processional avenue of sphinxes – and beyond it, the entrance to the underground galleries where the Apis bulls were buried. He also found, scattered through the rooms and corridors of the temple complex above, the papyrus archive that would become one of the most studied collections of personal documents from the ancient world.

The papyri were dispersed. Roughly sixty went to the Louvre. About thirty ended up in the British Library. Fifteen reached the National Museum of Antiquities in Leiden. Others scattered to the Vatican Library, the Hermitage Museum, and smaller collections.79 Ulrich Wilcken published the definitive edition in his Urkunden der Ptolemaerzeit (UPZ), Band I in 1927 and Band II beginning in 1934. Dorothy Thompson’s Memphis Under the Ptolemies (1988, second edition 2012) remains the standard scholarly study of the milieu in which Ptolemaios lived.80

In the nineteenth century, the German novelist Georg Ebers wrote a novel based on the archive, The Sisters, imagining the twins’ story for a popular audience.81 The archive inspired fiction because it reads like fiction – a man trapped in a temple, two orphan girls, a murdered father, a stolen house, dreams of bulls and blood money. But the papyri are not fiction. They are tax receipts and petitions and shopping lists and a note that says oligopsycho – I am small-souled, I am fading, where shall we live.

XVI. What We Do Not Know

The archive never explains why Ptolemaios entered the Serapeum in the first place, or what exactly “being held” by the god meant in daily practice. It does not tell us whether he ever left. It simply thins out and stops.

The same happens to the people around him. The twins vanish after years of petitions and missed rations. Apollonios vanishes after the bad dreams. Harmais vanishes after the beating. The promised bread appears and disappears in memoranda, but no final reckoning tells us whether the household was ever securely fed.

What remains is the pressure of a life lived inside walls. More than a hundred papyri preserve petitions, account notes, letters, dream records, and scraps of complaint from a man who could not go out and so kept trying to reach the world on paper. The line that survives best is still the one he wrote after the attack: “because I am a Greek.” He used it to explain why the sweepers came for him. It also explains the whole unstable position he occupied at Memphis – privileged and exposed at once, able to appeal upward and unable to defend himself in the room where he slept.

The documents were buried when the temple became ruin and the ruin became sand. They do not give us an ending. They leave Ptolemaios where they found him: still writing.
