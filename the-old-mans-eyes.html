The Old Man's Eyes

Pappos, Strouthos, and One Artaba of Grain, 222 BCE

Old Age Family Support Grain Petitions Ptolemaic Egypt Arsinoite Nome

4 primary documents · 7 notes

What the son owed

One artaba and four drachmas was not symbolic.

An artaba of wheat was a real monthly ration, the kind of amount that belongs to subsistence rather than ceremony.2 The cash beside it would cover what grain could not: oil, fuel, cloth, small household needs, perhaps some debt.12 Pappos was not claiming that Strouthos had failed in sentiment. He was claiming that Strouthos had stopped maintaining him.

The educational detail makes the petition sting harder. Pappos does not present himself as a helpless dependant who merely happened to have a son. He says he taught the boy grammar.1 In the Ptolemaic countryside, that meant labor, time, and ambition directed into a child who was meant to function in a written world.3 Pappos is defining what the son owes by naming what the father had already invested.

Then the petition turns uglier. Pappos says that Strouthos not only withholds support but reviles him whenever he meets him, insulting him “with the ugliest abuse” λοιδορεῖ με τὰ αἴσχιστα.1 What survives, then, is not silent neglect. It is a domestic economy broken into open contempt.

Why he petitioned

Pappos was writing from Ghoran in the Arsinoite nome, and the villages of this district had learned to turn small private injuries into royal paperwork.456

Other petitions from the same packet involve land, bathhouse violence, detention, and quarrels over paternal property.456 The scale changes from case to case. The habit does not. When a local arrangement failed, someone took it upward on papyrus.

That is what gives Pappos’s complaint its shape. He was not only appealing to pity. He was turning old age into something administratively legible: a promised ration, a named son, a named official, and arrears.

The petition system did not guarantee justice, but it gave ordinary people a way to drag family failure into the king’s file.7 Here the file preserves just enough to show the pressure of the arrangement. A father who had once taught grammar now needed a scribe. A son who should have been sending wheat and cash was sending insults instead.

What remains

We do not have Strouthos’s answer. We do not know whether he claimed poverty, denied the agreement, or simply ignored the demand. We do not know how old Pappos was, only that he had become old enough to frame the whole matter in terms of age, instruction, and support withheld.1

But the surviving lines are enough. They reduce family obligation to a measurable quantity and then show it failing. One artaba of grain. Four drachmas. A father who says he made the son into someone. A son who leaves him to petition.

The most painful thing in the papyrus is its scale. This is not an inheritance worth hundreds of drachmas or a grand lawsuit over estates. It is a monthly ration. Old age, in this file, is what happens when survival becomes something your own child can interrupt.
