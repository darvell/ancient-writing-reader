Love & Desire

The Lead Tablet of Apellis

Love, Curses, and Choral Competition in Greek Sicily, c. 475--450 BCE

The Text

The tablet was found near Gela, though the precise circumstances of its discovery are not well documented in the archaeological record.4 It is a single sheet of lead, inscribed on one side, and its text is unusually long for a defixio of this period – over a dozen lines of densely packed Doric Greek, far exceeding the terse name-lists that make up most early Sicilian curse tablets.5 The translation, by Peter Wilson and Federica Favi, reads in full:

Prayer: Apellis, for love of Eunikos (prays) that no one be taken more seriously or be more popular than Eunikos, but (all) should praise and admire (him) both willingly and unwillingly. For Love of Eunikos I mark down all the khoragoi so that they be ineffectual both in word and in deed, along with their sons and fathers, and so that they fail both in the contest and outside the contests. Whoever does not leave him (sc. Eunikos) with me. I mark down Kaledias to keep him apart from Apellis, along with all those in between there and here. I mark down Sosias, the one from the shop of the Alkiadai, because of his love of (Mel)anthios. Pyrias, Mysskelos, Damophantos and their khoragos I mark down, along with their sons and fathers, and all the others who arrive here. May no one be taken more seriously than Eunikos either among men or women. As this lead (does), so may my spells against those men draw away their honour. For Eunikos may there be victory always, everywhere. On the lead for love of Eunikos I write.6

The tablet opens and closes with the same phrase: “for love of Eunikos.” The text is framed by that declaration. Everything between those two appearances sits inside the gravitational field of desire. Apellis does not merely curse; he explains why he curses. He curses because he loves.

The Names

The tablet names nine individuals, an extraordinary density for a document of its kind and period.

Apellis: the author. The man who writes, who curses, who loves. The name is a shortened form of Apollonidas or a cognate – common in Doric-speaking communities, attested across the western Greek world.7 Unusually for a defixio, Apellis names himself. Most curse tablets are anonymous: the curser hides behind the spell, leaving no signature, relying on the gods to know who asks and whom to curse. Apellis does not hide. He writes his name twice. He wants the gods – and perhaps Eunikos himself – to know who did this.

Eunikos: the beloved. The name means “good victory” or “victorious.”8 The name is itself a kind of charm: Apellis prays for Eunikos’s victory, and the beloved’s very name already contains that victory within it. “For Eunikos may there be victory always, everywhere.” The prayer and the name are one.

Who was Eunikos? The tablet tells us that he was involved in choral competition – the khoragoi are cursed on his behalf. He was someone over whom multiple men competed. He was, in the economy of desire that governed fifth-century Greek communities, a prize. Whether he was a chorus member himself – a singer, a dancer – or the object of attention from those who organized and financed choruses, the tablet does not make entirely clear. What is clear is that his social world was one of public performance and public admiration.

Kaledias: a rival. Apellis marks him down specifically “to keep him apart from Apellis” – the clearest statement of sexual rivalry in the text.9 Kaledias is someone who might come between Apellis and Eunikos, someone who might draw Eunikos away. The curse is a fence. Keep out.

Sosias: another figure in the web. He is identified with unusual precision: “the one from the shop of the Alkiadai” – apo tou kapeleiou Alkidan.10 This is not a household identification but a commercial one. Sosias works at or near a kapeleion, a tavern or retail shop, run by or associated with a family called the Alkiadai. And he is cursed not for his designs on Eunikos but “because of his love of (Mel)anthios” – epi tai Melanthiou philotati. Sosias loves Melanthios. The same word – philotati – that describes Apellis’s feeling for Eunikos describes Sosias’s feeling for Melanthios. A parallel desire. A parallel threat.

Why does Apellis curse Sosias for loving Melanthios? Perhaps Melanthios is connected to one of the rival choral groups, and Sosias’s devotion to him strengthens the competition. Perhaps the web of desire is more tangled than we can see – Melanthios may also desire Eunikos, or Sosias may be an ally of Kaledias. The tablet does not explain. It operates within a social world whose connections are obvious to the author and invisible to us. Rival choruses, drinking places, shopfronts, and repeated names all seem to have sat close enough together that one man’s attachment could become another man’s danger.

Melanthios: the man loved by Sosias. His name means “flower-man” or “of the dark flowers” – from melas (dark) and anthos (flower).11 He appears only as the object of Sosias’s love. But a man with this same name appears on another lead tablet from the same region and roughly the same period – ISic030032, a curse tablet listing individuals “inscribed for failure,” including one Melanthios.12 If this is the same man, he moved through a world where lead tablets circulated like weapons, where being named on a sheet of scored metal was an occupational hazard of social prominence.

Pyrias, Mysskelos, Damophantos: three men cursed together with their khoragos – their chorus leader or chorus sponsor.13 The grouping suggests they belong to a single choral troupe, a team competing against Eunikos’s group. Their unnamed khoragos is cursed along with them, and the curse extends to “their sons and fathers” – a formula that aims to destroy not merely the individuals but their entire family lines, their social capital, their standing in the community across generations.

The Alkiadai: the family whose shop (kapeleion) identifies Sosias. The name may derive from alkē (strength, valor) and suggests a prominent local family – perhaps merchants, perhaps tavern-keepers – whose commercial establishment served as a social landmark in the city.14

Phintionos (possibly): the tablet may also name a figure called Phintionos in a partially damaged line, mentioned as someone who should not be taken more seriously than Eunikos.15 If so, this is yet another rival or competitor – another node in the web.

Nine names produce a social network inscribed in lead.

The Word for Love

Philotati.

The word matters. It is the Doric dative of philotēs – a noun whose semantic range runs from friendship to affection to sexual love to the act of lovemaking itself.16 In Homer, philotēs can mean the bond between host and guest, the love between husband and wife, or the sexual act between lovers. In the Iliad, when Hera seduces Zeus on Mount Ida, the poet uses philotēs for their union: “and they lay together in love (philotēti) on the peak of Gargaron.”17 The word does not distinguish between emotional attachment and physical consummation. It covers both.

Apellis uses this word three times. At the opening: epi philotati tai Eunikou – “for the sake of the love of Eunikos.” At the close: epi philotati tai Eunikou grapho – “for the love of Eunikos I write.” And once more, describing Sosias: epi tai Melanthiou philotati – “because of the love of Melanthios.” The repetition is deliberate. The word is the tablet’s engine.

Apellis does not use erōs, the word for passionate, consuming, often painful desire – the force that Sappho called “limb-loosening” and Plato called divine madness.18 He does not use potheo, to long for, to yearn. He uses philotēs, a word that suggests not the torment of unrequited desire but the warmth of a bond that already exists, a relationship he is fighting to protect. Apellis is not pursuing Eunikos. He already has him. The curse is not to win love but to keep it.

This matters. The tablet is not a love spell – those would come later, in the Hellenistic and Roman periods, with their agonized prayers to make someone burn with desire.19 It is a defixio – a binding curse aimed at rivals, a weapon deployed within a competitive social field. Apellis is not asking the gods to change Eunikos’s heart. He is asking them to destroy everyone who threatens what already exists between them.

“Whoever does not leave him with me” – hoitines mē par’ em’ apolepoien.20 The pronoun em’ – “me” – is the only first-person word of possession in the tablet, and it is devastating. Leave him with me. He is mine. Do not take him.

The Chorus Leaders

The khoragoi – chorus leaders or chorus sponsors – are the tablet’s primary targets. Apellis curses them all, collectively and individually, “so that they be ineffectual both in word and in deed” – ep’ ateleiiai kēpeōn kai ergōn.21 The phrase is comprehensive: their words will fail, their actions will fail, their preparations will come to nothing. They will fail “both in the contest and outside the contests” – kēn agōni kekthos agōnōn. The curse is not limited to the performance itself. It extends to every aspect of their lives.

What were these contests? In the Greek world, choral performance was among the most important forms of public competition. At Athens, the Great Dionysia featured choruses of fifty men and fifty boys performing dithyrambs in honor of Dionysus, organized and financed by wealthy citizens called chorēgoi who competed for prestige through the quality of their choruses.22 The chorēgia – the act of sponsoring a chorus – was one of the most expensive and most visible forms of public service (leitourgia) that a wealthy citizen could perform. Pericles himself served as chorēgos for the first production of Aeschylus’s Persians in 472 BCE.23

But this is not Athens. This is Sicily. And the nature of choral competition in the western Greek colonies, while clearly attested, is less well documented than its Athenian counterpart. Peter Wilson, in his study of Sicilian choruses, has argued that the curse tablet itself constitutes some of our best evidence for the existence of organized choral competitions in Greek Sicily during the early fifth century.24 The tablet tells us that there were khoragoi – plural, multiple sponsors or leaders – competing against one another in formal agōnes (contests). It tells us that these competitions were important enough to provoke magical intervention. And it tells us that the world of choral performance overlapped with the world of male desire in ways that were, apparently, so well understood that they needed no explanation.

Choral competition and male desire were connected because the chorus was a site of beauty. Young men and boys trained their bodies for months, learning synchronized movement, coordinated singing, and the display of physical grace under public scrutiny.25 The chorus was where the community’s most beautiful young men were put on display. The symposium was where desire for them was openly expressed. The curse tablet is where those two worlds – performance and desire – intersect.

Consider the Athenian parallel. In Athens, kalos inscriptions on drinking vessels – “So-and-so is beautiful” – celebrated the beauty of named youths, many of whom were known to participate in choral and athletic competitions.26 The symposium was the social space where older men (erastai) courted younger men (eromenoi), and the beauty on display at the festival was the same beauty admired over wine. When Xenophon describes the symposium of Callias, the host is “enamoured of the boy Autolycus,” a young pancratium champion whose beauty “compelled every one to look at him” – a boy whose physical splendor at an athletic competition becomes the centerpiece of a drinking party.27

Apellis’s tablet reveals the same overlap in a Sicilian context. Eunikos is connected to the choral competitions. The khoragoi are his rivals or the sponsors of his rivals. And Apellis, who loves Eunikos, deploys magic to ensure that Eunikos triumphs – “for Eunikos may there be victory always, everywhere.” The beloved’s success in the public arena and the lover’s possession of the beloved in the private sphere are not separate concerns. They are the same concern.

The World of Lead

Apellis’s tablet did not exist in isolation. It emerged from a world saturated with lead magic.

The earliest surviving defixiones in the Greek world come from Sicily – specifically from the cities of Selinus (modern Selinunte), Akragas (Agrigento), and Gela – dating to the late sixth and early fifth centuries BCE.28 Some twenty-two tablets were found at Selinus alone, mostly from the early fifth century, constituting the oldest known corpus of Greek curse tablets anywhere in the Mediterranean.29 Sicily was not the periphery of this practice. It was the origin.

A near-contemporary tablet from the same region, ISic030032, dated to approximately 460–440 BCE, preserves a simpler but equally chilling curse: “These following are inscribed for failure” – hoi de gegrabbatai epi dyspragiai – followed by a list of names and the closing imprecation: “May all these be bloodless; wretched may they be.”30 Among the names listed: Melanthios. And Geloios. Both names appear in other inscriptions from this world – Geloios is the lover who declares his passion for Aka on the Gela graffito, repeating it twice for emphasis; Melanthios is the man loved by Sosias in Apellis’s tablet.31 Whether these are the same individuals cannot be proven. But the coincidence is remarkable. The same names, circulating through a small community, appearing on drinking cups and curse tablets and graffiti walls, connected by threads of desire and rivalry that we can only partially reconstruct.

Another tablet from Gela, ISic030010, dated to the same half-century, curses a man named Chamis, binding him “in the crisis of fatal illness so that he cannot achieve his goals” – ep’ ateleiiai, the same word for futility that Apellis uses against the khoragoi.32 The vocabulary of cursing was shared. The metaphors of binding – failure, futility, impotence – formed a common language. When Apellis sat down to write his tablet, he was drawing on a tradition, a technology, a set of formulas that his community understood.

And the technology was specific. Lead was the medium of binding magic not by accident but by design. Its physical properties – grey, cold, heavy, inert, resistant to decay – made it a natural analog for the state the curser wished to impose on the victim.33 When Apellis writes, “As this lead, so may my spells against those men draw away their honour” – hos houtos ho bolimos – he is invoking sympathetic magic, the principle that like affects like.34 As the lead is cold, so may the rivals grow cold. As the lead is buried, so may their ambitions be buried. As the lead endures, so may the curse endure.

The formula “as this lead” (hos houtos ho bolimos) appears across the Greek curse-tablet tradition, from Sicily to Attica to the Black Sea coast. It is the fundamental spell-logic of the defixio: the tablet is not merely a message to the gods but a magical object in its own right, a material anchor for the spell’s power.35 When the tablet was folded – and many were folded, sometimes pierced with nails – the act of folding was itself part of the magic, a physical enactment of binding, of enclosure, of the victim’s entrapment.

Where was Apellis’s tablet deposited? The convention was burial – in a grave, in a sanctuary of chthonic deities, at a crossroads, near running water.36 The dead were the intermediaries. The tablet was placed among them so that the spirits of the underworld would carry out the curse. At Selinus, tablets were found in the sanctuary of Demeter Malophoros – the same goddess who appears on the great Selinunte victory inscription, one of the city’s divine protectors.37 At Athens, tablets were deposited in graves, in wells, in the Kerameikos cemetery. The geography of cursing was the geography of death.

Gela

The city behind the tablet deserves attention.

Gela was founded around 689 BCE by colonists from Rhodes and Crete – a Doric colony on the southern coast of Sicily, facing the open Mediterranean.38 By the early fifth century, it had become one of the most powerful cities in the Greek west. Its tyrants – Hippocrates and then Gelon – built an empire that stretched across eastern Sicily, and in 485 BCE Gelon transferred his capital to Syracuse, taking with him half of Gela’s citizen population.39 The city that remained was diminished but still substantial, still Greek, still a place where men trained choruses and competed for glory and wrote each other’s names on drinking cups.

At its peak, Gela may have housed 30,000 inhabitants.40 It was a city of temples and terracotta – famous across the Greek world for its painted vases and its magnificent architectural decorations, the antefixes and simas that adorned the rooflines of its sacred buildings.41 Aeschylus, the great Athenian tragedian, died at Gela in 456 or 455 BCE, and was buried there with public honors, his grave becoming a place of pilgrimage.42 The connection between Gela and choral performance is not incidental. This was a city that took performance seriously, that competed in the cultural arena of the Greek Mediterranean, that attracted poets and dramatists and – inevitably – the men who loved the performers.

The date of Apellis’s tablet – roughly 475–450 BCE – places it in the generation after Gelon’s depopulation of the city. This is a community recovering, rebuilding, reasserting itself. The choral competitions that the tablet attests may be part of that reassertion – civic festivals where the remaining citizens displayed their cultural vitality, their Greekness, their capacity to produce beauty under duress. In this context, Eunikos’s victory is not merely a personal triumph. It is a civic one.

The Symposium

The tablet makes better sense beside a few other Sicilian inscriptions from the same world of drinking, joking, and rivalry.43 A cup from Gela carries one of them:

Porkos returns this skyphos to the thiasos of the drinkers. Since he is in love with Phryna, it is not another who will take her. He has written, the one who reads will practice sodomy.44

Porkos returns a communal cup, boasts of his claim on Phryna, and finishes with a joke crude enough that no one needed it explained.45 The point is not that this text matches Apellis’s desire exactly. It places him in a local culture where erotic bragging and sexual mockery were written on drinking vessels and passed around by hand.

Another inscription from the same world, ISic020580, doubles down on the symposium’s erotic economy:

Geloios is in love with Aka. Geloios is in love with Aka. But it is Eumachos who pays.46

The line is half declaration, half joke. Geloios is the one in love; Eumachos is the one who pays.46 Money, desire, and performance belonged to the same public world.

A later inscription from the same region, ISic020594 (c. 420–380 BCE), is even simpler:

Tittabo, beloved.47

Tittabō philē. Two words. A name and an adjective. Beloved. The name Tittabo may be a nickname – perhaps onomatopoeic, from the sound of a bird (tittabizein, to chirp like a sparrow).48 Someone loved Tittabo enough to scratch the name into a surface and add the word for “dear.” That is all we have. Two words from the edge of silence.

Taken together, these pieces keep Apellis local. He did not write from nowhere. He wrote in a Sicilian Greek setting where names, rivalries, and erotic claims were already being scratched onto durable objects.

The Lovers

The tablet never tells us how Apellis and Eunikos were supposed to fit together. It gives no ages, no kinship terms, and no philosophical vocabulary. Apellis is simply the man who acts. He writes, curses, and asks that Eunikos be left with him.49 Eunikos is the one others admire, the one whose victory matters, the one around whom rivals gather. That is enough to show pursuit, attachment, and competition without forcing the pair into a later Athenian template they never use themselves.50 In that sense the tablet is striking for what it omits. There is no praise of beauty, no educational pose, no moral defense of the bond. There is only the struggle to keep hold of one particular man.

What matters on the lead is not theory but pressure. Someone else might take Eunikos. Someone else might outshine him in the contests. Apellis answers those threats by naming names and binding them.

The Economics of Magic

Who made the tablet? Did Apellis inscribe it himself, or did he commission a specialist?

The question matters because it touches on the social infrastructure of magical practice in the early Greek world. By the Hellenistic and Roman periods, professional magicians – goes (sorcerers), pharmakeis (potion-makers), agogimon specialists (attraction-spell writers) – plied a brisk trade in curse tablets and love spells, advertising their services to the lovesick and the vindictive.53 The great magical papyri of Roman Egypt preserve recipe books for such practitioners: “Take a lead tablet and write with a bronze stylus the following…” The spells were formulas; the clients paid for expertise.

But in the early fifth century BCE, the evidence for professional spell-writers in Sicily is slim. The Sicilian curse tablets of this period are highly individualized, varying widely in language, length, and sophistication – suggesting that they may have been written by the cursers themselves rather than produced to order by specialists.54 Apellis’s tablet is linguistically sophisticated – its vocabulary is rich, its syntax complex, its rhetoric purposeful – but that may simply mean that Apellis was a man of some education. The fact that his name appears elsewhere in the Gela epigraphic record – on a legal document, ISic030016, in which “Anthemokritos bade Apellis offer security” in what appears to be a dispute over cattle prices – suggests a man embedded in the economic life of the community, capable of writing legal and commercial texts as well as magical ones.55

If Apellis wrote the tablet himself, the act was deeply personal. He chose the lead, heated it or cold-worked it into a thin sheet, took a stylus, and pressed the letters into the soft metal, letter by letter and name by name. Then he folded it, carried it to a grave or sanctuary where the chthonic powers could receive it, and placed it in the earth.

The writing was itself a magical operation. The verb apographō that Apellis uses repeatedly (“I mark down,” “I register,” “I inscribe”) is not a neutral word for writing but a technical term drawn from legal and administrative language, meaning to register, to enroll, to add to an official list.56 When Apellis says “I mark down Kaledias,” he is performing a bureaucratic act in the realm of the supernatural: enrolling Kaledias on the register of the cursed, filing him in the archives of the underworld. The curse works like a document, and the gods play the clerks.

The Silence of Eunikos

We do not know what Eunikos thought.

The tablet is entirely Apellis’s voice. Eunikos does not speak, write, consent, or resist. He is the gravitational center of the text, the name around which every curse orbits, but he is absent from it as a person. He is present only as a beloved – the object of love, the cause of the prayer, the reason for the lead.

Did Eunikos know about the tablet? Did he ask for it? Was it a gift, a surprise, an unwanted intrusion? In the world of Greek erōs, the eromenos was supposed to be aloof – yielding only gradually, never appearing too eager, preserving the modesty that made the pursuit worthwhile.57 The lover chased; the beloved was chased. If Eunikos was playing this role, the tablet might be a desperate escalation – a lover’s attempt to shift the game in his favor by invoking powers beyond the social. If Eunikos welcomed Apellis’s attention, the tablet might be a collaborative weapon, a tool deployed by both of them against shared rivals.

We do not know. The lead is silent on this point. Eunikos is the most important person in the text and the one about whom we know the least.

This is a recurring problem in the archaeology of desire. The objects that survive – the curse tablets, the graffiti, the kalos inscriptions on pottery – record the desire of the desirer. They rarely record the response of the desired. When someone scratches “Geloios loves Aka” on a wall, we hear Geloios. We do not hear Aka. When someone writes on a cup that “the one who reads this will practice sodomy,” we hear the joker. We do not hear the butt of the joke. The archive of desire is an archive of one-sided declarations, of unilateral claims, of voices speaking into silence.

Apellis’s tablet is the fullest, richest, most emotionally urgent of these voices from Greek Sicily. But it is still only one voice. The beloved remains silent.

What Survives

Apellis’s tablet survives because lead survives. Papyrus rots. Wood burns. Wax melts. But lead endures in the soil for millennia, its soft surface holding the impressions of a stylus as faithfully as the day they were made. The material that Apellis chose for its magical properties – coldness, weight, association with death – also possessed the accidental property of archaeological permanence. His prayer endured because it was written on the one material that the earth does not consume.

This is the paradox of the defixio as a source for social history. We have Apellis’s curse because he intended it to be permanent – but permanent for the gods, not for us. He buried it so that it would work forever in the darkness. He did not intend for archaeologists to dig it up twenty-four centuries later and read it under electric light, parsing his Doric Greek for evidence of same-sex desire in the early classical period. The tablet was never meant to be read by human eyes again.

Before it became a source, it was a worked object: thin lead scored with names, folded and hidden in earth, meant to lie with the dead or the powers below them. That is the form in which Apellis trusted it.

And yet here we are, reading it. Knowing that a man named Apellis loved a man named Eunikos in a Greek city on the coast of Sicily, sometime around 470 BCE. Knowing that he cursed Kaledias and Sosias and Pyrias and Mysskelos and Damophantos and all the khoragoi and their sons and their fathers and everyone who might come between them. Knowing that he believed the lead could hold his prayer. Knowing that, in a way he never intended, it did.

The other documents from this world – the Porkos drinking cup, the Geloios graffito, the “beloved Tittabo” inscription – survive because they too were inscribed on durable materials: pottery, stone, metal. Together they compose a fragmentary mosaic of erotic life in a Greek colonial community: men loving men, men loving women, men paying for the privilege, men joking about sex, men cursing their rivals. The mosaic is incomplete. Most of the tiles are missing. But the ones that remain are vivid enough to suggest the whole.

Apellis did not know he was writing history. He was writing a prayer for Eunikos, for victory, for love that would not be taken from him. The lead held his words. The earth held the lead. Twenty-five centuries later, the words that were meant for the gods below have risen to the surface, carrying with them the voice of a man who loved, feared, and wrote in lead because he believed that writing could bind the world.

Epi philotati tai Eunikou graphō.

On the lead, for love of Eunikos, I write.

Further Reading

  • **Dover, Kenneth J.** *Greek Homosexuality*. London: Duckworth, 1978; 2nd ed. 1989. The foundational study of male same-sex desire in the ancient Greek world, drawing on vase-painting, literature, and legal texts.
  • **Wilson, Peter.** *The Athenian Institution of the Khoregia: The Chorus, the City, and the Stage*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2000. The definitive study of chorus sponsorship in classical Athens; essential for understanding the *khoragoi* in Apellis's tablet.
  • **Wilson, Peter.** "Sicilian Choruses." In P. Wilson (ed.), *The Greek Theatre and Festivals: Documentary Studies*. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2007, 351--377. The key scholarly treatment of the Apellis tablet in the context of western Greek choral competition.
  • **Faraone, Christopher A.** *Ancient Greek Love Magic*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1999. Comprehensive study of erotic magic in the Greek world, including curse tablets and attraction spells.
  • **Gager, John G.** *Curse Tablets and Binding Spells from the Ancient World*. New York: Oxford University Press, 1992. Anthology and analysis of defixiones with extensive commentary.
  • **Dubois, Laurent.** *Inscriptions grecques dialectales de Sicile*. 2 vols. Rome: Ecole francaise de Rome, 1989--2008. The standard corpus of Greek dialect inscriptions from Sicily, including the Gela and Selinus curse tablets.
  • **Eidinow, Esther.** *Oracles, Curses, and Risk Among the Ancient Greeks*. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2007. Innovative study of curse tablets as evidence for risk management and social competition in Greek communities.
  • **Halperin, David M.** *One Hundred Years of Homosexuality and Other Essays on Greek Love*. New York: Routledge, 1990. Influential theoretical study challenging modern categories of sexuality as applied to the ancient Greek world.
  • **Davidson, James.** *The Greeks and Greek Love*. London: Weidenfeld and Nicolson, 2007. A revisionist history arguing that Greek same-sex relationships were more diverse and egalitarian than the Dover model suggests.
  • **Dreher, Martin.** "Orality, Written Literacy, and Early Sicilian Curse Tablets." *Greece & Rome* 65.2 (2018): 176--195. Study of the relationship between oral and written practices in early Sicilian defixiones.
  • **Dunbabin, T.J.** *The Western Greeks*. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1948. Classic study of Greek colonization in Sicily and southern Italy.
  • **Murray, Oswyn, ed.** *Sympotica: A Symposium on the Symposion*. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1990. Collected essays on the symposium as a social institution.
  • **Hubbard, Thomas K., ed.** *Homosexuality in Greece and Rome: A Sourcebook of Basic Documents*. Berkeley: University of California Press, 2003. Essential sourcebook of primary texts in translation.
