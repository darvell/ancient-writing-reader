The Purple-Seller's House

Psates son of Abraamios, the Village of This, 592--616 CE

Purple Dye Trade Bilingual Documents House Sales Byzantine Egypt Coptic 6th–7th Century CE Upper Egypt

6 primary documents · 29 notes

What the deed secured

Psates and Maria were people from Panopolis who had lived in This for a long time.9 Panopolis was a cloth city, and the trade name that sits so casually in the deed, purple-seller, points back toward that larger textile world.1012 The family still held property there when the next paper in the archive was drawn up.11

That next paper came eight years later. By 14 July 600 CE both parents were dead.13 Their children divided the estate: Pachymios, Ioannes, and their sister Tarsene.14 The deed calls it a small estate.15 It still had enough in it to divide carefully. Movable property, immovable property, and self-moving property were all counted out; two men named Chariston, one a copper-worker and one an ironworker, appear among the assets.1617

Then the earlier sale comes back into view. Ioannes and Tarsene formally acknowledged that they had no claim against Pachymios or his heirs over the house their parents had sold him while alive.18 If they challenged that sale later, the penalty would be twelve gold nomismata.19

The house had cost just under three.1 Reopening the matter would cost four times that.

What Pachymios did with it

The later papers show Pachymios working from a more secure position than his siblings.

In 605 he hired a purple-worker named Dioskoros for two years.20 Dioskoros was to work alongside Pachymios’s other hired workers.21 The wages were paid in grain, nineteen artabas across the term, with food provided by the employer.22 If the worker walked out early, he owed a gold nomisma. If Pachymios dismissed him early, Pachymios still owed the full wage.23

A year later a man named Kallinikos borrowed from Pachymios and promised to repay in wine at the vintage.24 In 613 Kallinikos borrowed again on the same pattern.25 The house sale had not made Pachymios rich. It had helped place him where a workshop, credit, and property could stay in the same hands.

The lane in This

In 616 he bought another house in the village.26 The sellers were women who had inherited it, and the property stood on the lane of the guard-house.27 The price was thirteen keratia.28 One boundary of the new purchase already touched the house of the buyer Pachymios.29

The archive ends there: a house sold by parents to a son in 592, a sibling settlement in 600 that locks the sale in place, a workshop contract, two wine loans, then another house next door.

The papers never say whether Ioannes resented the arrangement, whether Tarsene argued before she agreed, or whether everyone in the family knew from the start that Pachymios was the child best placed to hold the property together. They do show the mechanics. Two parents who could not write paid for a proper deed, a proper valuation, and proper title papers so that one weak piece of the estate would not be torn apart later. Their children honored that decision, and Pachymios kept building from it.
