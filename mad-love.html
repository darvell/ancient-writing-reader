The Industry

The spell Theon commissioned was not unusual. It was a commodity.

Across the cities, towns, and villages of Roman Egypt – and across the wider Mediterranean – love magic was an industry with practitioners, clients, suppliers, and standardized products.4 The magical papyri that survive from Egypt, known collectively as the Papyri Graecae Magicae (PGM), contain dozens of spells designed to compel erotic desire: attraction spells (agogai), binding spells (katadesmoi), separation spells, jealousy-prevention spells, impotence spells aimed at rivals.5 These are not the idle scribblings of lonely people. They are the working manuals of professional magicians – recipe books compiled over centuries, copied and recopied, refined and adapted to local conditions, and sold to paying customers.6

The professionals who produced these spells are difficult to see in the historical record, partly because the authorities disapproved of their work and partly because they themselves cultivated mystery. Roman law criminalized certain forms of magic – the Lex Cornelia de sicariis et veneficis of 81 BCE lumped magical practitioners together with poisoners and assassins.7 Later emperors legislated repeatedly against divination, enchantment, and sorcery, suggesting that repeated legislation was necessary because the laws were repeatedly ignored.8 In Egypt, the practitioners appear to have been drawn from a varied population: displaced temple priests whose traditional institutions had lost state support, itinerant ritual specialists who moved between communities, literate scribes who supplemented their income by producing magical texts alongside legal documents.9

Some scholars have called them “freelance priests.”10 The phrase captures something real. These were overwhelmingly men, the few female practitioners attested in the sources being exceptional, who possessed specialized knowledge of religious ritual, divine names, liturgical formulae, and sacred languages, and who deployed that knowledge commercially.11 They operated in the margins between official religion and criminal enterprise.

Their clients, by contrast, are vividly present. The applied spells – the finished products deposited in graves and at crossroads and in rivers – name real people. Theon son of Proechia. Euphemia daughter of Dorothea. Horion son of Sarapous, who wanted Nike daughter of Apollonoutos to fall in love with Pantoutos son of Tmesios, and paid for a spell that specified a duration of five months.12 The unnamed person who sought “attraction” of one person to another, filling in the blanks of a formulary that used the placeholder “NN” – ho deina, “such-a-one” – where the specific names would be inserted.13 These are named individuals, fixed in time and place by the matronymic identifications that magical practice demanded. In magic, you were identified not by your father but by your mother, because maternity was certain and paternity was not, and the gods – or the demons – needed certainty to find the right target.14

Two Kinds of Love

Christopher Faraone, in a landmark study published in 1999, identified a distinction in ancient love magic that had eluded previous scholars. There were not one but two different types of love spell, aimed at two different emotional states.15

The first type, which Faraone called eros magic, was designed to induce uncontrollable passion – the maddening, burning, sleepless desire that the Greeks called eros and that Theon’s spell describes in anatomical detail. These were aggressive spells, used primarily by men against women. Their language was the language of torture: burning, binding, dragging, piercing. Their goal was to destroy the victim’s capacity for autonomous will and replace it with a compulsive need to seek out the spell’s commissioner. The verb most commonly used in these spells was agein – “to lead,” “to drag,” “to bring” – and the spells themselves were called agogai, “drawings” or “attractions.”16

The second type, philia magic, aimed at a different target. These spells sought to induce affection, warmth, loyalty, fondness – the steady companionship that the Greeks called philia. They were used primarily by women to retain or recover the love of husbands and partners. Their language was the language of healing and protection, not assault. Their ingredients tended toward consumable substances – potions mixed into food or drink, ointments applied to the body – rather than the grave deposits and lead tablets of eros magic.17

The distinction matters because it reveals the gendered structure of ancient desire. Men, in the magical imagination, were hunters. Their love magic was predatory, aimed at capturing a quarry that had not consented to be caught. Women were defenders, seeking to maintain what they already had against the threat of loss or abandonment. The magical practitioners who served both clienteles operated on different registers, offering different products to different markets.

Theon’s spell belongs entirely to the first category. It is eros magic of the most extreme kind. Its language is that of assault, seizure, and the annihilation of the victim’s will. “Burn her limbs, her liver, her female body,” the spell commands the demons of the grave.18 “If she has another in her embrace, let her cast him aside and forget him and hate him.”19 This is not a request for mutual affection. It is an attempt to engineer obsession – a one-sided, coercive, consuming desire that would strip Euphemia of everything she valued and deliver her, broken, to Theon’s feet.

The Technology

The evidence of the PGM and the surviving applied spells allows us to reconstruct the process with considerable specificity. The client – Theon, or someone like him – would approach a magical practitioner with a problem. The practitioner would consult his repertoire – a papyrus roll or codex containing formulae for various occasions – and select the appropriate procedure.20

Some procedures were simple. A spell might require nothing more than writing certain words on a piece of papyrus or a lead tablet, folding it, and depositing it in the appropriate place. Others were elaborate. The most famous love spell in the PGM – the great binding spell of PGM IV, lines 296 through 466 – required the construction of two wax figurines: one representing the war god Ares, standing with a sword, and the other representing the female target, kneeling with her hands bound behind her back.21 Thirteen bronze needles were to be driven into specific parts of the female figurine’s body – brain, eyes, ears, mouth, hands, heart, stomach, genitals, feet – while the practitioner recited a formula naming the target and describing the desired effect.22

The figurine and a lead tablet inscribed with the full text of the spell were then bound together with a thread and deposited in the grave of someone who had died young (aoros) or by violence (biaiothanatos).23 This was not a casual choice. The entire theory of love magic rested on the power of the restless dead.

The materials themselves carried meaning. Lead was the preferred medium for binding spells – katadesmoi, from the verb katadein, “to bind down” – because it was cold, heavy, and associated with death and the underworld.24 The coldness of lead mirrored the coldness the spell sought to impose on the target’s relations with everyone except the commissioner. The weight of lead would press down on the victim’s will. The malleability of the metal made it easy to inscribe, fold, and sometimes pierce with a nail – the nail itself an act of sympathetic binding, fixing the target in place.25

Papyrus served as an alternative medium, especially in Egypt, where it was cheaper and more readily available. Theon’s spell was written on papyrus. So was the spell commissioned for Horion son of Sarapous.26 In some cases, the papyrus was rolled tightly, inserted into a hollow reed or a small metal tube, and deposited alongside the other magical objects.

Other materials included the hair of the target (if available), clothing fibers, objects the target had touched, and – in some recipes – bodily fluids: menstrual blood, semen, saliva.27 These materials created what practitioners called a “sympathetic link” between the spell and its target. The logic was simple and ruthless: matter that had been part of the target’s body, or in intimate contact with it, retained a connection that magic could exploit. A spell inscribed on a lead tablet and wrapped with a strand of Euphemia’s hair would have been understood as physically touching Euphemia through the medium of the hair, even at a distance.

The ink used for writing spells was itself often magical. Some recipes call for ink mixed with myrrh, others for blood – the blood of a donkey, a bat, or a goat, depending on the specific procedure.28 The PGM occasionally specifies the use of “Typhon’s ink” or “Isis’s ink” – proprietary formulations whose exact compositions may have been trade secrets of particular practitioners.29

The Grave

The grave was not merely a convenient hiding place. It was the engine of the spell.

Ancient Mediterranean religion held that certain categories of dead persons were not at rest. Those who died before their allotted time – the aoroi, “untimely dead” – lingered in a state of frustrated incompleteness, unable to pass fully into the underworld until their natural lifespan had elapsed.30 Children, adolescents, women who died in childbirth, soldiers killed in their youth – all were aoroi. Similarly, those who died by violence – the biaiothanatoi – were believed to remain agitated and volatile, their spirits unable to find peace because their deaths had been unnatural.31

These categories of the dead were believed to be both powerful and available. Powerful, because their frustrated energy could be directed toward worldly tasks. Available, because they had not yet departed into the deep reaches of the underworld, where Osiris ruled and from which communication was more difficult. They were, in effect, spiritual laborers – idle, restless, and susceptible to commands from those who knew how to address them.32

Theon’s spell addresses these spirits directly. “I adjure you, demons lying here,” it begins. “You who are lying here and who are nourished here and who linger here, and the untimely dead youths who are here.”33 The Greek text uses the word kouroi aoroi – “untimely youths” – specifying that the dead being invoked are young and therefore especially charged with unfulfilled energy.34

The spell then offers both a reward and a threat. If the demons comply and bring Euphemia to Theon, he promises to give them “Osiris Nophrioth, the brother of Isis, and he will take away the cold water and your souls will rest.”35 This is an extraordinary offer. The restless dead are suffering – specifically, they are denied the cool water that Osiris provides to the blessed dead in the Egyptian afterlife. The spell promises to rectify this: deliver Euphemia, and you will receive the comfort of Osiris’s care. Your suffering will end.

But if the demons disobey, the threat is cosmic. “The sun will not set beneath the earth, neither Hades nor the cosmos will exist.”36 And more specifically: “Eonebouth will burn you.”37 The threats escalate – the practitioner invokes the “inexorable god Abrasax,” the “seven thrones,” the god “who holds the air” – piling divine authority upon divine authority, making disobedience not merely dangerous but cosmologically impossible.38

This theology of the grave – the restless dead as cosmic errand-runners, bribable with Osirian blessings and threatened with annihilation – was neither exclusively Greek nor exclusively Egyptian. It was both, and more. The spells of Roman Egypt are products of a religious landscape in which Egyptian funerary beliefs, Greek underworld mythology, Jewish divine names, and Mesopotamian demonology had been blended over centuries of cultural contact into something genuinely new.39

The Language

The most striking feature of Theon’s spell is the density of its magical language. Alongside the intelligible Greek – the commands, the names, the promises and threats – runs a parallel text of voces magicae: sequences of syllables that do not belong to any natural language but were believed to possess intrinsic power.40

Iao Barbathi ao brimiavo chermari. These are the first magical words in Theon’s spell, invoked as attributes of “the unconquered god.”41 Iao is immediately recognizable to scholars of ancient religion: it is the Greek rendering of the Hebrew divine name YHWH, the tetragrammaton, the unpronounced name of the God of Israel.42 That a fifth-century Egyptian love spell opens by invoking the God of Israel – alongside an entity called Barbathi and another called Brimiavo – tells us everything about the syncretic religious environment in which this magic operated.

Later in the spell, the magical names multiply. The practitioner invokes the “all-seeing god” with a litany: ia ia a a io io io ie ie ie ouoa Adonai.43 Adonai is another Hebrew divine name – “my Lord” – further binding Jewish theology into the spell’s architecture. The text then invokes “him who sits upon the four corners of the winds” – a phrase that could be Egyptian, Jewish, or Christian in origin – before launching into the great alphabetic sequence: twenty-four magical names, one for each letter of the Greek alphabet, from Akrammachamari through Orion, culminating in Psychopompoiaps – a Greek compound meaning “soul-guide,” a title of Hermes – and Orion alethine, “true Orion.”44

The formula Maskelli Maskello Phnoukentabaoth oreobazagra appears in the spell’s climactic section.45 This is one of the most widely attested magical formulae in the ancient world, appearing in spells from Egypt, Greece, and across the Roman Empire. Scholars have debated its origin – some derive Maskelli from the Hebrew maskil (“psalm of wisdom”), others connect it to Egyptian or Coptic roots – but its function is clear: it is a formula of cosmic compulsion, believed to bind not merely human victims but the gods themselves.46

Following the Maskelli formula, the spell deploys a second series of power words: rhexichthon hippochthon pyrichthon pyripeganyux.47 These are Greek compounds – “earth-breaker, horse-earth, fire-earth, fire-peg” – that seem to invoke elemental forces of destruction. Their presence alongside Hebraic divine names and Egyptian funerary theology exemplifies the radical syncretism of Greco-Egyptian magic: any source of power, from any tradition, could be harnessed to the task of making Euphemia love Theon.

The voces magicae were not incidental decoration. Ancient magical theory held that certain sounds possessed inherent power, independent of their meaning in any human language.48 The seven Greek vowels – alpha, epsilon, eta, iota, omicron, upsilon, omega – were particularly potent, associated with the seven planets and the seven tones of the cosmic harmony. Theon’s spell contains sequences of vowels (ieo iiiiaïa eia iao iae iao) that were understood as resonating with these celestial frequencies, tuning the spell to the music of the spheres.49

The theory, in other words, was acoustic. The correct sounds, pronounced in the correct sequence, vibrated in sympathy with cosmic forces and compelled those forces to act. The practitioner was not merely requesting divine intervention. He was triggering it, the way a key triggers a lock – mechanically, inevitably, irresistibly.

The Client

Who was Theon?

We know his name: Theon, son of Proechia. The matronymic identification – standard in magical texts – tells us his mother’s name but nothing about his father or his social position. “Theon” was one of the most common names in Greek-speaking Egypt, meaning “divine” or “of the gods.”50 “Proechia” is rarer but attested. Neither name tells us whether Theon was wealthy or poor, educated or illiterate, young or old.

We can infer some things. He had money. Commissioning a love spell was not free. The practitioner’s time and expertise, the materials (papyrus, ink, possibly wax figurines or lead tablets), the access to a suitable grave – all of this cost something. The price is never stated in the surviving texts, but parallels from the ancient world suggest that magical services were neither trivially cheap nor ruinously expensive.51 They were accessible to people of moderate means – artisans, merchants, minor officials – and it was precisely these people, the people whose desires exceeded their social power, who formed the core clientele of the love-magic industry.

Theon could write, or he employed someone who could. The spell is literate – its Greek, while full of phonetic spellings and grammatical irregularities that suggest a scribe working in a living dialect rather than literary Attic, is competent and purposeful.52 The phonetic spellings are themselves revealing. Forms like demones for daimones, kimenos for keimenos, tychin for tychein – these are not errors but reflections of the spoken Greek of fifth-century Egypt, a language in which vowels had shifted and diphthongs had simplified.53 The scribe wrote the spell as it sounded, not as a classical grammarian would have demanded.

The date – “Hathyr 25, second indiction” – places the spell in the Egyptian calendar, in the month of Hathyr (roughly November), during a year whose indiction cycle was the second.54 If we take the indiction as a Roman tax cycle, the possibilities include 413/414, 428/429, 443/444, 458/459, 473/474, 488/489, or 503/504 CE – any second-indiction year within the fifth-century range that the papyrus’s handwriting suggests.55

The ten-month duration is perhaps the most human detail in the spell. Theon did not want Euphemia forever. He wanted her for ten months. This specificity argues against a simple narrative of obsessive madness. Theon was calculating. He knew what he wanted, he knew how long he wanted it, and he budgeted accordingly. Perhaps ten months was the term the practitioner offered at a particular price. Perhaps Theon envisioned a specific outcome – a pregnancy, a period of cohabitation that would create social facts on the ground, a trial period after which Euphemia’s “choice” would be confirmed by habit. We do not know. But the precision reminds us that love magic was a transaction, not a frenzy.

Euphemia

Of Euphemia daughter of Dorothea, we know nothing beyond her name and her mother’s name.

We know that Theon wanted her. We know that she did not, apparently, want him – or at least did not want him enough. The spell’s language makes this clear: “If she has another in her embrace, let her cast him aside.”56 Euphemia may have been attached to someone else. She may have been married. She may simply have been uninterested.

We know that she had parents – a mother named Dorothea, and presumably a father whose name the spell did not need because magical identification ran through the maternal line.57 She had brothers and sisters, because the spell commands her to leave them. She had a home, because the spell commands her to “leap from every place and every house.”

What we do not know is whether any of this mattered. The spell was written, deposited in a grave, and entrusted to the restless dead. Did Euphemia wake that night in November with an inexplicable longing? Did she find herself unable to eat, as the spell demanded? Did she abandon her family and go to Theon?

Or did she live her life entirely unaware that somewhere in a cemetery, in the dirt beside a corpse, a piece of papyrus was commanding the universe to destroy her autonomy?

The silence of the evidence is itself a kind of testimony. We have the spell. We do not have Euphemia’s diary, or her response, or her complaint to the authorities, or her letter to a friend describing a strange and unwelcome visitor. The archive of love magic is, by its nature, one-sided. It preserves the voices of those who desired, not those who were desired. It documents the demand, not the supply.

Horion's Spell

Three centuries before Theon, in the second century CE, another man made a similar purchase.

His name was Horion, son of Sarapous. He wanted Nike, daughter of Apollonoutos, to fall in love with a man named Pantoutos, son of Tmesios.58 The spell was brief – far shorter than Theon’s – and its most striking feature was its reference to the medium through which it would operate. The papyrus text includes the word “mummy” – the dead body beside which the spell was to be deposited – as a technical notation rather than an invocation.59

Horion’s spell also specified a duration: five months.60 This is half the time Theon requested, and raises the question of whether duration was a variable that could be adjusted – whether the practitioner offered, in effect, a menu of options. Five months of compelled love. Ten months. A year. The pricing, if there was pricing, may have scaled accordingly.

The names in Horion’s spell are worth pausing over. Nike is a common name – “Victory” – but Apollonoutos is less common, a compound name meaning “of Apollonou” or possibly deriving from a specific settlement. Pantoutos is unusual, perhaps a local Egyptian name rendered in Greek. Tmesios, Pantoutos’s mother, is similarly distinctive. These are not the names of the Alexandrian elite. They are the names of the Egyptian provincial population – men and women of mixed Greek and Egyptian heritage, living in the towns of the Fayum or the Nile Valley, speaking Greek as a second language, and turning to magical practitioners when their love lives required supernatural intervention.61

Was Horion acting on his own behalf, seeking Nike for himself under a pseudonym? Or was he a go-between, commissioning the spell on behalf of Pantoutos? The text does not say. Love magic sometimes involved intermediaries – friends or relatives who approached the practitioner on behalf of the lovesick client, perhaps because the client was too distraught, too embarrassed, or too geographically distant to do so personally.62

The Formulary

Not all surviving magical texts are applied spells – finished products deposited with the dead. Many are formularies: the recipe books of the practitioners themselves, containing instructions for producing spells on demand.

The attraction spell fragment from the third or fourth century CE (corpus ID 25722) is such a formulary. Its text uses the placeholder ho deina – “NN,” “such-a-one” – where the names of the client and target would be inserted: “(Let) NN (attract) NN.”63 Alongside the attraction formula, the same papyrus contains a “sleeplessness spell” – a procedure for depriving the target of sleep until they capitulated – and references to a lamp ritual, roses, and the command “not to endure.”64 These are the component parts of a love spell, laid out for the practitioner’s reference, waiting to be assembled and customized for a paying client.

Another formulary fragment, from the third century CE (corpus ID 36209), describes an “attraction procedure using a dog” – agoge epi kunos.65 The spell was to be written in myrrh ink on a flax leaf using hieratic script, then deposited with the body of someone who had died by violence (biothanatos). The use of a dog in the ritual may connect to the role of dogs as liminal creatures, associated with both the domestic and the wild, the living and the dead – creatures that moved between worlds and could therefore serve as messengers between them.66

These formularies reveal the practitioner’s craft in its raw state. They show us not the finished product but the workshop: the shelves of recipes, the categorized procedures, the modular components that could be combined in different ways to address different client needs. A practitioner with a well-stocked formulary could offer a complete range of erotic services: attraction spells, binding spells, separation spells (to break up a rival couple), sleeplessness spells, burning spells (to inflame desire), cooling spells (to extinguish a rival’s passion), and spells to ensure sexual potency or its absence.67

The Figurines of Antinoopolis

The most spectacular archaeological evidence for ancient love magic comes not from a papyrus but from a clay pot.

In the late nineteenth century, excavators working in the cemetery of Antinoopolis – the city Hadrian founded in Middle Egypt in 130 CE, after the drowning of his beloved Antinous – discovered a small clay vessel containing two objects.68 The first was a clay figurine of a woman, kneeling with her hands bound behind her back. Thirteen needles had been driven into her body – into her brain, her eyes, her ears, her mouth, her solar plexus, her hands, her genitals, her feet. The second was a thin lead tablet, inscribed with a spell.

The spell named its target: Ptolemais, daughter of Aias, also known as Horigenes. And its commissioner: Sarapammon, son of Area.69 The text was a variant of the great binding spell preserved in PGM IV, lines 296 through 466 – the same spell that called for the construction of two wax figurines, one representing Ares with a sword and the other representing the bound woman.70

In Sarapammon’s applied version, the spell commands the spirit of a deceased person to go to Ptolemais and “not allow her to eat, not allow her to drink, not allow her to sleep, not allow her to be at peace, but drag her by the hair and the guts until she does not reject me, Sarapammon.”71 The language is nearly identical to Theon’s spell, three centuries later, suggesting that both drew on a common tradition of magical formulae that was transmitted, with variations, across centuries and hundreds of miles.

The Antinoopolis find was extraordinary because it united what scholars usually encounter separately. The PGM provides the recipe. The lead tablets and papyri provide the applied text. But only at Antinoopolis do we have the complete assemblage: the figurine, the tablet, the container, and the archaeological context of their deposition in a grave. We can see, for once, the entire process – from the practitioner’s instruction manual to the finished product, buried in the earth beside the corpse whose restless spirit would carry the message to its target.72

The Letter of Serenus

Not everyone who was desperate in love turned to magic.

In the second century CE, a man named Serenus wrote a letter to a woman named Isidora – “his beloved sister,” in the conventional address of Egyptian family correspondence – that stands as one of the most raw documents of emotional anguish in the ancient world.73

“I assure you that ever since you left me I have been in mourning,” Serenus writes, “weeping by night and lamenting by day. Since we bathed together on Phaophi 12, I never bathed nor anointed myself until Athur 12.”74

One month. Serenus did not bathe for one month after Isidora left. In a culture where daily bathing was a social expectation and personal hygiene was closely linked to religious purity and civic respectability, this was not merely unpleasant. It was a declaration of collapse. Serenus was performing his grief on his own body, making his interior desolation visible and smellable to everyone around him.

“You sent me letters which would have shaken a stone,” he continues, “so much did your words move me.”75 Isidora, wherever she was, had written to him – letters whose content we can only guess at, but whose emotional force Serenus acknowledges with a phrase borrowed from epic poetry. Letters that could shake a stone.

Then the accusation: “Apart from your saying and writing ‘Colobus has made me a prostitute,’ he said to me, ‘Your wife sent me a message saying: He himself has sold the chain and himself put me in the boat.’”76 This is a tangle of competing narratives. Isidora accuses a man named Colobus of having “made her a prostitute” – a claim whose precise meaning is uncertain but whose emotional register is unmistakable. Colobus, in turn, tells Serenus that Isidora has been telling people that Serenus sold her chain (a gold necklace, perhaps part of her dowry) and “put her in the boat” – abandoned her, cast her out, trafficked her.

The letter ends with a plea that is almost a threat: “See how many times I have sent to you! Whether you are coming or not, let me know.”77

Serenus’s letter is not a magical text. It contains no voces magicae, no invocations of the dead, no promises to Osiris or threats of cosmic annihilation. It is a letter from a man who is miserable because a woman he loves has left him, and who wants her back, and who cannot make her come back by any means other than words.

The contrast with Theon’s spell is instructive. Theon, faced with a similar situation – a woman who would not come to him – turned not to persuasion but to compulsion. He did not write to Euphemia. He wrote to the dead, commanding them to override Euphemia’s will and deliver her body to his control. Serenus, by contrast, wrote to Isidora herself. He begged. He wept. He stopped bathing. He reminded her of what they had shared. And then he asked the only question that a letter can ask: are you coming, or not?

Two men, two strategies, and between them the entire spectrum of how the ancient world dealt with the agony of desire: from prayer to magic, from appeal to coercion, from the recognition of the other’s autonomy to its denial.

The World That Made the Spells

What kind of world produces an industry in erotic compulsion?

The answer begins with demography. In Roman Egypt, as in much of the ancient Mediterranean, life was precarious and short. Average life expectancy at birth was perhaps twenty-five to thirty years, though those who survived childhood could expect to reach their forties or fifties.78 Women married young – often in their early to mid-teens – and men somewhat later.79 The resulting pool of potential partners was small, and it was further constrained by social conventions about status, ethnicity, religion, and geography. A man in a small Egyptian town might realistically have access to a very limited number of marriageable women. If the one he wanted did not want him, his options were few.

The legal structure was no help. Roman law, as codified in the Gnomon of the Idios Logos for Egypt, regulated marriage with bureaucratic precision.80 Romans could not marry their sisters (Egyptians traditionally could and did, but this practice was increasingly curtailed). Women under fifty could not marry men over sixty without fiscal penalty. The unmarried and childless faced inheritance restrictions.81 A man who wanted a woman who was already married, or who belonged to a different civic status category, or who was simply unwilling, had no legal mechanism of compulsion.

Magic offered what law did not. It promised to override the will of the target, to circumvent social barriers, to make the impossible possible. And the cost was, by ancient standards, manageable. The materials were cheap – papyrus, lead, ink, wax. The practitioner’s fee was negotiable. The only thing the client needed, beyond money, was access to a grave, and in a land where the dead were everywhere – where the cemeteries of thousands of years pressed against the edges of every settlement – that was no obstacle at all.82

The world that made the spells was also a world of religious pluralism that had, by the Roman period, become religious eclecticism. The traditional Egyptian gods – Osiris, Isis, Horus, Thoth, Anubis – coexisted with the Greek Olympians, the Jewish God (whose secret name, never pronounced by the faithful, was freely deployed in magical formulae), early Christian theology, and the broader apparatus of Near Eastern demonology.83 Magic drew on all of these traditions at once, not out of curiosity but out of pragmatic maximalism. If one divine name might compel the demons of the grave, twenty divine names from five different traditions might compel them more effectively.

This syncretism was not unique to magic. It was the religious condition of the eastern Roman Empire, intensified in Egypt by the unusual survival of a functioning priestly class whose traditional temple cult had lost state patronage but whose knowledge of ritual, sacred language, and divine names remained commercially valuable.84 The magical papyri are the most vivid surviving documents of this religious world – a world in which Osiris and Yahweh and Abrasax and Jesus could all be invoked in the same breath, not because the practitioner believed in all of them equally but because the client’s emergency – love, sickness, legal trouble, financial ruin – demanded every available weapon.

The Spell's Afterlife

Theon’s spell survives because Egypt’s climate preserved the papyrus and because an archaeologist found it. Most love spells did not survive. They were written on lead, which was melted down and reused. They were deposited in graves that were never excavated. They were written on papyrus that rotted in the damp soil of Greece or the rain-soaked ground of Italy. The surviving corpus of ancient love magic is a tiny fraction of what once existed – the fossil record of a once-thriving ecosystem of desire and coercion.

What we have is enough to show us the scope of the industry. Hundreds of love spells survive from the ancient world – from Greece, from Egypt, from North Africa, from Syria, from Italy, from Gaul, from Britain.85 They span a millennium, from the fifth century BCE to the fifth century CE and beyond. They were written in Greek, Latin, Demotic Egyptian, Coptic, and occasionally in multiple languages on the same object. They targeted men and women, heterosexual and homosexual desires, marriages and affairs and one-night obsessions.86

The magical traditions of the PGM fed directly into Coptic Christian magic, which preserved many of the same formulae, divine names, and ritual structures, repackaged with Christian vocabulary but fundamentally continuous.87 The practitioners adapted. When the temples closed and the old gods lost their public cults, the ritual specialists who had long operated in the margins added new divine names to their repertoire. Abrasax and Iao were joined by the Trinity and the archangels. The grave deposits continued. The lead tablets were inscribed with crosses as well as pentacles. The industry endured because the demand endured and people continued to want what they could not have.

Theon wanted Euphemia. He wanted her for ten months. He paid someone to write the words. He paid someone to place the papyrus in a grave, beside the bones of a person who had died too young or too violently to be at rest. He believed – or at least he acted as though he believed – that the dead would hear him, that the cosmos would bend toward his need, that the universe itself would conspire to deliver one woman’s body to the feet of one man.

We have the papyrus. We do not have Euphemia’s answer.
