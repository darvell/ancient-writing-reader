The Manager's Accounts

Heroneinos, Phrontistes of Theadelphia, the Fayum, 249-268 CE

Estate Management Wine Production Inflation Crisis Agricultural Labor 3rd Century CE Fayum

51 primary documents · 73 notes

I. The Account

The document opens with a formula that tells us nearly everything about the social structure it serves:

“To Aurelius Apianos, former exegetes, councillor of the most illustrious city of the Alexandrians, and however he is styled, from Aurelius Heroneinos, phrontistes of Theadelphia.”6

Two men, both named Aurelius – the universal nomen that every free person in the empire had carried since Caracalla’s edict of 212 CE.7 But the similarity ends there. Apianos is a former exegetes, one of the senior magistrates of Alexandria, a member of the city council – the boule – of the greatest city in the eastern Mediterranean. He is a man of enormous wealth, a landowner whose holdings stretched across multiple villages, a figure of civic consequence. The phrase “and however he is styled” – kai hos chrematizei – is a bureaucratic courtesy acknowledging that his titles are too numerous to list in full.

Heroneinos is his phrontistes. The word means something like “the one who thinks about things” – from phrontis, thought, care, anxiety. In practice it meant estate manager, steward, the man on the ground. He lived in Theadelphia. He supervised the workers. He kept the books. He sent the accounts up to his master in Alexandria. He did this for at least nineteen years, from roughly 249 to 268 CE, across the reigns of Decius, Trebonianus Gallus, Aemilian, Valerian, and Gallienus.8

Nineteen years of monthly accounts. Nineteen years of receipts and expenditures, wine tallies and wheat distributions, donkey-loads and canal repairs. Nineteen years of letters from an imperious master who complained about jujubes and rotten figs. The archive that survives from those years is one of the most detailed records of economic life to come down to us from the ancient world.

P.Flor. 3.321 is striking for the system it reveals. The format is standardized. Receipts are listed by category – wine, bank transfers, wheat – with quantities, unit prices, and totals. Expenditures follow the same logic: each worker named, his role specified, his monthly wage recorded to the obol. The whole thing balances. It is a formal report, submitted monthly to an absentee landlord who expected precision.9

II. The Estate

The document tells us who Heroneinos managed for. What it cannot convey is the scale of what he was managing.

The Appianus estate was not a single farm. It was an enterprise spread across the Arsinoite nome – the Fayum – with holdings in at least half a dozen villages: Theadelphia, Euhemeria, Philoteris, Dionysias, Sathro, Sentrepaei, and smaller parcels at Boubastos, Ptolemais Hormou, Ibion Argaiou, and elsewhere.10 Each village had its own phrontistes. Heroneinos managed Theadelphia. Eirenaios managed Euhemeria. Palas managed Philoteris. Horion managed Dionysias and perhaps Sentrepaei. Above them all sat Alypios, the chief administrator, based not in any village but at the nome capital of Arsinoe – modern Medinet el-Fayum – the hub from which orders radiated outward and accounts flowed back.11

The Theadelphia division alone comprised roughly 250 arourai of arable land, mostly wheat, and approximately 100 arourai of vineyards.12 An aroura was about two-thirds of an acre. So we are talking about something like 165 acres of grain land and 65 acres of vines – a substantial operation but not a latifundium on the scale of the great Roman senatorial estates in North Africa or southern Italy. What distinguished the Appianus estate was not its size but its organization.

Dominic Rathbone, whose 1991 study Economic Rationalism and Rural Society in Third-Century AD Egypt remains the definitive analysis of the archive, demonstrated that the estate operated with what he called a “sophisticated, standardized system of accounting” that coordinated activities across all its village divisions.13 Every transfer of goods between villages required a written receipt. Every monthly account followed the same format. Cash accounts were kept separate from commodity accounts – wheat had its own ledger, wine its own. The system was not double-entry bookkeeping in the Venetian sense, but it approached that level of methodical tracking. Rathbone argued that it challenged the common assumption of economic primitivism in the ancient world.

Apianos himself never appears in Theadelphia. He ruled from Alexandria, communicating by letter, receiving monthly reports, issuing instructions that ranged from strategic decisions about wage rates to petulant demands for fruit. He was, in every respect that matters, an absentee landlord.

The distance was not trivial. Alexandria sat at the northern edge of the Nile Delta. The Fayum was a depression in the desert southwest of Memphis, connected to the Nile valley by a single canal. Letters and goods moved by donkey, camel, ox-cart, and boat. A letter might take days. A shipment of wine, longer. And yet the system functioned – month after month, year after year, through plague and inflation and the capture of an emperor by the Persians.14

III. The Wine

Wine was the centerpiece of the Theadelphia economy, and the archive allows us to track its production with a specificity that borders on the obsessive.

On the thirtieth of November in the year 264 CE, Heroneinos’s son Maximos – who served as diastoleus, an agent or distributor, for the estate – signed a receipt. He had received, he wrote, from the produce of the estates of “the euschemon” – the honorable one, a common euphemism for the landowner – “produce of year 12, wine: one thousand nine hundred monochra.”15

One thousand nine hundred single-capacity jars. From a single harvest season. And that was not the end of it. Subsequent entries in the same document record additional shipments: 266 monochra on the twenty-ninth of Hathyr, 242 on the first of Choiak, 244 on the fourth – a running total that exceeded 2,600 jars from one harvest.16

A second document, from the following year, records daily wine shipments over the course of a single month – October 265 CE. The entries are relentless: the fourteenth of Phaophi, 102 Oxyrhynchite jars plus 3 more plus 40 double-jars. The sixteenth: 114 jars plus 6 double-jars. The eighteenth: 73 jars plus 26 double-jars. The twenty-first, the twenty-third, the twenty-ninth, the twenty-eighth – the clerk recording entries slightly out of order, perhaps catching up at the end of a long day. Grand total for the month: over 1,500 single-jar equivalents, plus additional shipments rolling into early November.17

The vocabulary alone tells a story. Monochra: single-capacity jars, the standard unit. Dichora: double-capacity jars, worth two monochra each. Oxyrhynchite jars: a regional type, named for the city of Oxyrhynchus. Knidion: Knidian jars, an imported form that carried prestige. Spathion: another jar shape. Deuterochytoi: second-pressings, the inferior wine wrung from already-exhausted grapes. This was a world that knew its containers the way a modern winery knows its barrel sizes.18

The harvest was a military operation. In August of 260, Alypios wrote to Heroneinos that he was sending a steward named Herakleides “so that he may make the arrangement for the grape-harvest.” But Heroneinos was to wait two or three days “so that the light tasks may also come together for you, and also so that the wine may become properly good, for you know that the season is now later than usual.” Then the warning: “do not trust the vintagers – carry out the harvest and write to me about it.”19

Do not trust the vintagers. The karponai, the hired grape-harvesters, were seasonal workers with their own interests. Perhaps they skimmed. Perhaps they rushed, picking grapes too early or pressing them carelessly. The instruction suggests that quality control was Heroneinos’s personal responsibility, and that lapses had occurred.

Quality was tested by the most direct method available. In March of 258, an administrator wrote to Heroneinos: “From testing by smell, you yourself select them, knowing that if poor quality wine is found among them – or anything else of poor quality – it is sent back to you.”20 Ek tes osmes dokimazon – having been tested by smell. The manager was expected to nose every batch. If vinegar slipped through, it came back.

And vinegar did slip through. In December of 257, a recipient named Monimos acknowledged a shipment – twelve dichora and twenty-four monochra, making fifty-six single-jar equivalents in total, delivered by donkey-drivers on seven female donkeys – and added, bluntly: “But do not wish to send me vinegar; for I have received instructions through the letter from the euschemon.”21 The landowner himself had apparently complained about the wine quality. The rebuke was passed down the chain.

Where did all this wine go? To Alexandria, primarily, via the nome capital. To other estate divisions as internal transfers. To wine-sellers – the oinopoloi – stationed at market towns, at a rate of fourteen drachmas per single-chous jar.22 To workers as part of their wages – the estate’s lowest-quality wine was used for this purpose.23 To VIP guests. To officials on inspection visits. And, at least once, to an old woman who had earned the landowner’s gratitude: “Release to Herais, the former wet-nurse, two two-chous jars of wine, without asking anything from her.”24

Two jars for a wet-nurse. A small kindness. Perhaps she had nursed Apianos himself. The document does not say. It says only: give her the wine, and do not charge her.

IV. Letters from the Master

Apianos’s letters to Heroneinos survive in enough quantity to reconstruct a personality – and it is not a pleasant one.

On the second of August, 252 CE, Apianos wrote about jujubes:

On other occasions it was written to you to send up the jujubes, and up to now you have not sent them. So at least now, send them, lest we treat you accordingly. If you are neglecting such trifling matters, how much more so the more important ones!25

The Greek word is zypha – the fruit of the ziziphus tree, a small, sweet, date-like berry that grew in the Fayum. Apianos was writing about a snack, but his tone is that of a man issuing a military command. The threat – “lest we treat you accordingly” – is left deliberately vague. The logic is familiar: if you cannot be trusted with the small things, what about the big ones?

Less than a year later, in July of 253, the figs:

Even if someone sends up the smallest thing, he ought to send it with a letter and indicate what was sent and through whom. But what you sent up was not so much as to keep a man and a donkey idle for four small bundles of rotten figs. From the poor quality and dryness and thirstiness of the figs, the neglect of the estate was apparent.26

The reasoning is devastatingly specific. The cost of transport – a man and a donkey, both occupied for the journey from the Fayum to Alexandria – exceeded the value of the goods transported. This was not merely disappointing fruit. It was bad logistics. And from the bad logistics, Apianos extrapolated the condition of the entire estate. Dry figs meant a dry manager. Rotten fruit meant rotten management.

In November of 257, bread and hares:

Some days ago I wrote that good small loaves should be made and sent up to us, and you did not do it… There is also need for hares… The physicians need it both for eye-salves and for other medical uses.27

The small loaves – artidia – were a recurring demand. The hares are stranger. Ancient physicians used hare meat and fur in the preparation of kollyria – eye-salves – and other compounds.28 The connection between the Fayum’s hare population and the Alexandrian physicians’ medicine cabinets ran through Heroneinos’s office.

When a VIP visited the estate, the demands escalated:

Let my lord, his son Primus, the most worthy, have every provision and suitable wines and a piglet and small loaves and everything else, so that he may speak well of me to his father.29

“So that he may speak well of me.” The hospitality was reputation management. Primus was the son of someone whose opinion mattered to Apianos. Heroneinos was to produce the piglet, the wine, the loaves – and through them, the good word.

But the master was not only imperious. On the 28th of February, 258, the order to release wine to the former wet-nurse Herais, “without asking anything from her,” suggests a capacity for kindness – or at least for sentimental loyalty to the household’s past.30 And when the question of settler wages arose, Apianos responded with money, not reprimand: “Another one hundred drachmas have now been sent to you for the pulling of the hay. You wrote asking to be informed of the wage that the settlers are accustomed to receive.”31 The relationship was not only tyranny. It was also, at times, functional.

V. Alypios and the Thin Piglet

If Apianos was the voice of the master, Alypios was the voice of the machine.

The chief administrator’s letters are models of bureaucratic efficiency. Each one specifies a task, a deadline, and a consequence. But one letter, from the 17th of January, 266 CE, rises above the functional:

God willing, expect us on the 23rd, arriving at your place. The moment you receive my letter, have the bath-house heated by all means, and have beams brought into it; also collect chaff from everywhere, so that we may bathe in warm water, since it is winter. For we have decided to stay with you, since we intend to inspect the remaining small estates.32

Chaff for fuel. Beams for the bath-house structure. An inspection tour in winter, requiring hot water, provisioning, and accommodations. And then the sentence that has survived eighteen centuries because it is, in its way, perfect:

And take care to have ready all the other provisions as well; but above all, a fine piglet for those who are with us – and let it be a good one this time, not like last time, thin and useless!33

Me hosper tote ischnon kai achreion. Not like last time, thin and useless. Somewhere in the recent past, the inspection party had arrived at Theadelphia, sat down to dinner, and been served a pig that was not up to standard. Alypios remembered. He would not allow it again.

The same letter orders fish: “send to the fishermen so that they may bring us fish.”34 The estate maintained its own fishermen on the irrigation canals and lake margins of the Fayum. Their names survive in a separate document – Alleion, Polion, Pellas, Melas, Peras – registered under Heroneinos’s supervision.35 The fishing grounds were contested. A fragment records a dispute: “he said to him that it was not permitted for another to set foot in Sathro, it was appropriate for him to fish in other places; for the shore there extends six schoinoi” – roughly thirty-six kilometers of coastline, fought over.36

Alypios also handled the festival. On the 6th of June, 257: “Knowing that your management needs expenditures, and especially on account of the festival of the Amesysia and the provision of hay, I have sent you a release-order for one hundred single-chous jars of wine from Ibion Argaiou.”37 One hundred jars for a local festival whose deity is now forgotten. The wine came from another estate division – an inter-village transfer, recorded on both ends, the accounting system grinding through even the rituals of communal celebration.

VI. The Sacrifice

On the 14th of July, 250 CE, a slave named Aurelius Euprodokios stood before the officials appointed to superintend sacrifices in Theadelphia and performed a ritual that the emperor Decius had ordered of every person in the empire:

I have always continued to sacrifice to the gods, and now in your presence, in accordance with the orders, I have sacrificed and made libation and tasted of the offerings, and I request you to certify this.38

Euprodokios was a slave – doulos – of Aurelius Apianos. The certificate he submitted was a libellus, a bureaucratic proof of compliance. A second certificate from the same period belongs to Aurelius Sarapammon, identified as a servant – hyperetes – of Appianus: “Always sacrificing to the gods, now too, in your presence, in accordance with the orders, I sacrificed and poured the libations and tasted the offerings.”39

The Decian edict targeted Christians, but it required compliance from everyone – free, slave, servant, citizen, alien. That even slaves on a private estate in a Fayum village were required to file certificates tells us how far the machinery of imperial religious policy reached. Whether Heroneinos himself filed a certificate we do not know. None survives in his name. But these documents were generated in his village, concerning his employer’s people, in the same filing system that held his monthly accounts.

VII. The Workforce

The monthly accounts allow us to reconstruct the permanent staff at Theadelphia with unusual precision. A separate wheat account from 251-252 CE confirms what the cash accounts show: each worker received one artaba of wheat per month – an artaba being roughly 40 liters of grain, enough to feed a person for about a month – in addition to cash wages – a subsistence floor beneath the hierarchy of pay:

Heroneinos: 1 artaba; Pontikos: 1 artaba; Pouaris donkey-driver: 1 artaba; Abies donkey-driver: 1 artaba; Kastor donkey-driver: 1 artaba; Polion cowherd: 1 artaba; Deios donkey-guard: 1 artaba; Kiot foreman: 1 artaba.40

The wheat ration was egalitarian. The cash was not. Cowherds like Hatres and Aseis earned forty-eight drachmas per month – more than the assistant manager Pontikos at twenty, and only eight less than Heroneinos himself.41 Ox-drivers earned comparably: Dorion at forty-eight, Patas at thirty-four drachmas and two obols.42 Donkey-drivers fell between eight and seventeen drachmas.43 At the bottom, the household slave Hermes received four drachmas – a pittance, likely supplemented by food and lodging.44

The pay structure tells its own story. Cowherds and ox-drivers were specialists. They managed the animals on which everything depended – the oxen for ploughing and water-wheels, the cattle for trampling grain. Their skills commanded premiums. Donkey-drivers, by contrast, were transport workers, numerous and replaceable. The builder Dionysios, at four drachmas, was perhaps intermittent.

Workers also received low-quality wine and oil as part of their compensation.45 The estate fed its people from its own production, reserving the best wine for sale and distributing the rest internally. When inflation accelerated in the early 260s, the system adapted. In November 262: “Give to Sarmates and Neilos, in exchange for what they receive monthly as rations, each one whole artaba of wheat, from the present month of Hathyr one and a half artabai, while they continue to receive what they previously received in money and oil.”46 The shift from cash to wheat was protective. Grain held its value. The coinage did not.

VIII. Sodike

Among the ostraca – the pottery fragments used as receipts for grain transport – a name appears.

Theadelphia, produce of year 1, by private beast. Sodike, shepherdess: 1 donkey-load.47

A woman. A poimenissa – the feminine of poimen, shepherd. She appears on at least five ostraca between 254 and 258 CE, carrying grain from Theadelphia’s granary by donkey. Sometimes one animal. Sometimes four: “Year 5, Tybi 23, Teis. Sodike, shepherdess: donkeys 4.”48

Four donkeys is not a token contribution. It is real transport work, the same job the men did. Women are nearly invisible in the operational records of the Appianus estate – absent from the wage lists, absent from the management correspondence. Sodike appears not as wife or mother or dependent but as a worker with her own animals, receiving receipts under her own name.

We know nothing else about her. The ostraca are receipts, not biographies.

IX. The Children of Pontikos

On the 29th of October, 255 CE, a letter arrived from Syros, one of the mid-level administrators:

Many times I have instructed you concerning the children of Pontikos, who is in your keeping; for they are not strangers to the household, as you yourself know. Dioskoros has therefore requested that a letter be delivered to you, so that in whatever he may need from you, you may offer yourself.49

“Many times I have instructed you.” Syros had written before. Heroneinos had not acted – or had not acted enough.

Pontikos appears in the wage accounts of April 252 at twenty drachmas per month, but by the time of the complete account from the mid-to-late 250s, he is gone.50 Dead, perhaps. Incapacitated. The letter does not say. It says only that his children needed help, that they were “not strangers to the household” – ou gar allotrioi eisin tou oikou – and that Heroneinos had been neglecting them.

We should not judge too quickly. Heroneinos had workers to pay, vines to prune, grain to ship, donkeys to manage, wine to test by smell, and an employer who threatened him over jujubes. The children of a former assistant may have been one obligation too many. But the letter survives, and its tone – patient, firm, slightly exasperated – suggests a man who needed to be reminded of his debts to the dead.

X. The Infrastructure

Irrigation was the foundation. The Fayum’s agriculture depended entirely on a canal system that channeled water from the Nile via the Bahr Yussef. Every canal required maintenance; every sluice gate required tending; every season required coordination.

A letter from August 257:

I have learned from Syros that there is some work remaining in the canal near the sluice-gates, and the work urgently needs to be done… I have sent a soldier to you, so that from all those who benefit, the workforce may be deployed and the work completed quickly before the irrigation season presses, in order that all of us may easily have water supply.51

A soldier – dispatched for canal maintenance. The military was involved in civilian infrastructure, the army filling gaps as the civil administration weakened. And the ploughs needed building: “I have sent Maximos, the steward of Pakis, to you, so that a plough may be made for him… I have also sent four nails for its assembly.”52 Four nails. The record is that specific. And when an axle broke at Ptolemais Hormou in July 253, Apianos wrote directly: “By all means find us another suitable axle of ten cubits or even more… since the one there suddenly broke.”53 Ten cubits – about fifteen feet. The carpenter Perigenes and an ox-cart were dispatched to handle the transport.

Forty thousand reeds were ordered for the estate in a single transaction – “Herakleides the oikonomos reminded me that 40,000 reeds should be released to you from the Ktesis” – for trellising, fencing, or construction.54 An irrigation machine at Moithymis needed completion: “make every effort to have it completed, since there is need of it.”55 The estate was infrastructure. Without the canals, the machines, the ploughs, the axles, and the nails, the wine and grain were nothing.

XI. The Transport

Donkeys were the workhorses. Organized into four-animal teams, they hauled wine, grain, building materials, and provisions along the tracks between villages. A letter from September 252: “I have sent to you today through Karbon, donkey-driver, with four pack-animals, and Aionios, donkey-driver, with five pack-animals – altogether 9, with 16 two-chous jars, individually: 32 single-chous jars.”56 Nine donkeys, thirty-two jars. One day’s shipment on one route.

Camels handled the long-distance work: “Load the camels that have come down with dates and send them up to the city, reporting the quantity… If you do not have the full load ready, let them be loaded with firewood.”57 Never send a camel back empty. Dead freight is wasted freight.

The ostraca from the Theadelphia granary record the daily rhythm: “Of the granary of the village of Theadelphia, from the produce of the 7th year. Apianos, former exegetes: to Koba, 5 donkeys.”58 “Of the granary of Theadelphia, produce of the 1st year, by beasts of Mouchis. Appianos, former exegetes: to Alexandria, 11 sacks.”59 “Heroninos, manager, on behalf of Dionysias, private beasts: donkeys 20.”60

Twenty donkeys in a single convoy. Eleven sacks to Alexandria. Five donkeys to Koba. The numbers accumulate, receipt after receipt, into a picture of constant motion.

And then the emergencies. In August 255: “My brother Apollonios has cut wood in the field. Therefore have it dragged to the village before the flood waters by the bull-teams at your disposal.”61 The Nile flood was coming. Timber cut and left in the low-lying fields would be swept away. The bull-teams had to drag it to higher ground before the water arrived. A race against the river, and Heroneinos was expected to win it.

XII. The Prices

The archive is, among other things, a record of inflation.

In August 250: wine sold at ten drachmas and six obols per jar.62 By April 252: twelve drachmas.63 By roughly 256: fourteen.64 By 261: sixteen.65 And then, in 262, the currency collapsed: forty-two to forty-four drachmas per jar.66

The quadrupling between 261 and 262 reflects the severe debasement of the antoninianus under Gallienus, when the silver content of Roman coins fell below five percent.67 The Alexandrian tetradrachm suffered proportionally. Prices had been rising steadily for a decade; now they lurched. The Plague of Cyprian – perhaps smallpox, perhaps a hemorrhagic fever, spreading along military and trade routes since roughly 250 – compounded the monetary crisis with a demographic one.68

And yet the estate continued to function. The monthly accounts still balanced. The workers were still paid. The wine was still shipped. When the coinage could no longer hold value, wages shifted to wheat. The system adapted. It did not collapse.

This is Rathbone’s central insight. The archive does not document an economy in free fall. It documents an economy under stress, managed by people who understood what was happening and adjusted.69 Heroneinos did not write treatises about monetary policy. He switched his workers’ pay from cash to grain. That was his treatise.

XIII. The Taxes

The estate existed within a fiscal system that was, by the 260s, backed by force.

A letter from Alypios, December 267: “Let me know how many artabas you have measured out to the dekaprotoi for the tax-payments, and how many you still owe as arrears… And if there is any remainder, do not measure it out without a letter from me.”70 The dekaprotoi – literally “the ten first men” – were local tax collectors personally liable for the taxes of their district. The instruction to hold any surplus reveals the tension: Alypios wanted to control the timing, to manage the flow.

A year earlier, in December 266, the threat became explicit: “Make those who are at your place from the estate of Sabeina pay our men the equal-rent, since they are being troubled about the arrears of the per-capita tax. A soldier was about to be sent against them, but I held back.”71

A soldier. For tax collection. Alypios had restrained himself – “I held back” – but the weapon was visible. Tax enforcement backed by military force was becoming the norm, not the exception. The soldier dispatched for canal maintenance, the soldier threatened for tax arrears – these were the symptoms of an empire whose civilian institutions were thinning out.

XIV. What We Cannot Know

The archive never lets us properly see Heroneinos. No physical description survives. None of his own letters survive. We hear Apianos complaining at him, Alypios instructing him, workers passing through his accounts, and tax officials pressing from the edges. Heroneinos himself appears mostly as the man who keeps the columns straight.

That is the odd intimacy of the dossier. We know what he paid a cowherd, how many jars of wine left the estate in October, what sort of piglet Alypios did not want served again, and that someone had once sent rotten figs to Alexandria. We do not know whether Heroneinos was proud of a good vintage, frightened by a bad tax season, or exhausted by masters who noticed missing jujubes faster than they noticed competent management.

An earlier text from 206 CE mentions another Heroneinos at Theadelphia, probably an older relative.72 The name suggests a family rooted in the same village across generations. Then the papers fade in the late 260s.73 Gallienus was killed in September 268. The empire staggered on. The archive does not say whether Heroneinos did.

What it leaves is a working life compressed into figures: forty drachmas a month, one artaba of wheat, shipment after shipment, order after order. The great crisis of the empire is present here, but mostly as pressure on a man still expected to make sure the books balanced by the end of the month.
