The Ties That Bind

Herakleides Registers His Sons at Antinoopolis, 133 CE

Social Mobility Citizenship Family Archives Antinoopolis Fayum 2nd Century CE

12 primary documents · 26 notes

What he brought to the office

The paperwork behind him came from Tebtunis in the Fayum, where his family had spent years lending, borrowing, and settling.

One early loan says silver was handed over “from hand to hand out of his house” ἐκ τοῦ οἴκου αὐτοῦ.67 Another measures a grain loan by the lender’s own standard.8 Tebtunis had a notarial office, temple land, and enough surviving documents to show how tightly village families could bind one another with paper.910

Herakleides knew what that world could do when things went wrong. In one settlement an older Herakleides was still paying part of an obligation that had already moved through one set of heirs to another.1112 Other papers show taxes paid on property held in a woman’s name and penalties paid over trouble in the public archives.1314

The worst of the lot belongs to Herakleides himself. He had lived in an unwritten marriage with a woman named Apia, also called Herakleia.15 She died after bearing a child. Her mother accused him of presenting another infant in place of the one her daughter had borne.16 By the time the settlement was drafted, the baby was dead as well. The text speaks of “the child, either of Apia also called Herakleia or a strange one.”17

That was the man in the Antinoopolis office in 133: not someone starting from nothing, but a Fayum villager with a paper trail already running behind him.

What the boys received

The registration did not end with that one visit.

By 151 CE Lysimachos, now grown, was filing his own child’s registration within the deadline for Antinoopolis’s support fund.1819 He swore by the emperor’s fortune and by Osirantinoos, Antinous remade as a local god.20 His younger brother Philosarapis appears in the same civic world in an epikrisis tied to ephebic status.21

The father had got them into the city. The sons show what entry meant. Their names were in its lists. Their children could claim support through it.

What the city did not erase

Antinoopolis did not remove the family from the Fayum.

In 168 CE Lysimachos and Philosarapis petitioned against their brother Philantinoos.22 He had pledged their jointly owned maternal slave, Martilla, to a creditor in the Arsinoite and kept the money for himself.23 The brothers wanted the petition entered into the record as evidence.24 They were still handling danger the old way: by writing it down before the next round began.

The last papers leave them there. A descendant hired musicians for a six-day festival in Tebtunis in 223.25 Another was delivering wheat to the granary officials there in 224.26 The registry at Antinoopolis had done its work long before. The archive ends with village business again: music for a festival, grain for the storehouse, and a family that never quite left the place it had come from.
